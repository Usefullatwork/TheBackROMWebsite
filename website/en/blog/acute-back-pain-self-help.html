Acute Back Pain: What to Do in the First Hours and Days

Self-help, exercises, and when to seek treatment

January 20, 2026 10 min read

When your back suddenly "gives out" - often called lumbago or a back spasm - it's natural to be scared. The pain can be so intense you can barely move. But remember: even though the pain is enormous, the cause is rarely dangerous.

This guide will help you through the first hours and days with practical advice that works. Most people improve significantly within a few weeks with the right approach.

TL;DR - Acute Back Pain

  • Don't panic: Rarely dangerous, even if intense
  • Movement over rest: Short walks beat bed rest
  • Ice then heat: Ice first 48h, then heat
  • 90% improve: Within 6 weeks
  • Red flags: Groin numbness or bladder issues = seek help

Seek Emergency Care Immediately For:

  • Saddle anesthesia: Numbness in buttocks, around genitals, or inner thighs
  • Bladder/bowel problems: New difficulty urinating or incontinence
  • Progressive weakness: Increasing weakness in one or both legs
  • Fever + back pain: May indicate infection

These symptoms are rare but require urgent evaluation.

The First Minutes: Don't Panic

When your back suddenly "gives out", the pain can be so intense you can barely move. But remember:

  • Even though the pain is enormous, the cause is rarely dangerous
  • Most acute back episodes are due to muscle or joint problems that resolve
  • The body's protective reaction (muscle tension) often amplifies the pain
2

The First 24-48 Hours

Find a Comfortable Position

  • On your back: With a pillow under your knees to relieve the lower back
  • Side lying: With a pillow between the knees
  • Avoid: Positions that significantly worsen pain

Move Gently - Don't Just Lie There!

Important

Prolonged bed rest (more than 1-2 days) makes the situation worse! Research shows that short walks every hour are better than lying still.

  • Gentle movements within pain limits promote healing
  • Total immobilization leads to stiffer muscles and longer illness

Ice or Heat?

  • First 48 hours: Ice can reduce inflammation and relieve pain (15-20 min, always with cloth between)
  • After 48 hours: Heat loosens muscle tension and increases blood circulation
  • Use what feels best - the body often gives good signals

Pain Relief

  • Paracetamol: First choice for the first days
  • Ibuprofen: If needed (follow package instructions, take with food)
  • Avoid: Long-term use without medical evaluation
3

Days 2-7: Gradual Progression

Movement Is Medicine

Research consistently shows that gradual increase in activity gives better results than rest:

  • Increase walks gradually - start with 5-10 minutes, build up to 20-30
  • Simple movement exercises (see below)
  • Avoid heavy lifting and forceful twisting
  • Return to normal activities as soon as pain allows

Sleep and Rest

  • A firm mattress often provides better support
  • Side lying with a pillow between the knees relieves the back
  • Avoid stomach sleeping which increases the curve in the lower back
4

Exercises for the First Days

1. Pelvic Tilts (Lying)

  1. Lie on your back with bent knees and feet on the floor
  2. Gently roll your pelvis forward (lower back flattens against the surface)
  3. Roll your pelvis back (lower back gets a slight curve)
  4. 10 repetitions, 3 times daily

2. Knee-to-Chest

  1. Lie on your back with bent knees
  2. Gently pull one knee toward your chest
  3. Hold for 20-30 seconds
  4. Switch sides. Repeat 3 times per side.

3. Cat-Camel

  1. Get on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips
  2. Arch your back up toward the ceiling (cat) - let the head drop
  3. Sway your back down (camel) - lift the head
  4. Move gently through a pain-free range, 10 repetitions

4. Side-Lying Rotation

  1. Lie on your side with bent knees
  2. Gently rotate your upper body toward the ceiling
  3. Hold for 10 seconds
  4. Switch sides

When Should You Seek Treatment?

Seek Treatment Early If:

  • Pain is very intense and prevents daily activities
  • You have radiating pain to the leg or foot (possible sciatica)
  • You struggle to walk or stand upright
  • Pain prevents sleep multiple nights in a row
  • You've had similar episodes before
  • You're unsure about the cause

Benefits of Early Treatment

Research suggests that early intervention can:

  • Reduce duration of problems - shorter illness course
  • Prevent chronification - break the vicious cycle before it establishes
  • Give you self-management tools - exercises and strategies you can use yourself
  • Identify underlying cause - so treatment can be targeted

  2. Pain relief through gentle techniques adapted to the acute phase
  3. Mobilization of stiff joints and muscles
  4. Advice and exercises tailored to your situation
  5. Plan for gradual return to normal activities

Prognosis & Prevention

Typical Recovery Timeline

  • 1 week: Many notice significant improvement
  • 2-4 weeks: Most are much better
  • 6 weeks: About 90% are back to normal activities

But: Recurrence is common - 40-70% have new episodes within a year. That's why prevention is important!

Prevention of Future Episodes

  • Regular strength training for core muscles
  • General physical activity - walking, swimming, cycling
  • Good ergonomics at work and home
  • Varied work positions - don't sit too long in the same position
  • Healthy body weight - reduces load on the back
  • Lifting technique - bend your knees, hold the load close to your body

FAQ

Should I get an X-ray or MRI right away?
No, usually not. Imaging is rarely necessary for acute back pain without red flags - even if disc herniation is suspected. Most improve anyway within 6 weeks. MRI may be relevant if pain doesn't improve or with neurological symptoms.
How long should I be off work?
As short as possible! Research shows that early return to (adapted) work gives better prognosis than prolonged sick leave. Discuss graded sick leave or workplace adaptations with your practitioner.
Can I exercise with acute back pain?
Light activity like walking is good. Intense training should be postponed until the acute phase is over (typically 1-2 weeks). Heavy strength training and activities with lots of twisting/bending should wait until pain is under control.
Is a hard or soft mattress better?
A moderately firm mattress is often best. Too hard causes pressure, too soft provides poor support. Most important is finding a position where you can sleep - sleep is important for healing!
Does chiropractic help with acute back pain?
Yes. Chiropractors have good experience with acute back episodes and use adapted techniques in the acute phase. Often early treatment can shorten the course and give you concrete strategies for faster improvement.
