ADHD, Autism and Jaw Pain: An Often Overlooked Connection

December 28, 2026 8 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Jaw Problems
  4. ADHD, Autism and Jaw

TL;DR - Neurodivergent and Jaw

  • Increased risk: Studies show 2-3x higher incidence of bruxism in people with ADHD
  • Stimulant medications: Can worsen teeth grinding as a side effect
  • Sensory challenges: Autism can cause hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity in the jaw
  • Stimming: Jaw clenching and chewing can be self-regulating behavior
  • Adapted treatment: Shorter sessions, clear communication, sensory considerations

Why Neurodivergent Individuals Have Higher Risk

Several factors contribute to increased incidence of jaw problems in people with ADHD and autism:

With ADHD

  • Motor restlessness: Constant movement can include unconscious jaw activity
  • Impulsivity: Tendency to bite on things, gum, nails
  • Attention difficulties: Hard to notice own jaw tension
  • Sleep problems: Common with ADHD, increases risk of nocturnal bruxism
  • Stress regulation: Challenges with emotion regulation can increase muscle tension

With Autism (ASD)

  • Repetitive movements: Jaw clenching or chewing can be stimming (self-stimulation)
  • Sensory needs: Strong sensory preferences can include proprioceptive input from the jaw
  • Anxiety: High incidence of anxiety in autism, which increases muscle tension
  • Interoceptive challenges: Difficulties perceiving body signals like pain
  • Masking: Social masking requires energy and can increase tension

Research on the Connection

Studies show a clear connection:

  • Children with ADHD have 2-3 times higher incidence of bruxism
  • Sleep bruxism is significantly more common in children with ADHD
  • Autistic children often have atypical chewing patterns
  • Sensory sensitivity in ASD correlates with oral motor dysfunction

Important to Know

Many adults with undiagnosed ADHD or autism are first discovered when they seek help for physical complaints like chronic jaw pain or headaches. If you recognize yourself in many points here, it may be worth exploring further.

Medications and Jaw Problems

Stimulant Medications (Ritalin, Vyvanse, Dexedrine)

Common ADHD medications can affect the jaw:

  • Bruxism: Teeth grinding is a known side effect
  • Jaw clenching: Increased muscle tension
  • Dehydration: Dry mouth can worsen oral problems

Tip: Often worst at the start, may improve over time. Talk to your doctor about dose adjustment if problems are significant.

Other Medications

  • SSRI/SNRI: Can cause bruxism (20-24% of users)
  • Antipsychotics: Can cause movement disorders including in the jaw

Sensory Challenges and Treatment

Autistic individuals may have unique challenges with jaw treatment:

Hypersensitivity

  • Discomfort with face/jaw touch
  • Overwhelming experience of treatment situation
  • Sound, light, and smells at the clinic can disturb

Hyposensitivity

  • Doesn't notice pain until it's severe
  • Can bite hard without noticing
  • Needs stronger pressure to perceive treatment

Adapted Treatment Approach

I adapt treatment for neurodivergent patients:

Practical Adaptations

  • Predictability: Clear explanation of what will happen
  • Shorter sessions: If necessary, several shorter treatments
  • Breaks: Option to pause at any time
  • Sensory considerations: Adjust light, sound, temperature
  • Communication: Direct and concrete information

Home Exercises

I adapt the exercise program:

  • Simple, short routines that are easy to follow
  • Visual instructions/reminders
  • Connect exercises to existing routines
  • Realistic goals that account for executive function challenges

Self-Help Strategies

For ADHD

  • Fidgets: Use other channels for motor restlessness than the jaw
  • Reminders: Alarm or post-its to check jaw tension
  • Chewing resistance: Chew jewelry or sugar-free gum for short periods (better than teeth/nails)
  • Physical activity: Regular movement reduces general tension

For Autism

  • Alternative stimming: If jaw clenching is stimming, find other proprioceptive activities
  • Sensory tools: Chew jewelry designed for adults
  • Stress reduction: Reduce masking where possible
  • Routines: Build jaw relaxation into existing routines

When Stimming Is Involved

If jaw activity is part of self-regulation:

  • I don't try to eliminate all stimming
  • Focus on replacing harmful stimming with safer alternatives
  • Acknowledge that stimming has a function
  • Collaborate on solutions that work for you

Neuro-Affirming Approach

I view neurodivergent conditions as part of natural human variation, not as defects that need to be fixed. The goal is to help you with physical complaints, not to change who you are.

Collaboration With Other Practitioners

For best results, I'm happy to collaborate with:

  • GP/psychiatrist about medications
  • Psychologist/occupational therapist about strategies
  • Dentist about bite splint if needed
