Botox vs Manual Treatment for Jaw Pain: What Does Research Say?

December 28, 2026 8 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. TMJ Disorders
  4. Botox vs Manual Treatment

TL;DR - Botox vs Manual Treatment

  • Botox: Can provide pain relief, but evidence is limited and effect is temporary (3-6 months)
  • Manual treatment: Strong recommendation in guidelines, equal pain relief, better functional improvement
  • Guidelines: Conservative treatment (incl. manual therapy) recommended first
  • Cost: Botox must be repeated every 3-6 months, manual treatment gives lasting effect
  • Side effects: Botox can cause temporary weakness, swallowing difficulties, asymmetry

What is Botox Treatment for Jaw?

Botox (botulinum toxin) is a neurotoxin that temporarily paralyzes muscles by blocking nerve signals. For TMD and bruxism, it is typically injected into:

  • Masseter: The chewing muscle on the side of the jaw
  • Temporalis: The temple muscle
  • Sometimes the pterygoid muscles

The idea is that by weakening the overactive muscles, the force during teeth grinding is reduced and thus the pain.

What Does Research Say About Botox?

Effect on Pain

Studies show mixed results:

  • Some studies find significant pain relief compared to placebo
  • Other studies find no difference from saline injections (placebo)
  • A Cochrane review concluded that evidence is of low to moderate quality

Effect on Bruxism

  • Botox reduces muscle force during teeth grinding
  • But it is unclear if it actually reduces grinding - some studies show no effect on number of grinding episodes
  • It protects teeth by reducing force, but does not treat the cause

Important Limitation

Botox is not approved for TMD or bruxism by drug authorities (FDA/EMA). All use is "off-label," meaning the practitioner takes responsibility for the indication.

What Does Research Say About Manual Treatment?

Manual treatment for TMD has a stronger evidence base:

  • BMJ guideline (2023) gives strong recommendation for therapist-assisted mobilization and trigger point treatment
  • Systematic reviews show approximately 4 point pain reduction on 10-point scale
  • Significant improvement in mouth opening ability (approx. 15mm immediately)
  • Effect persists over time, especially combined with exercises

Comparison: Botox vs Manual Treatment

Factor Botox Manual Treatment
Pain relief Moderate evidence Good evidence
Functional improvement Limited Significant (opening, movement)
Duration 3-6 months Long-term with self-training
Treats cause No (symptom only) Partly (muscle, joint, posture)
Side effects Weakness, swallowing difficulties, asymmetry Temporary soreness
Cost over time High (repeated injections) Lower (typically 6-12 treatments)
Guideline recommendation Second-line treatment First-line treatment

Side Effects of Botox in Jaw Muscles

  • Muscle weakness: Temporary reduced chewing ability
  • Swallowing difficulties: If it spreads to swallowing muscles
  • Asymmetry: Uneven distribution can give crooked face
  • Pain at injection site
  • Bone thinning: Newer research suggests possible reduced bone thickness in jaw with prolonged use
  • Reduced bite force: Can affect ability to chew hard food

When Might Botox Be Appropriate?

Botox may be considered as second-line treatment in special cases:

  • Severe bruxism not responding to conservative treatment
  • Significant muscle hypertrophy (enlarged masseter)
  • Long-term symptoms after 3-6 months of manual treatment without improvement
  • As supplement to, not replacement for, other treatment

Recommended Approach

Based on research and guidelines, I recommend this sequence:

Stepwise Treatment for TMD

  1. First 2 months: Information, self-exercises, habit management
  2. Month 2-4: Manual treatment (chiropractor/physiotherapist), possibly bite splint
  3. After 4-6 months without improvement: Consider specialist, possibly Botox

Conclusion

Botox can be a useful tool in treating severe TMD cases, but it should not be the first choice. Manual treatment has:

  • Stronger evidence base
  • Fewer side effects
  • Lower cost over time
  • Better functional improvement
  • Potential long-term effect

Try conservative treatment first - most respond well without needing Botox.
