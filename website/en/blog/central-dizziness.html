Central Dizziness - When the Brain is the Cause

January 20, 2026 10 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Dizziness caused by disease in the brain (brainstem/cerebellum), not the ear
  • Most common causes: Vestibular migraine (1-3%), posterior circulation stroke, MS, vertebral dissection
  • Chiropractor's role: Identify red flags and refer quickly - HINTS examination for acute vertigo
  • Red flags: Call 113 for acute dizziness together with double vision, slurred speech, severe unsteadiness, intense headache
  • Consultation: 45 min first consultation, 30 min follow-up

What is Central Dizziness?

Most people who experience dizziness have a harmless cause in the inner ear - like BPPV or inflammation of the balance nerve. But in about 10-15% of cases, the problem is in the brain itself.

Central dizziness occurs when the brainstem or cerebellum - the areas that interpret signals from the balance organ - become damaged or disturbed. The cause can be blood clot, bleeding, MS plaque, or other neurological conditions.

This is the "serious" dizziness that requires rapid clarification. A cerebellar stroke can in some cases present with only dizziness as the sole symptom - without paralysis or speech difficulties. Studies show that 10.4% of isolated cerebellar infarctions present with vertigo, nystagmus, and unsteadiness that look exactly like ordinary ear dizziness.

The Difference from Ear Dizziness

In central dizziness, symptoms are often paradoxically milder than in peripheral dizziness - the carousel spins slower - but the consequences are potentially much more serious.

Characteristic Peripheral (ear) Central (brain)
Intensity Often intense spinning Milder, but persistent
Nystagmus Always same direction Can change direction
Hearing loss Common Rare
Walking Can walk with support Often cannot sit without support
Improvement with rest Yes No

Severe unsteadiness is an important distinguishing feature. Severe truncal ataxia - defined as inability to sit without using arms for support - occurred exclusively in stroke patients (34% vs. 0% in vestibular neuritis).

Causes - From Most to Least Common

1. Vestibular Migraine (most common for recurring episodes)

Vestibular migraine is the most common cause of recurrent central dizziness. The condition affects 1-3% of adults and accounts for about 7% of patients at dizziness clinics.

2. Posterior Circulation Stroke

Cerebellar or brainstem strokes account for 20-25% of all ischemic strokes. These strokes are particularly insidious because symptoms can resemble ordinary ear dizziness.

Among patients presenting to emergency departments with isolated dizziness, about 4.5% have a stroke. But among high-risk patients with acute vestibular syndrome, the proportion rises to 20.7%.

TIA (mini-stroke) is an important warning sign. 22% of patients with posterior circulation stroke had transient neurological symptoms in the previous 90 days - most commonly dizziness.

3. Vertebral Dissection (especially important in younger people)

A tear in the wall of the neck artery (vertebral artery) is rare but important - especially in people under 50. Vertebral dissection causes 10-25% of strokes in adults under 50 and presents with dizziness or vertigo in 58% of patients.

4. Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Vestibular symptoms affect 49-59% of MS patients during the course of the disease. Symptoms are caused by demyelinating plaques affecting the brainstem's vestibular nuclei.

5. Vestibular Schwannoma (benign tumor)

A slow-growing, benign tumor on the balance nerve. Annual incidence is about 1 per 100,000. Typically causes gradual hearing loss in one ear and unsteadiness, rarely acute vertigo.

Red Flags - Call 113 for acute dizziness together with:

The 5 D's (symptoms from posterior circulation):

  • Dizziness - dizziness/vertigo
  • Diplopia - double vision
  • Dysarthria - slurred speech
  • Dysphagia - swallowing difficulties
  • Drop attacks - sudden falls without loss of consciousness

Other critical signs:

  • Severe unsteadiness (cannot sit without support)
  • Sudden, intense headache in back of head/neck
  • Numbness in face or body
  • New neurological symptoms after neck treatment

When stroke is suspected, minutes matter.

Diagnostics: The HINTS Examination

Clinical examination beats early MRI

A groundbreaking study showed that a simple clinical examination - HINTS - achieved 100% sensitivity and 96% specificity for detecting central causes of acute vestibular syndrome.

Even more important: HINTS detected strokes that early MRI-DWI missed in 12% of cases when imaging was performed within 48 hours.

The Three Components of HINTS

  1. Head Impulse Test: In peripheral damage, the reflex is impaired. In central damage, the reflex is intact - "normal is dangerous" in acute vertigo.
  2. Nystagmus: Peripheral nystagmus always beats in the same direction. Central nystagmus can change direction or be purely vertical.
  3. Test of Skew: Checks if one eye is higher than the other. Positive test indicates brainstem involvement with 96-98% specificity.

Rehabilitation After Central Damage

The brain is plastic - but it takes time

Vestibular rehabilitation has moderate evidence for effect in central injuries. A review of 15 randomized studies found that the combination of gaze stabilization exercises and balance training over four weeks gave the best results.

Rehabilitation Principles

  • Volume training: Thousands of repetitions to establish new nerve pathways
  • Compensation: Train vision and body sense to take over for damaged areas
  • Balance training: Uneven surfaces, stairs, dual-tasking
  • Safety: Fall prevention is critical in the initial phase
