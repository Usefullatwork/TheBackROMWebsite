Concussion in Sports: When Can You Play Again?

January 20, 2026 12 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Dizziness
  4. Concussion in sports

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Highest risk: Rugby (12 per 1000 hours), ice hockey (1.6-2.3), football (1.2)
  • New approach: Early controlled activity (24-48 hours) leads to faster recovery than strict rest
  • Typical recovery: 80-90% symptom-free within 14 days in adults; children often take longer
  • Return-to-play: 6-step protocol with minimum 24 hours at each step
  • Red flags: Worsening consciousness, seizures, repeated vomiting - call emergency services

What is sports-related concussion?

A concussion occurs when the brain is shaken inside the skull after a blow or impact. It doesn't have to be a direct hit to the head - a tackle, fall, or collision can be enough.

For an athlete, this is more than just discomfort. You need to be able to run at high speed, orient yourself visually, make quick decisions, and do all this simultaneously. Therefore, "feeling okay" on the couch isn't enough - the brain needs to be tested under load.

Concussion accounts for 5-9% of all sports injuries. In Norway, NRK recorded a minimum of 32 concussions in elite ice hockey during one season - approximately one every ten days. The concerning fact is that 2 out of 3 concussions are not recognized by the athlete themselves.

Which sports have the highest risk?

Injury rates vary significantly between sports. A large meta-analysis with over 3 million participants shows the following numbers per 1,000 playing hours:

Sport Concussions per 1000 hours
Rugby 7s12.3
Rugby league12.0
Rugby union7.9
Ice hockey1.6-2.3
Football1.2
Handball0.8-0.9

Sex differences: Women have 1.4-1.5 times higher risk in sports where both sexes participate.

Age: 70% of all sports-related concussions treated at emergency departments involve people under 18 years old.

Symptoms of concussion

Symptoms can vary significantly and don't necessarily appear immediately:

  • Headache
  • Dizziness and balance problems
  • Blurred vision or light sensitivity
  • Concentration difficulties ("foggy head")
  • Nausea
  • Fatigue and sleep problems
  • Irritability or mood swings

Important to know: A symptom picture that worsens over hours or days requires immediate medical evaluation.

Red Flags - Call emergency services for:

  • Worsening consciousness or increasing confusion
  • Seizures or twitching
  • One pupil larger than the other
  • Weakness, numbness or tingling in arms/legs
  • Severe or increasing headache
  • Repeated vomiting (more than 2 times)
  • Neck pain or stiffness
  • Loss of consciousness over 1 minute

With loss of consciousness, a neck injury should be suspected until proven otherwise.

What does new research say about treatment?

End of the "cocoon" approach

The Amsterdam Consensus Statement from 2023 - the most comprehensive review of concussion research ever (78,000 studies evaluated) - turns old practice upside down.

  • Previous recommendation: Total rest from all physical and cognitive activity until symptom-free.
  • New recommendation: 24-48 hours of relative rest, then gradual initiation of light aerobic activity. Strict rest ("cocooning") is not beneficial and can actually prolong recovery.

A randomized study with 103 adolescents showed that those who started controlled exercise early recovered in a median of 13 days, compared to 17 days for the rest-only group. Prolonged recovery over 30 days affected only 4% in the exercise group versus 14% in the rest group.

Return-to-Play: The 6-Step Protocol

You must be symptom-free for a minimum of 24 hours at each step before progressing. If symptoms worsen, go back one step.

Step Activity Goal
1Symptom-limited activityDaily activities that don't provoke symptoms
2Light aerobic exerciseWalking, swimming, stationary bike. Heart rate under 70% of max
3Sport-specific trainingRunning drills, passing, technique. No head contact
4Non-contact trainingMore complex drills, tactics, strength training
5Full contact trainingAfter medical clearance. Tackling, heading
6Return to competitionUnrestricted participation

Minimum time: With one step per day, the earliest return to competition is 6 days after becoming symptom-free. In practice, it often takes longer, especially for children and adolescents.

When do most people recover?

Adults (over 18 years):

  • 7 days: 85% symptom-free
  • 14 days: 80-90% clinically recovered
  • 30 days: Most fully recovered

Children and adolescents take longer:

  • 4 weeks: About 70% recovered
  • Average time to full clearance: 20 days
  • Prolonged recovery over 3 months occurs, especially in girls

Risk factors for prolonged recovery

  • Concentration difficulties at first examination (3.4 times increased risk)
  • Previous anxiety, depression, or sleep problems (2.5 times increased risk)
  • Female sex (1.7 times increased risk)
  • Loss of consciousness or memory loss at injury (1.9 times increased risk)
