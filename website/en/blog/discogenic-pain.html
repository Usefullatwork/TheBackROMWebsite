You're sitting in a meeting. After 45 minutes it starts - a deep, diffuse pain in the middle of your low back. Not sharp, not electric, just a relentless aching feeling. You've seen several practitioners and had an MRI, and everyone says the same thing: "Just mild wear and tear." But your pain feels anything but mild.

If this sounds familiar, you may have discogenic pain - pain that comes directly from the intervertebral disc without a visible herniation.

What is Discogenic Pain?

Discogenic pain means the pain comes from the intervertebral disc itself - without visible herniation pressing on nerves. It involves damage to the disc's internal structures, often called "Internal Disc Disruption" (IDD). The condition was first described by Crock in 1970.

Contrary to what many believe, this is one of the most common causes of chronic low back pain. A large study by DePalma and colleagues (Pain Medicine, 2011) examined 156 patients with persistent low back pain and found that 42% had discogenic pain as the main cause - the most frequent single diagnosis. Facet joints accounted for 31% and sacroiliac joints for 18%.

What Happens When the Disc Becomes Painful?

In discogenic pain, tears (annular tears) develop in the outer fiber ring. These tears trigger a cascade of events:

  • Chemical irritation: Inflammatory substances (TNF-a, IL-1b) build up in the tear zone
  • Nerve ingrowth: New pain nerve fibers grow into the normally nerve-free inner disc
  • Vascular ingrowth: New blood vessels follow the nerves as part of the body's repair attempt
  • Mechanical failure: Load redistributes from the disc center to the posterior fiber ring - exactly where the pain nerves are located

Who is Affected?

Discogenic pain differs from other back problems in that it typically affects younger adults. The DePalma study (2011) found that the younger the patient, the more likely chronic low back pain was caused by the disc. After about age 65, facet joint problems become the dominant cause.

Anatomically, 95% of disc problems occur at the two lowest levels: L4-L5 and L5-S1. Problems higher up in the spine only become more common after age 55.

Symptoms

Characteristics of Discogenic Pain:

  • Location: Deep, central pain in the middle of the low back
  • Character: Dull, aching - not sharp or electric
  • Possible radiation: Diffuse to buttock or thigh, but without sharp nerve pattern
  • Key finding: Poor sitting tolerance - often under 30 minutes

What Worsens and Relieves?

Worsened by: Prolonged sitting, forward bending, coughing, sneezing, lifting

Relieved by: Lying down, standing up, moving around, changing position regularly

What You DON'T Have with Pure Discogenic Pain

With pure discogenic pain, I see no signs of nerve root involvement: no numbness, no tingling, no muscle weakness, normal reflexes, and negative straight leg raise test. If you have such symptoms, it may point toward herniation with nerve compression - a different condition requiring a different approach.

Diagnosis

The DISCS Criteria

The DISCS criteria are a validated clinical tool for identifying discogenic pain. When 4 of 5 criteria are met, discogenic pain is very likely:

  • D - Duration: Chronic pain over 6 months, intensity at least 4/10
  • I - Increased pain: Worsening with forward bending and flexion activities
  • S - Sitting intolerance: Limited sitting tolerance, typically under 30 minutes
  • C - Central axial pain: Deep, central pain in the midline
  • S - Sustained hip flexion test: Positive test where lying on back with legs raised to 45 degrees reproduces the pain

MRI Limitations

MRI cannot reliably distinguish a painful disc from one that is just aging. While absence of degeneration on MRI makes discogenic pain less likely, presence of degenerative changes - which are very common in pain-free people - is not diagnostic.

Treatment

What Can a Chiropractor Do?

Manual therapy has moderate evidence for short-term pain relief in chronic low back pain. The Cochrane review found high-quality evidence for a small, statistically significant effect. A meta-analysis of 51 studies showed manipulation reduced pain and disability compared to exercise alone.

In practice, I combine manual therapy with active measures. The combination of manipulation, exercises and patient education produces better results than any of these alone.

Evidence-Based Exercises

Motor control training (core stabilization) has moderate evidence for chronic back pain.

The McKenzie method has moderate evidence especially for chronic discogenic presentation. Meta-analyses show McKenzie is better than other rehabilitation for chronic - but not acute - back pain.

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Avoid prolonged sitting: Get up and move every 25-30 minutes
  • Consider a standing desk: Alternating between sitting and standing can reduce load
  • Lifting technique: Keep back straight and bend at knees
  • Sleeping position: Side-lying with pillow between knees
  • Stay active: Discogenic pain is not dangerous - normal activity doesn't damage the disc

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

Seek Medical Help Immediately For:

  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Numbness in the saddle area (groin, inner thighs)
  • Rapidly increasing muscle weakness in the legs
  • Fever along with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Known cancer history with new back pain

Prognosis

Discogenic pain has a more challenging prognosis than many other musculoskeletal conditions. A 4-year follow-up study by Peng and colleagues (2012) followed 156 patients with confirmed discogenic pain and found:

  • 13% significantly improved
  • 7.6% somewhat improved
  • 67.2% remained unchanged
  • 12.2% got worse

This doesn't mean treatment is useless. On the contrary, it emphasizes that active management is necessary. Many patients learn to manage symptoms well with the right approach - even if they don't become completely pain-free.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can MRI show if I have discogenic pain?

Not reliably. MRI can show degenerative changes, but these are very common in pain-free people as well. Clinical examination with DISCS criteria and centralization tests are often more diagnostically valuable than imaging.

Is discogenic pain the same as a herniation?

No. With a herniation, disc material bulges out and can press on nerve roots, causing leg radiation with numbness or weakness. With discogenic pain, the pain comes from the disc itself without visible bulging or nerve involvement.

Can I exercise with discogenic pain?

Yes, and you should. Discogenic pain is not dangerous, and the disc doesn't get worse from normal activity. Avoid prolonged sitting and heavy loading in bent-forward positions, but otherwise stay active.

Why does it hurt to sit?

Sitting significantly increases pressure in the discs and pushes the core material backward toward the painful fiber ring. Additionally, the back bends slightly forward when sitting, which further loads the posterior part of the disc where the pain nerves are located.

Do I need surgery?

Surgery is rarely the first choice for discogenic pain. Conservative treatment with exercises, manual therapy and lifestyle changes should be tried first. Surgery is only considered if symptoms are severe and persistent despite adequate conservative treatment over time.
