When Dizziness Doesn't Fit: Is It BPPV or Something Else?

January 20, 2026 12 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog

  4. When Dizziness Doesn't Fit

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • BPPV accounts for only 17-39%: Most dizziness patients have another cause
  • Most common alternatives: Vestibular migraine (6-29%), PPPD (15-20%), Meniere's disease, vestibular neuritis, cervicogenic dizziness
  • Duration is key: Seconds = BPPV, minutes-hours = migraine/Meniere's, days = neuritis, months = PPPD
  • Red flags: Speech difficulties, double vision, swallowing problems, headache, can't stand/walk - call 113
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

Not All Dizziness is BPPV

You're not alone with this experience. BPPV actually accounts for only 17-39% of all dizziness cases in clinical practice (Bosner, BMC Primary Care, 2018). This means the majority of those seeking help for dizziness have something other than classic BPPV.

The problem is that many other conditions can resemble BPPV - or that BPPV itself can present in unusual ways. In this article, I'll go through the most important alternative causes of dizziness and how to recognize the differences.

Why Duration is Key to Diagnosis

The simplest way to distinguish between different causes of dizziness is to ask: How long do the episodes last?

  • Seconds (under one minute): Classic BPPV. Crystals in the ear trigger brief, intense dizziness with head movements.
  • Minutes to hours (5 min - 72 hours): Vestibular migraine. About 30% have attacks lasting minutes, 30% hours, and 30% several days.
  • 20 minutes to 12 hours: Meniere's disease. Attacks come with hearing loss, tinnitus, and pressure sensation in the ear.
  • Days to weeks (constant): Vestibular neuritis. Acute inflammation of the balance nerve causes persistent dizziness that gradually subsides.
  • Months (persistent daily): PPPD. Chronic unsteadiness worsened by movement and visual stimuli.

If your dizziness lasts longer than one minute per episode, it's probably not classic BPPV.

Most Common Causes of Dizziness - Ranked

1. BPPV (17-39% of clinical cases)

The most common vestibular diagnosis, but far from the only one. Lifetime prevalence in the population is 2.4%, and cumulative risk is approximately 10% by age 80.

Characteristics:

  • Brief dizziness (seconds) with head movements
  • Triggered by lying down, turning over in bed, looking up
  • Positive Dix-Hallpike test

But note: Up to 41% of BPPV patients do NOT have classic spinning sensation. Many describe rather a "nautical" feeling, unsteadiness, or lightheadedness.

2. Vestibular Migraine (6-29% of dizziness patients)

The second most common cause of recurrent dizziness, and severely underdiagnosed. Only 10% of those meeting diagnostic criteria are told that migraine causes their symptoms. The condition affects 0.98-2.7% of the general population.

Characteristics:

  • Dizziness episodes lasting minutes to days
  • Headache, light/sound sensitivity, or visual disturbances in at least half of attacks
  • Previous or current migraine history
  • Migraine patients have three times higher risk of developing BPPV

Important: When repositioning maneuvers don't work after three attempts, vestibular migraine should be strongly suspected.

3. PPPD - Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness (15-20%)

PPPD is the most common chronic dizziness condition in people aged 30-50. It often develops as a complication of other vestibular conditions - including untreated BPPV.

Characteristics:

  • Dizziness/unsteadiness most days for at least 3 months
  • Worsened by standing upright, movement, and busy visual environments
  • Often triggered by a previous vestibular event
  • Anxiety worsens the condition

Approximately 70% of PPPD cases start with a structural vestibular disorder like BPPV. This underscores the importance of early and adequate treatment of acute dizziness complaints.

4. Vestibular Neuritis (3.5-15.5 per 100,000/year)

Acute inflammation of the balance nerve, often following viral infection.

Characteristics:

  • Sudden onset, severe dizziness
  • Persists constantly for days to weeks (not attacks)
  • Nausea and vomiting common in acute phase
  • Worsened by head movements, but not triggered by specific positions
  • Hearing unaffected

BPPV can develop secondary to vestibular neuritis in approximately 10% of cases.

5. Meniere's Disease (0.12-0.5% of population)

Despite Meniere's being well-known, it's actually quite rare - about 10 times less common than BPPV.

Characteristics (the classic triad):

  • Dizziness attacks lasting 20 minutes to 12 hours
  • Fluctuating hearing loss (low-frequency)
  • Tinnitus and pressure sensation in the ear

Meniere's ALWAYS has hearing symptoms in addition to dizziness - unlike BPPV.

6. Cervicogenic Dizziness (6-10% of non-traumatic cases)

Cervicogenic dizziness is a controversial diagnosis without validated gold-standard criteria.

Characteristics:

  • Unsteadiness and "lightheadedness" - rarely true spinning
  • Temporal association with neck pain

Many initially diagnosed with neck-related dizziness turn out to have other conditions, especially BPPV.

7. Central Causes - Stroke and Tumors (3-5% in emergency departments)

The most dangerous category. Stroke in the posterior circulation causes dizziness as the main symptom in 47-75% of cases. About 25% present with isolated dizziness WITHOUT other neurological signs.

Red Flags - Call 113 for:

  • The dangerous D's: Dysarthria (speech difficulties), Diplopia (double vision), Dysphagia (swallowing difficulties), Dysmetria (missing with directed movements), intense spinning
  • Cannot stand or walk without support
  • Sudden, severe headache (especially in back of head)
  • Decreased consciousness or confusion
  • Sudden hearing loss with dizziness
  • Weakness or numbness in arm/leg

Why So Many Are Misdiagnosed

Misdiagnosis of dizziness is widespread. In emergency departments, 74-81% of peripheral vestibular disorders (including BPPV) are missed.

Atypical BPPV Presentations

37-41% of BPPV patients don't have classic "everything is spinning" experience. Many describe rather:

  • Nautical or rocking feeling
  • Foggy thinking ("brain fog")
  • General unsteadiness between attacks
  • Persistent "lightheaded" feeling

Diagnostic Tests Have Limitations

The Dix-Hallpike test for BPPV has only 48-88% sensitivity. A negative test does not rule out the diagnosis.

Overlapping Conditions

Vestibular migraine and BPPV can exist simultaneously. Migraine patients have three times higher BPPV risk, and 25% of vestibular migraine patients report positional dizziness.

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

If dizziness that isn't classic BPPV is suspected, I offer:

  • Thorough history: Mapping of duration, triggers, and accompanying symptoms
  • VNG examination: Videonystagmography for objective measurement of eye movements
  • TRV chair: Precise diagnostics and treatment of all BPPV variants
  • Neck examination: If cervicogenic component suspected
  • Referral: If central causes suspected or further investigation needed
