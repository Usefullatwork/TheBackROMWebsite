Dizzy from Stress and Anxiety - When Brain and Balance Are Connected

January 20, 2026 10 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Dizziness
  4. Dizziness and Stress/Anxiety

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Dizziness triggered or worsened by stress and anxiety, often through shared neural pathways
  • Most common causes: Hyperventilation (25%), vestibular disease with secondary anxiety (30-50%), PPPD (15-23%)
  • Treatment: Vestibular rehabilitation + cognitive therapy has the strongest evidence
  • Red flags: Acute dizziness with headache, visual disturbances, paralysis, new-onset hearing loss
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

Why does stress cause dizziness?

Studies show that 30-50% of patients who visit specialist clinics for dizziness have concurrent psychological complaints. Interestingly, neurologists detect only 10% of these cases during routine examination - structured interviews reveal four times as many.

This doesn't mean the dizziness is "in your head." It means your brain connects the balance system and anxiety system closely.

The biological connection: Parabrachial nucleus

Why do you panic when you get dizzy? The answer lies in evolution.

If one of our ancestors lost their balance, it often meant danger - falling from heights, attacks from predators. Therefore, the balance organ in the ear is "hardwired" to the brain's alarm system.

An fMRI meta-analysis from 2023 showed extensive overlap between brain areas activated during vestibular stimulation and fear learning:

  • Insula (both sides) - responsible for body awareness
  • Anterior cingulate cortex - attention and emotional processing
  • Thalamus and cerebellum

The parabrachial nucleus in the brainstem functions as a hub. It receives signals directly from the balance nerve nuclei and sends them to the amygdala - the brain's fear center.

When you become dizzy, these areas fire automatically. Your heart pounds, you sweat, and you get an intense feeling of danger. It's not you being "hysterical" - it's a biological reflex.

Hyperventilation: When breathing makes you more dizzy

Up to 25% of patients referred to dizziness clinics have disturbed breathing patterns.

The mechanism is simple: When you breathe rapidly and high in the chest (hyperventilation), you blow out too much CO2. This creates a chain of reactions:

  1. Low CO2 levels (hypocapnia)
  2. Blood vessels in the brain constrict - about 2-4% reduction in blood supply per 1 mmHg CO2 drop
  3. Dizziness, tingling in fingers and around the mouth

Many experience this during panic attacks or periods of high stress. The paradox is that the fear of becoming dizzy can trigger breathing changes that actually make you dizzy.

PPPD: When dizziness becomes chronic

Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness (PPPD) is the most common form of chronic functional dizziness. The condition affects 15-23% of patients at specialist clinics.

Who gets PPPD?

  • Women are affected four times more often than men
  • Typical age of onset: 30-50 years
  • Often triggered by a vestibular event, migraine, panic attack, or concussion

Diagnostic criteria (Barany Society 2017)

All five criteria must be met:

  1. Duration: Dizziness, unsteadiness, or non-rotational vertigo most days for at least 3 months
  2. Aggravating factors: Symptoms worsen with upright posture, movement, and visually complex environments
  3. Triggering event: Started after vestibular disease, migraine, panic attack, or concussion
  4. Symptoms cause significant discomfort or functional impairment
  5. Not better explained by another condition

Fear-Avoidance: When fear takes over

To protect themselves, many patients begin avoidance behavior:

  • "I don't go to the store because I get dizzy there"
  • "I don't turn my head quickly because then it spins"
  • "I'm afraid to exercise"

Research shows that 63.9% of patients with chronic dizziness meet criteria for agoraphobic avoidance.

In the short term, this feels safe. In the long term, it's destructive:

  • The brain never gets to train on what's difficult (no habituation)
  • The body becomes weaker (deconditioning)
  • The fear of "the next attack" grows larger than the attack itself

This is the main mechanism behind chronic dizziness.

Red Flags - Seek Medical Attention or Call Emergency Services For:

  • Acute dizziness with severe headache
  • Double vision or vision loss
  • Paralysis or numbness (especially one-sided)
  • Speech problems
  • Loss of coordination
  • Fainting
  • Chest pain

Treatment: What does research say?

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) - strongest evidence

A meta-analysis from 2024 examined 416 patients across 6 randomized studies:

  • CBT + standard treatment vs. standard alone: 8.17 point improvement on DHI
  • Anxiety reduction (GAD-7): 2.50 points
  • Depression reduction (PHQ-9): 2.29 points

Vestibular rehabilitation

A meta-analysis from 2025 with 522 patients found that vestibular rehabilitation provides significant improvement in DHI score - on average 21.84 points.

A typical program lasts 4-12 weeks with 2-3 sessions weekly and includes:

  • Gaze stabilization exercises
  • Balance training
  • Habituation to movement
  • Visual desensitization

Manual therapy for neck component

A randomized study from 2014 (n=86) with 12-month follow-up examined mobilization techniques for cervicogenic dizziness:

  • Passive joint mobilization: 13.6 point improvement on DHI
  • Sustained natural apophyseal glides (SNAGs): 8.9 point improvement

A meta-analysis from 2022 (13 studies, 898 patients) concluded with moderate evidence that manual therapy reduces cervicogenic dizziness and balance problems.

Prognosis: What can you expect?

Timeline

  • Vestibular rehabilitation: Noticeable improvement after median 6 weeks
  • Cognitive therapy: Effect over 8-12 weeks
  • Full recovery: Typically 6 months to 2 years with significant individual variation

Recurrence risk

Studies show 33-35% recurrence rate over 14+ months. Risk factors include premature termination of treatment, ongoing stress, and incomplete initial treatment.

Important: The goal is not necessarily to never feel a hint of dizziness again. The goal is to regain trust in your body and have the tools to manage symptoms when they occur.
