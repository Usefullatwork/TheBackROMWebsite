Types of Dizziness: How to Tell the Difference Between Different Forms

Vertigo, unsteadiness or lightheadedness? A guide to the 12 most common types of dizziness

January 30, 2026 Mads Finstad

You're dizzy. But what kind of dizziness? Is the room spinning, or do you just feel unsteady? Is it like being on a boat, or more like you're about to faint? The answers to these questions are the key to the right diagnosis and treatment.

Dizziness isn't one condition - it's an umbrella term covering many different experiences. By understanding which type of dizziness you have, you get closer to the right treatment.

Quick Summary

  • Vertigo: Room spinning - often from the balance organ (BPPV, Meniere's)
  • Unsteadiness: Rocking sensation - can be neck, vestibular or PPPD
  • Lightheadedness: Foggy head - often blood pressure, anxiety or medications
  • 80-90% of BPPV cases successfully treated with the Epley maneuver

Three Main Categories of Dizziness

Before we get into specific diagnoses, it's helpful to understand the three main types of dizziness:

Vertigo - Room Spinning

With vertigo, you experience that you or your surroundings are rotating. There's a clear sensation of movement even when you're sitting still. This is almost always caused by problems in the balance organ in the inner ear.

  • Typical description: "The room is spinning", "I'm whirling around"
  • Common causes: BPPV, vestibular neuritis, Meniere's disease
  • Often accompanied by: Nausea, vomiting, nystagmus (involuntary eye movements)

Unsteadiness - Rocking or Swaying Sensation

With unsteadiness, you feel off-balance without the room spinning. It can be described as standing on a boat deck or walking on an unstable surface.

  • Typical description: "Like being on a boat", "I feel unstable"
  • Common causes: Cervicogenic dizziness, PPPD, vestibular hypofunction
  • Often accompanied by: Neck pain, anxiety, worsening in crowds

Lightheadedness - Foggy Head

With lightheadedness, you feel "light-headed", distant, or like you're about to faint. There's no clear sensation of movement.

  • Typical description: "Foggy", "almost like I'm going to faint"
  • Common causes: Orthostatic hypotension, anemia, medications, anxiety
  • Often accompanied by: Palpitations, sweating, vision darkening when standing up

The 12 Most Common Types of Dizziness

Peripheral Causes (from the balance organ)

1. BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo) - Most Common

Prevalence: 17-42% of all dizziness cases

Small calcium crystals detach from their place in the balance organ and end up in the semicircular canals, where they disturb balance registration.

  • Brief episodes (10-60 seconds) with head movements
  • Triggered by turning in bed, looking up, or bending down
  • Symptom-free between episodes

Treatment: The Epley maneuver has an 80-90% success rate.

Read more about BPPV →

2. Vestibular Migraine

Prevalence: 1-3% of the population

Dizziness as part of migraine episodes - can occur with or without headache. The Barany Society calls this "by far the most common cause" of combined neck pain and vestibular symptoms.

  • Episodes from minutes to days
  • Often light and sound sensitivity
  • Migraine history in patient or family

Read more about vestibular migraine →

3. Meniere's Disease

Prevalence: About 10 times rarer than BPPV

Chronic condition with increased fluid pressure in the inner ear. Causes classic episodes with four main symptoms.

  • Severe vertigo (20 minutes to several hours)
  • Fluctuating hearing loss (low-frequency tones first)
  • Tinnitus (ear ringing)
  • Pressure sensation in the affected ear

Read more about Meniere's disease →

4. Mal de Debarquement (MdDS)

Persistent rocking or swaying sensation - typically after a boat trip, flight, or car ride. Can last months or years.

  • Paradoxically: Better during movement, worse at rest
  • Most commonly affects women in their 40s-50s

Read more about MdDS →

5. Cervicogenic Dizziness

Dizziness related to neck problems. The diagnosis is controversial - the Barany Society refrains from proposing diagnostic criteria due to lack of documentation.

  • Unsteadiness related to neck position
  • Often accompanied by neck pain and stiffness
  • Some patients experience improvement from neck treatment

Read more about neck-related dizziness →

6. PPPD (Chronic Functional Dizziness)

Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness - classified by the Barany Society as a functional (not psychiatric) disorder.

  • Daily dizziness for at least 3 months
  • Worsens with upright position, movement, visual stimuli
  • Often triggered by acute vestibular episode, anxiety, or concussion

Read more about PPPD →

7. Vestibular Neuritis

Prevalence: 3-10% in dizziness clinics

Viral inflammation of the balance nerve causing sudden, intense dizziness lasting several days.

  • Sudden onset, often after a cold
  • Constant, intense vertigo for several days
  • No hearing loss (distinguishes from labyrinthitis)
  • 10-15% develop BPPV afterward

Read more about vestibular neuritis →

8. Post-Concussion Dizziness

50-84% experience dizziness after concussion. Can persist for months (Post-Concussion Syndrome).

  • Can be BPPV, vestibular dysfunction, or oculomotor
  • 73% return to activity within 8 weeks with vestibular rehabilitation

Read more about post-concussion dizziness →

9. Vestibular Hypofunction

Reduced or lost function in the balance organ - either on one side or both.

  • Persistent unsteadiness, especially with head movements
  • Oscillopsia (unstable vision with movement)
  • Vestibular rehabilitation has strong evidence

Read more about vestibular hypofunction →

10. POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome)

Autonomic dysfunction causing dizziness, palpitations, and fatigue when standing up.

  • Heart rate increases over 30 beats/min when standing
  • Most commonly affects young women
  • Can be triggered by infection or COVID-19

Read more about POTS →

11. SCDS (Superior Canal Dehiscence Syndrome)

Rare condition with a hole in the bone wall above the superior semicircular canal.

  • Dizziness triggered by loud sounds or pressure (coughing, sneezing)
  • Can hear own eye movements or footsteps
  • Diagnosed with CT

Read more about SCDS →

12. Whiplash and Dizziness

40-70% of those with persistent whiplash symptoms experience dizziness.

  • Can have both cervicogenic and vestibular components
  • Multimodal treatment with manual therapy and exercises

Read more about whiplash and dizziness →

How to Find Out Which Type You Have

Ask yourself these questions:

Key Questions

  • Is the room spinning? → Likely peripheral vestibular cause (BPPV, Meniere's, neuritis)
  • Triggered by head movements? → Think BPPV first
  • Does it last days? → Could be vestibular neuritis
  • Do you have hearing loss/tinnitus? → Think Meniere's
  • Worsens in crowds/shops? → Think PPPD
  • Concurrent headache? → Think vestibular migraine
  • Concurrent neck pain? → Cervicogenic component possible

A thorough clinical examination with specific tests such as Dix-Hallpike, VNG, and balance tests is often necessary for accurate diagnosis.

Red Flags - When Dizziness Is Serious

Call emergency services immediately if you experience:

  • Difficulty speaking (dysarthria)
  • Facial weakness or numbness
  • Double vision (diplopia)
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)
  • Severe headache ("thunderclap headache")
  • Weakness in arm or leg

These symptoms may indicate stroke and require immediate evaluation.

The five D's that warn of central problems: Diplopia, Dysarthria, Dysphagia, Dysmetria, Drop attacks.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between vertigo and dizziness?

Dizziness is an umbrella term covering all forms of balance disturbances. Vertigo is a specific type where you experience that you or your surroundings are rotating - typically described as "the room is spinning".

Which type of dizziness is most common?

BPPV is the most common, accounting for 17-42% of all dizziness cases investigated. Vestibular migraine is second most common among those with recurring episodes.

Can stress cause dizziness?

Yes. PPPD (Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness) is a condition where chronic dizziness is maintained by anxiety and stress. It is a real physiological condition, not imaginary.

How do I know if my dizziness is serious?

Warning signs include difficulty speaking, facial weakness, double vision, severe headache, or difficulty swallowing along with dizziness. Call emergency services immediately if you experience these symptoms.

Can the neck cause dizziness?

Yes, cervicogenic dizziness from neck problems is real, though the diagnosis is controversial. The neck contains sensors that send position information to the brain, and dysfunction here can cause unsteadiness.
