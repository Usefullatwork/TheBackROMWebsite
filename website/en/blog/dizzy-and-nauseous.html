Dizzy and Nauseous - Causes, Treatment and When to Seek Help

January 20, 2026 10 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Dizziness
  4. Dizzy and Nauseous

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Dizziness combined with nausea - a common symptom pattern that can have many different causes
  • Most common causes: BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo), vestibular migraine, orthostatic hypotension, anxiety/stress, low blood sugar
  • Treatment: Strong evidence for Epley maneuver for BPPV (74% become symptom-free), moderate evidence for manual therapy for cervicogenic dizziness
  • Red flags: Acute dizziness with paralysis, speech difficulties, double vision, chest pain or severe headache - call emergency services
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

What does it mean to be dizzy and nauseous?

The combination can feel dramatic - many describe it as literally losing their footing. There are many possible explanations, from completely harmless conditions that can be treated in one consultation, to more serious causes that require prompt medical attention.

This article helps you understand what might be behind your symptoms, and when you should seek what type of healthcare.

Causes of dizziness and nausea

Vestibular causes (balance organ)

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo) - most common

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), often called "crystal disease," is the most common cause of dizziness when you seek specialist help. The condition accounts for 17-42% of cases in dizziness clinics. Lifetime prevalence is 2.4%.

Small calcium crystals in the inner ear become dislodged and end up in the semicircular canals, where they disturb balance signals. The result is intense, brief dizziness attacks (under one minute) triggered by head movements - typically when lying down, turning over in bed, or looking up. Nausea is common during attacks.

Women are affected 2.2 times more often than men, and the condition is most common between ages 50-70.

Vestibular migraine - underdiagnosed

Vestibular migraine affects 1-2.7% of the population and is the second most common cause of recurring dizziness attacks. Yet only 10% of those who meet diagnostic criteria are told that migraine is the cause of their dizziness.

Attacks last from 5 minutes to 72 hours and often include headache, light or sound sensitivity. An important diagnostic criterion is precisely that head movement-triggered dizziness WITH nausea is a characteristic symptom.

Vestibular neuritis

Vestibular neuritis is an inflammation of the balance nerve, often following a viral infection. Annual incidence is 3.5-15.5 per 100,000, equivalent to about 500-1000 cases in Norway per year.

Symptoms come suddenly: severe rotational dizziness, pronounced nausea and vomiting, and difficulty walking. Unlike BPPV, symptoms are constant (not episodic). Most experience significant improvement within 1-3 days, and 50% are symptom-free within 6 weeks.

Meniere's disease - rare

Meniere's disease is relatively rare with a prevalence of about 43 per 100,000. The condition causes attacks with dizziness (20 minutes to 12 hours), hearing loss, tinnitus, and pressure sensation in the ear.

Non-vestibular causes

Orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drop)

When blood pressure drops sharply with position changes, you can become dizzy and nauseous. This affects about 20% of adults over 60 and up to 50% of nursing home patients.

Anxiety and functional dizziness

28.3% of patients with dizziness have symptoms of at least one anxiety disorder. Persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD) is a chronic condition that often starts after an acute dizziness episode, affecting 15-20% of patients in specialist clinics.

Low blood sugar

Hypoglycemia often gives dizziness as the first symptom - 72.3% report this. Nausea, trembling, and hunger typically follow.

Medication side effects

Over 1000 medications have dizziness as a known side effect. Blood pressure medication, sedatives, painkillers, and epilepsy medications are common culprits. In elderly patients, medications explain 23% of persistent dizziness.

Red Flags - Call Emergency Services for Acute Dizziness With:

  • Paralysis or numbness in face/arm/leg
  • Speech difficulties or comprehension problems
  • Double vision or vision loss
  • Chest pain
  • Severe headache - especially if new onset
  • Cannot stand or walk

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)

The Epley maneuver is the gold standard. A meta-analysis shows that 74% become symptom-free - 6.5 times better than control groups. A systematic review from 2022 shows NNT of 3, meaning that every third patient treated becomes symptom-free compared to no treatment.

The modified Epley technique gives 85% success on first treatment versus 63% for traditional technique. For comparison: Untreated BPPV gives only 18% improvement after one month.

Recurrence is common - about 30% experience symptoms again within one year, and 50% within five years. This means most will need treatment again at some point.

Neck-related dizziness

Cervicogenic dizziness is a debated diagnosis. The Barany Society has explicitly refused to propose diagnostic criteria because evidence for a direct mechanistic connection between neck and dizziness is lacking.

Nevertheless, there are patients who experience improvement of dizziness after neck treatment. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 13 randomized studies (898 patients) found moderate quality evidence that manual therapy reduces dizziness, neck symptoms, and balance problems.

What can you expect?

  • BPPV: Many experience significant improvement after 1-3 treatments
  • Neck-related: Typically 4-8 treatments over several weeks
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: 4-12 week program for more complex conditions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does nausea last with dizziness?

It depends on the cause. With BPPV, nausea typically lasts seconds to minutes after head movement. With vestibular neuritis, nausea can persist for days. With migraine, it can last for hours.

Can stress cause dizziness and nausea?

Yes. Stress and anxiety can cause hyperventilation which causes dizziness. Additionally, 30-50% of dizziness patients are also affected by anxiety or depression - this is a biological connection, not "imagined."

Is dizziness and nausea dangerous?

Usually not. Most causes are benign and treatable. But acute dizziness with neurological symptoms (paralysis, speech difficulties, visual disturbances) can be signs of stroke and requires immediate medical evaluation.

What helps against nausea?

Treat the cause of dizziness. With BPPV, repositioning maneuver gives rapid improvement. During attacks, it can help to lie still, focus on a fixed point, and take deep breaths. Anti-nausea medications can be used short-term but should not be used for more than 2-3 days as they can delay healing.
