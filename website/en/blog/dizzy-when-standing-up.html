Dizzy When Standing Up - Causes and What You Should Know

January 20, 2026 8 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Dizziness
  4. Dizzy When Standing Up

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Dizziness when changing position - either from blood pressure drop (orthostatic) or balance organ (vestibular)
  • Most common causes: Orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drop) is most common overall, BPPV is most common vestibular cause
  • Key distinction: Orthostatic dizziness only happens when you stand up. Vestibular dizziness can also occur when lying down and turning
  • Red flags: Fainting during activity, chest pain, paralysis, speech difficulties - call 113 immediately
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

How Common is Dizziness When Standing Up?

Dizziness when rising is surprisingly common. A large German population study found that around 11% of adults experience this within a year. Women are affected more often than men - 13% vs. 8%.

A perhaps surprising finding: The symptom itself is actually most common in young adults. 22% of people under 30 report orthostatic dizziness. However, severity and risk of complications increase significantly with age.

Nordic figures show similar patterns. A Norwegian survey found that 16% of Norwegians reported dizziness or balance problems during a three-month period. In Denmark, a study showed that 20% of adults over 50 were affected.

Age Group Prevalence
Under 50 years ~5%
60+ years 22%
Over 70 years 30%
Nursing home residents 24-50%

Causes - From Most to Least Common

1. Orthostatic Hypotension (Blood Pressure Drop When Rising)

This is the most common cause overall, accounting for around 42% of all dizziness cases. When you stand up, 500-1000 ml of blood collects in your legs due to gravity. Normally, the body compensates by increasing heart rate and constricting blood vessels. In orthostatic hypotension, these mechanisms fail, and the brain temporarily receives too little blood.

Who is most affected? Elderly over 65, people taking blood pressure medication, diabetics (24% prevalence), and Parkinson's patients (30-40% prevalence).

Typical symptoms: Lightheadedness and unsteadiness occurring seconds to minutes after standing up. Many experience "graying out" or tunnel vision. Symptoms disappear when sitting down again. Important: Symptoms never occur when lying flat.

2. BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)

BPPV is the most common vestibular cause of position-related dizziness. Around 2.4% of the population experiences this during their lifetime, and the condition accounts for 17-42% of all dizziness consultations. Women are affected twice as often as men.

BPPV occurs when small calcium crystals in the inner ear become dislodged and end up in the semicircular canals. This sends false rotation signals to the brain.

Typical symptoms: Intense sensation of the room spinning. Episodes typically last 10-60 seconds and are triggered by head movements - including when turning over in bed. Nausea is common.

3. Medication Side Effects

Over 250 medications can cause orthostatic hypotension. The main culprits include:

  • Alpha blockers carry highest risk
  • Tricyclic antidepressants give six times increased risk
  • Diuretics
  • All types of blood pressure medication

How to Distinguish Between Orthostatic and Vestibular Dizziness?

Characteristic Orthostatic (blood pressure) Vestibular (ear)
When does it occur? Only when standing up With all head movements
Symptom type Lightheadedness, "graying out," tunnel vision Spinning sensation
Duration Seconds to minutes 10-60 seconds per episode
In bed? No, never Yes, when turning
Nausea Rare Common

Red Flags - Call 113 for:

  • Fainting during physical activity
  • Chest pain together with dizziness
  • Paralysis or numbness in face/arm/leg
  • Speech difficulties or comprehension problems
  • Severe headache - especially if new onset
  • Irregular heartbeat that starts suddenly

What Can You Do Yourself?

For Orthostatic Hypotension

  • Stand up slowly - sit on the edge of the bed before standing
  • Drink enough fluids (1.5-2 liters daily)
  • Avoid prolonged standing
  • Use compression stockings if needed
  • Review medications with your doctor

If You Suspect BPPV

  • Book an appointment for examination and possible treatment
  • Avoid rapid head movements in the acute phase
  • Sleep with head slightly elevated the first nights

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

  • Thorough examination: Distinguish between orthostatic and vestibular causes
  • Dix-Hallpike test: Diagnose BPPV
  • VNG testing: Objective measurement of eye movements
  • Epley maneuver: Treatment of BPPV with over 90% success rate
  • Referral: If orthostatic hypotension or other causes requiring medical evaluation are suspected
