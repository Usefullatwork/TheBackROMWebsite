Dizziness in Elderly and Fall Risk

January 20, 2026 12 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 1 in 3 over 65 years experience dizziness - up to 50% over 80 years
  • Most common causes: BPPV (20-46%), medication side effects, multisensory deficits
  • Fall risk: Dizziness increases fall risk by 63%
  • Treatment: Epley maneuver has over 90% success rate for BPPV
  • Red flags: Dizziness with speech difficulties, visual disturbances, weakness - call 113

Why Dizziness in Elderly is Serious

Approximately one in three people over 65 experience dizziness. In people over 80, this rises to around 50%. The problem is that dizziness is not just uncomfortable - it often starts a negative spiral that can have serious consequences.

In Norway, approximately 9,000 hip fractures occur annually - that's one fracture every hour. Fall-related injuries are the second most costly health condition after dementia. Norway actually has the highest incidence of fall injuries among 22 Western European countries.

The Dangerous Spiral: From Dizziness to Functional Loss

Dizziness in elderly often triggers a self-reinforcing downward spiral:

  1. Step 1 - Dizziness occurs: The balance organ, vision, or sensation in the feet weakens. The person feels unstable.
  2. Step 2 - Falls occur: Dizziness increases fall risk by 63%. About 27% of elderly fall at least once annually.
  3. Step 3 - Fear of falling: Almost half of all elderly (49.6%) develop fear of falling. This applies even to those who have never fallen.
  4. Step 4 - Activity reduction: 15-55% of those with fall anxiety begin avoiding activities. They no longer go to the store, visit friends, or go outside.
  5. Step 5 - Muscle wasting: Inactivity causes muscle strength to decrease by 2-5% per day. Weaker muscles lead to poorer balance.
  6. Step 6 - Increased fall risk: The circle is complete. The person with fall anxiety actually has 12 times higher risk of falling.

This spiral can be broken at multiple points - and the earlier, the better.

Causes of Dizziness in Elderly

1. BPPV - 20-46% of cases

By far the most common cause of dizziness in elderly. Small calcium crystals become dislodged in the inner ear and disturb balance signals. Typically causes brief, intense dizziness with head movements.

In elderly, symptoms may be less typical. Many experience only general unsteadiness rather than the classic "carousel feeling." Therefore, the condition is often overlooked.

2. Medication Side Effects

Many elderly take 5-10 different medications daily. The side effects - or combination of them - are a frequent cause of dizziness. The worst offenders include:

  • Blood pressure medications (can cause too low pressure when standing)
  • Sleep medications and sedatives
  • Antidepressants
  • Diuretics

3. Multisensory Deficits

Balance depends on three systems: vision, balance organ, and sensation in the feet. With age, all three weaken slightly. Cataracts reduce vision, hair cells in the inner ear gradually die (presbyvestibulopathy), and nerve signals from the feet are slower. When the brain receives unreliable signals from all three sources simultaneously, balance becomes unstable.

4. Orthostatic Hypotension

Blood pressure drop when standing up. Common in elderly, especially those taking blood pressure medications.

Consequences of Falls in Elderly

Physical Injuries

Approximately 20-30% of falls lead to moderate or severe injuries. The most common are:

  • Hip fracture: 1-2% of falls result in hip fracture, but consequences are serious. One-year mortality after hip fracture is around 22%. Patients with hip fracture have 6.7 times higher risk of dying in the first 6 months.
  • Head injuries: Falls cause 70-80% of traumatic brain injuries in people over 65.
  • Other fractures: Wrist, upper arm, and pelvis are also vulnerable areas.

Loss of Independence

A serious fall dramatically increases the risk of nursing home admission:

  • One fall without injury increases risk 3.1 times
  • Multiple falls without injury increase risk 5.5 times
  • One fall with serious injury increases risk 10.2 times

Among those who get hip fractures, over 50% end up in nursing homes after hospital discharge.

Red Flags - Call 113 for:

  • Sudden dizziness with speech difficulties
  • Visual disturbances (double vision, vision loss)
  • Weakness in arm or leg
  • Severe headache
  • Chest pain with dizziness

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

For BPPV: The Epley maneuver is first-line treatment. This is a series of position changes that move the loose crystals back to where they belong in the inner ear.

Treatment takes 10-15 minutes and has very good effect. The Cochrane review from 2014 (11 studies, 745 participants) showed that the Epley maneuver provides complete improvement in the vast majority. Individual studies report success rates of 74% after one treatment and 90-98% within one week.

For general unsteadiness and balance problems: Vestibular rehabilitation - exercises that train the balance system - has documented effect. The Cochrane review from 2015 showed that vestibular rehabilitation nearly triples the chance that dizziness resolves.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Balance training: The Otago program is developed specifically to prevent falls in elderly over 80. A review of 7 studies with 1503 participants showed 32% reduction in falls. The program also gave a surprising 55% reduction in mortality after 12 months.

Example exercises:

  • Stand up from chair without using hands
  • Toe raises while holding kitchen counter
  • Stand with one foot in front of the other (tandem standing)
  • Walk in figure-8 patterns

Home safety: Most falls happen at home. Simple measures can prevent:

  • Remove loose rugs
  • Install night lights in hallway
  • Non-slip surfaces in shower and bathtub
  • Handrails in stairs and bathroom
  • Use indoor shoes with good soles - not slippery socks

Review medications: Have an annual medication review with your GP. Can any medications be reduced or discontinued?
