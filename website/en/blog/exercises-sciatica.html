5 Exercises That Relieve Sciatica Pain - Step-by-Step Guide

January 20, 2026 12 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Low Back
  4. Exercises for Sciatica

TL;DR - Exercises for Sciatica

  • Centralization: Exercises that draw pain from the leg back toward the spine are positive
  • Peripheralization: Does pain increase down the leg? Stop and try something else
  • McKenzie extension: Helps many with herniation, but not everyone
  • Nerve mobilization: Gentle "gliding exercises" for the nerve
  • Avoid: Aggressive hamstring stretching and deep forward bending
  • Progression: Start gently, increase gradually over weeks
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

Important Before You Start

  • Stop the exercise if pain increases down the leg
  • Seek treatment for increasing numbness, weakness or worsening
  • Red flags: Groin numbness or bladder/bowel problems = emergency room

Why Exercises Help with Sciatica

With sciatica, the nerve is irritated - either from a herniation, stenosis or inflammation. The right exercises can:

  • Reduce pressure on the nerve by changing load distribution
  • Improve nerve gliding through gentle mobilization
  • Strengthen supporting muscles for long-term prevention
  • Reduce inflammation by promoting blood circulation

The Most Important Principle: Centralization vs. Peripheralization

Before you start exercising, you must understand this:

  • Centralization: Pain withdraws from the leg and back toward the spine. This is a good sign! It means the exercise is helping the nerve.
  • Peripheralization: Pain spreads further down the leg. Stop and try a different approach!

If an exercise causes centralization, continue with it. If it causes peripheralization, stop.

The 5 Best Exercises for Sciatica

Exercise 1: McKenzie Extension (Lying Down)

What it does: Relieves pressure on the disc and "pushes" the herniation back. Classic McKenzie exercise that helps many.

  1. Lie on your stomach with hands under shoulders (like push-up starting position)
  2. Gently press upper body up while hips and pelvis stay on the floor
  3. Hold 2-3 seconds at top position
  4. Lower yourself down in a controlled manner
  5. Repeat 10 repetitions, every 2 hours in the acute phase

Tip: You don't need to push all the way up - go as far as comfortable. Observe if pain centralizes (moves toward the spine).

Exercise 2: Nerve Mobilization (Nerve Flossing)

What it does: Improves nerve gliding through tissue. Gentle "flossing" that doesn't stretch the nerve too much.

  1. Lie on your back and hold the affected thigh vertically with hands behind knee
  2. Gently bend and straighten the knee - not to the pain threshold!
  3. When knee straightens, bend foot upward (dorsiflexion)
  4. When knee bends, relax the foot down
  5. 10-15 gentle repetitions, 2-3 times daily

Tip: Movement should be smooth and controlled. This is not stretching - it's mobilization.

Exercise 3: Knee-to-Chest

What it does: Opens foramina (nerve exit points) and stretches buttock and lower back muscles.

  1. Lie on your back with bent knees
  2. Gently pull one knee toward chest with your hands
  3. Hold for 20-30 seconds
  4. Switch sides
  5. Repeat 3 times per side

Tip: For some it works better to pull both knees toward chest simultaneously.

Exercise 4: Cat-Camel

What it does: Gently mobilizes the spine through flexion and extension. Increases mobility without heavy load.

  1. Get on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips
  2. Cat: Arch back up toward ceiling - let head drop
  3. Camel: Sway back down - lift head
  4. Move calmly between positions
  5. 10 repetitions, 2-3 times daily

Tip: Move within pain-free range. Don't force the movement.

Exercise 5: Bird-Dog

What it does: Strengthens core muscles and multifidi without heavy spinal load. Important for long-term prevention.

  1. Get on all fours with neutral spine
  2. Extend opposite arm and leg horizontally
  3. Keep body stable - don't rotate the pelvis
  4. Hold for 5-10 seconds
  5. Switch sides. 10 repetitions each side.

Tip: Focus on keeping the low back stable. That's more important than reaching high.

Progression: From Acute to Maintenance

Phase 1: Acute Phase (Day 1-14)

  • Focus on pain relief and unloading
  • McKenzie extension and nerve mobilization
  • Short walks, avoid prolonged sitting

Phase 2: Recovery Phase (Week 2-6)

  • Gradual increase in activity
  • Add cat-camel, bird-dog and glute bridge
  • Start general core training

Phase 3: Maintenance Phase (From Week 6+)

  • Full core training: plank, side plank, dead bug
  • Gradual return to normal training
  • Continue with preventive exercises 2-3 times per week

Exercises You Should Avoid

  • Seated forward bend: Increases pressure on the disc
  • Straight Leg Raise (SLR) as stretching: Stretches the nerve too much
  • Aggressive hamstring stretching: Can irritate the nerve
  • Heavy deadlifts and squats: Too much load in the acute phase
  • Sit-ups and crunches: Increases flexion pressure on the disc

When Do Exercises Work Best?

Research shows exercises are most effective when:

  • Done regularly (daily in the acute phase)
  • They don't worsen leg pain
  • They're combined with other treatment when needed
  • Progression is gradual - don't skip phases

General Activity

In addition to specific exercises, general activity is important:

  • Walking: Start with short walks (10-15 min), increase gradually
  • Swimming: Gentle activity without axial loading
  • Cycling: Can be good - adjust seat height for comfort

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I do the exercises?

In the acute phase: Nerve mobilization and McKenzie extension 4-6 times daily, few repetitions each time. In recovery phase: 2-3 times daily. In maintenance phase: 2-3 times per week.

Should I exercise through the pain?

No! Mild discomfort in the back is acceptable, but the exercise should not increase leg pain. Centralization (pain moving toward spine) is OK. Peripheralization (pain increasing down leg) is not OK - stop then.

Why aren't the exercises helping me?

There may be several reasons: wrong exercises for your condition, too early progression, or need for additional treatment. Not everyone responds to the same exercises - some need flexion rather than extension. An individual assessment can help.

Can I continue running with sciatica?

It depends on severity. Mild sciatica - maybe. Moderate to severe sciatica - wait until symptoms calm down. Listen to your body. If running increases leg pain, take a break.

How long before exercises work?

Many notice improvement after a few days of regular exercises. Others need 2-4 weeks. Be patient and consistent. If no improvement after 2 weeks, seek professional assessment.

Can I combine exercises with treatment?

Absolutely! The combination of manual treatment and self-exercises often gives the best results. A chiropractor can also give you individually tailored exercises.
