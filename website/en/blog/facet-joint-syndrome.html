Facet Joint Syndrome: When the Small Joints in Your Back Hurt

January 20, 2026 9 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Low Back
  4. Facet Joint Syndrome

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Prevalence: 15-45% of chronic low back pain is caused by facet joints
  • Typical: Morning stiffness, worse with backward bending
  • Age-related: Degenerative changes increase with age
  • Treatment: Manual therapy, exercises and mobilization often work well
  • Prognosis: Most people experience significant improvement with conservative treatment

What is Facet Joint Syndrome?

The facet joints, also called zygapophyseal joints, are small joints that connect the vertebrae on each side of the spine. Each vertebra has four facet joints - two above and two below. These joints control movement in the back and bear part of the body's weight.

Facet joint syndrome occurs when these joints become irritated, inflamed or degenerated. This can cause local pain in the back that often radiates to the buttock or thigh, but rarely below the knee.

How Common Is It?

Studies show that the facet joints are responsible for:

  • 15-45% of chronic low back pain
  • More common in older people (increases with age)
  • More frequent in people with poor posture or sedentary work

Symptoms of Facet Joint Syndrome

Typical Characteristics

  • Morning stiffness: Worst in the first 30-60 minutes after getting up
  • Pain with extension: Hurts to bend backward
  • Pain with rotation: Hurts to twist the back
  • One-sided pain: Often on one side of the back
  • Local tenderness: Point tenderness over the small joints
  • Radiation: Can radiate to buttock and thigh, but rarely below knee

What Makes the Pain Worse?

  • Prolonged standing
  • Bending backward
  • Rotating the back
  • Sitting for long periods in one position

What Relieves the Pain?

  • Movement and light activity
  • Bending forward
  • Heat
  • Lying with bent hips

Causes of Facet Joint Syndrome

  1. Degeneration: Natural aging and wear of the joints
  2. Disc problems: Reduced disc height increases load on facet joints
  3. Trauma: Acute injury or repeated microtrauma
  4. Posture: Increased lordosis in the low back increases load
  5. Overweight: More stress on the joints

How is the Diagnosis Made?

The diagnosis is primarily made through clinical examination. Typical findings include:

  • Pain with backward bending
  • Point tenderness over the facet joints
  • Improvement with forward bending
  • Positive Kemp's test (rotation + extension)

Imaging such as X-ray or MRI can show degenerative changes, but these are also common in people without pain. Therefore, the clinical picture is most important.

Treatment of Facet Joint Syndrome

Conservative Treatment (First Choice)

  • Manual therapy: Joint mobilization and manipulation can provide good pain relief
  • Soft tissue treatment: Treatment of tight muscles around the joints
  • Exercises: Core strength training and flexibility training
  • Posture correction: Reduce excessive lordosis

Important Exercises

  • Cat-camel: Alternate between arching and swaying the back on all fours
  • Core training: Plank, bird-dog, dead bug
  • Flexibility: Gentle stretching of hip and back
  • General activity: Walking, swimming

Prognosis

Most people with facet joint syndrome experience significant improvement with conservative treatment. Treatment duration varies, but many notice improvement after 2-4 weeks of regular treatment and exercises.

For persistent problems, other options such as diagnostic/therapeutic injections exist, but these are rarely necessary.

Prevention

  • Stay physically active
  • Strong core muscles
  • Good work posture
  • Avoid prolonged static positions
  • Maintain healthy body weight
