Hypermobility, EDS and Jaw Pain: When Loose Joints Cause Problems

December 28, 2026 8 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Jaw Problems
  4. Hypermobility and Jaw

TL;DR - Hypermobility and Jaw

  • Increased risk: Hypermobile individuals have 2-3x higher prevalence of TMD
  • EDS: Ehlers-Danlos syndrome affects connective tissue throughout the body, including the jaw joint
  • Challenges: Subluxations, clicking, instability, chronic pain
  • Treatment: Stabilization more important than mobilization - strength training, not stretching
  • Beighton score: Simple test for generalized hypermobility

What Is Hypermobility?

Hypermobility means your joints have greater range of motion than normal. This is due to looser connective tissue and ligaments. Some people have only hypermobile joints without symptoms (benign hypermobility), while others develop problems.

Hypermobility Spectrum

  • Asymptomatic hypermobility: Loose joints without problems - common in dancers, gymnasts
  • Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder (HSD): Loose joints with pain and other symptoms
  • Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS): Genetic connective tissue disorder with more extensive symptoms

An estimated 10-20% of the population has generalized hypermobility. Women are affected more often than men.

Beighton Score - Self-Test

The Beighton score is a simple test for generalized hypermobility. You get points for:

  1. Can the little finger bend backwards over 90 degrees (1 point per hand)
  2. Can the thumb touch the forearm when bending the wrist (1 point per hand)
  3. Can the elbow hyperextend more than 10 degrees (1 point per arm)
  4. Can the knee hyperextend more than 10 degrees (1 point per leg)
  5. Can the palms touch the floor with straight knees (1 point)

Interpretation: A score of 5 or more (out of 9) indicates generalized hypermobility. For adults over 50, a lower cutoff (4+) is often used.

Important

The Beighton score only measures generalized hypermobility. You can have a hypermobile jaw joint without scoring high on Beighton, and you can score high without jaw problems. The jaw joint is assessed separately.

How Hypermobility Affects the Jaw Joint

The jaw joint is particularly vulnerable in hypermobility because:

Structural Challenges

  • Loose ligaments: Provide less support to the joint
  • Increased mobility: The jaw can move too far
  • Disc problems: Increased risk of disc displacement
  • Subluxations: The joint can partially slip out of position

Common Symptoms

  • Frequent clicking and popping sounds
  • Feeling that the jaw "slips"
  • Episodes where the jaw dislocates
  • Pain when opening wide
  • Fatigue in jaw muscles
  • Chronic pain that is difficult to treat

EDS and the Jaw Joint

Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS) is a group of genetic conditions that affect connective tissue. The most common type is hypermobile EDS (hEDS).

EDS-Specific Challenges

  • Collagen defect: Weaker connective tissue in ligaments and cartilage
  • Multisystem: Affects many organs, not just joints
  • Chronic pain: Often persistent pain that is difficult to treat
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Can affect blood pressure and dizziness
  • Slow healing: Takes longer to recover after treatment

Studies on EDS and TMD

  • Up to 70% of EDS patients report jaw symptoms
  • Subluxation of the jaw joint is significantly more common in EDS
  • TMD symptoms correlate with EDS severity

Treatment for Hypermobility

Treating hypermobile patients requires a different approach:

What Does NOT Work

  • Manipulation/cracking: Can make the joint even more unstable
  • Stretching: The problem is already too much mobility
  • Aggressive mobilization: Can worsen symptoms

What Works

  • Stabilization exercises: Strengthen muscles that support the joint
  • Proprioceptive training: Better control of joint movements
  • Soft tissue treatment: Gentle work to release overactive muscles
  • Movement limits: Learn to avoid extreme movements
  • Posture training: Reduce load on the jaw joint

Exercises for Hypermobile Individuals

Controlled Opening

  1. Place tongue on roof of mouth behind front teeth
  2. Open only as far as the tongue stays in contact with the palate
  3. Close slowly
  4. 10 repetitions, 3 times daily

Isometric Strengthening

  1. Place hand under chin
  2. Press gently down with jaw against resistance (no movement)
  3. Hold 5 seconds
  4. Repeat to the sides with hand against cheek
  5. 10 repetitions each direction

Proprioceptive Control

  1. Stand in front of mirror
  2. Open slowly and observe the jaw
  3. Try to keep the jaw on midline (don't let it slide to the side)
  4. Correct with light fingertips if needed

Lifestyle Advice

Avoid

  • Opening mouth extremely wide
  • Hard and chewy food that requires a lot of chewing
  • Resting chin in hand (asymmetric loading)
  • Activities that cause repeated joint stress

Do

  • Cut food into smaller pieces
  • Take breaks while chewing
  • Maintain good posture
  • Support head when resting
  • Regular strength training

For EDS - Seek Specialist Help

If you suspect EDS (especially with skin symptoms, organ complications, or severe joint symptoms), you should be evaluated by a doctor with EDS expertise. EDS can affect more than just the joints and requires multidisciplinary follow-up.

Prognosis

With the right approach, most people with hypermobility-related jaw problems can achieve significant improvement:

  • Focus on strength and control gives better long-term results than passive treatment
  • Self-effort is especially important with hypermobility
  • Symptoms can vary over time - some periods better, some worse
  • Complete symptom freedom is not always realistic, but good function is achievable
