The Connection Between Jaw, Neck and Dizziness

January 20, 2026 10 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Dizziness
  4. Jaw, neck and dizziness

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Jaw, neck, and balance system are connected through shared nerve pathways
  • Why they're connected: The trigeminocervical complex - an area in the brainstem where nerve signals from face, jaw, and upper neck meet
  • Treatment: Moderate evidence that treatment of one area can improve symptoms in the others
  • Red flags: Visual disturbances with dizziness, sudden hearing loss, neurological deficits, fainting with head rotation
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

Why Jaw, Neck and Dizziness Often Occur Together

You're not alone. The numbers are striking:

  • Approximately 70% of TMD patients have concurrent neck pain
  • 37-40% experience dizziness

This article explains why these complaints are connected - and how treatment of one area can improve symptoms in the others.

The Trigeminocervical Complex - The Body's Connection Hub

The key to understanding the jaw-neck-dizziness connection lies in the nervous system. The trigeminocervical complex (TCC) is an area in the brainstem where the trigeminal nerve (which controls sensation in the face and jaw) meets the upper cervical nerves from C1-C3.

What's special is that nerve cells in this area receive signals from both the jaw and neck simultaneously. The brain therefore can't always distinguish where the pain actually comes from. Jaw pain can be felt as neck pain, and vice versa.

Research from 2022 showed that stimulation of the C2 nerve inhibited signals from the trigeminal nerve in 53% of the nerve cells tested - direct evidence of this crossover.

How the Balance System Gets Involved

The balance system (the vestibular system) also has direct connections to the upper neck. The same cervical vertebrae that participate in the trigeminocervical complex send proprioceptive signals (position sense) directly to the balance nuclei in the brainstem.

The suboccipital muscles (the small muscles at the top of the neck) have the highest concentration of position sense cells in the entire body - 242 muscle spindles per gram compared to only 2.2 per gram in the trapezius.

When these muscles are tense or dysfunctional, signals to the brain can become disturbed. The result is a sensory mismatch - the brain receives conflicting information from eyes, ears, and neck, and this is experienced as dizziness.

How Common Is It to Have All Three Problems?

Research has primarily looked at each symptom pair separately. Here are the numbers:

  • TMD and neck pain: 51-70% of TMD patients report concurrent neck problems
  • TMD and dizziness: 37-40% of TMD patients experience dizziness or vertigo
  • Neck pain and dizziness: 40% of neck patients have cervicogenic dizziness
  • Dizziness and musculoskeletal symptoms: 43-100% of dizzy patients report musculoskeletal problems

There is no formal diagnosis for the triad "jaw + neck + dizziness," but clinically the combination is well known. Some use the term craniocervical dysfunction as an umbrella term.

Who Is Typically Affected?

A review from AAFP (2023) found the following symptom distribution in TMD patients:

  • Headache: 79%
  • Bruxism/teeth grinding: 58%
  • Jaw pain: 54%
  • Ear pain: 52%
  • Neck pain: 51%
  • Tinnitus: 37%
  • Dizziness: 37%

Women are affected three times more often than men, with peak occurrence at ages 21 and 53.

Symptoms of Combined Problems

When jaw, neck, and balance system are involved simultaneously, I typically see:

  • Pain that's affected by both jaw and neck movements - yawning worsens neck pain, or neck rotation causes jaw tension
  • Dizziness that's diffuse and variable (not strong rotational feeling as with BPPV)
  • Headache, often in the temple or behind the eye
  • Stiffness in both jaw and neck in the morning
  • Clicking or locking of the jaw joint
  • Ear symptoms like fullness, tinnitus, or ear pain without infection
  • Feeling of unsteadiness or being "off-balance" (reported by 77-82% of TMD patients with dizziness)

One study found a correlation of r = 0.915 between jaw and neck function scores - meaning those with the worst jaw problems also have the worst neck problems.

Red Flags - See a Doctor For:

  • Visual disturbances together with dizziness
  • Sudden hearing loss
  • Neurological deficits (paralysis, numbness)
  • Fainting with head rotation
  • Severe headache - especially if new onset
  • Speech difficulties

Diagnosis

When combined jaw-neck-dizziness problems are suspected, all three areas should be examined. In clinical practice, I use DC/TMD (Diagnostic Criteria for TMD) as the international standard for jaw assessment, combined with neck examination and simple balance tests.

Distinguishing Cervicogenic Dizziness from Inner Ear Dizziness

The head-neck differentiation test provides valuable diagnostic information:

  • With eyes closed in a rotating chair, the body is rotated under a stabilized head - dizziness here indicates cervicogenic (neck-related) cause
  • Then head and body are rotated together - dizziness here indicates vestibular (ear-related) cause
  • If both produce dizziness, it may indicate combined problems

When Is Imaging Necessary?

MRI or X-ray is rarely necessary for typical combined problems. Clinical examination usually provides more useful information. Imaging is considered when structural damage is suspected, with neurological findings, or if treatment doesn't produce expected results after 3-6 months.

Treatment

For combined problems, an approach addressing multiple areas often gives the best results:

What Can a Chiropractor Do?

  • Neck treatment: Manual treatment of the upper neck has moderate evidence for effect on cervicogenic dizziness
  • Jaw treatment: Myofascial techniques for chewing muscles
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: Exercises that train the balance system
  • Holistic approach: Treatment of the interplay between jaw, neck, and balance

What Can You Expect?

  • Many experience improvement after 4-8 treatments
  • Home exercises are often an important part of treatment
  • With complex problems, it may take longer to achieve full improvement
