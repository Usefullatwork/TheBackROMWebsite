Jaw Pain in Children and Adolescents: When Should You Take Action?

December 28, 2026 8 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Jaw Problems
  4. Children and Adolescents

TL;DR - TMD in Children and Adolescents

  • Prevalence: 7-30% of children and adolescents have TMD symptoms
  • Bruxism: Common in children - often temporary, but can cause headaches (3x increased risk)
  • Causes: Stress (school, social media), screen use, growth, orthodontics
  • Prognosis: Usually good - most cases resolve
  • Treatment: Conservative - exercises, habit management, possibly manual treatment

Is TMD Common in Children?

Yes, temporomandibular joint problems can affect younger age groups too:

  • 7-30% of children and adolescents report TMD symptoms in studies
  • Prevalence increases through the teenage years
  • Girls are affected more often than boys, especially from puberty
  • Many cases are mild and temporary

Teeth Grinding in Children

Bruxism is common in children:

  • 15-40% of children grind their teeth
  • Most common in the 3-10 age group
  • Many "grow out of it" when permanent teeth come in
  • Children with nighttime teeth grinding have 3 times higher risk of headaches

When Is a Child's Teeth Grinding a Problem?

Seek evaluation if the child has:

  • Obvious tooth wear (dentist notices)
  • Jaw pain or headache, especially in the morning
  • Sleep problems related to grinding
  • Grinding that continues into the teenage years

Causes of TMD in Youth

Stress and Pressure

Today's children and young people experience significant stress:

  • School pressure and performance anxiety
  • Social media and comparison
  • Bullying or social challenges
  • Family situations (divorce, illness, moving)

Screen Use and Posture

  • Many hours with bent neck (text neck)
  • Static position in front of gaming/PC
  • Forward head position stresses the jaw
  • Lack of physical activity

Growth and Development

  • Growth spurts can cause temporary asymmetry
  • The temporomandibular joint develops throughout childhood
  • Hormonal changes in puberty

Orthodontics

  • Can cause temporary discomfort in the jaw joint
  • Changed bite relationship during treatment
  • Usually temporary

Symptoms to Watch For

Children don't always express pain directly. Be alert to:

  • Complaints of headache, especially in the morning
  • Rubbing their face or jaw
  • Avoiding hard or chewy foods
  • Clicking sounds from the jaw
  • Problems opening mouth wide
  • Pain when chewing
  • Ear pain without ear infection
  • Sleep problems or fatigue

Examination

When TMD is suspected in children, examination includes:

  • Conversation with child and parents about symptoms
  • Examination of temporomandibular joint and muscles
  • Assessment of opening ability and movement
  • Posture assessment
  • Mapping of stress and life situation

Treatment

Treatment of TMD in children is almost always conservative:

1. Information and Reassurance

  • Explaining that the condition usually resolves
  • Reducing worry in children and parents
  • Avoiding creating illness focus

2. Habit Management

  • Awareness about teeth clenching
  • Resting position for the jaw
  • Avoiding gum and chewy foods during periods

3. Exercises

  • Simple mobilization exercises
  • Relaxation exercises adapted to age
  • Posture exercises (chin tucks)

4. Stress Management

  • Identifying stress factors
  • Relaxation techniques adapted for children
  • Possibly consultation with school nurse or psychologist

5. Manual Treatment

When needed, gentle treatment can help:

  • Soft tissue treatment of jaw muscles
  • Neck mobilization
  • Adapted to the child's age and tolerance

Prevention

Tips for preventing jaw problems in children:

  • Limit screen time and ensure breaks
  • Encourage physical activity
  • Good sleep routines
  • Open dialogue about stress and worries
  • Ergonomic homework space
  • Model good posture

Prognosis

Good news for parents:

  • Most cases of TMD in children are mild and temporary
  • Conservative treatment is almost always sufficient
  • Many "grow out of it"
  • Surgery or extensive measures are very rarely necessary

When Should You See a Doctor?

  • Jaw lock that doesn't resolve
  • Asymmetry in the face that develops
  • Swelling in the jaw area
  • Fever with jaw pain
  • Significant functional impairment (cannot eat normally)
