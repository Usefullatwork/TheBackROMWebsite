L4-L5 Disc Herniation

Symptoms, diagnosis and treatment of disc herniation at the lower back's most loaded level
January 15, 2025 Mads Finstad Lower Back

TL;DR - L4-L5 Herniation

  • What it is: Bulging of disc material between the 4th and 5th lumbar vertebrae (disc herniation)
  • How common: Approximately 45-50% of all lumbar herniations (StatPearls, 2024)
  • Typical course: 60-90% improvement within 3-6 months with conservative treatment
  • Red flags: Foot drop, progressive weakness, bladder/bowel problems
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

Why is L4-L5 so vulnerable?

The L4-L5 segment functions as the workhorse of the lower back. The disc here has the highest disc height in the entire spine - approximately 11 mm - and handles most of the bending and extending movements in the lower back. The combination of high mobility and high load makes this level particularly vulnerable to wear and injury.

Another important factor is that the posterior longitudinal ligament, which normally protects against the disc bulging backward, is thinnest right here. This provides less resistance when disc material is pushed toward the nerve structures.

Approximately 45-50% of all lumbar herniations affect this single level (StatPearls, 2024). Together with L5-S1, these two levels account for about 95% of all symptomatic herniations in the lower back in adults between 25 and 55 years.

Who is affected?

  • Age group: 30-50 years are most at risk
  • Gender distribution: Men are affected approximately twice as often as women (2:1 ratio)
  • Incidence: Annual incidence of 5-20 cases per 1,000 adults (WFNS, 2024)

Risk factors include overweight, physically demanding work, diabetes, smoking, and genetic predisposition.

Symptoms of L4-L5 herniation

L5 nerve root (most common)

With the common paracentral herniation type, the L5 nerve root is compressed. This produces a characteristic symptom pattern:

Typical symptoms:

  • Pain: Radiates from the buttock along the outer thigh, down the lateral calf to the top of the foot and big toe (sciatica). The pain is often described as sharp, burning, or electric.
  • Sensory changes: Reduced sensation or numbness between the big toe and second toe is a relatively specific finding (75% specificity). Many also experience numbness on the top of the foot and lateral calf.
  • Muscle weakness: Can affect hip abduction, dorsiflexion of the foot, and big toe extension. Many notice this as difficulty walking on heels.
  • Reflex: There is no reliable reflex for the L5 nerve root, which can make diagnosis more challenging.

L4 nerve root (with far lateral herniation)

With far lateral herniation, the L4 nerve root can be compressed. This causes pain on the front of the thigh and medial calf, weakness in knee extension, and reduced or absent knee reflex.

Red Flags - When should you see a doctor immediately?

URGENT - Seek emergency care for:

  • Cauda equina syndrome: Saddle numbness, bladder/bowel problems, progressive bilateral weakness
  • Acute foot drop: Cannot lift foot/toes - requires rapid evaluation
  • Progressive weakness: Noticeable worsening of strength over days

Cauda equina syndrome is a rare but serious condition where the nerve bundle at the bottom of the spinal cord is compressed. It affects approximately 1 in 65,000 people annually, but requires immediate surgical treatment to prevent permanent damage.

Prognosis and Treatment

Spontaneous resorption - the good news

Meta-analyses show that 66-70% of all lumbar herniations resorb (go back) spontaneously with conservative treatment (Zou et al., Clin Spine Surg, 2024). For sequestered herniations, the resorption rate is even higher - up to 87%. Most experience significant improvement within 3-6 months.

Conservative treatment

In the absence of serious neurological deficits, international guidelines recommend conservative treatment as the first choice (WFNS, 2024). This includes:

  • Activity and movement
  • Pain-relieving medications
  • Manual treatment
  • McKenzie method
  • Nerve mobilization exercises

When surgery?

Surgery is considered for:

  • Cauda equina syndrome (immediate)
  • Significant motor weakness that does not improve
  • Persistent symptoms after 6-12 months of adequate conservative treatment

Studies show that surgery provides faster pain relief initially, but after 2 years of follow-up, results are often comparable to conservative treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can L4-L5 herniation resolve on its own?

Yes, studies show that 66-70% of herniations resorb completely or partially. Larger herniations (extrusion/sequester) actually resorb more often than smaller bulges.

Do I need surgery?

Most people don't need surgery. Conservative treatment is the first choice, and 60-90% recover without surgery.

What is foot drop?

Foot drop means you cannot lift the front of your foot. With L4-L5 herniation, this can be due to compression of the L5 nerve root. This is a serious sign that requires rapid evaluation.

How long does it take to recover?

The main improvement typically occurs within 6-12 weeks. Full recovery can take 3-6 months.
