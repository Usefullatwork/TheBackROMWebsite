L5-S1 Disc Herniation

The Lumbosacral Junction - Chiropractor in Oslo
January 15, 2025 Mads Finstad Low Back

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Disc herniation at the junction between the low back (L5) and sacrum (S1)
  • Prevalence: L4-L5 and L5-S1 together account for 95% of all lumbar herniations (StatPearls 2025)
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence for manual therapy combined with exercises
  • Red flags: Groin numbness, urinary/bowel problems - call emergency services

What is L5-S1 Disc Herniation?

The L5-S1 level is the junction between the mobile low back and the rigid sacrum. Here two structures with completely different biomechanics meet, which exposes the disc to significant stress.

Disc herniation occurs when the gelatinous core (nucleus pulposus) pushes through the outer fiber ring (annulus fibrosus) and presses on nerve structures. At L5-S1, the S1 nerve root is typically affected, causing pain and symptoms down the back of the leg.

Key Statistics:

  • Lifetime risk for lumbar disc herniation: about 30% (WFNS 2024)
  • Symptomatic herniation affects 1-3% of the population
  • Annual incidence: 5-20 per 1000 adults
  • Highest prevalence between ages 30-50
  • Men are affected twice as often as women

Why L5-S1 Specifically?

L5-S1 and L4-L5 together account for approximately 95% of all lumbar disc herniations (StatPearls 2025). These levels are also the main cause of low back pain. Several factors contribute:

  • Large forward angle creates high shear stress on the disc
  • Bears most body weight of all lumbar levels
  • Transition from mobile to rigid structure creates mechanical stress

A British study showed that in patients aged 25-45, 97% had herniation at L4-L5 or L5-S1. In patients over 65, only 40% were at these levels - herniations move upward with age.

Symptoms of L5-S1 Herniation

S1 Nerve Root (Most Common)

With typical posterolateral herniation at L5-S1, the S1 nerve root is compressed. The symptom picture is characteristic and often causes sciatica:

Pain:

  • Starts in low back/buttock
  • Radiates down the back of thigh and calf
  • Often described as a "stripe" down the leg
  • Continues to the outer foot and small toes

Sensory Changes:

  • Numbness or tingling on outer foot
  • Reduced sensation in fifth toe
  • Altered sensitivity on back of calf

Muscle Weakness:

  • Difficulty standing on toes (toe raise)
  • Weakened plantar flexion (pointing toes down)
  • Reduced strength with hip extension

Reflex:

  • Reduced or absent Achilles reflex
  • This is an objective and reliable finding with 91% specificity for S1 involvement

Differentiation: L5-S1 vs L4-L5

It's clinically important to distinguish which nerve root is affected:

Characteristic L5 root (L4-L5 herniation) S1 root (L5-S1 herniation)
Pain distribution Lateral calf, top of foot, big toe Back of calf, outer foot
Muscle weakness Foot lift (heel walking difficult) Toe raise (can't stand on toes)
Reflex Normal Achilles reflex Reduced/absent Achilles reflex
Typical sensory loss Between 1st and 2nd toe, top of foot Outer foot, 5th toe

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Straight Leg Raise (Lasegue's test): Positive with pain down back of thigh/calf when leg raised between 30-70 degrees. Sensitivity: 72-97%, Specificity: 11-66%. Most sensitive for pain between 30-45 degrees.

Crossed Lasegue: Pain in the affected leg when the healthy leg is raised. Lower sensitivity (23-42%), but high specificity (85-100%). Suggests larger, central herniation when positive.

Achilles reflex: Reduced or absent with S1 involvement. 91% specificity.

Imaging

MRI is the gold standard and shows herniation degree, nerve root compression and any other findings. Important to remember: MRI often shows findings that don't explain the symptoms. A meta-analysis of asymptomatic people found disc bulging in 10-81%, protrusions in 3-63%. Clinical examination is therefore often more important than the images.

Red Flags - When to Seek Medical Help Immediately

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

  • Numbness in groin or buttock area ("saddle anesthesia")
  • Urinary problems (can't start, don't feel bladder is full)
  • Bowel control problems
  • Rapid or progressive weakness in both legs
  • Acute-onset impotence/sexual dysfunction

Cauda equina syndrome: Occurs in about 1-3% of all disc herniations. Requires surgery within 24-48 hours for best outcome.

Treatment

Conservative Treatment - First Choice

Most people with L5-S1 herniation don't need surgery. Conservative treatment is the first choice.

What Can a Chiropractor Do?

  • Thorough examination and diagnosis
  • Joint mobilization and soft tissue treatment
  • Individual exercises based on your directional preference
  • Nerve mobilization (nerve flossing)
  • Guidance on activity and prognosis

Realistic expectations: A 2017 meta-analysis found that 67% of herniations completely or partially resorb within one year. Herniations that protrude fully (extrusion/sequestration) resorb more often than those that just bulge.

When is Surgery Considered?

WFNS guidelines from 2024 indicate that surgery should be considered for:

  • Cauda equina syndrome - acute indication
  • Significant motor deficit that's worsening - urgent
  • Persistent disabling pain after 6-12 weeks of adequate conservative treatment

Surgery provides faster pain relief in the first weeks, but at 1-2 year follow-up, results are often comparable between surgical and conservative treatment.

Prognosis

The prognosis for L5-S1 herniation is generally good:

Positive Factors:

  • 60-90% improve with conservative treatment (WFNS 2024)
  • Spontaneous resorption of the herniation is common
  • Larger herniations (extrusion/sequestration) actually resorb more often

Challenges:

  • Recurrence is common - about 5-15% experience new herniation
  • Achilles reflex may remain reduced even after symptom improvement
  • Some have persistent mild numbness or discomfort

Timeline:

  • Main improvement typically occurs within 6-12 weeks
  • Full recovery can take 3-6 months or longer

What Can You Do Yourself?

In the Acute Phase:

  • Keep moving, but avoid activities that worsen symptoms
  • Lying on back with bent knees can reduce nerve stretch
  • Avoid prolonged sitting or forward bending

When Pain Subsides:

  • Gradual increase in activity level
  • Focus on core muscles and general strength
  • Walking is often well-tolerated and beneficial

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a herniation resolve on its own?

Yes, studies show that 67% of herniations completely or partially resorb within one year. Larger herniations that protrude through the disc ring actually resorb more often than smaller bulges.

Do I need surgery?

Most people don't need surgery. Conservative treatment is the first choice, and 60-90% recover without surgery.

Can I exercise with a herniation?

Yes, but it's important to adapt the training. Avoid exercises that worsen symptoms. Walking, swimming and exercises that don't strain the back are often well-tolerated.

What's the risk of getting another herniation?

About 5-15% experience a new herniation (re-herniation). Good core strength and proper lifting technique can reduce the risk.
