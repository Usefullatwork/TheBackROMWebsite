Martial Arts and Jaw Injuries: Prevention and Treatment

December 28, 2026 7 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Jaw Problems
  4. Martial Arts and Jaw

TL;DR - Martial Arts and Jaw

  • High-risk sport: Jaw injuries are among the most common injuries in contact sports
  • Injury types: Punches, impacts, chokes, mouthguard-related issues
  • Mouthguard: Reduces injury risk by 80-90%, custom-fitted is best
  • Early signs: Clicking, pain when opening mouth, headache after training
  • Treatment: Early intervention is important - most recover with conservative treatment

Why Martial Arts Cause Jaw Injuries

The jaw joint is particularly vulnerable in martial arts for several reasons:

Direct Trauma

  • Punches to the jaw: The "knockout point" hits the jaw joint directly
  • Uppercuts: Push the jaw joint backward and upward
  • Elbows and knees: High force on a small area
  • Falls: Landing on chin or side of face

Indirect Loading

  • Clenching during fights: Extreme muscle tension
  • Chokes: Pressure on jaw and neck
  • Neck holds: Indirect impact on the jaw joint
  • Adrenaline: Masks pain during training/competition

Types of Injuries in Different Martial Arts

Boxing

  • High risk of direct blows to the jaw
  • Cumulative microtraumas over time
  • Mouthguard-related clenching

MMA / Mixed Martial Arts

  • Combination of strikes, kicks, and grappling
  • Chokes put pressure on jaw and neck
  • High total load on the jaw joint

Kickboxing / Muay Thai

  • Kicks to the head from unexpected angles
  • Elbows and knees in the clinch
  • Often less head protection than in boxing

BJJ / Grappling

  • Chokes (guillotine, RNC) stress the jaw
  • Pressure against face in guard
  • Neck manipulations affect the jaw
  • Lower risk than striking sports, but still significant

Common Symptoms

Be aware of these signs:

Acute Symptoms (right after injury)

  • Pain in jaw joint or jaw
  • Difficulty opening or closing mouth
  • Feeling that the bite is "wrong"
  • Swelling in the jaw area

Chronic Symptoms (develop over time)

  • Clicking or popping sounds when opening mouth
  • Morning stiffness in the jaw
  • Headache in the temples
  • Pain when chewing
  • Increased sensitivity when biting on mouthguard

Red Flags - See a Doctor Immediately

  • Locked jaw: Cannot open or close mouth
  • Visible deformity: Asymmetry in the jaw
  • Numbness: In lips, chin, or tongue
  • Severe bite misalignment: Teeth don't meet normally
  • Severe swelling: That increases rapidly

Prevention

Mouthguard - First Line of Defense

A good mouthguard reduces the risk of jaw injuries by 80-90%:

Types of Mouthguards

  • Standard ("boil and bite"): Cheap, poor fit, least protection
  • Custom-fitted by dentist: Best fit, best protection, more expensive
  • Semi-custom: Middle ground, good value

Important Features

  • Thickness at least 3-4 mm on the bite surface
  • Covers all teeth properly
  • Fits well without falling out
  • Allows breathing through the mouth

Investment That Pays Off

A custom mouthguard costs 2,000-4,000 NOK, but can save you thousands in dental treatment and jaw problems. Consider it part of your equipment budget.

Technique and Training

  • Learn to take hits: Tuck your chin, don't tense muscles excessively
  • Strengthen neck muscles: Better support for the jaw
  • Tap out in time: Give up before injury occurs in grappling
  • Gradual progression: Don't spar too hard too early

Training Routines

  • Avoid hard sparring the day after jaw problems
  • Take rest days between heavy sessions
  • Vary between technique training and sparring

Treatment

Acute Phase (0-72 hours)

  • Ice: 15-20 min, several times daily
  • Rest: Avoid hard chewing and opening wide
  • Soft diet: Reduce load on the jaw
  • Pain relievers: Paracetamol or ibuprofen as needed

Subacute Phase (3-14 days)

  • Gentle movement of the jaw
  • Gradual return to normal diet
  • Manual treatment can begin

Rehabilitation Phase

  • Mobilization exercises: Regain full mobility
  • Strengthening exercises: Rebuild musculature
  • Coordination training: Control over movements

Return to Training

Guidelines for safe return:

Criteria for Return

  • Pain-free opening with full range of motion
  • No pain when biting on mouthguard
  • Normal strength in jaw muscles
  • No clicking or locking

Gradual Progression

  1. Technique training without contact
  2. Light sparring with experienced partner
  3. Moderate sparring
  4. Full training/competition

Each phase should be pain-free before the next step.

Long-term Perspective

With repeated jaw injuries:

  • Increased risk of chronic problems
  • Possible early degeneration of the jaw joint
  • Consider reducing contact training
  • Regular follow-up can prevent worsening
