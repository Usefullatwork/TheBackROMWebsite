Do You Need an MRI for Back Pain?

When imaging is necessary - and when it does more harm than good
January 15, 2025 Mads Finstad Lower back

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Key message: MRI findings like disc bulging and wear are common in healthy people without pain - and often don't explain your pain
  • Fact: 80% of 50-year-olds without pain have degenerative changes on MRI (Brinjikji, 2015)
  • Clinical examination: Often more important than images for finding the cause of pain
  • Red flags: Acute neurological symptoms, urinary retention, unexplained weight loss require prompt evaluation

"Can I get an MRI?"

This is a question I hear often at the clinic. Many believe an MRI will give them the answer to why their back hurts. The thinking is understandable: modern technology should be able to show what's wrong, right?

The reality is more nuanced. International research over the past 10-15 years has turned thinking about imaging for back pain upside down. What once seemed like obviously good medicine - taking a picture to see what's wrong - often turns out to do more harm than good.

MRI Findings Are Common in Healthy People

Here comes perhaps the most important insight: most of what I see on MRI is completely normal - even if it sounds scary.

A systematic review from 2015 (Brinjikji et al., published in American Journal of Neuroradiology) reviewed 33 studies with over 3,100 people without back pain. The results are striking:

MRI Finding Age 20 Age 40 Age 60 Age 80
Disc degeneration37%68%88%96%
Disc bulge30%50%69%84%
Disc herniation29%33%38%43%
Facet joint osteoarthritis4%18%50%83%

In other words: by age 50, nearly 7 out of 10 have degenerative changes on MRI - without any pain at all. By age 80, almost everyone has them.

These findings have been described as "gray hair on the inside" - visible changes that represent aging, not disease.

Why Can MRI Actually Make Things Worse?

When you're told you have "herniation," "degeneration," or "wear" in your back, something happens in your mind. Words have power. In medicine, this is called the nocebo effect - negative expectations actually worsen the experience and outcome.

A 2022 study (O'Keeffe et al.) showed that patients who received descriptive diagnoses like "disc bulge," "osteoarthritis," or "degeneration" experienced:

  • Increased perceived severity of the condition
  • Reduced expectations of improvement
  • Greater belief that they needed surgery

The result? Research shows that early MRI for uncomplicated back pain is associated with:

  • Over 8 times higher risk of surgery (Webster, 2010)
  • Twice as long sick leave
  • Higher pain scores at one-year follow-up - despite more treatment

What Do the Guidelines Say?

All major international professional bodies agree: routine imaging for non-specific back pain is not recommended.

  • American College of Physicians (2011): "Clinicians should not routinely order imaging in patients with non-specific low back pain"
  • NICE (UK, 2020): "Do not routinely offer imaging in primary care"
  • ACR Appropriateness Criteria (2021): Imaging considered "usually NOT appropriate"
  • Norwegian Directorate of Health: Recommends against imaging for acute and subacute low back pain

When IS Imaging Necessary?

All of the above does not mean MRI is never useful. There are clear situations where prompt investigation is essential.

Red Flags Requiring Prompt Evaluation - Seek medical care immediately for:

Cauda equina syndrome:

  • Numbness in the "saddle area"
  • Urinary difficulty or incontinence
  • Fecal incontinence
  • Bilateral leg weakness

Signs of cancer: Previous cancer + new back pain, unexplained weight loss, night pain that doesn't improve with rest

Signs of infection: Fever combined with back pain, recent surgery or injection in the back

Fracture: Trauma, long-term corticosteroid use, osteoporosis

Other Situations Where MRI May Be Appropriate

  • Persistent severe pain after 6-8 weeks of adequate conservative treatment
  • You are a candidate for surgery or injection treatment
  • Suspicion of a specific cause requiring different treatment

Clinical Examination Is Often More Important Than Images

A thorough clinical examination can provide a lot of information. By testing movement, strength, reflexes, and sensation, an experienced practitioner can often:

  • Rule out serious causes
  • Identify which structures are likely involved
  • Assess whether there is nerve root involvement
  • Create a treatment plan

At the chiropractor, you get a systematic review of the entire musculoskeletal system. This typically takes 45 minutes at the first consultation and provides a good basis for assessing whether further investigation is necessary.

What Can You Do Instead of Getting an MRI?

For the vast majority with back pain - over 90% - the cause is non-specific. This means there is no dangerous or specific disease, but rather muscle and joint-related problems that respond well to:

  • Early treatment: Acute back pain should not be "waited out" - early intervention often leads to faster improvement
  • Activity over rest: Prolonged bed rest worsens back pain
  • Manual therapy: Joint mobilization, soft tissue treatment, and exercises
  • Exercises: Individually tailored exercises for core muscles and mobility

Frequently Asked Questions

Shouldn't I check if something is wrong with an MRI?

For most people with back pain, MRI will show "findings" that don't explain your pain - because these findings are equally common in healthy people. A clinical examination often provides more useful information.

I've had pain for a long time - doesn't that qualify for an MRI?

Duration alone is not a reason for imaging. If you've had problems for over 6-8 weeks without improvement despite active treatment, and surgery or injection is being considered, MRI may be appropriate.

What if my MRI already shows herniation or degeneration?

Remember that these findings are very common in pain-free people. A finding on MRI doesn't necessarily mean it's the cause of your pain. Treatment should always be based on clinical evaluation, not just the images.

Can a chiropractor order an MRI?

Yes, chiropractors in Norway with approved additional training can order MRI and other imaging. Referrals are covered by the national insurance system.
