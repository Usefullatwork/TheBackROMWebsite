Muscle Pain vs. Nerve Pain - The Complete Guide

TL;DR - Muscle Pain vs. Nerve Pain

  • Muscle pain: Aching, sore, local pain that worsens with muscle use
  • Nerve pain: Sharp, burning, electric pain that radiates along the nerve path
  • Muscle pain: Rarely numbness or weakness
  • Nerve pain: Often accompanied by tingling, numbness, or muscle weakness
  • Treatment: Different approaches - muscles need soft tissue work, nerves need decompression
  • Combination: Many have elements of both - thorough examination is key
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

Why Is It Important to Distinguish Between Them?

Many patients come to the clinic describing their pain as "sciatica" or "muscle tension" - but the reality is often more nuanced. Understanding whether pain primarily comes from muscles or nerves is critical because:

  • Treatment is fundamentally different
  • Prognosis varies
  • Self-help strategies differ
  • Red flags are different

A patient with a trigger point in gluteus minimus can have pain radiating all the way down to the calf - just like sciatica. But treatment is completely different!

Muscle Pain (Myalgia) - Characteristics

How Does Muscle Pain Feel?

  • Aching, deep quality: Often described as "grinding" or "heavy"
  • Stiffness: Especially after rest or in the morning
  • Tenderness with pressure: You can often point to a sore spot
  • Local: Pain usually stays in the muscle's area
  • Movement-related: Worse when the muscle is used or stretched

Common Causes of Muscle Pain

  1. Overuse: Too much, too fast, too long
  2. Trigger points: Hypersensitive knots in muscle fibers
  3. Muscle strain: Acute injury from sudden movement
  4. Static load: Prolonged sedentary work
  5. Stress and tension: Chronic muscle tension
  6. Deconditioning: Weak muscles that are easily overloaded

Trigger Points - The Great Imitator

Trigger points deserve special attention because they can cause referred pain that resembles nerve pain:

  • Gluteus minimus: Can cause pain down the entire back of the leg - almost identical to sciatica!
  • Quadratus lumborum: Refers to hip, buttock, and groin
  • Piriformis: Can compress the sciatic nerve and cause true nerve pain (piriformis syndrome)
  • Iliopsoas: Causes pain in groin and lower back

Nerve Pain (Neuropathy/Radiculopathy) - Characteristics

How Does Nerve Pain Feel?

  • Sharp, burning quality: Often described as "electric" or "stabbing"
  • Radiation: Follows the nerve path down the arm or leg
  • Numbness: Reduced sensation in skin along nerve distribution
  • Tingling/pins and needles: Paresthesias are typical
  • Weakness: Reduced strength in specific muscles

Typical Radiation Pattern in Sciatica

  • L4 nerve root: Front of thigh to inside of calf
  • L5 nerve root: Outside thigh and calf to big toe
  • S1 nerve root: Back of thigh and calf to sole and little toe

Common Causes of Nerve Pain

  1. Disc herniation: Disc material pressing on nerve root
  2. Spinal stenosis: Narrow spinal canal, often age-related
  3. Foraminal stenosis: Narrowing where nerve exits the spine
  4. Piriformis syndrome: Piriformis muscle pinching sciatic nerve
  5. Spondylolisthesis: One vertebra has slipped forward

Self-Tests You Can Try at Home

1. Slump Test (Tests Nerve Irritation)

  1. Sit on a chair with legs hanging
  2. Slouch your back and bend your head forward (like a "slump")
  3. Straighten one leg while bending foot upward
  4. Lift your head and see if pain eases

Interpretation: If you get pain/tingling down the leg that eases when you lift your head, it indicates nerve irritation.

2. Straight Leg Raise (SLR)

  1. Lie flat on your back
  2. Keep leg completely straight
  3. Lift leg slowly upward

Interpretation: Pain radiating down the back of leg at 30-70 degrees elevation indicates nerve irritation.

3. Trigger Point Palpation

  1. Press on sore points in muscles
  2. Hold pressure for 10-15 seconds
  3. Observe if pain "radiates" to other areas

Interpretation: Referred pain from trigger points can resemble nerve pain, but disappears when pressure is released.

When Both Are Present

Often patients have elements of both pain types:

  • Nerve irritation causes secondary muscle tension: Body "protects" the painful area
  • Muscle tension can irritate nerves: Piriformis syndrome is a classic example
  • Deconditioning: Pain avoidance leads to weaker muscles that are more easily overloaded

In these cases, both components must be treated for full recovery.

Treatment Differences

For Muscle Pain

  • Soft tissue treatment and massage
  • Trigger point treatment
  • Stretching and mobilization
  • Heat
  • Gradual strength training
  • Ergonomic adjustments

For Nerve Pain

  • Joint manipulation/mobilization to decompress nerve
  • Nerve mobilization (gentle "flossing")
  • Treat underlying cause (prolapse, stenosis)
  • Rest in acute phase
  • Gradual exposure when nerve calms down

Red Flags - Seek Help Quickly

See a doctor or emergency room if you experience:

  • Cauda equina symptoms: Numbness around buttocks/genitals, problems with urination/bowel control
  • Progressive weakness: Increasing loss of strength in leg
  • Foot drop: Difficulty lifting foot
  • Bilateral radiation: Nerve symptoms in both legs simultaneously
  • Fever combined with back pain: May indicate infection

Prognosis

Muscle Pain

Generally good prognosis. Most trigger points and muscle tension respond well to treatment within 2-6 weeks. Chronic cases may take longer, but improvement is expected with the right approach.

Nerve Pain

Varies more depending on cause:

  • Disc herniation: 80-90% get better without surgery within 6-12 weeks
  • Stenosis: Often fluctuating course, needs active management
  • Piriformis syndrome: Good prognosis with proper treatment

Frequently Asked Questions

Can muscle pain radiate to the leg?

Yes! Trigger points in gluteal muscles can cause referred pain radiating all the way to the calf, easily confused with sciatica. The difference is that muscle-referred pain doesn't follow a specific dermatomal pattern and rarely causes numbness or weakness.

Why do I have numbness if it's "just" a muscle?

Tight muscles can actually compress nerves locally. Piriformis syndrome is a classic example where the piriformis muscle pinches the sciatic nerve. Then you actually have both - a muscle causing nerve irritation.

Can nerve pain and muscle pain occur simultaneously?

Absolutely, and it's actually quite common. Nerve irritation often leads to secondary muscle tension because the body tries to "protect" the area. Likewise, chronic muscle tension can irritate nearby nerves. Treatment must often address both components.

Is heat or cold better?

Muscle pain: Heat is usually best - increases circulation and loosens tight muscles.
Nerve pain: Cold can help in acute phase to reduce inflammation, but many prefer heat here too. Try what works for you.

When should I get imaging?

MRI is rarely necessary in the first 4-6 weeks unless there are red flags. Imaging is most relevant for nerve pain that doesn't respond to treatment, or when serious underlying pathology is suspected.

Can a chiropractor treat both types?

Yes. Chiropractors are trained to differentiate between different pain types and adapt treatment accordingly. For muscle pain, soft tissue work and trigger point treatment are used; for nerve pain, focus is on decompressing the nerve through joint treatment and specific exercises.
