Musicians and Jaw Pain: An Occupational Risk

December 28, 2026 8 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. TMJ Disorders
  4. Musicians and Jaw

TL;DR - Musicians and Jaw

  • High-risk group: 30-75% of professional musicians report musculoskeletal pain
  • Wind players: Embouchure position significantly stresses the jaw
  • Violinists/violists: Chin rest causes asymmetric jaw loading
  • Singers: Increased jaw activity and tension for voice control
  • Prevention: Breaks, exercises, ergonomics, varied repertoire

Musicians Have Increased Risk

Research shows that professional musicians have significantly higher prevalence of musculoskeletal complaints than the general population:

  • 30-75% of orchestra musicians report playing-related pain
  • TMD is especially common in wind players and string players
  • Problems increase with practice hours
  • Many underreport due to fear of career consequences

Wind Instruments and the Jaw

Wind players use embouchure - the specific position of lips, tongue, teeth, and jaw required to play the instrument. This stresses the jaw joint in various ways:

Woodwinds (Clarinet, Oboe, Bassoon, Flute, Saxophone)

  • Clarinet/Saxophone: Underbite position, pressure on lower teeth
  • Oboe/Bassoon: Double reed requires precise jaw control
  • Flute: Asymmetric head position, jaw position for air stream

Brass (Trumpet, French Horn, Trombone, Tuba)

  • High pressure against mouthpiece
  • Lip vibration requires jaw control
  • Long high notes cause intense muscle tension

Common Problems in Wind Players

  • Fatigue in jaw muscles after long sessions
  • Pain when opening wide
  • Clicking in the jaw joint
  • Embouchure dystonia (focal movement disorders)
  • Headache in the temples

String Instruments and the Jaw

Violin and Viola

Violinists and violists are particularly vulnerable because:

  • The chin rest is clamped between chin and shoulder
  • Head is rotated and tilted to the left
  • Asymmetric loading over many hours
  • Often increased jaw tension for stability

Studies show that violinists have higher prevalence of TMD compared to other orchestra musicians.

Cello and Double Bass

Less direct jaw loading, but:

  • Posture can affect neck and indirectly the jaw
  • Some cellists clench teeth during intense playing

The Chin Rest Problem

A poorly fitted chin rest is a common cause of jaw problems in violinists. Consider a custom-fitted chin rest, and try different types to find one that gives less jaw loading.

Singers and the Jaw

Singers have special challenges:

Requirements for Singing

  • Optimal jaw opening for resonance
  • Control over tongue position
  • Release of jaw tension for good voice quality
  • Pronunciation and diction require precise jaw control

TMD Affects Singers

  • Limited opening ability affects repertoire
  • Pain can alter singing style and technique
  • Muscle tension in jaw transfers to throat
  • Clicking can be heard in microphone recordings

Prevention

Practice Hygiene

  • Breaks: At least 5-10 minute break every hour
  • Variation: Alternate between repertoire and techniques
  • Gradual build-up: Don't increase practice time too quickly
  • Warm-up: Include jaw exercises in warm-up

Exercises for Musicians

Before Practice (Warm-up)

  1. Gently open and close mouth 10 times
  2. Side movements with jaw 10 times each direction
  3. Massage of jaw muscles for 1-2 minutes
  4. Neck stretches

During Breaks

  1. Relax jaw completely - let mouth be slightly open
  2. Shake out shoulders
  3. Stretch neck gently

After Practice

  1. Relaxed yawning - don't force
  2. Heat on jaw if stiff/sore
  3. Relaxation exercises

Ergonomic Adaptations

  • Violinists: Custom chin rest and shoulder rest
  • Wind players: Consider ergonomic mouthpieces
  • Everyone: Good music stand height, lighting, sitting position

Treatment

Musicians with jaw problems should seek help early to prevent the problem from developing:

Conservative Treatment

  • Manual therapy: Soft tissue treatment and mobilization
  • Exercises: Adapted to musician-specific needs
  • Posture correction: Evaluate playing position
  • Stress management: Performance anxiety can worsen symptoms

Dental Involvement

  • Bite splint for use between practice
  • Assessment of dental problems from instrument use
  • Clarinetists may need tooth protectors

Returning to Playing

For acute jaw problems:

  1. Reduce: Shorter sessions, lighter repertoire
  2. Modify: Adapt technique if possible
  3. Gradual increase: Build up again over time
  4. Listen to your body: Pain is a warning signal

Career Protection

Early treatment of jaw problems can prevent small problems from becoming career-threatening. Don't wait until symptoms become so severe they affect your playing.

Performance Anxiety and the Jaw

Many musicians experience increased jaw tension during performances:

  • Adrenaline increases muscle tension
  • Teeth clenching as stress response
  • Symptoms may be worse before important concerts

Strategies

  • Relaxation exercises before performance
  • Awareness of jaw tension as part of mental preparation
  • If needed: psychologist with experience in performance anxiety
