Neck Pain During Pregnancy - Causes and Safe Treatment

January 2026 Mads Finstad 10 min read

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Muscle and joint pain in neck and shoulders that occurs during pregnancy
  • Most common causes: Postural changes, hormonal changes (relaxin), sleep disturbances
  • Treatment: Chiropractic treatment is safe throughout pregnancy and has good documentation
  • Red flags: Neck/shoulder pain combined with vaginal bleeding, severe abdominal pain, or visual disturbances

How common is neck pain during pregnancy?

Neck pain is a common complaint among pregnant women. A large Australian study of over 1,800 pregnant women found that 12.4% experienced neck pain during pregnancy. Other studies report up to 20% prevalence when shoulder complaints are included.

For comparison, about 40% of pregnant women are affected by back pain, and this proportion increases throughout pregnancy - from around 28% in the first trimester to nearly 48% in the third trimester.

What many don't know is that neck problems often worsen after birth. Japanese studies show that as many as 73% of women report neck and shoulder pain in the first month after delivery.

Causes of neck pain in pregnant women

Neck pain during pregnancy is caused by a combination of factors:

Hormonal changes (affects all pregnant women)

During pregnancy, the body produces the hormone relaxin, which loosens ligaments and connective tissue to prepare the body for childbirth. Relaxin levels are highest in the first trimester, but the hormone remains in the body throughout pregnancy and for several months after delivery.

Postural changes and center of gravity shift

As the belly grows, the body's center of gravity shifts forward. To compensate, many pregnant women lean backward, leading to increased curvature in the lower back. This postural change propagates up the spine and affects the neck.

Weight gain and breast enlargement

The natural weight gain during pregnancy - typically 10-16 kilograms - puts extra strain on all weight-bearing joints. Breasts can increase significantly in size, pulling shoulders forward and creating tension in the upper back and neck.

Sleep disturbances

Good sleep becomes more difficult as pregnancy progresses. Many pregnant women struggle to find a comfortable sleeping position, need to get up multiple times during the night, and generally have restless sleep. Poor sleeping positions can lead to stiff and painful neck in the morning.

Red flags - when should you contact a doctor?

Contact doctor or emergency services for:

Ectopic pregnancy (week 4-12):

  • Sudden, sharp pain in shoulder tip
  • Abdominal pain combined with vaginal bleeding
  • Dizziness or fainting

Preeclampsia:

  • Neck/shoulder pain + high blood pressure
  • Visual disturbances (flashes, flickering)
  • Swelling in face and hands

Chiropractic treatment - safe and effective

Chiropractic treatment during pregnancy has good documentation for safety and efficacy. A review of research literature concludes that manual therapy and chiropractic are safe and effective treatment options for pregnant women with mechanical back and pelvic pain.

What can the chiropractor do?

  • Thorough examination: First consultation includes complete examination to map the cause of the problems and rule out conditions requiring other follow-up
  • Gentle joint treatment: Pregnant women have increased joint mobility due to the relaxin hormone, so treatment is done with gentler techniques. The bench is adapted so the belly has room
  • Muscle treatment: Soft techniques like massage and trigger point treatment can relieve muscle tension in neck and shoulders
  • Exercises and guidance: You receive individually tailored exercises and advice on ergonomics, sleeping positions, and daily activities

Expected treatment course

Most pregnant women with neck problems experience improvement within 2-4 treatments. One study found that pregnant women on average needed 1.8 treatments to achieve significant pain relief.

What can you do yourself?

Exercises and stretching

  • Neck stretch: Sit or stand with straight back. Lower ear toward shoulder and hold for 20-30 seconds. Repeat on the other side. Do this 2-3 times daily.
  • Chin tucks: Gently pull chin backward as if making a double chin. Hold for 5 seconds. Repeat 10 times.
  • Shoulder rolls: Roll shoulders backward in large circles. Do 10 repetitions several times daily.
  • Chest stretch: Stand in a doorway with arms out to the sides. Lean gently forward until you feel stretch in the chest. Hold for 20-30 seconds.

Ergonomics and sleep

  • Screen should be at eye level when working at computer
  • Take breaks every 30 minutes
  • Use pregnancy pillow or regular pillow between knees when side sleeping
  • Choose a pillow that keeps neck in neutral position

Neck problems after birth and during breastfeeding

Neck problems don't necessarily disappear after birth - in fact, up to 72% of breastfeeding women report musculoskeletal complaints. "Nursing neck" describes neck pain that occurs from repeated forward-bent position during breastfeeding.

Tips for breastfeeding mothers:

  • Bring the baby to the breast, not the breast to the baby
  • Vary breastfeeding positions - try side-lying feeding
  • Support neck and back with good chair and pillows
  • Take breaks and do stretching exercises between feeding sessions

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe to see a chiropractor when pregnant?

Yes, chiropractic treatment is documented safe throughout pregnancy when performed by a qualified practitioner. Research shows side effects are rare and mild.

When during pregnancy should I seek treatment?

You can see a chiropractor at any time during pregnancy. Early treatment of musculoskeletal problems often leads to faster improvement than waiting. No referral is needed.
