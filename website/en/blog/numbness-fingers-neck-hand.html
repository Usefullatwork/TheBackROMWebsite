Numbness in Fingers: Is It the Neck or the Hand?

January 2026 Mads Finstad 12 min read

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • The issue: Finger numbness can come from the wrist (carpal tunnel) or neck (radiculopathy)
  • Carpal tunnel: Nighttime symptoms (84%), relief from "shaking" the hand, worsens with hand work
  • Neck: Neck pain (76%), relief from raising arm overhead, worsens with neck movements
  • Important: 14-26% have both conditions simultaneously ("double crush")

Two Common Causes of Numb Fingers

Tingling, numbness and "sleeping" fingers are symptoms many recognize. When symptoms appear, most people first think of carpal tunnel syndrome - and that's understandable, since this is the most common nerve compression in the body.

But in surprisingly many cases, the problem actually comes from the neck. Cervical radiculopathy - a pinched nerve in the neck - can cause almost identical symptoms in the hand.

A thorough examination is therefore crucial to find the right treatment.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome - When the Nerve is Compressed in the Wrist

The carpal tunnel is a narrow passage in the wrist where the median nerve passes through. When space becomes too tight, typically due to swelling in tendons or surrounding tissue, the nerve doesn't have enough room.

Prevalence

Carpal tunnel syndrome affects 1-5% of the population. The condition is three to four times more common in women than men, and is most often seen between ages 40 and 60.

Typical Symptoms

Carpal tunnel syndrome causes numbness and tingling in the thumb, index finger, middle finger and half of the ring finger - the median nerve's distribution area. The little finger is usually unaffected.

Characteristic of the condition is that symptoms are often worst at night. In one study, 84% of carpal tunnel syndrome patients reported nocturnal paresthesia, compared to only 10% of patients with neck problems.

Many wake up from numbness and need to "shake out" the hand - a movement that actually provides relief with carpal tunnel.

Cervical Radiculopathy - When the Nerve is Compressed in the Neck

With cervical radiculopathy, a nerve root in the neck is pinched or irritated, usually due to degenerative changes (70-75%) or disc herniation (20-25%).

Typical Symptoms

Symptoms of cervical radiculopathy depend on which nerve root is affected:

  • C6 nerve root (25% of cases): Numbness in thumb and index finger, pain along the forearm
  • C7 nerve root (60% - most common): Numbness in middle finger, triceps weakness
  • C8 nerve root: Numbness in ring and little finger, finger weakness

What's characteristic of neck-related problems is that neck pain is often present. In studies, 76% of patients with neck problems had neck pain, compared to only 14% of those with carpal tunnel syndrome.

How to Distinguish Between the Conditions

Symptom Comparison:

Symptom Carpal Tunnel Neck
Nighttime numbness/tingling 84% 10%
Worsens with hand work 82% 7%
Neck pain 14% 76%
Relief from "shaking" the hand Yes No

Key Questions You Can Ask Yourself

Points toward carpal tunnel:

  • Do you wake up at night with numbness in your fingers?
  • Does shaking your hand help?
  • Do symptoms worsen with repetitive hand work?
  • Is the little finger completely free of symptoms?

Points toward the neck:

  • Do you have pain or stiffness in your neck?
  • Do symptoms worsen with certain neck positions?
  • Does pain radiate down the arm?
  • Does raising your arm overhead help?

When Both Conditions Are Present - Double Crush

In some cases, the patient actually has both conditions simultaneously. This is called "double crush syndrome", where irritation of the nerve at one level makes it more vulnerable to effects elsewhere.

Prevalence varies from 14-26% depending on study design. Practically, this means thorough examination of both neck and wrist is important in all patients with finger numbness.

Red Flags - When You Should Contact a Doctor Immediately

Seek Medical Help the Same Day for:

  • Numbness or weakness in both arms/hands simultaneously
  • Difficulty walking, unsteadiness or feeling of stiff legs
  • Problems with bladder or bowel control
  • Symptoms that rapidly worsen
  • Fever combined with neck stiffness

Treatment

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Conservative treatment is first choice for mild to moderate symptoms:

  • Night splints that keep the wrist in neutral position
  • Ergonomic adjustments at the workplace
  • Treatment of underlying causes

Cervical Radiculopathy

Most people with neck-related numbness don't need surgery. Studies show that 80-90% improve with conservative treatment.

Chiropractic treatment may include:

  • Joint mobilization and manipulation of neck and thoracic spine
  • Soft tissue treatment
  • Neural mobilization
  • Postural and ergonomic guidance
  • Tailored exercises
