Piriformis Syndrome: False Sciatica

When it feels like sciatica but isn't - Chiropractor in Oslo
January 15, 2025 Mads Finstad Lower Back

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: A condition where the piriformis muscle in the buttock irritates the sciatic nerve and causes pain similar to disc herniation - but without back problems
  • How common: Accounts for 6-17% of cases with sciatica-like pain (Hopayian & Heathcote, 2019). More common in women (3:1 to 6:1)
  • Most common causes: Fall/trauma (~50%), overuse from running/cycling, prolonged sitting, anatomical variations (10-16%)
  • Treatment: 79-85% improve with conservative treatment such as manual therapy and exercises
  • Red flags: Bladder dysfunction, numbness in the groin, increasing weakness in both legs - requires urgent medical evaluation

What is piriformis syndrome?

Piriformis syndrome occurs when the piriformis muscle - a small, pear-shaped muscle deep in the buttock region - irritates or compresses the sciatic nerve. The result is pain radiating down the back of the thigh, often easily confused with "true" sciatica from a disc herniation in the lower back.

What makes the condition difficult is that it doesn't show on a standard MRI of the back. Many therefore go a long time with symptoms without getting the correct diagnosis. Piriformis syndrome is today considered part of the broader term "deep gluteal syndrome," which describes various causes of nerve compression in the buttock region (Kizaki et al., 2020).

The condition most commonly affects people between 30-50 years of age, with a clear predominance of women. Estimates suggest that piriformis syndrome accounts for 6-17% of cases where patients come to a specialist with sciatica-like symptoms (Hopayian & Heathcote, 2019).

Causes and risk factors

Piriformis syndrome is typically divided into primary (congenital anatomical cause) and secondary (acquired) types. The secondary type accounts for over 85% of cases (Boyajian-O'Neill et al., 2008).

Causes ranked from most common to rarest:

  • Macrotrauma (~50% of cases): Fall on the buttock, direct impact to the hip region, car accidents
  • Overuse/microtrauma: Running, cycling, repetitive hip movements over time
  • Prolonged sitting: Office workers, truck and taxi drivers
  • Anatomical variations (10-16%): The sciatic nerve may have an unusual course through or over the piriformis muscle
  • Postoperative (rare): After hip replacement or back surgery

Piriformis syndrome produces a characteristic symptom picture that differs from discogenic sciatica. A systematic review identified four cardinal symptoms (Hopayian & Danielyan, 2018):

Characteristics of piriformis syndrome:

  • Deep pain in the buttock region (50-95% of cases)
  • Worsening with sitting (39-97%) - a key sign that often distinguishes it from disc herniation
  • Tenderness on pressure near the hip joint (59-92%)
  • Pain with movements that stretch the piriformis muscle (30-74%)

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Conservative treatment is the first choice for piriformis syndrome. Studies show that 79-85% of patients achieve good improvement with manual treatment combined with exercises (Benson & Schutzer, 1999; Fishman et al., 2002).

Treatment may include:

  • Manual therapy: Mobilization of hip joint and pelvis, soft tissue treatment of piriformis and surrounding muscles
  • Dry needling: A randomized study showed that ultrasound-guided dry needling provides significant pain reduction
  • Stretching with optimal positioning: Optimized position (hip flexion 115-120 degrees, external rotation 40-50 degrees) can provide 30-40% lengthening of the muscle
  • Strengthening exercises: For hip muscles and core stability

What can you do yourself?

  • Avoid prolonged sitting: Take breaks, stand up and move every 30-45 minutes
  • Don't sit on your wallet: A classic among taxi drivers - remove objects from your back pocket
  • Stretch piriformis daily: Hold the stretch 15-30 seconds, repeat 2-4 times, do it 2-3 times daily
  • Modify activity temporarily: Reduce load from running or cycling in the acute phase, but avoid complete rest
  • Heat or ice: Many benefit from a heat pack on the buttock region

Red flags - when you should see a doctor

URGENT MEDICAL EVALUATION FOR:

  • Urinary difficulty or loss of bladder control
  • Numbness in the groin/buttock ("saddle numbness")
  • Increasing weakness in both legs
  • Bowel incontinence

This may be signs of cauda equina syndrome requiring urgent treatment.

OTHER SERIOUS SIGNS:

  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Previous cancer history with new pain
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Increasing foot drop (difficulty lifting the forefoot)

Prognosis

The vast majority with piriformis syndrome improve significantly with conservative treatment. A study on patients with normal imaging showed that 98% (41 of 42) recovered within 35 days (Vassalou et al., 2018).

For the 5-12% who don't respond to conservative treatment, surgical release of the piriformis muscle may be relevant. Systematic reviews show 75-82% good/very good results after surgery (Kay et al., 2017).
