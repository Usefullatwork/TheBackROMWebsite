Why Is This So Confusing?

Most people with back pain google their symptoms. The terms "lumbago", "disc herniation" and "sciatica" appear interchangeably - often used as if they mean the same thing. They don't.

The problem is that symptoms can overlap, and many believe they have a serious injury when the pain is actually caused by something far less dramatic. Around 85-95% of all back pain is non-specific, meaning without a detectable structural cause (WHO, 2023). True disc herniation with nerve involvement accounts for less than 5% of cases (StatPearls, 2024).

What Is Lumbago?

Lumbago is what most people have when their back "locks up". The medical term is acute lumbago or non-specific low back pain. The pain comes suddenly, often after an awkward movement, lift or twist - but can also occur seemingly without reason.

Characteristics of lumbago:

  • Localized pain in the lower back
  • Pain may radiate diffusely to the buttocks or thighs, but never below the knee
  • Stiff back with limited mobility
  • Worst in the first few days, then gradual improvement
  • No tingling, numbness or muscle weakness

With lumbago, no specific structural cause is found. MRI or X-ray often shows "normal" age-related changes that don't explain the pain. This is actually good news: it means your body isn't damaged.

Most people improve significantly within 6 weeks. Pain scores typically fall from around 56/100 to 26/100 during this period (Pengel et al., BMJ 2003). However, we must be honest: 33-73% experience recurrence within one year, and some develop chronic symptoms.

What Is Disc Herniation?

Disc herniation (slipped disc or disc prolapse) occurs when the soft core of an intervertebral disc bulges out or pushes through the outer layer. Think of the disc as a jelly donut - with disc herniation, the "jelly" is pushed out.

Important to know about disc herniation:

Disc herniation is rarer than people think. Less than 5% of back pain is caused by symptomatic disc herniation. The vast majority of herniations occur at the two lowest levels: L4-L5 and L5-S1, which together account for 95% of cases.

Here's the real surprise: You can have a disc herniation without knowing it. A large review of MRI studies (Brinjikji et al., AJNR 2015) showed that many healthy, pain-free people have disc herniations on MRI:

Age Disc bulge Protrusion Degeneration
20 years 30% 29% 37%
40 years 50% 33% 68%
60 years 69% 38% 88%

The good news: 66-77% of disc herniations resolve on their own (Zhong et al., 2017). In fact, research shows that the most dramatic herniations - where the disc has completely detached (sequestration) - have the highest resorption rate at 88% (Zou et al., 2023).

What Is Sciatica?

Sciatica (or lumbar radiculopathy) is not a diagnosis in itself, but a symptom pattern. It describes pain that radiates along the sciatic nerve - from the lower back, through the buttock, down the back of the thigh and often all the way to the foot.

Characteristics of true sciatica:

  • Sharp, burning or electric pain that radiates past the knee
  • Often worse than the back pain itself
  • Tingling or numbness in specific areas
  • May have weakness in the foot or toes
  • Worsens with sitting, coughing and sneezing
  • Positive "straight leg raise" test by practitioner

Sciatica affects 5-10% of those with back pain. So it's far more common than disc herniation, but still uncommon compared to simple muscle pain.

Can you have sciatica without disc herniation? Yes. Other causes include piriformis syndrome (0.3-6% of sciatica cases), spinal stenosis and spondylolisthesis.

How to Tell Them Apart

Characteristic Lumbago Disc herniation without nerve involvement Sciatica
Pain location Lower back only, possibly diffuse to buttock/thigh Lower back, possibly slight radiation Below the knee, often to foot
Pain type Aching, stiff Aching Sharp, burning, electric
Neurological symptoms No No Yes (tingling, numbness, weakness)
Prevalence 85-95% <5% 5-10%

The most important difference is whether the pain radiates past the knee and whether you have neurological symptoms. Tingling in the toes, numbness on the outside of the calf, or difficulty lifting the big toe - these point towards nerve involvement.

Red Flags - When You Must Seek Medical Help Immediately

Cauda equina syndrome is an emergency requiring immediate help. Call emergency services if you experience:

  • Sudden urinary retention (cannot urinate) or incontinence
  • Numbness in the "saddle area" (around genitals, inner thighs, buttocks)
  • Bowel incontinence or reduced control
  • Progressive weakness in both legs
  • Extensive and increasing numbness in both legs

Cauda equina affects approximately 1:33,000-100,000 in the population and accounts for about 2% of all disc herniations. The condition requires decompressive surgery within 24-48 hours to avoid permanent damage.

When Do You Need MRI or X-ray?

Short answer: Less often than you think.

International guidelines do not recommend routine imaging in the first 6 weeks for back pain without red flags.

Why? Because MRI findings correlate poorly with symptoms - up to 97% of asymptomatic adults have "abnormal" findings. Early MRI is linked to 8 times higher surgery rate without better outcomes (Webster et al., 2010).

Treatment - What Can Help?

For acute back pain, you can get help immediately - you don't need to "wait and see" for several weeks. Early treatment can contribute to faster pain relief and return to normal activities.

What can the chiropractor do?

  • Joint mobilization and manipulation
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight muscles
  • Nerve mobilization techniques for radiating pain
  • Guidance on activity and exercises
  • Gradual rehabilitation

What can you do yourself?

  • Keep moving. Bed rest is discouraged in all guidelines.
  • Avoid prolonged sitting. Change positions often, stand up and walk regularly.
  • Pain relief as needed. Paracetamol or ibuprofen for short periods.
  • Don't catastrophize. Back pain is common and usually resolves.

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

Lumbago: Most people improve significantly within 6 weeks. About 82% are back to work within a month.

Disc herniation: Surprisingly good prognosis without surgery. 66-77% of herniations resorb spontaneously over 3-6 months.

Sciatica: 60% improve within 3 months, and 70-75% within one year with conservative treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I have a disc herniation without knowing it?

Yes, absolutely. Studies show that 30-50% of healthy, pain-free people have visible disc changes on MRI. Disc herniation is only a problem when it causes symptoms.

Do I need surgery if I have a disc herniation?

No, most people don't need surgery. Conservative treatment gives equally good long-term results for most. Surgery is considered for cauda equina syndrome, progressive paralysis, or persistent severe symptoms despite treatment.

How long does lumbago last?

Most people experience significant improvement within 2-6 weeks. Some have mild residual symptoms longer. Pain lasting over 12 weeks is considered chronic back pain.

Is it dangerous to see a chiropractor for sciatica?

No, chiropractors are trained to assess and treat sciatica. If a serious underlying cause is suspected, you will be referred. Early treatment can actually contribute to faster recovery.

Can stress cause back pain?

Yes. Psychosocial factors such as stress, anxiety and depression are strong predictors of chronic back pain. Body and mind are connected - muscle tension and altered pain perception during stress are real phenomena.

What's the difference between a chiropractor and physiotherapist for back problems?

Both professions treat back problems. Chiropractors have particular expertise in joint manipulation and diagnosis of musculoskeletal conditions, and have primary contact status in Norway - you don't need a referral.

Should I get an MRI?

Not routinely in the first 6 weeks. MRI is indicated for red flags, progressive neurological deficits, or persistent symptoms where surgery is being considered. Remember that MRI findings often don't explain your pain.

Expected Treatment Course

For acute low back pain, clinical guidelines (CCGPP) typically recommend 6-12 treatments over 2-4 weeks, depending on response. For sciatica, the course may be longer. The treatment plan is always tailored to your individual situation.

If there's no improvement after 4-6 weeks, the diagnosis is reassessed and further measures discussed.
