Subtle BPPV: When Dizziness Doesn't Spin

January 20, 2026 10 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Dizziness
  4. Subtle BPPV

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: BPPV without classic spinning dizziness - an underdiagnosed variant
  • Most common symptoms: Seasick feeling (81%), fatigue (85%), concentration problems (81%), unsteadiness
  • Diagnosis: Standard examination in daylight catches only 40% of cases - VNG or Frenzel goggles required
  • Treatment: Repositioning maneuver works in 67-87% of cases - even without visible nystagmus
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

Why many walk around with undiagnosed BPPV

You may have been to the doctor, had blood tests and blood pressure checked, maybe even had an MRI - all normal. Some are told it's stress, burnout, or "psychological." Others give up finding answers.

What they probably haven't received is a thorough examination of the balance organ - with the right equipment.

Research from recent years reveals something surprising: Classic "spinning dizziness" is actually the exception, not the rule, in BPPV. A study from 2013 found that among patients with long-term BPPV, 81% experienced a seasick feeling (like being on a boat in waves), while only 20% had rotational dizziness.

In other words: Most people with BPPV don't spin.

What is subtle BPPV?

Subtle BPPV - also called "subjective BPPV" or "Type 2 BPPV" in the literature - is the same condition as regular BPPV: Loose calcium crystals in the balance organ's semicircular canals send incorrect signals to the brain. The difference lies in how symptoms are experienced.

In classic BPPV, head movements trigger intense, brief attacks of spinning dizziness. In subtle BPPV, the signals are weaker or differently processed, and the patient experiences:

  • A vague nausea or "seasickness on land"
  • Fatigue that can't be explained by lack of sleep
  • Brain fog and concentration problems
  • A feeling that something is "wrong," without being able to put a finger on it
  • Mild unsteadiness, especially when turning around or looking up

Harmat and colleagues' study from 2022 estimated that about one in three BPPV patients has this Type 2 variant.

Why standard examination misses these patients

Here comes the clinically important part: Standard examination for BPPV - the Dix-Hallpike test - is typically performed in normal lighting, where the patient can fixate their gaze on a point. This suppresses the characteristic eye movements (nystagmus) that confirm the diagnosis.

Examination Conditions Proportion of Nystagmus Detected
Normal room lighting 40%
Frenzel goggles, light on 66%
Frenzel goggles, darkness 83%
Videonystagmography (VNG) in darkness 100%

(Hirvonen et al., Acta Otolaryngol 2012)

This means that examination in normal lighting misses about 60% of eye movements that would be visible with proper equipment.

Symptoms that don't "fit"

Patients with subtle BPPV often present a confusing symptom picture that doesn't match the textbook description. Research has mapped what chronic BPPV patients actually experience:

From a study of 69 patients with long-term BPPV (median duration 5 years):

  • Neck pain: 87%
  • Fatigue: 85%
  • Visual disturbances: 84%
  • Reduced concentration: 81%
  • Seasick dizziness: 81%
  • Rotational dizziness: Only 20%

"Brain fog" is formally recognized as a BPPV symptom, with significant differences in memory, attention, and executive function compared to healthy controls.

Why do you get so tired?

When the balance organ sends incorrect signals, the brain must work overtime to compensate. This process - vestibular compensation - requires significant neural resources.

According to research from 2016, it takes up to 3 months for postural compensation (posture and stability), and up to 1 year for perception of vertical to normalize.

The consequence? 75% of patients with chronic BPPV were on 50-100% sick leave in one study (median illness duration over 5 years before adequate treatment).

Red Flags - Contact Doctor Immediately For:

  • Call emergency services: Sudden severe headache, weakness/numbness in face/arm/leg, speech problems, double vision, chest pain
  • Contact doctor same day: Dizziness lasting continuously over 24 hours, sudden hearing loss, fever and neck stiffness, dizziness after head trauma

Treatment works - even without classic presentation

Perhaps the most important finding: Subtle BPPV responds to repositioning maneuver even without visible nystagmus.

Treatment success for subjective BPPV across studies:

  • Balatsouras study: 87.5% (45 patients)
  • Meta-analysis 2013: 67.64% remission for BPPV without nystagmus
  • Gonzalez-Aguado 2021: 48.4% vs 50.8% for objective BPPV (no significant difference)

The study of chronic BPPV with non-rotatory symptoms (81% seasick dizziness) found that repositioning maneuver improved symptoms in 90% of patients - even after years of problems.

When subtle BPPV is suspected:

  1. Thorough medical history: Map character, duration, triggers of symptoms

  3. TRV chair: Achieves 92.4% diagnostic accuracy, can test all semicircular canals individually
  4. Trial treatment: With strong suspicion, repositioning maneuver can be attempted even without confirming nystagmus
  5. Follow-up: Assessment of response and possible adjustment

Prognosis

The prognosis for subtle BPPV is generally good once the condition is identified. Treatment success of 46-90% depending on variant and chronicity.

Realistic expectations:

  • Many experience immediate improvement after first treatment
  • Some need 2-3 treatments
  • 31-61% experience residual dizziness for days to weeks after successful treatment
  • Recurrence is common: 15-20% within one year, up to 50% within 5 years
  • Vitamin D supplementation can reduce recurrence risk in deficiency

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I have BPPV without the world spinning?

Yes. Research shows that 81% of chronic cases experience seasick dizziness or unsteadiness rather than classic spinning. Subtle BPPV is actually more common than the classic variant.

Why doesn't the doctor find anything wrong?

Standard Dix-Hallpike has only 48-88% sensitivity. Examination in room lighting misses about 60% of eye movements. Repeated testing with infrared camera or VNG may be necessary.

Can treatment help even if the doctor found nothing?

Yes. Studies show that 67-87% of patients with "subjective BPPV" (symptoms without visible eye movements) improve from repositioning maneuver.

Why am I so tired when I'm "only" a little dizzy?

The brain uses enormous energy compensating for confusing balance signals. 85% of chronic BPPV patients report fatigue, and 75% are on sick leave.
