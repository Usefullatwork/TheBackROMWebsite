Text Neck and Jaw Pain: How Mobile Use Affects the Jaw

December 28, 2026 7 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Jaw Problems
  4. Text Neck and Jaw

TL;DR - Text Neck and Jaw

  • 60-66% of office workers have forward head posture (FHP)
  • Increased load: For every 2.5 cm the head moves forward, neck load increases by ~4.5 kg
  • Jaw effect: FHP pulls the lower jaw backward and activates jaw muscles compensatorily
  • 70-90% of TMD patients have concurrent neck problems
  • Solution: Posture work, chin tucks, conscious mobile use

What Is Text Neck?

"Text neck" (tech neck) refers to neck and head problems arising from prolonged use of mobile devices with a bent head. When you look down at your phone, your neck bends forward and your head moves in front of the line of gravity.

This is not just a modern phenomenon - the same posture is seen with:

  • Desk work with a low screen
  • Reading books in your lap
  • Handicrafts
  • Gaming on tablets

But smartphones have dramatically increased the time spent in this position.

The Numbers Speak for Themselves

  • Average smartphone use: 3-5 hours daily
  • Young people often spend 7+ hours on screens daily
  • 60-66% of office workers have forward head posture
  • Every 15 degrees of forward neck adds 12-15 kg extra load

How Text Neck Affects the Jaw

The connection between forward head posture and jaw problems is well documented:

1. Biomechanical Change

When the head moves forward:

  • The lower jaw is pulled backward and down by gravity
  • The jaw muscles must work to keep the mouth closed
  • The jaw joint is loaded asymmetrically
  • The posterior part of the joint is compressed

2. Muscular Compensation

  • Masseter and temporalis are activated unnecessarily
  • Constant low-level tension leads to fatigue
  • Increased risk of trigger points
  • Tension headaches in the temples

3. Nerve Connections

  • The trigeminocervical complex connects neck and jaw
  • Tight neck muscles can cause referred pain to the jaw
  • Suboccipital tension affects jaw muscles reflexively

Research Confirms the Connection

Studies show that 70-90% of TMD patients have concurrent neck problems, and that treatment of the neck often improves jaw symptoms - even without direct jaw treatment.

Symptoms of Text Neck with Jaw Involvement

  • Neck stiffness and pain
  • Headache, often from neck to temple
  • Jaw pain that worsens with screen use
  • Increased teeth grinding or clenching
  • Shoulder and upper back pain
  • Fatigue in the jaw muscles

Self-Test: Do You Have Text Neck?

Stand with your back against a wall with your heels, buttocks, and shoulder blades against the wall:

  • If the back of your head naturally rests against the wall: Normal posture
  • If you have to stretch to reach the wall: Possible FHP
  • If you can't reach the wall without bending your neck backward: Likely FHP

Solutions and Prevention

1. Change Mobile Habits

  • Hold your phone higher: Raise it to eye level
  • Limit time: Set limits on screen time
  • Take breaks: 20-20-20 rule (every 20 min, look at something 20 m away for 20 sec)
  • Alternate: Switch between holding the phone with right and left hand

2. Optimize Your Workstation

  • Screen height: Top edge at eye level
  • Screen distance: One arm's length
  • Chair: Back support and correct height
  • Standing desk: Variation is good

3. Exercises

Chin Tuck

  1. Sit or stand straight
  2. Pull your chin straight back (like a "double chin")
  3. Hold for 5 seconds
  4. Repeat 10 times, several times daily

Neck Stretch

  1. Gently tilt your head to the side (ear toward shoulder)
  2. Hold 20-30 seconds
  3. Repeat on the opposite side

Chest Muscle Stretch

  1. Stand in a doorway with forearms against the frame
  2. Take a step forward
  3. Feel the stretch in your chest
  4. Hold 30 seconds

Shoulder Blade Retraction

  1. Pull your shoulder blades together and down
  2. Hold 5 seconds
  3. Repeat 10 times

Tips for Children and Adolescents

Text neck problems start early. For parents:

  • Limit screen time (AAP recommends max 1-2 hours for children)
  • Encourage physical activity
  • Set up an ergonomic study area
  • Model good posture yourself
  • Be aware of headaches and neck problems
