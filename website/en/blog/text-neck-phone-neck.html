Text Neck and Phone Neck - Neck Pain from Screen Use

January 2026 Mads Finstad 14 min read

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Neck pain and stiffness resulting from prolonged forward-bent screen use
  • Load: At 60 degrees forward head tilt (typical when texting) the neck is loaded with 27 kg
  • Risk: 2.3x increased risk with excessive smartphone use, 6x for office workers
  • Treatment: Moderate evidence for manual therapy combined with exercises

What Is Text Neck?

Text neck - also called phone neck, iPad neck or "text neck syndrome" - describes neck problems that arise from prolonged use of digital devices with the head bent forward. The term was first used by American chiropractor Dean Fishman in 2008.

When you hold your head in neutral position, it weighs approximately 4.5-5.5 kg. A biomechanical study from 2014 showed that the load on the neck increases dramatically with forward bending:

  • 15 degrees: 12 kg load
  • 30 degrees: 18 kg load
  • 60 degrees (typical when texting): 27 kg load

This equals almost a fivefold increase in load.

Norway has the highest prevalence of neck pain in the world, with 1,145 per 100,000 inhabitants. Meanwhile, surveys show that 97% of children between 9-18 years have their own mobile phone, and 74% use screens more than 3 hours daily outside of school.

Causes and Risk Factors

Prolonged Screen Use in Forward-Bent Position (Most Common)

The clearly most common cause of text neck is simply the time spent with the head bent over the phone, tablet or PC. A meta-analysis from 2025 found that excessive smartphone use gives 2.34 times higher risk of neck pain. Among office workers, the risk was even higher - 6 times increased risk.

Poor Workplace Ergonomics

Screen placed too low, chair without support, or lack of height adjustment contributes to the neck being held in unfavorable positions hour after hour.

Lack of Breaks and Movement

Static work without micro-breaks overloads the neck musculature. Health guidelines recommend varied activity, different work positions and frequent micro-breaks to prevent neck problems.

General Physical Inactivity

Weak musculature in neck, shoulders and upper back makes you more vulnerable to load-related problems. Studies report that 40% of Norwegian workers experience monthly pain in neck, shoulders or upper back.

Symptoms

Text neck typically gives a combination of the following problems:

  • Neck pain and stiff neck: Aching, nagging pain that worsens throughout the day, especially after longer periods of screen use
  • Pain in upper back and between shoulder blades: Forward-bent posture strains the musculature in the entire upper part of the back
  • Tension headache: Tight musculature in the neck can cause headache that starts at the back of the head and radiates up toward the temples or forehead
  • Reduced mobility: Stiffness when turning the head to the sides or looking up toward the ceiling
  • Shoulder pain: Shoulder musculature compensates for weak neck musculature

A Norwegian study based on the Ungdata survey (253,968 adolescents) showed that 24% of Norwegian youth had neck and shoulder pain, and among girls with such problems, 70% also had headache.

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

The diagnosis of text neck is made primarily through a thorough clinical examination. The chiropractor will assess:

  • Posture and craniovertebral angle (CVA): The angle between C7 and the ear's tragus. Normal CVA is over 53 degrees. Forward head posture is diagnosed when CVA is under 48-50 degrees.
  • Mobility test: How far you can move your neck in all directions
  • Palpation: Tight muscles, trigger points and tenderness
  • Neurological screening: If nerve involvement is suspected

Imaging - Rarely Necessary

X-ray or MRI is usually not necessary for typical text neck. Such examinations often show normal findings like wear or degenerative changes - changes found in most adults without explaining the pain.

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

A systematic review from 2023 found that the combination of manual therapy and stabilization exercises gave better results than training alone for patients with forward head posture and chronic neck pain.

Typical treatment measures for text neck:

  • Joint mobilization and manipulation: Treatment of stiff joints in neck and upper back
  • Soft tissue treatment: Treatment of tight and overloaded musculature
  • Exercise instruction: Targeted exercises to strengthen the deep neck flexors
  • Ergonomic guidance: Concrete advice about screen height, chair, work position and break routines

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Adjust screen height: The screen should be at eye level. Use stands for tablet and laptop.
  • Take frequent breaks: Follow the 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 meters away for 20 seconds. Stand up and move every half hour.
  • Chin tucks: Pull your chin straight back as if making a "double chin", hold 5-10 seconds, and repeat 10 times. Do this several times daily.
  • Stretch chest and neck muscles: Stretch pectoralis and upper trapezius daily to counteract the forward-bent posture.
  • Be aware of your posture: Think about keeping your ears over your shoulders when sitting and standing.

Treatment Course

Studies on forward head posture show that exercise programs typically last 4-10 weeks with good results. For acute text neck problems, I often see improvement within 2-4 weeks with treatment and self-effort.

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

See a Doctor or Emergency Room if You Experience:

  • Radiation to both arms simultaneously, or gait disturbances and clumsiness in hands/feet
  • Sudden, severe headache combined with neck pain
  • Fever together with neck pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
