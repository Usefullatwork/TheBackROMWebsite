TMD and IBS: The Surprising Connection Between Jaw and Gut

December 28, 2026 7 min read
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Jaw Problems
  4. TMD and IBS

TL;DR - TMD and IBS

  • Connection: Up to 64% of IBS patients also have TMD symptoms
  • Shared factors: Stress, central sensitization, autonomic dysfunction
  • Gut-brain axis: Same nervous system affects both gut and jaw
  • Overlapping conditions: Part of a larger group of functional disorders
  • Treatment: Holistic approach addressing both gives better results

Research-Based Connection

Studies show a surprisingly strong connection between jaw problems and digestive issues:

  • Up to 64% of IBS patients report TMD symptoms
  • TMD patients have 2-3 times higher incidence of IBS
  • Both conditions share many risk factors
  • Treatment of one condition can often improve the other

What Is IBS?

Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) is a functional gastrointestinal disorder characterized by:

  • Abdominal pain or discomfort
  • Changed bowel patterns (diarrhea, constipation, or both)
  • Bloating
  • Symptoms that worsen with stress

Approximately 10-15% of the population has IBS, and women are affected more often than men.

Why Do They Occur Together?

There are several explanations for the overlap between TMD and IBS:

1. Central Sensitization

Both TMD and IBS can involve central sensitization - a condition where the central nervous system becomes hypersensitive to signals:

  • The brain interprets normal signals as pain
  • Pain thresholds are lowered throughout the body
  • Explains why people can have pain in multiple locations simultaneously

2. Stress and the Autonomic Nervous System

Stress affects both jaw and gut through the autonomic nervous system:

  • Fight-or-flight: Activates jaw muscles AND affects gut function
  • Vagus nerve: Connects brain, jaw, and gut system
  • Chronic stress: Maintains activation of both systems

3. The Gut-Brain Axis

The so-called "gut-brain axis" explains communication between gut and brain:

  • The gut has its own nervous system (the enteric nervous system)
  • Gut flora affects mood and pain perception
  • The brain can affect gut movements and sensitivity
  • The same mechanisms can affect jaw function

Serotonin Connection

95% of the body's serotonin is found in the gut. Serotonin affects gut function, mood, AND pain modulation. Imbalance in the serotonin system can contribute to both IBS and TMD.

4. Shared Psychological Factors

Both conditions are associated with:

  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Catastrophizing
  • Sleep problems
  • Feeling of lack of control

Other Overlapping Conditions

TMD and IBS are often part of a larger picture with multiple functional disorders:

  • Fibromyalgia: Widespread muscle pain
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: Persistent tiredness
  • Migraine: Recurrent headaches
  • Interstitial cystitis: Bladder pain
  • Chronic pelvic pain

This pattern is sometimes called "central sensitivity syndromes".

Practical Consequences

Examination

With TMD, I often ask about:

  • Digestive problems
  • Other chronic pain conditions
  • Stress levels and sleep quality
  • Previous diagnoses (IBS, fibromyalgia, etc.)

Treatment Strategy

When TMD and IBS overlap:

  • Local treatment: Still important for jaw symptoms
  • Stress management: Central for both conditions
  • Sleep optimization: Affects both gut and jaw
  • Holistic approach: Look at the whole picture, not just individual symptoms

Self-Help Strategies

For Stress and Nervous System

  • Breathing techniques: Calm, deep breathing activates "rest and digest" system
  • Meditation/mindfulness: Reduces both TMD and IBS symptoms in studies
  • Regular physical activity: Moderate exercise helps both
  • Sleep routines: Regular sleep stabilizes the nervous system

For the Gut

  • Regular meals
  • Avoid known triggers
  • Adequate fiber and fluids
  • Consider low-FODMAP diet with guidance

For the Jaw

  • Jaw exercises and relaxation
  • Awareness about teeth clenching
  • Good posture
  • Avoid overstimulation

When Should You Seek Help?

Seek help if:

  • You have persistent symptoms from both jaw and gut
  • Stress worsens both conditions
  • Symptoms affect quality of life
  • You've tried self-help without adequate effect

Red Flags - Contact a Doctor

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Blood in stool
  • Nighttime symptoms that wake you
  • Symptom onset after age 50
  • Fever together with abdominal pain

These may indicate other conditions that need to be ruled out.

Multidisciplinary Collaboration

For overlapping conditions, multiple professionals can contribute:

  • Chiropractor: Jaw treatment and holistic assessment
  • GP: Evaluation and coordination
  • Gastroenterologist: For severe or unclear IBS
  • Psychologist: Cognitive therapy for stress management
  • Dietitian: Diet optimization

Prognosis

With the right approach, many can experience significant improvement:

  • Symptoms can vary over time
  • Stress reduction often improves both conditions
  • Complete symptom freedom is not always the goal - good function is more important
  • Self-help strategies provide better control
