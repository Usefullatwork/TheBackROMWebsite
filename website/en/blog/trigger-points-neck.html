Trigger Points and Myofascial Pain Syndrome in the Neck

January 2026 Mads Finstad 14 min read

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Tender, tight spots in muscle tissue that can cause local pain and referred pain
  • How common: 88-100% of patients with chronic neck pain have trigger points
  • Most common muscles: Upper trapezius (93%), levator scapulae, sternocleidomastoid
  • Treatment: Manual therapy and dry needling provide short-term relief. Combined stretching and strength training is most important

What is myofascial pain syndrome and trigger points?

Myofascial pain syndrome (MPS) is a condition where painful "knots" - called trigger points - develop in muscles and connective tissue (fascia). These points are tender when pressed and can cause pain that radiates to other areas of the body. A trigger point in the upper trapezius can, for example, cause headache up toward the temple.

The condition is very common. Among patients with chronic neck pain, 88-100% have at least one trigger point. In pain clinics, up to 85% of patients have a myofascial component.

Causes and Risk Factors

Trigger points develop when muscle fibers become overloaded or damaged. The most common causes:

  • Static loading and poor ergonomics: Prolonged desk work, screen use with forward head posture, and repetitive work are by far the most common causes. Office workers have an average of 6 active and 10-11 latent trigger points.
  • Muscular overload: Repetitive movements, heavy lifting, or sudden increased training load.
  • Stress and tension: Psychological stress often leads to chronic muscle tension, especially in the neck and shoulders.
  • Sleep problems and fatigue: Poor sleep reduces the muscles' ability to recover.
  • Vitamin D deficiency: Approximately one-third of MPS patients have low vitamin D levels.
  • Trauma: Among whiplash patients, 55-65% have trigger points in the levator scapulae.

MPS occurs most often in the 27-50 age group. Women are affected more often than men - 65% versus 37% among middle-aged adults.

Symptoms

Trigger points in the neck produce a characteristic symptom pattern:

  • Local tenderness: A distinct point that is very tender when pressed.
  • Referred pain: The pain radiates in a predictable pattern. Upper trapezius often causes pain toward the back of the head and temple. Sternocleidomastoid can cause pain around the eye and forehead.
  • Stiffness and reduced mobility: Difficulty turning the head or looking up.
  • Headache: 80% of patients with chronic tension headache have active trigger points in the upper trapezius.
  • "Stiff neck" sensation: Typical with trigger points in the levator scapulae.

Most Common Muscles with Trigger Points in the Neck

Muscle and referred pain:

  • Upper trapezius (93% of chronic neck pain patients): Causes pain along the neck, up toward the back of the head, temple, and behind the eye. The classic "tension headache."
  • Levator scapulae (55-65%): Causes the typical "stiff neck" sensation with pain at the neck-shoulder junction and along the inner shoulder blade edge.
  • Sternocleidomastoid (SCM): Can cause headache in the forehead and around the eye, as well as dizziness and balance disturbances.
  • Scalene muscles: Can cause radiation to the chest, shoulder, arm, and thumb - sometimes confused with heart symptoms or nerve root involvement.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made through clinical examination. An international Delphi study concluded that the most important diagnostic criteria are: a taut band in the muscle, a hypersensitive spot, and reproduction of referred pain with pressure.

Important to know: There are no internationally validated diagnostic criteria for MPS. Reliability between examiners is only moderate. I therefore use the clinical examination as a starting point, but also emphasize your experience and response to treatment.

Differential Diagnoses

  • Cervical radiculopathy: Follows dermatomal patterns with tingling/numbness and weakness. Trigger points follow muscle-specific radiation patterns.
  • Fibromyalgia: Widespread pain for more than 3 months, with tenderness in many places on the body.
  • Cervical facet joint arthrosis: Pain from the joints between vertebrae, often with morning stiffness that improves throughout the day.

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

A network meta-analysis from 2024 shows that manual therapy provides short-term improvement in pain, pressure sensitivity, and function. Treatment includes:

  • Ischemic compression: Sustained pressure on the trigger point provides significant improvement in pressure pain threshold.
  • Dry needling: Thin needles are inserted directly into the trigger point. Equally effective as manual trigger point treatment in the short and medium term.
  • Myofascial techniques and mobilization: Soft tissue treatment of muscles and fascia.
  • Joint mobilization/manipulation: For concurrent joint dysfunction in the neck or thoracic spine.
  • Exercise-based rehabilitation: The most important for lasting improvement.

Honest about expectations

The short-term effects of treatment are well documented. Long-term effects (6-12 months) are less well studied. What provides the best lasting effect is combining treatment with self-exercise and addressing underlying factors such as stress, ergonomics, and sleep.

What can you do yourself?

A systematic review shows that combined stretching and strength training provides the best effect on myofascial pain:

  • Stretching: Hold for 30 seconds - this is the optimal duration according to research. Stretch the trapezius, levator scapulae, and scalenes daily.
  • Strength training: Exercises for deep neck muscles and shoulder blade stabilizers.
  • Ergonomics: Screen at eye level, forearms supported, regular breaks. Avoid forward head posture.
  • Self-massage: Use a tennis ball or lacrosse ball against the wall for gentle pressure on tender points. 30-60 seconds per point.
  • Heat packs: Can provide temporary relief and increase blood flow.
  • Stress management: Chronic stress maintains muscle tension. Consider breathing techniques or mindfulness.

Red Flags - When to see a doctor

See a doctor if you experience:

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fever
  • Neurological deficits (numbness, weakness)
  • Gait disturbances
  • Night sweats
