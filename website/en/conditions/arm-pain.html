Pain in Arm, Elbow and Hand

Complete guide to diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua Oslo

Your arm is an advanced system where bones, muscles, tendons and nerves work together from shoulder to fingertips. When something becomes irritated or overloaded in one place, you can often feel it elsewhere. A tight muscle in the forearm can cause pain in the wrist. A pinched nerve at the elbow can cause tingling all the way to the little finger.

The good news is that most problems in the arm, elbow and hand have a clear cause and good prognosis. With the right diagnosis and approach, 80-90% improve significantly - often without the need for surgery or long-term medication use.

Quick Summary

  • Most problems aren't dangerous - 80-90% improve significantly with proper treatment
  • Four main categories: Tendon problems, nerve compressions, joint wear and muscular problems
  • Adapted activity works better than complete rest
  • Early treatment gives faster improvement than waiting
  • No referral necessary - chiropractor is primary contact in Norway

Symptoms of Arm, Elbow and Hand Pain

Symptoms vary depending on the underlying cause. Here are the four main categories of problems and their characteristic symptoms:

1. Tendon problems (tendinopathies)

Gradual development over weeks/months. Aching or burning pain with specific movements.

  • Tennis elbow (1-3% annually)
  • Golfer's elbow
  • De Quervain's tenosynovitis
  • Trigger finger

2. Nerve compressions

Tingling, numbness or "sleeping" sensation. Follows the nerve's distribution area.

  • Carpal tunnel syndrome (2-3%)
  • Cubital tunnel syndrome

3. Joint wear

Pain with gripping and pinching, stiffness, gradual loss of function. Common after 50 years.

  • Thumb arthritis (CMC-1)
  • Finger arthritis

4. Muscular problems

Trigger points that can give referred pain - pain in a completely different place than the problem.

  • Trigger points
  • Mouse arm/WRULD

Common Conditions Explained

Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis)

1-3% annually

Go to full article on tennis elbow →

Tennis elbow is the most common cause of elbow pain. Despite the name, only 5-10% of cases are related to tennis. Most develop the condition through repetitive manual work - painters, carpenters, butchers and office workers with heavy mouse use are typically overrepresented.

Symptoms: Burning or aching pain on the outside of the elbow that can radiate down towards the wrist. You notice it particularly with gripping and lifting - lifting the coffee pot, opening a door or unscrewing a lid. Many experience 50-75% reduced grip strength.

Prognosis: 89% report improvement within 12 months. Eccentric strength training shows 81% pain reduction after 7 weeks. Most notice significant improvement after 6-8 weeks with proper treatment.

Carpal tunnel syndrome

2-3% of population

Go to full article on carpal tunnel syndrome →

Carpal tunnel syndrome is the most common nerve compression in the body. Women are affected three times more often than men, with peak between 45 and 60 years. The condition occurs when the median nerve is compressed in the narrow passage through the wrist.

Classic symptoms: Numbness and tingling in the thumb, index finger, middle finger and half the ring finger. Symptoms are typically worst at night - many wake up with a "sleeping" hand and instinctively shake it to get the feeling back.

Risk factors: Overweight (8% increased risk per BMI unit), repetitive hand work, pregnancy, type 2 diabetes and low metabolism.

Treatment

A night splint that keeps the wrist straight provides improvement in 50-70% within 2-3 months. In severe cases with muscle weakness, surgery may be relevant - with 90% success rate.

Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis)

7-10x rarer than tennis elbow

Go to full article on golfer's elbow →

Golfer's elbow is like tennis elbow, except the pain is on the inside of the elbow. The condition is 7-10 times rarer than tennis elbow and affects 0.4-1% of the population. Over 90% of cases are due to work-related strain - tradespeople, carpenters, plumbers and butchers are far more often affected than golfers.

Important to know

The ulnar nerve runs just behind the tender area. 20-60% of patients with golfer's elbow have concurrent nerve irritation causing tingling in the ring and little finger. This can significantly prolong treatment time.

De Quervain's tenosynovitis

6-10x more common in women

Go to full article on De Quervain's →

De Quervain's tenosynovitis causes pain on the thumb side of the wrist. The condition is especially common among new parents - hence the nickname "mommy's thumb". Symptoms typically appear around 8 months after birth from repeated lifting of the baby.

Test: Finkelstein's test - where you bend the thumb into the palm and twist the wrist towards the little finger side - typically reproduces the pain.

Trigger finger (snapping finger)

2-3% lifetime prevalence

Go to full article on trigger finger →

Trigger finger causes the finger to "catch" when you bend or straighten it. Prevalence rises to 5-20% in diabetics. Women are affected 2-6 times more often than men. The ring finger and thumb are most commonly affected.

Symptoms: Often starts with morning stiffness and a palpable lump at the finger's base. Eventually you notice clear clicking and snapping with movement. In severe cases, the finger can lock in a bent position.

Treatment

Cortisone injection gives 63-80% success (unlike tennis elbow, where cortisone isn't recommended). Around 80% avoid surgery with conservative treatment.

Cubital tunnel syndrome

Second most common nerve compression

Go to full article on cubital tunnel syndrome →

The ulnar nerve - the "funny bone nerve" - runs along the inside of the elbow in a shallow groove. When you bend the elbow, space in this channel reduces by up to 55%, and pressure inside the nerve increases fivefold. This explains why symptoms often worsen at night when many sleep with bent elbows.

Symptoms: Tingling or numbness in the little finger and the half of the ring finger nearest the little finger. Many describe it as electric shocks.

Risk factors: Heavy physical work (3x risk), smoking (2.5x), overweight, vibration exposure and prolonged elbow bending during PC work or phone use.

Thumb arthritis (CMC-1 arthritis)

>33% at 80 years

Go to full article on thumb arthritis →

Thumb arthritis is surprisingly common. Prevalence increases from around 6-7% at 50 years to over 33% at 80 years. Women are affected 10-15 times more often than men after menopause. Genetics is the strongest risk factor - heritability is a full 86%.

Important point

X-ray findings correlate poorly with symptoms. Up to 72% of women with visible arthritis on images have no pain whatsoever. Clinical examination is more important than images.

Mouse arm (WRULD)

57% of computer users

Go to full article on mouse arm →

"Mouse arm" isn't a medical diagnosis, but a collective term for work-related problems in the neck, shoulders, arms and hands. A Dutch study found that 57% of computer users experience symptoms.

Causes: Repetitive small movements, static work posture for hours, unfavourable ergonomics and psychosocial stress. Intensive mouse use over 25 hours per week quadruples the risk.

Surprising finding

Regular breaks alone have little effect without active movement. Strength training 2-3 times per week has the strongest evidence for reducing symptoms.

Red Flags - When to Seek Emergency Care

Seek Immediate Medical Attention For:

  • Suspected fracture: Severe pain after fall with visible deformity or inability to move
  • Rapid numbness/paralysis: Quickly increasing loss of sensation or strength
  • Infection signs: Fever combined with redness, swelling and warmth in arm/hand
  • Muscle wasting: Visible loss of muscle mass developing rapidly
  • Circulation problems: Cold, pale hand with absent pulse
  • Severe swelling: Rapid onset after injury with significant pain

What Can You Do Yourself?

Three principles apply to most problems in arm, elbow and hand:

1

Stay active within pain limits

Complete rest usually worsens the condition. Move the arm, but avoid movements that cause clear pain. Adapted activity keeps blood flow up.

2

Adjust ergonomics

Small changes in how you work can make a big difference. Check mouse height, screen position and how you hold tools. Active breaks are more effective than passive ones.

3

Seek help early

If you wait too long, acute problems can become chronic. Early treatment gives faster improvement. If problems last over 2-3 weeks, get in touch.

Eccentric Wrist Extension (for tennis elbow)

Hold a light weight (0.5-1 kg) with your arm on a table, wrist over the edge. Slowly lower the weight over 3-4 seconds. Use your other hand to lift back up. 3 sets of 15 repetitions, 2x daily.

Nerve Gliding (for carpal tunnel)

Straighten your arm, palm up. Make a fist, then open your fingers and extend the wrist back. Hold 2-3 seconds. 10 repetitions, 3x daily. Stop if symptoms worsen.

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

Most problems in arm, elbow and hand have a good prognosis with the right approach. 80-90% improve significantly, often without the need for surgery. But it takes time - tendons need 3-6 months to heal properly, and nerve compressions can require patience over several months.

Key point: Active rehabilitation with specific exercises gives better results than passive treatment alone. Your own effort is crucial for long-term success.

Honest about relapses

Many conditions have a risk of relapse if underlying factors aren't addressed. Ergonomics, training load and work conditions are often just as important as the treatment itself.

When should you seek help?

  • Pain lasting over 2-3 weeks despite self-treatment
  • Symptoms affecting sleep or daily activities
  • Uncertainty about what the problems are caused by
  • Numbness or tingling that doesn't go away
  • Pain that changes rapidly or gets worse

You don't need a referral to see a chiropractor. As authorised healthcare professionals with primary contact status, I can examine and treat you directly - and refer onwards to specialists or diagnostic imaging when necessary.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for tennis elbow to heal?

89% report improvement within 12 months. With proper treatment and exercises, you can often notice significant improvement after 6-8 weeks. Eccentric strength training shows 81% pain reduction after 7 weeks.

Does cortisone help with tennis elbow?

No, cortisone is not recommended for tennis elbow. Research shows that cortisone provides short-term improvement but poorer long-term results. For trigger finger, however, cortisone injection gives 63-80% success.

Can I train with arm pain?

Yes, adapted activity works better than complete rest. Move the arm, but avoid movements that cause clear pain. Strength training 2-3 times per week has the strongest evidence for reducing symptoms.

Do I need surgery for carpal tunnel syndrome?

Not necessarily. A night splint that keeps the wrist straight provides improvement in 50-70% within 2-3 months. In severe cases with muscle weakness, surgery may be relevant - with 90% success rate.

Do I need a referral to see a chiropractor?

No. As authorised healthcare professionals with primary contact status, chiropractors can examine and treat you directly - and refer onwards to specialists or diagnostic imaging when necessary.
