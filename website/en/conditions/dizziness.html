Dizziness - Complete Guide

Everything you need to know about causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Dizziness affects 15-30% of the population during their lifetime (Neuhauser, Handb Clin Neurol, 2016). It's one of the most common reasons people seek healthcare. Many go around with symptoms for weeks or months because they don't know where to turn.

You wake up in the middle of the night and the room is spinning. Or you get up from your desk after a long day and the world sways. Perhaps you just feel "fuzzy" and unsteady without knowing why. Sometimes it goes away on its own. But often there's a concrete cause that can be treated.

In Brief

  • Symptom - not a disease - with many possible causes
  • Most common: BPPV 17-42% of cases
  • Treatment: 80-90% success rate for BPPV
  • Red flags: Speech difficulties + dizziness = call 113
  • First appointment: 45 min, no referral necessary

What does "dizziness" mean?

Dizziness isn't one thing. It's an umbrella term covering vertigo (the room spinning), unsteadiness (swaying feeling) and light-headedness (foggy in the head). Identifying which type you have is key to the right treatment.

1

What is Dizziness?

Dizziness isn't one thing. It's an umbrella term covering several different experiences:

Three Types of Dizziness

  • Vertigo: The sensation that you or your surroundings are rotating. The room spins, or you feel like you're spinning even though you're standing still. This almost always stems from disturbances in the balance organ.
  • Unsteadiness: Often described as a swaying or rocking feeling - like standing on a boat deck. You don't feel quite stable on your feet.
  • Light-headedness: A diffuse feeling of being "foggy" in the head. Some describe it as almost fainting.

How Balance Works

Three systems continuously send information to the brain:

👂

Balance Organ

In the inner ear - registers the head's movements and position

👁

Eyes

Tell the brain where you are in relation to your surroundings

🦶

Body Sense

Sensors in muscles, tendons and joints report the body's position

When all three systems send matching signals, you feel stable. When one system sends incorrect information - or the systems send conflicting signals - dizziness occurs.

Causes of Dizziness - From Most to Least Common

Here are the main causes, sorted by how often they occur:

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)

17-42% of cases

Go to full article on BPPV →

BPPV is the most common cause of dizziness investigated in clinics (Bosner, BMC Fam Pract, 2018). The condition occurs when small calcium crystals detach from their place in the balance organ and end up in the semicircular canals.

Characteristics:

  • Short, intense spinning attacks (10-60 seconds)
  • Triggered by specific head movements - turning in bed, looking up, bending down
  • Nausea during attacks
  • Symptom-free between attacks

Vestibular Migraine

approx. 1-3% of population

Go to full article on vestibular migraine →

Lifetime prevalence around 1%, but newer American data suggests up to 2.7% (Formeister, Otol Neurotol, 2018). The Barany Society has identified this as "by far the most common cause" of combined neck pain and vestibular symptoms. Can occur with or without headache.

Vestibular Neuritis

3-10% in dizziness clinics

Go to full article on vestibular neuritis →

Annual incidence of 3.5-7 per 100,000 (Iwasaki, Auris Nasus Larynx, 2024). Accounts for 3-10% of diagnoses in specialised dizziness clinics. Viral inflammation of the balance nerve causing sudden, intense dizziness over several days. Hearing is not affected. 10-15% develop BPPV afterwards.

Orthostatic Hypotension

Drop in blood pressure when you stand up that temporarily reduces blood supply to the brain. Causes blackouts and brief dizziness. Usually harmless and improves by standing up more slowly.

Cervicogenic Dizziness

Go to full article on cervicogenic dizziness →

I must be honest here: Cervicogenic dizziness is controversial. The Barany Society published a position statement in 2022 where they "refrain from proposing diagnostic criteria for clinical use" due to lack of documentation for a mechanistic link.

They point out that 68% of the population experiences neck pain annually, and 11-20% experience dizziness annually. Much overlap occurs purely by chance. Nevertheless, some patients experience improvement when the neck is treated - we just don't know for certain why.

M�ni�re's Disease

Go to full article on M�ni�re's disease →

Chronic condition in the inner ear with episodic dizziness, fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus and pressure sensation in the ear. M�ni�re's is approximately 10 times rarer than BPPV (Neuhauser, 2016). Requires evaluation by an ENT specialist.

PPPD

Go to full article on PPPD (chronic dizziness) →

Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness. Chronic functional dizziness condition that often develops after an acute vestibular episode. Classified by the Barany Society as a functional - not psychiatric - disorder (2017). Daily dizziness over at least 3 months.

Vestibular Hypofunction

Go to full article on vestibular hypofunction →

Reduced or lost function in the balance organ - either on one side (unilateral) or both sides (bilateral). For unilateral, vestibular neuritis is the most common cause. Vestibular rehabilitation has strong evidence.

Dizziness After Concussion

Go to full article on problems after concussion →

50-84% experience dizziness after concussion. Post-Concussion Syndrome (PCS) occurs when symptoms persist beyond 3 months. 73% back to activity within 8 weeks with vestibular rehabilitation.

Whiplash and Dizziness

Go to full article on whiplash and dizziness →

40-70% of those with persistent whiplash complaints experience dizziness. Can have both cervicogenic component and vestibular dysfunction. Multimodal treatment with manual therapy and exercises has strong evidence.

How Can Chiropractic Help?

Let me be direct about what I can and cannot do.

Conditions with Strong Evidence

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)

Go to full article on BPPV →

Here I have solid evidence. Repositioning manoeuvres like Epley and Semont have success rates of 80-90% (StatPearls, 2024). Many patients notice immediate improvement after the first treatment.

Newer data from 2023 shows that horizontal canal BPPV is far more common than previously thought - up to 46% of cases (Frontiers in Neurology, 2023).

Conditions with Moderate Evidence

Vestibular rehabilitation: Cochrane reviews show that exercise-based vestibular rehabilitation provides improvement measured with DHI (Dizziness Handicap Inventory) - improvements of 5-42 points (Cochrane, 2015).

Neck-related dizziness: The evidence is limited. Some studies show that combined manual therapy and exercises can reduce symptoms, but good long-term studies are lacking.

Conditions Where I Refer Onwards

  • M�ni�re's disease - ENT specialist
  • Vestibular migraine - Neurologist
  • Central causes (stroke, MS) - A&E/neurologist
  • PPPD with significant anxiety component - Physiotherapist with vestibular specialisation

Realistic Expectations

For BPPV you can expect immediate to rapid improvement - often in 1-3 treatments. For other conditions it takes longer. Vestibular rehabilitation usually requires 4-8 weeks of exercises for noticeable improvement. Relapses are common - 30% within 1 year, up to 50% within 5 years for BPPV (Brandt, J Neurol, 2006).

Treatment Methods

Repositioning Manoeuvres for BPPV

Common Manoeuvres

  • Epley manoeuvre for posterior canal: A series of controlled head and body movements that guide the crystals back to the utricle
  • Semont manoeuvre - alternative for posterior canal
  • Barbecue manoeuvre (Lempert) for horizontal canal
  • Gufoni manoeuvre - alternative for horizontal canal

Vestibular Rehabilitation

A training programme that helps the brain compensate for vestibular dysfunction:

  • Gaze stabilisation: Exercises where you keep your gaze fixed on a point whilst moving your head
  • Habituation: Gradual exposure to movements that provoke dizziness
  • Balance training: Exercises on unstable surfaces, with eyes closed, or with head movements

Red Flags - When You Must Seek Emergency Help

CALL 113 IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • Sudden severe dizziness together with speech difficulties, facial paralysis, paralysis in arm/leg, or double vision
  • Unusually severe headache ("thunderclap headache") together with dizziness
  • Dizziness with chest pain or breathing difficulties
  • Disturbances of consciousness or severe confusion

These symptoms may indicate stroke or other serious condition.

The "Five Ds" that warn of central problem:

  • Diplopia (double vision)
  • Dysarthria (slurred speech)
  • Dysphagia (swallowing difficulties)
  • Dysmetria (clumsy movements)
  • Drop attacks (sudden falls)

Book appointment with doctor or chiropractor if:

  • Dizziness lasting over 3 days without improvement
  • Repeated dizziness attacks that disrupt daily life
  • Dizziness with hearing loss or tinnitus
  • Falls or repeated near-falls

Prevention and Self-Treatment

Measures You Can Do Yourself

  • Move: The myth of bed rest for dizziness has been debunked. Adapted movement stimulates the balance system and helps the brain recalibrate.
  • Vitamin D: Several meta-analyses show a connection between low vitamin D levels and BPPV. Supplementation may reduce relapse risk (Yang, J Neurol, 2020).
  • Hydration: Drink sufficient fluids. Dehydration can worsen dizziness symptoms.
  • Ergonomics: Screen at eye level, avoid forward-leaning head posture, take breaks every 30 minutes.
  • Stress management: Stress worsens many forms of dizziness. Simple breathing exercises can calm an overactive nervous system.
  • Secure your home: Remove loose rugs, ensure good lighting, use support handles in the bathroom.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a referral from a doctor?

No. Chiropractors have had primary contact status in Norway since 2006. You can book an appointment directly. I can also refer you onwards to a specialist if necessary.

How many treatments do I need?

For BPPV: Often 1-3 treatments. Most notice improvement after the first session. For other conditions: Usually 4-8 weeks with treatment and exercises.

Can dizziness come back?

Yes, especially BPPV. Around 30% experience new episodes within 1 year, and up to 50% within 5 years (Brandt, J Neurol, 2006). Vitamin D supplementation and home exercises can potentially reduce the risk.

Do I need MRI or CT?

In most cases clinical examination is sufficient. Diagnostic imaging is only necessary with red flags, suspected central cause, or when treatment doesn't give expected results.

Do medicines help?

Medicines don't cure BPPV - it's a mechanical problem requiring a mechanical solution. Vestibular suppressants can be used briefly in the acute phase, but delay the brain's compensation.

Is neck treatment dangerous?

Vertebral artery dissection is a very rare but serious complication. Before treatment I conduct screening for vascular risk factors. If I suspect vascular problems I refer without treating.

Can I exercise with dizziness?

Yes, adapted activity is important. Only avoid movements that cause severe worsening. Light cardio training (walking, cycling) is good. Be careful with yoga positions with extreme neck extension.

What's the difference between chiropractor and physiotherapist for dizziness?

Both can perform vestibular rehabilitation and repositioning manoeuvres. Chiropractors additionally have competence in joint treatment of the neck. For complex PPPD a physiotherapist with vestibular specialisation may be most appropriate.
