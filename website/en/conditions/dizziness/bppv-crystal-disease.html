BPPV (Crystal Disease)

Symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Crystal disease, medically called Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV), is the most common cause of dizziness. The condition accounts for 20-30% of all cases where patients seek help for dizziness problems, and constitutes over half of all cases of peripheral vertigo (StatPearls, 2022).

The lifetime prevalence in the adult population is 2.4%, with an annual incidence of 0.6% (von Brevern, J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry 2007). Among 75-year-olds, the incidence increases to 4-4.5%. Women are affected approximately twice as often as men.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Loose calcium crystals in the inner ear causing short, intense episodes of spinning dizziness
  • Prevalence: 20-30% of all dizziness cases - the most common cause
  • Treatment: Epley maneuver with 75-85% success rate after first treatment
  • Red flags: Hearing loss, persistent dizziness, headache, vision disturbances

When Symptoms Are NOT BPPV

With hearing loss, persistent dizziness that never stops, headache, speech problems or vision disturbances - see a doctor to rule out other conditions.

1

How Does the Condition Develop?

Inside the inner ear lies the balance organ with three semicircular canals that register head movements. Additionally, there are the utricle and saccule which contain small calcium carbonate crystals (otoliths). These crystals normally sit attached to a gel membrane and help register gravity and linear acceleration.

BPPV occurs when some of these crystals loosen and float into one of the semicircular canals. When you move your head, the loose crystals move the fluid in the canal in an unexpected way. This sends error signals to the brain that the head is spinning - even though it is not. The result is intense, brief dizziness.

75-85% symptom-free after 1 treatment

Causes and Risk Factors

From most common to rarest:

Idiopathic/Unknown Cause (50-70%)

Most cases occur without an identifiable triggering factor.

Age

The incidence increases with age. People over 60 have almost seven times higher risk.

Head Injuries (7-17%)

Trauma to the head can physically dislodge crystals from their attachment - see also whiplash and dizziness.

Previous Ear Infection/Vestibular Neuritis (10-15%)

Viral infection in the inner ear can trigger BPPV.

Meniere's Disease (0.5-31%)

Patients with Meniere's have higher incidence of BPPV.

Vitamin D Deficiency

Studies show a connection between low vitamin D levels and increased risk.

3

Symptoms

The most characteristic symptom is short episodes of intense spinning dizziness (vertigo) lasting from seconds to a maximum of one minute. The dizziness is typically triggered by:

  • Turning over in bed
  • Getting up from lying position
  • Looking up at the ceiling or high shelves
  • Bending forward

The episode usually comes after a few seconds delay (latency) from when you move your head. It is most intense at the beginning and gradually subsides. Between episodes, many feel completely normal.

Symptoms That Do NOT Belong to BPPV

  • Hearing loss or tinnitus
  • Persistent dizziness that never stops - may indicate PPPD or vestibular hypofunction
  • Headache, speech problems or vision disturbances
4

Which Semicircular Canal Is Affected?

The inner ear has three semicircular canals on each side:

Posterior Canal (80-90% of cases)

By far the most common form. Diagnosed with the Dix-Hallpike test and treated with the Epley or Semont maneuver.

Horizontal/Lateral Canal (5-30%, possibly up to 46%)

May be underdiagnosed. Treated with Barbecue roll (Lempert) or Gufoni maneuver.

Anterior Canal (1-3%)

The rarest form. Crystals rarely fall upward due to anatomical position.

5

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is primarily made through medical history and clinical examination. The most important tests are:

Dix-Hallpike Test

The patient sits on the bench with head turned 45 degrees toward the suspected side, then is quickly laid back until the head hangs 20 degrees below the horizontal plane. With posterior canal BPPV, upbeating, rotational nystagmus is seen after a few seconds delay.

Supine Roll Test

The patient lies flat on their back and the head is quickly rolled from side to side. Tests for horizontal canal BPPV.

Do I Need MRI or CT?

In the vast majority of cases, clinical examination is sufficient. Imaging is only relevant if symptoms do not match BPPV, you have red flags (headache, neurological symptoms), or treatment fails repeatedly without explanation.

6

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Treatment consists of specific repositioning maneuvers that move the loose crystals out of the semicircular canal and back to the utricle where they belong.

Epley Maneuver

The most used and studied treatment for posterior canal BPPV. A meta-analysis showed symptom relief in 74-75% of patients after one treatment - over six times better than the control group. With repeated treatments, over 90% achieve improvement.

Other Maneuvers

  • Semont Maneuver: Alternative to Epley, especially useful for cupulolithiasis
  • Gufoni Maneuver and Barbecue Roll: Used for horizontal canal BPPV

What Can You Do Yourself?

Once the diagnosis is confirmed and you have learned the correct technique, you can perform repositioning maneuvers at home during new episodes. Other measures that can help include vitamin D supplements if blood tests show deficiency, avoiding rapid head movements in the acute phase, and continuing normal activities as soon as possible.

Treatment Course

Most patients need 1-3 treatments. Median duration of symptoms is 2 weeks. Early treatment often leads to faster improvement.

7

Prognosis

BPPV is a benign condition with very good prognosis. Repositioning maneuvers provide immediate improvement for most.

Residual Dizziness After Treatment

31-61% of patients report persistent mild dizziness or unsteadiness after successful treatment, even though nystagmus and positional vertigo are gone. This residual dizziness usually lasts a median of 10 days and typically resolves within 3 months. In some cases, this can develop into PPPD (Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness).

Recurrence Is Common

  • Approximately 15-23% experience recurrence within the first year
  • 5-year recurrence rate is around 40%
  • Up to 50% may experience a new episode within 5 years

Prevention of Recurrence

Vitamin D supplementation in patients with documented deficiency can reduce the risk of new episodes. A meta-analysis showed significant reduction in recurrence rate (relative risk 0.37).

8

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

See a Doctor for the Following Symptoms

  • Hearing loss or tinnitus together with dizziness
  • Persistent dizziness that does not stop between episodes
  • Headache - especially new onset, severe headache
  • Neurological symptoms: Double vision, speech problems, swallowing difficulties, numbness, muscle weakness
  • Walking difficulties beyond what is normal with dizziness
  • Neck pain after trauma
9

Frequently Asked Questions

How quickly can I expect improvement?
Most notice immediate improvement after the first treatment. Some experience complete relief on the treatment table. Others need 2-3 treatments.
Can BPPV come back?
Yes. Approximately 15-23% experience recurrence within one year, and up to 40-50% within 5 years. The risk increases with age, vitamin D deficiency and previous episodes.
Do medications help?
No. BPPV is a mechanical problem that requires a mechanical solution. Vestibular suppressant medications can be used briefly for nausea, but they do not cure the condition.
Is it dangerous to do the maneuvers?
No, repositioning maneuvers are very safe. Temporary dizziness during the maneuver itself is normal. Caution should be exercised with severe neck stiffness or recent neck trauma.
Can I fly with BPPV?
Yes, BPPV is not dangerous and flying does not worsen the condition. You may experience discomfort with rapid head movements.
