Horizontal Canal BPPV

When crystal disease causes sideways dizziness | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Horizontal canal BPPV, also called lateral canal BPPV, is a specific variant of crystal disease where loose ear crystals (otoconia) end up in the horizontal semicircular canal of the inner ear. This canal lies horizontally and registers sideways head movements - it is activated when you shake your head to say "no."

Traditionally, horizontal canal BPPV has been assumed to account for only 5-30% of all BPPV cases. A large study from 2023 with 3,975 patients found that 46.3% had horizontal canal involvement - almost as common as posterior canal BPPV (47.8%). This difference is likely due to underdiagnosis.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Crystals in the horizontal semicircular canal that registers sideways head movements
  • Prevalence: Traditionally 5-30%, but newer studies show up to 46%
  • Two variants: Geotropic (60-70%) and apogeotropic (30-40%)
  • Treatment: BBQ-roll 74-85% improvement, Gufoni 71-92% effectiveness

When Should You Seek Immediate Help?

With continuous dizziness lasting over 2 minutes, sudden hearing loss, double vision, severe headache, facial paralysis, or coordination problems - contact a doctor immediately.

1

The Two Main Variants

Geotropic Variant (60-70%)

The crystals float freely in the posterior part of the canal. When you turn your head to one side, the crystals move toward the ground. Dizziness is typically strongest when lying on the affected side. This is the most common and easiest variant to treat.

Apogeotropic Variant (30-40%)

The crystals are located either in the anterior part of the canal near the ampulla, or they are attached to the sensory cells (cupulolithiasis). Eye movements beat away from the ground. Confusingly, dizziness is often strongest on the unaffected side. Treatment is more challenging.

60-70% Geotropic variant
30-40% Apogeotropic variant
46% Of all BPPV cases
2

Symptoms

Horizontal canal BPPV differs from the posterior canal variant in several ways:

  • Sideways dizziness: The feeling that the room is rolling sideways, often described as being "in a tumble dryer"
  • Longer duration: Episodes may last longer than posterior canal BPPV - often over 60 seconds
  • More severe nausea: Autonomic symptoms are often more pronounced
  • Triggered from both sides: Can be triggered when turning to either side in bed
  • Shorter or no latency: Dizziness may start immediately with head movement
3

Diagnosis

Supine Roll Test (The Gold Standard)

The patient lies on their back with the head elevated 30 degrees. The head is quickly turned 90 degrees to one side and held for 30-60 seconds while eye movements are observed. The test is then repeated to the opposite side. The test's sensitivity is reported at 89.5% (Barany Society Guidelines).

With Geotropic Variant

Eyes beat toward the ground on both sides, but strongest on the affected side.

With Apogeotropic Variant

Eyes beat away from the ground, and the weakest response is on the affected side.

Important Sequence

The supine roll test should ideally be performed before the Dix-Hallpike test, as Dix-Hallpike can move crystals and affect the results.

Red Flags - Contact a Doctor Immediately for:

  • Dizziness lasting continuously over 2 minutes (BPPV causes brief episodes)
  • Sudden hearing loss or severe tinnitus
  • Double vision or other visual disturbances
  • Severe headache unlike any previously experienced
  • Facial paralysis, numbness, or speech difficulties
  • Coordination problems or unsteady gait
4

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Treatment of horizontal canal BPPV requires specialized repositioning maneuvers:

BBQ-Roll (Lempert Maneuver)

The patient is rotated 360 degrees in four steps of 90 degrees each, with head elevated 30 degrees. Each position is held for 30-60 seconds. A systematic review from 2025 concluded that BBQ-roll is an effective treatment. Studies show 74% improvement after one treatment and 85% within three treatments.

Gufoni Maneuver

A systematic review from 2024 with 10 studies and 1,025 patients showed that the Gufoni maneuver is effective. For geotropic variant, success rate is 71-92%, while apogeotropic variant has lower response at 63-78%. The advantage is that it is easier to perform on elderly patients.

TRV Chair

For complex cases, a TRV chair (a specialized treatment chair) can be used. This method provides precision control over speed, angle, and amplitude.

Treatment Course - Realistic Expectations

  • Best case (approx. 40-50%): Symptom-free after 1-2 treatments. 75% are symptom-free after the first consultation
  • Typical case (approx. 30-40%): Requires 3-5 treatments over 2-3 weeks
  • Complicated cases (approx. 15-20%): Bilateral involvement or apogeotropic variant may require 6+ treatments
5

Prognosis and Recurrence

Horizontal canal BPPV generally has good prognosis with treatment, but recurrence is relatively common:

13-23% Recurrence first year
13-65% Recurrence over 2+ years

Risk Factors for Recurrence

  • Vitamin D deficiency: Patients with levels below 12.74 ng/mL had significantly higher recurrence rate (70.5% vs. 22.5%)
  • Advanced age
  • Hypertension and diabetes
  • Previous recurrence
6

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is horizontal canal BPPV worse than posterior canal?
Many patients experience the symptoms as more intense. This may be due to the horizontal canal's larger diameter and stronger response to head movements.
Can I do exercises at home?
Some home exercises can be useful as a supplement, but initial diagnosis and treatment should be performed by qualified healthcare professionals. It is important to know which variant you have.
Is horizontal canal BPPV dangerous?
The condition itself is not dangerous, but the intense dizziness significantly increases fall risk. It is important to get a correct diagnosis to rule out other causes.
