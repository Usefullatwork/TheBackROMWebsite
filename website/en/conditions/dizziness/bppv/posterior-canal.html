Posterior Canal BPPV: When the World Spins Backwards

The most common form of crystal disease (80-90%) | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) is a mechanical disorder of the inner ear's balance system. The condition occurs when small calcium crystite (otoliths) detach from their normal position in the utricle and migrate into one of the three semicircular canals.

The posterior semicircular canal is the most commonly affected canal, accounting for 60-90% of all BPPV cases (Frontiers in Neurology, 2023). This is due to the anatomical position of the canals - the posterior canal is positioned lowest both when standing and lying down, so gravity naturally pulls loose crystals there.

BPPV is the most common cause of vestibular dizziness and accounts for 20-30% of all dizziness consultations (StatPearls, 2022). The lifetime prevalence is approximately 2.4%, and women are affected twice as often as men.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Loose crystals in the posterior semicircular canal - the most common form of BPPV (80-90%)
  • Most common causes: Idiopathic (50-83%), head trauma (10-20%), vestibular migraine
  • Treatment: Epley maneuver with 75-95% success rate
  • Red flags: Constant dizziness, double vision, speech difficulties, muscle weakness

When Should You Seek Immediate Help?

With constant dizziness (not triggered by head movements), double vision, speech difficulties, muscle weakness, or loss of consciousness - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

Causes and Risk Factors

From Most Common to Rarest:

Idiopathic (50-83%)

No known triggering cause. The crystals likely detach due to age-related degeneration.

Head Injury and Trauma (10-20%)

Even mild head injury or whiplash can dislodge crystals. Traumatic BPPV has a higher recurrence rate.

Vestibular Migraine (5-15%)

Patients with migraine have increased risk for BPPV.

Inner Ear Diseases (2-5%)

Meniere's disease, vestibular neuritis, labyrinthitis.

Risk Factors for Development and Recurrence:

  • Age over 50 years
  • Female sex
  • Hypertension and diabetes
  • Vitamin D deficiency
  • Osteoporosis
2

Symptoms

The characteristic symptom of posterior canal BPPV is short, intense episodes of rotational dizziness triggered by specific head movements.

Typical Triggering Movements:

  • Lying down in bed
  • Rolling over in bed from side to side
  • Tilting the head backward (looking up at the ceiling)
  • Bending forward to pick something up from the floor

Characteristics of Symptoms:

10-60 sec Typical duration
1-5 sec Latency after movement
80-90% Of BPPV cases

Accompanying Symptoms

Nausea, sweating, and unsteadiness are common. BPPV normally does not cause hearing loss, tinnitus (ringing in the ears), or neurological symptoms.

3

Diagnosis

Dix-Hallpike Test (The Gold Standard)

The patient sits on the examination bench with the head turned 45 degrees to one side. The patient is quickly laid backward so that the head hangs over the edge of the bench. The examiner observes the eyes for nystagmus (involuntary eye movements).

A positive test shows characteristic upbeat and rotational nystagmus that begins after a few seconds, reaches a peak, and then subsides within one minute.

Precise Diagnostics at the Chiropractor

At Chiropractor Mads Finstad, VNG glasses (videonystagmography) and TRV chair are used for precise diagnostics. VNG glasses provide a magnified image of eye movements and remove the patient's ability to fixate their gaze, making nystagmus more visible.

Red Flags - Seek Emergency Care or Call 113 for:

  • Acute dizziness with muscle weakness or numbness in arm/leg
  • Speech or swallowing difficulties
  • Double vision or visual disturbances
  • Severe headache, especially "the worst headache of your life"
  • Constant dizziness not triggered by head movements
  • Loss of consciousness
4

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Posterior canal BPPV is treated with repositioning maneuvers that move the loose crystals out of the semicircular canal and back to the utricle. The most commonly used and best documented method is the Epley maneuver.

Effect and Evidence

  • 75-95% of patients become symptom-free after one or more treatments (Cochrane Review, 2014)
  • 32-90% experience improvement after the first treatment
  • Success rate increases to nearly 100% after 3-4 treatments
  • The Epley maneuver is significantly more effective than placebo

What Can You Do Yourself?

After treatment:

  • Avoid lying flat for the first few hours after treatment
  • Consider sleeping with your head slightly elevated the first night
  • Avoid sudden head movements for the first few days

Treatment Course

1-3 treatments are usually sufficient. Many become symptom-free after one treatment. Follow-up 1-4 weeks after treatment is recommended.

5

Prognosis

BPPV generally has a very good prognosis. Most become symptom-free after treatment, and many experience spontaneous improvement even without treatment (median duration 2 weeks).

Recurrence Is Common:

  • 15-40% experience recurrence within 1-2 years
  • 50% may experience recurrence over a 10-year period
  • Recurrence most often occurs in the same ear and same canal

New Treatment Is Equally Effective

Although recurrence is common, new treatment is just as effective as the first treatment.

6

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between posterior canal BPPV and horizontal canal BPPV?
Posterior canal BPPV is the most common form (60-90%) and is typically triggered by lying down or rolling over in bed. Horizontal canal BPPV accounts for 5-30% and is often triggered by turning the head from side to side while lying down. Treatment is different.
How long does an episode last?
A typical episode lasts 10-60 seconds. If the dizziness lasts for minutes, hours, or days, other causes should be considered.
Can BPPV be dangerous?
BPPV is a benign condition that is not dangerous in itself. However, the intense dizziness can lead to falls, especially in the elderly.
Can vitamin D prevent BPPV?
Research shows that vitamin D supplementation combined with calcium can reduce the recurrence rate in patients with proven vitamin D deficiency (Jeong et al., 2020).
