Cervicogenic Dizziness (Neck-Related Dizziness)

Symptoms, diagnosis, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Cervicogenic dizziness (CGD) describes a diffuse feeling of unsteadiness, spatial disorientation, or a swaying sensation that occurs in connection with neck pain or neck dysfunction. The condition is debated in medical circles, and the Barany Society published a position paper in 2022 concluding that there is currently insufficient evidence to establish diagnostic criteria.

Nevertheless: Dizziness is one of the most common reasons people seek healthcare - up to 20% of adults experience dizziness annually. A large study from 2022 with 2,361 patients showed that 40.1% of patients with neck pain also experienced dizziness. Many of these respond well to neck treatment.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Dizziness and unsteadiness that occurs in connection with neck pain - often feels like a swaying or floating sensation
  • Most common causes: Myofascial dysfunction (~60%), joint stiffness in the upper neck (C1-C3), poor work posture, whiplash
  • Treatment: Moderate evidence for manual therapy + exercises - 60-85% improve after 4-8 treatments
  • Red flags: Double vision, speech difficulties, swallowing problems, numbness, weakness

When should you seek immediate help?

For double vision, speech difficulties, swallowing problems, numbness or sudden weakness in arms/legs - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

How Does It Differ from Other Types of Dizziness?

Cervicogenic dizziness typically feels like:

  • A swaying or floating sensation ("like walking on a boat")
  • Spatial disorientation - difficulty orienting yourself
  • Unsteadiness that worsens with neck movements or prolonged sitting
  • Symptom duration from minutes to hours (not seconds like with BPPV)

The classic spinning dizziness - the room spinning around - is less typical of cervicogenic dizziness and often points to other causes such as BPPV (crystal disease) or vestibular migraine.

Causes and Risk Factors

From most common to rarest:

Myofascial Dysfunction and Trigger Points (most common)

58.5% had myofascial pain syndrome, and 59.7% of these had dizziness. Tight muscles - especially in the suboccipital muscles, upper trapezius and sternocleidomastoid - can disturb proprioceptive signaling.

Joint Stiffness in the Upper Neck

The upper cervical joints (C0-C1-C2) contain a very high density of proprioceptive sensors. When these joints become stiff, inaccurate signals are sent to the brain.

Whiplash and Neck Injuries

Dizziness is reported in 20-58% of patients with whiplash-associated injury.

Prolonged Poor Head Posture

"Tech neck" - constantly leaning head forward in front of screens.

Risk Factors

  • Gender: Women have 1.64 times higher risk
  • Occupation: Office work with lots of screen time
  • Stress: Increases muscle tension in neck and shoulders
  • Previous neck injury: Whiplash, falls, sports injuries - including concussion
3

Symptoms

Typical Presentation:

  • Diffuse unsteadiness or swaying feeling lasting minutes to hours
  • Feeling that the head is "heavy" or "clumsy"
  • Worsening with prolonged sitting, especially in front of a computer screen
  • Neck pain or stiffness occurring together with the dizziness
  • Headache starting in the neck and radiating forward
  • Mild nausea (rarely severe vomiting)

What Does NOT Fit with Cervicogenic Dizziness:

  • Short, intense spinning attacks (10-60 sec): Suggests BPPV
  • Hearing loss or tinnitus: Points to vestibular pathology like Meniere's disease or vestibular neuritis
  • Severe attacks with light/sound sensitivity: Vestibular migraine
  • Neurological symptoms: Double vision, speech difficulties - requires immediate evaluation
4

Diagnosis

Cervicogenic dizziness is a diagnosis of exclusion - other causes such as BPPV, vestibular hypofunction and PPPD must be ruled out first. There is no gold standard test, but the combination of medical history, clinical examination and specific tests provides good diagnostic accuracy.

Clinical Tests

Cervical Torsion Test

The body is rotated while the head is held still. Specificity 92%, sensitivity 72%.

Cervical Repositioning Test

The patient should return the head to neutral position with closed eyes. Deviation over 4.5 degrees suggests disturbed proprioception.

Flexion-Rotation Test

Sensitivity 91%, specificity 90% for C1-C2 dysfunction.

Red Flags - Seek immediate medical help for:

  • Double vision (diplopia)
  • Slurred speech (dysarthria)
  • Swallowing difficulties (dysphagia)
  • Sudden falls without warning
  • Severe nausea with vomiting
  • Numbness in face, arms or legs
  • Weakness in arms or legs
  • Severe headache of new type
5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

A systematic review from 2022 (De Vestel et al.) concluded that there is moderate evidence that manual therapy reduces dizziness symptoms, and that the effect is enhanced when manual therapy is combined with exercises.

Manual Therapy

Reid et al. (2014, 2015) conducted several randomized controlled trials showing that both Mulligan SNAGs and Maitland mobilizations gave significant improvement compared to placebo - effects that persisted at 12-month follow-up.

Exercise Program

  • Deep neck flexors: Strengthening the deep stabilizing muscles
  • Eye-neck coordination: Recalibrating the cooperation between vision and neck
  • Balance training: Progressive challenge of the balance system

What Can You Do Yourself?

Chin Tuck Exercise

Pull the chin straight back. Hold 5 seconds, repeat 10 times.

Ergonomic Adjustments

Screen at eye level, breaks every 30 minutes.

Stress Management

Breathing techniques, good sleep hygiene.

Treatment Course

  • Acute cases: Noticeable improvement within 2-4 treatments
  • Typical course: 4-8 treatments over 4-6 weeks
  • Chronic cases: 8-12 weeks

Treatment Response

60-85% of patients respond well to combined manual therapy and exercises (Lystad et al., 2011).

6

Prognosis - What to Expect

The prognosis for cervicogenic dizziness is generally good with proper treatment. Reid et al. (2015) showed that the effects of manual therapy persist at 12-month follow-up.

60-85% respond well to treatment
4-6 weeks typical time to improvement
12 mo lasting effect

Realistic Expectations

  • Most notice improvement within 4-6 weeks
  • Complete symptom freedom is achieved in many, but not all
  • Relapses can occur with persistent risk factors
  • Long-term results depend on personal effort with exercises
7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the neck really cause dizziness?
Yes. The neck contains a high density of proprioceptive sensors. When these send inaccurate signals, a "sensory mismatch" can occur that is experienced as dizziness. The Barany Society (2022) acknowledges that this can probably occur in a subset of patients.
How do I distinguish cervicogenic dizziness from BPPV?
BPPV causes short (10-60 sec), intense spinning attacks. Cervicogenic dizziness causes a more persistent unsteadiness that worsens with neck movements and is often accompanied by neck pain.
Do I need an MRI of my neck?
Usually not. MRI is primarily relevant for red flags or lack of treatment response after 8-12 weeks. Degenerative findings are very common even in symptom-free people.
How long until I get better?
Most notice improvement within 4-6 weeks. Acute cases can respond faster (2-4 treatments), chronic cases may require 8-12 weeks.
