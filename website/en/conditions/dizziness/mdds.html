Mal de Debarquement Syndrome (MdDS)

When land sickness won't go away | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Mal de debarquement - French for "sickness of disembarkation" - is a condition most people have experienced. After a boat trip, it can feel like the ground is still swaying for a few hours. For most people, this sensation disappears within one to two days. Dizziness can have many causes, and MdDS is one of the rarer ones.

For some, the experience becomes long-lasting. When the rocking sensation persists for more than 48 hours, and often months to years, we talk about Mal de Debarquement Syndrome (MdDS). This is a rare neurological condition affecting approximately 1.3% of patients at neuro-otological clinics.

The condition is characterized by a persistent, non-spinning dizziness - often described as "rocking", "swaying motion" or "bobbing". Many use descriptions like "it feels like being on a boat all the time" or "the ground is moving under me".

Key Summary

  • What it is: A rare neurological condition with persistent sensation of rocking/swaying motion after sea travel or other passive transport
  • Most common triggers: Cruise/boat travel (most common), air travel, long car journey, spontaneously without motion trigger (rarest)
  • Who is affected: Women (80-90%), typical age 40-50 years, increased risk with migraine
  • Chiropractor's role: Rule out other causes of dizziness, address neck/back pain - MdDS requires specialist treatment

When to Seek Help Immediately?

With hearing loss, double vision, numbness in face/arms/legs, muscle weakness, speech difficulties or sudden severe headache - call 113 or go to emergency room.

Causes and Risk Factors

Triggering Factors - From Most Common to Rarest

Motion-Triggered MdDS (Most Common)

  • Cruise or boat travel - by far the most common trigger
  • Air travel
  • Long car journey or train ride
  • Elevator ride or walking on a pier

Temporary "mal de debarquement" after sea travel occurs in 73-86% of people, even among experienced sailors. Longer trips and rough seas can increase the risk, but the duration of exposure does not seem to determine severity.

Spontaneous/Non-Motion-Triggered MdDS (Rarer)

  • Hormonal changes - studies show that ~44% had symptom onset during perimenopause or menopause
  • Psychological stress or major life events
  • Surgical procedures
  • Head injury
  • Without identifiable cause

Who Is Affected?

MdDS has a clear demographic profile:

  • Women make up 80-90% of cases
  • Average onset age: 43-50 years
  • Sex ratio: Women:men approximately 9:1
  • Migraine: People with migraine have increased risk of developing MdDS. These patients may also experience vestibular migraine
3

Symptoms

Main Symptoms

The characteristic symptom is a persistent oscillating dizziness - a sensation of:

  • Rocking
  • Swaying motion
  • Bobbing (up-down movement)

Symptoms are present continuously or most of the day. They are not accompanied by the spinning sensation (vertigo) seen in BPPV (crystal disease).

Associated Symptoms

Many with MdDS develop additional symptoms:

  • Spatial disorientation
  • Fatigue
  • Intolerance to visual motion (busy environments, escalators)
  • Headache - often migraine-like
  • Anxiety and depression
  • Cognitive difficulties ("brain fog")
  • Concentration problems
  • Sleep difficulties
  • Neck and back pain

The Paradox: Better in Motion

An important characteristic of MdDS is that symptoms often improve when in passive motion - such as driving a car or sitting on a train. But symptoms return - and may temporarily worsen - when the motion stops.

This distinguishes MdDS from PPPD (Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness), where symptoms typically worsen with motion.

4

Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

MdDS is diagnosed based on medical history and symptom profile. There is no blood test, MRI or other test that can confirm the diagnosis. In 2020, the Barany Society published international diagnostic criteria:

All five criteria must be met:

  1. Non-spinning dizziness with oscillating sensation (rocking, swaying, bobbing) - present continuously or most of the day
  2. Symptom onset within 48 hours after ending passive motion
  3. Symptoms improve temporarily with new exposure to passive motion (e.g., car driving)
  4. Symptoms last >48 hours
  5. Symptoms are not better explained by another disease or condition

Classification by Duration

  • MdDS "in evolution": Symptoms less than 1 month, still under observation
  • Transient MdDS: Symptoms resolve within 1 month
  • Persistent MdDS: Symptoms last more than 1 month

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor Quickly

  • Hearing loss in one or both ears
  • Double vision or vision disturbances
  • Numbness or tingling in face/arms/legs
  • Muscle weakness in arms or legs
  • Speech or swallowing difficulties
  • Sudden, severe headache ("worst headache of my life")
  • Fever combined with dizziness
  • Consciousness disturbances
5

Treatment

Important to Know

MdDS is a challenging condition to treat. Research is limited, and there is no standard treatment that helps everyone. A scoping review from 2023 identified improvement with several treatment methods, but emphasized that evidence is still limited.

Specialized Treatment - VOR Readaptation (Dai Protocol)

The most promising treatment is optokinetic VOR readaptation, developed by Dr. Mingjia Dai at Mount Sinai Hospital. The treatment involves visual stimulation with moving stripes in the visual field, while passively rolling the head from side to side, over repeated sessions.

Results:

  • ~70% achieved significant improvement immediately after treatment
  • After 1 year, 52% of motion-triggered cases were still significantly improved
  • Patients treated within 1 year of symptom onset had better prognosis (62%) than those with symptom duration over 3 years (30%)

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Honest expectation management: Research shows that chiropractic and physiotherapeutic treatment has no documented effect on the MdDS symptoms themselves.

However, the chiropractor can contribute with:

  • Thorough assessment - Many with dizziness have other causes that can be treated
  • Rule out mechanical causes - Neck problems can cause dizziness, but this is a different condition than MdDS
  • Refer to the right specialist - ENT doctor or neurologist for further assessment
  • Treat associated complaints - Neck and back pain that often accompanies long-term MdDS
  • General vestibular screening - Detect other balance problems
6

Prognosis

Transient Cases

Most people who experience "mal de debarquement" after a boat trip become symptom-free within 24-48 hours. Even without treatment, most improve within one year.

Persistent MdDS

For those with persistent MdDS (more than 1 month), prognosis is more variable:

  • Spontaneous improvement can occur, but symptoms can last months to years
  • Longer symptom duration = poorer prognosis - those treated early (within 1 year) have better results
  • Recurrence is common - later episodes often last longer than the first
  • Quality of life can be significantly affected
7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can MdDS become chronic?
Yes, for some the symptoms persist for many years. Studies show that the longer symptoms have lasted, the more difficult they can be to treat. Early treatment (within 1 year) gives better results.
Is MdDS the same as motion sickness?
No. Motion sickness occurs during movement, while MdDS occurs after the movement has stopped. With MdDS, one often feels better in motion - the opposite of motion sickness.
Can a chiropractor help with MdDS?
Chiropractic treatment has no documented effect on the MdDS symptoms themselves. But a chiropractor can help rule out other causes of dizziness, treat associated neck/back pain, and refer to the right specialist.
Is MdDS a psychiatric condition?
No. MdDS is a neurological condition that probably involves maladaptation in the brain's balance centers. Anxiety and depression can follow prolonged illness, but are not the cause of MdDS. Another functional dizziness disorder is vestibular hypofunction, which is due to actual damage to the balance organ.
