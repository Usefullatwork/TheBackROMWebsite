Meniere's Disease

Symptoms, causes and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Meniere's disease is a chronic inner ear disorder characterized by episodic attacks of intense rotational dizziness (vertigo), fluctuating hearing loss, tinnitus (ringing in the ear), and a feeling of pressure or fullness in the affected ear. See our main article on dizziness for an overview of all dizziness conditions.

The disease was first described by the French physician Prosper Meniere in 1861. He was the first to link the symptoms to the inner ear rather than the brain, as previously assumed.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Chronic inner ear disorder with attacks of vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus and pressure sensation
  • Prevalence: Rare - affects approximately 50-200 per 100,000, most common between ages 40-60
  • Treatment: Combination of lifestyle measures, medication and vestibular rehabilitation - no cure exists
  • Important: Diagnosis is made by ENT specialist. Chiropractors can help with secondary neck problems

When should you seek immediate help?

For sudden, total hearing loss, dizziness with double vision, swallowing or speech difficulties, fainting, or severe headache with dizziness - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

How Common is Meniere's Disease?

Meniere's disease is relatively rare. Prevalence varies between studies, but estimates are 50-200 per 100,000 adults. The disease most often affects people between 40 and 60 years old, with a peak in the 40s and 50s. Women are somewhat more commonly affected than men, with a ratio of approximately 1.5-1.9:1. Less than 3% of cases occur in people under 18 years old.

What Happens in the Ear?

The underlying mechanism is believed to be endolymphatic hydrops - an accumulation of fluid (endolymph) in the inner ear. The inner ear consists of a labyrinth system filled with fluid that must have the right pressure and chemical composition to function normally.

In Meniere's disease, too much fluid is produced, or drainage does not work properly. This causes the pressure to rise. At high pressure, a thin membrane (Reissner's membrane) can rupture, and fluids with different chemical compositions mix. This disrupts the signals to the brain and triggers the characteristic attack.

Genetic Component

Approximately 7-10% of patients with Meniere's disease have a family history of the condition.

3

Symptoms

The Classic Symptoms:

1. Vertigo (Rotational Dizziness)

  • Sudden, intense attacks where the world "spins"
  • Duration typically 20 minutes to 12 hours
  • Often accompanied by nausea and vomiting

2. Hearing Loss

  • Fluctuating, sensorineural hearing loss
  • Typically affects low frequencies first ("bass loss")
  • May be temporary initially but often becomes permanent over time

3. Tinnitus (Ear Ringing)

  • Usually low-frequency, "humming" or "buzzing" sound
  • May become stronger before and during attacks
  • Often becomes constant over time

Additionally, many experience a feeling of pressure or "fullness" in the affected ear, aura before attacks (changed tinnitus, increasing pressure), and extreme exhaustion after attacks.

Phases of the Disease

  • Early phase: Intense vertigo attacks. Hearing often normalizes between attacks
  • Middle phase: Attacks continue. Hearing loss becomes more permanent. Tinnitus often becomes constant
  • Late phase ("burn-out"): The violent dizziness attacks decrease or disappear. About 34% experience spontaneous remission from episodic vertigo
4

Diagnosis

There is no single test that can confirm Meniere's disease. The diagnosis is made clinically based on international criteria (Barany Society/AAO-HNS, 2015/2020). It is important to distinguish Meniere's from other conditions such as vestibular migraine, vestibular neuritis and BPPV (crystal disease).

Criteria for Definite Meniere's Disease:

  • At least two spontaneous vertigo episodes lasting 20 minutes to 12 hours
  • Audiometrically documented low- to mid-frequency sensorineural hearing loss in the affected ear
  • Fluctuating symptoms (hearing loss, tinnitus or pressure sensation) in the affected ear
  • Other causes excluded

Red Flags - Seek medical help immediately for:

  • Sudden, total hearing loss
  • Dizziness with double vision, swallowing or speech difficulties
  • Fainting or loss of consciousness
  • Severe headache with dizziness
  • High fever with dizziness and ear symptoms
  • Frequent falls without warning (drop attacks)
5

Treatment

There is no cure for Meniere's disease. The goal of treatment is to reduce attack frequency, relieve symptoms and improve quality of life.

Conservative Treatment and Lifestyle

Salt Reduction

A stable, low salt intake (<1500-2000 mg sodium daily) is often recommended as first-line treatment. The theory is that this can stabilize the fluid balance in the inner ear. The Cochrane review from 2018 found no randomized studies supporting this, but many patients report improvement.

Medical Treatment

Betahistine (Betaserc)

The most commonly used medication in Europe. The BEMED study (2016) found no significant difference between betahistine and placebo, but the medication is well tolerated and is often tried for 3-6 months.

Vestibular Rehabilitation

AAO-HNS recommends vestibular rehabilitation for patients with chronic unsteadiness between attacks. A meta-analysis from 2023 found moderate effect on quality of life.

Important About Vestibular Rehabilitation

Vestibular rehabilitation is not recommended during acute attacks - it is most effective when vertigo episodes are under control.

What Can the Chiropractor Contribute?

Chiropractors cannot treat the fluid accumulation in the inner ear itself. But many people with Meniere's disease develop secondary problems that I can help with:

  • Neck and shoulder tension: Chronic stress and abnormal head position during attacks often lead to muscle tightness, which can cause cervicogenic dizziness
  • Balance training: During "good" periods, adapted training can strengthen compensation mechanisms, similar to treatment for vestibular hypofunction
  • Guidance: Advice on lifestyle, ergonomics and stress management
6

Prognosis

57% full control within 2 years
71% full control within 8 years
35-47% bilateral involvement
  • Spontaneous improvement: 57% achieve full control of vertigo within 2 years, and 71% within 8 years - without surgical treatment
  • Hearing loss: Most develop permanent hearing loss of 50-60 dB over 5-10 years. Total deafness is uncommon
  • Bilateral involvement: The risk of the other ear being affected increases - up to 35% within 10 years and 47% within 20 years

In some cases, the chronic dizziness can develop into PPPD (persistent postural-perceptual dizziness), which requires a different treatment approach.
