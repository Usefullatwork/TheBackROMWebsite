Persistent Symptoms After Concussion

Post-Concussion Syndrome and Vestibular Rehabilitation | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You remember the impact. Maybe it was on the soccer field, in a car accident, or from a fall at home. The CT scans showed nothing, and the doctor said "take it easy for a few days." But three weeks later, you still wake up with a headache that starts at the back of your head and pulses down your neck. Light from computer screens hurts your eyes. You get dizzy just from turning on the sofa. And the mental fog that makes even simple tasks feel overwhelming - it just won't go away.

This is not imagined. It's Post-Concussion Syndrome (PCS) - and effective treatment exists.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Persistent symptoms after concussion lasting more than 3 months
  • Prevalence: 10-30% develop PCS after mild head injury; 50-84% experience dizziness
  • Who is affected: Women have 2-3 times higher risk than men
  • Treatment: 73% return to activity within 8 weeks with vestibular rehabilitation vs. 7% without

Related Dizziness Conditions

Dizziness after concussion can resemble several other conditions:

Whiplash Dizziness Dizziness after whiplash BPPV (Crystal Disease) Loose crystals in the balance organ Cervicogenic Dizziness Dizziness from the neck PPPD Chronic functional dizziness

When should you seek immediate help?

For worsening headache, repeated vomiting, seizures, confusion, unequal pupil size or clear fluid from nose/ear - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

What is Post-Concussion Syndrome?

Post-Concussion Syndrome (PCS) occurs when symptoms after a concussion persist longer than expected. In medical terminology, the condition is defined as symptoms lasting more than 3 months after a mild traumatic brain injury. PCS is one of several causes of persistent dizziness and can resemble other vestibular conditions.

The important thing to understand: A concussion is a functional injury, not a structural injury. Therefore, nothing is visible on regular CT or MRI. Think of it as an energy crisis in the brain - the nerve cells are stretched during impact, which triggers a massive release of chemicals and disrupts the brain's normal energy production.

90% symptom-free within 10-14 days
10-30% develop persistent symptoms (PCS)
11-14% children with symptoms after 3 mo

Symptoms

Physical Symptoms (Most Common)

Headache: 78%

Often located in the back of the head, worsens with bending or exertion.

Fatigue: 91%

Mental tiredness after just 20-30 minutes of concentrated work.

Dizziness: 50-84%

Both spinning dizziness and feeling of unsteadiness.

Sleep Problems: 70%

Difficulty falling asleep and poor sleep quality.

Cognitive Symptoms

  • Concentration difficulties: about 64%
  • Reduced memory: 73%
  • Delayed mental processing: Feeling of "thinking through cotton"

Sensory Symptoms

  • Light sensitivity: 55% - computer screens and bright lights are especially problematic
  • Sound sensitivity: 40% - background noise that previously went unnoticed becomes bothersome
  • Blurred vision: 30% - difficulty focusing, especially when reading

Vestibular Symptoms

The vestibular system - meaning the balance system in the inner ear and its connections to the brain - is particularly vulnerable to head injuries. Pressure waves from the impact can damage the delicate structures in the inner ear.

  • Dizziness that worsens with head movements
  • Difficulty focusing vision during movement
  • Balance difficulties especially on uneven surfaces or in darkness
  • Increased sensitivity to visual stimulation like escalators or busy environments
3

Who is Most at Risk?

High Risk (Strong Evidence)

Female Gender

Women have 2-3 times higher risk of PCS than men. Adult women have an odds ratio of 2.57 for elevated symptom burden.

Previous Concussions

Multiple previous head injuries significantly increase risk. Median number of concussions in PCS patients is 3.3, but 23% develop PCS after just one concussion.

Neck Problems at Time of Injury

Neck pain at initial evaluation is strongly associated with delayed recovery.

Dizziness at Time of Injury

Gives 6.3 times higher odds of prolonged recovery.

4

Types of Dizziness After Concussion

Crystal Dizziness (BPPV) - 10-57%

Here, small calcium crystals have loosened from their normal position in the balance organ and ended up in the semicircular canals. Post-traumatic BPPV often behaves differently than regular BPPV - it more often affects multiple canals simultaneously and can be more resistant to treatment.

Vestibulo-ocular Dysfunction - Over 50%

Problems with the reflex that keeps your gaze stable when the head moves. Symptoms are often blurred vision during head movements, difficulty reading, and dizziness in visually demanding environments.

Neck-Related Dizziness - 20-58%

Occurs in people with whiplash and often appears together with vestibular complaints. The neck contains many sensitive receptors that give the brain information about head position.

5

Red Flags - When You MUST Seek Medical Help

Call Emergency Services or Go to Emergency Room Immediately For:

  • Worsening headache ("the worst headache of my life")
  • Repeated vomiting (more than 3 episodes in 24 hours)
  • Seizures or convulsions
  • Increasing confusion or reduced consciousness
  • Unequal pupil size
  • Focal neurological deficits (paralysis, speech difficulties)
  • Clear fluid from nose or ear
6

Treatment

The latest international consensus (Amsterdam 2022) has turned previous treatment advice upside down. Complete rest is outdated and can actually prolong symptoms.

Graded Activity and Early Exercise

After the first 24-48 hours of relative rest:

  • Light physical activity like walking can be started
  • Activity that only causes mild and short-term (< 1 hour) symptom worsening is safe
  • Prescribed aerobic exercise within 2-10 days after injury reduces the risk of persistent symptoms

Vestibular Rehabilitation - Strong Evidence

The most cited study by Schneider and colleagues (2014) showed that 73% of patients who received cervicovestibular rehabilitation returned to sport within 8 weeks, compared to only 7% in the control group. This gives almost four times higher chance of rapid recovery with treatment.

Treatment Includes:

  • Gaze stabilization exercises: Trains the vestibulo-ocular reflex
  • Habituation: Gradual exposure to provoking movements
  • Balance training: Exercises with different surfaces and demands
  • Crystal repositioning for BPPV: Epley maneuver gives symptom relief in about 80% after just one treatment

Neck Treatment

Neck-focused treatment is often integrated with vestibular rehabilitation, especially when there are concurrent neck problems. The combination of neck and vestibular treatment was precisely what showed the strong results in the Schneider study.

When Should Treatment Start?

Early start gives better results. Patients who start treatment 8-10 days after injury have median improvement after 26 days, versus 31 days for those who start later. The 6th International Consensus Conference recommends starting cervicovestibular rehabilitation if dizziness, neck pain or headache persists beyond 10 days.

7

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

The good news: Most people with PCS improve over time, especially with proper treatment.

90% symptom-free within 10-14 days
77% meaningful improvement with treatment
4-8 weeks to improvement with vestibular rehab

Realistic Expectations:

  • 90% of acute concussion symptoms disappear within 10-14 days
  • For those who develop PCS, 77% show meaningful improvement with specialized treatment
  • With active vestibular rehabilitation, most see significant improvement within 4-8 weeks
  • Median duration of PCS is about 7 months, but about 12% have symptoms for more than 2 years
8

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does dizziness last after concussion?
For most, dizziness resolves within days to a few weeks. With vestibular rehabilitation, most are significantly better within 4-8 weeks. Around 10-15% develop persistent problems beyond 3 months, and some may develop PPPD (chronic dizziness).
Can a chiropractor treat symptoms after concussion?
Yes, chiropractors with expertise in vestibular rehabilitation can provide effective treatment. The combination of vestibular rehabilitation and neck treatment has strong research support.
Should I avoid activity if I'm dizzy after concussion?
No, prolonged inactivity can actually delay recovery. After 24-48 hours of relative rest, you should gradually resume activity adapted to your symptoms.
Do I need an MRI or CT?
In typical concussion and PCS, imaging shows normal findings - the injury is functional, not structural. MRI/CT is indicated for red flag symptoms to rule out bleeding or structural damage.
