POTS and Orthostatic Dizziness

When the pulse races out of control - symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

POTS stands for Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome - a condition where the heart beats abnormally fast when you stand up from a lying or sitting position. POTS is one of many causes of dizziness, but differs from vestibular conditions in that the symptoms are primarily caused by disturbed blood pressure regulation.

Normally, the pulse increases by 10-15 beats per minute when you stand up. In POTS, the pulse increases by 30 beats or more (40 beats for youth 12-19 years) within 10 minutes of standing up - without the blood pressure dropping significantly.

Key Summary

  • What it is: POTS is a condition where the pulse increases abnormally when standing - without blood pressure dropping
  • Who is affected: Primarily women 15-50 years (75-80%), often after viral infection
  • Prevalence: Estimated 0.2-1% of the population
  • Treatment: Structured exercise (Levine protocol), fluids, salt and compression - about 53% achieve improvement

When should you seek immediate help?

For chest pain, severe shortness of breath, repeated fainting or irregular heartbeat - contact a doctor or call emergency services.

1

What is POTS?

POTS belongs to the group of dysautonomias - conditions where the autonomic nervous system does not function as it should. The autonomic nervous system controls functions you normally do not think about: pulse, blood pressure, sweating, digestion and temperature regulation.

When you stand up, gravity pulls 500-800 ml of blood down toward the legs and abdomen. Normally, sensors in the carotid artery detect this and send signals to the brain to increase the pulse slightly and tighten the blood vessels in the legs. In POTS, this mechanism does not work as it should.

0.2-1% of the population
75-80% are women
15-50 yrs most common age group

Increased Prevalence After COVID-19

After the COVID-19 pandemic, the prevalence has increased significantly. A study showed that the incidence rate increased from 1.42 to 20.3 per million persons.

2

Causes and Triggering Factors

POTS has no single cause, but is often triggered by an "immunological stressor." The most common triggering factors are:

Viral Infection (most common)

About 50% of POTS cases start after a viral infection - especially mononucleosis (glandular fever), influenza and COVID-19. Among patients with long COVID, 2-14% report POTS symptoms.

Physical Trauma

Whiplash injuries, concussion and other trauma to head and neck are known triggers. There is a documented connection between neck injuries and POTS development.

Associated Conditions

POTS often occurs together with other conditions: Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (25% of POTS patients), mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), migraine (40%), irritable bowel (30%), and ME/CFS (21%).

3

Symptoms

POTS gives a wide spectrum of symptoms that vary from person to person.

Cardiovascular Symptoms

  • Heart palpitations and racing pulse
  • Dizziness and lightheadedness when standing
  • Feeling of being about to faint (presyncope)
  • Actual fainting in some cases

Neurological Symptoms

  • "Brain fog" - problems with concentration and memory
  • Headache
  • Trembling
  • Visual disturbances

Other Symptoms

  • Extreme fatigue
  • Nausea and stomach problems
  • Purple/red discoloration of feet when standing
  • Exercise intolerance

Symptom Worsening

Symptoms typically worsen when standing still for long periods, in hot weather, after large meals, in the morning, and during menstruation.

4

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Criteria (Heart Rhythm Society 2015)

To receive a POTS diagnosis, you must meet:

  1. Pulse increase of >=30 beats/min (>=40 for ages 12-19) within 10 minutes of standing
  2. Or pulse over 120 beats/min when standing
  3. Absence of orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drop >=20 mmHg)
  4. Symptoms have lasted more than 3 months
  5. No other condition that explains the symptoms

Active Standing Test (can be done in clinic)

A simple test: You lie flat for 5-10 minutes, pulse and blood pressure are measured, you stand up and stay still, and measurements are taken at 1, 3, 5 and 10 minutes. Pulse increase >=30 without blood pressure drop suggests POTS.

Red Flags - Seek medical help immediately for:

  • Chest pain or pressure in the chest
  • Severe shortness of breath
  • Repeated fainting
  • Irregular heartbeat
  • Symptoms that worsen rapidly
  • Neurological symptoms (vision loss, speech difficulties, one-sided weakness)

POTS itself is not dangerous, but the symptoms can mimic other conditions that require acute treatment.

5

Treatment

There is no cure for POTS, but most people experience significant improvement with the right treatment. Non-pharmacological measures are first-line treatment.

Non-Pharmacological Treatment

  • Fluids and salt: Drink 2-3 liters of water daily, start the day with 500 ml water BEFORE getting up. For POTS (without high blood pressure), increased salt intake is often recommended (6-10 grams daily). Always consult a doctor
  • Compression: Class 2 compression stockings, best effect with stockings that go to the waist
  • Diet: Eat small, frequent meals. Avoid large carbohydrate-rich meals

The Levine Protocol: Exercise as Medicine

Exercise is the most effective treatment for POTS, but must be done correctly:

  • Phase 1 (month 1-2): Horizontal exercise: rowing machine, recumbent bike, swimming
  • Phase 2: Gradual introduction of upright exercise
  • Phase 3: Maintenance with combined training

Results from Research

In Fu and Levine's study from 2011, 3 months of training showed that 53% of participants no longer met POTS criteria. Exercise was more effective than beta-blockers for reducing pulse and improving quality of life.

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

POTS is primarily a medical condition that requires evaluation by a doctor. But the chiropractor can play a role in several areas:

  • Diagnostic contribution: I can perform active standing tests and identify if your dizziness may be due to POTS - rather than problems in the balance organ. If suspected, I refer for further evaluation by a doctor
  • Neck treatment for trauma-triggered POTS: There are case reports of improvement in autonomic symptoms after neck treatment, but the evidence is currently limited. For concurrent cervicogenic dizziness, treatment can be helpful, but does not replace medical evaluation
  • Exercise guidance: I can guide you through the Levine protocol and adapt exercises to your symptoms
6

Prognosis

The prognosis for POTS varies significantly:

80% experience symptom improvement
50% spontaneous improvement (post-viral)
90% respond to treatment

Realistic Expectations

  • Full recovery is not always realistic - many live with persistent mild symptoms
  • Relapses are common
  • Most learn to manage the condition and achieve significant improvement in quality of life over time
  • POTS does not affect life expectancy
7

Frequently Asked Questions

Is POTS a mental illness?
No. POTS is a physical condition in the autonomic nervous system. The symptoms can mimic anxiety, and many are incorrectly diagnosed with anxiety before receiving the POTS diagnosis.
Can I exercise with POTS?
Yes - exercise is actually one of the most important treatments. But it must be done correctly: start with lying/sitting exercises and increase gradually.
What is the difference between POTS and orthostatic hypotension?
In orthostatic hypotension, blood pressure drops. In POTS, blood pressure remains stable, but the pulse increases significantly.
