Chronic Dizziness and PPPD

The complete guide | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Dizziness that persists for more than three months is called chronic dizziness. A Korean population study from 2022 found that 4.8% of adults over 40 experience such long-lasting problems. Among those who have an acute dizziness episode, around 17% develop chronic symptoms.

Chronic dizziness differs from acute vertigo in several ways. While acute vertigo causes intense spinning sensation lasting seconds to days, most people with chronic dizziness experience a vaguer feeling - unsteadiness, swaying, "foggy head" or a feeling of being on a boat.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Chronic dizziness (3+ months) affects about 5% of adults over 40. PPPD is the most common cause
  • Most common causes: PPPD (15-20%), vestibular migraine (2-3%), residual symptoms after vestibular neuritis
  • Treatment: Vestibular rehabilitation is first-line - 2025 meta-analysis shows significant effect
  • Red flags: Acute dizziness with headache, double vision, speech difficulties, paralysis

Other Types of Dizziness

When should you seek immediate help?

For acute dizziness with headache, double vision, speech difficulties or paralysis - call 113 or go to the emergency room.

1

What is PPPD?

PPPD stands for Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness. The condition was formally defined by the Barany Society in 2017 and is now included in the WHO's International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11 code AB32.0).

PPPD is a functional disorder. This means the problem is not in the balance organ itself - the ear and nerves often function completely normally during testing. The problem lies in how the brain processes and responds to the signals it receives. This distinguishes PPPD from conditions like vestibular hypofunction, where there is measurable damage to the balance organ.

15-20% of dizziness patients
30-50 yrs most common age group
4:1 women vs men

When All Tests Are "Normal"

One of the most frustrating experiences for patients with chronic dizziness is that all examinations come back as "normal." This does not mean the symptoms are imagined. On the contrary: Normal test results are precisely what characterizes the most common form of chronic dizziness - PPPD.

2

The Five Diagnostic Criteria (Barany Society, 2017)

1. Duration

Dizziness, unsteadiness or non-rotational vertigo present most days for at least three months

2. Aggravating Factors

Symptoms worsen with upright posture, active or passive movement, and visually complex or moving stimuli

3. Triggering Event

The condition started after an acute vestibular disorder, other medical illness, or psychological stress

4. Symptom Impact

Symptoms cause significant distress or functional impairment

5. No Better Explanation

Symptoms cannot be better explained by another disease

3

Why the Brain "Forgets" to Turn Off the Alarm

Imagine a burglar alarm in your house. After an actual break-in (an acute dizziness episode), you adjust the sensitivity to maximum. Suddenly the alarm goes off every time the wind blows. This is how PPPD works.

After an acute event - such as crystal disease, vestibular neuritis, concussion or a period of intense stress - the brain enters "crisis mode." In PPPD, the brain forgets to turn off this crisis mode.

Three Things Happen:

Visual Dependence

The brain no longer trusts the balance system in the ear, so it clings to vision. That's why you become dizzy in the supermarket or when scrolling on your phone. This is also common in vestibular migraine.

Body Stiffness

You unconsciously tense muscles in your neck and legs to "hold on." This causes secondary neck pain and a stiff gait, similar to what is seen in cervicogenic dizziness.

Anxiety Loop

Every time you feel unsteady, the brain thinks "Danger!" and turns up the alarm even more.

What Happens in the Brain with PPPD?

Neuroimaging studies have shown that this is not "imagination" - there are measurable changes in brain function. Reduced activity in the parieto-insular vestibular cortex (PIVC), and increased activity in visual areas. The good news is that the brain is plastic - with proper treatment, these patterns can be reversed.

4

Symptoms and Characteristics

Main Symptoms:

  • Non-rotational dizziness: It doesn't spin, but it rocks, sways or feels like being on a boat
  • Brain fog: Concentration difficulties, mental fatigue - reported by about 81%
  • Exhaustion: Extreme tiredness, worst in the afternoon - reported by about 85%
  • Visual hypersensitivity: Worsening in busy environments (shopping centers, supermarkets)
  • Movement intolerance: Symptoms worsen with both active and passive movements (car, bus)

Mental Health and PPPD

60% of PPPD patients have clinically significant anxiety and 45% have depression, but 25% have no psychiatric condition at all. This underscores that PPPD is a neurological condition - not "just anxiety." Similar mental health challenges are also seen in other chronic dizziness conditions like Meniere's disease and MdDS.

Red Flags - Call 113 or go to emergency room for:

  • Acute dizziness with headache - especially "the worst headache ever"
  • Double vision, visual disturbances or visual field loss
  • Speech difficulties or swallowing problems
  • Numbness, weakness or paralysis in face, arm or leg
  • Sudden one-sided hearing loss
  • Dizziness with chest pain or palpitations
5

Treatment

Treatment rests on three pillars. Recent research shows that the combination gives the best results.

1. Vestibular Rehabilitation (VRT) - The Cornerstone

A recent meta-analysis from 2025 (Li et al., Frontiers in Neurology) showed significant improvement: DHI-total improved by 21.84 points.

  • Gaze stabilization exercises: Teaches the brain to trust vestibular signals again
  • Habituation exercises: Gradual, repeated exposure to movements that provoke symptoms
  • Balance training: Challenges the balance system to reduce over-control
  • Visual desensitization: Gradual exposure to visually "noisy" environments

Timeline: Improvement often starts after 6-8 weeks. Maximum effect may require 3-6 months.

2. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Because fear-avoidance behavior is part of the mechanism that maintains PPPD, it is often valuable to work on thought patterns. A 2024 meta-analysis showed that CBT combined with other treatment gives significantly better results.

3. Medication (SSRI/SNRI)

Antidepressants of the SSRI or SNRI type are used for PPPD - not primarily for depression, but because they stabilize the nervous system. A 2025 study showed sustained improvement over 3 years.

6

Exercises You Can Do at Home

Gaze Stabilization Exercises (VOR x1)

  • Hold a finger or a letter at arm's length
  • Focus on the target
  • Move your head slowly from side to side while keeping your gaze on the target
  • Start with 10 repetitions, 3 times daily
  • Gradually increase speed over weeks

Balance Exercises

  • Level 1 - Romberg: Stand with feet together, arms at sides, hold for 30 seconds. Progression: Open eyes - Closed eyes - Soft surface
  • Level 2 - Tandem stance: Stand with one foot in front of the other (heel-to-toe), hold for 30 seconds on each side
  • Level 3 - Single leg stance: Stand on one leg, goal: 30 seconds on each side

Important Principles

  • Consistency is key: Daily practice gives better results than intense, sporadic training
  • Mild symptom provocation is the goal: Exercises should cause mild-moderate dizziness that resolves within 15-20 minutes
  • Document progress: Keep a diary of symptoms and exercises
7

Prognosis

Studies show that 60-80% of patients with PPPD experience significant symptom reduction with proper treatment. Complete symptom freedom is not always achieved, but functional improvement - where dizziness no longer controls your life - is realistic for most.

60-80% significant improvement
6-8 weeks before first improvement
3-6 months for maximum effect
