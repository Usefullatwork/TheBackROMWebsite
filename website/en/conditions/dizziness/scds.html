Superior Canal Dehiscence Syndrome (SCDS)

Symptoms, diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Superior canal dehiscence syndrome (SCDS) is a rare inner ear condition first described by Lloyd Minor and colleagues in 1998. The condition occurs when the bone covering the superior semicircular canal has a hole or is abnormally thin. SCDS is one of several conditions that can cause dizziness, but differs from other diagnoses by its characteristic sound- and pressure-induced symptoms.

Anatomical studies show that approximately 0.5% of the population has complete dehiscence (hole), while an additional 1.4% have very thin bone (less than 0.1 mm) over the canal. Far from everyone with anatomical dehiscence develops symptoms - many live their entire lives without problems.

Key Summary

  • What it is: A rare condition where a hole in the bone over the superior semicircular canal causes dizziness and hearing disturbances
  • Most common symptoms: Autophony (hearing own voice/heartbeat abnormally loud), sound-induced dizziness, pressure-induced dizziness
  • Chiropractor's role: Identify the condition and refer to ENT specialist - vestibular rehabilitation can help after surgery
  • Red flags: Dizziness triggered by loud sounds, hearing your own eye movements, pulsatile tinnitus

When should you contact an ENT specialist?

Dizziness triggered by loud sounds, you hear your own eye movements, footsteps or heartbeat abnormally clearly, dizziness when straining, coughing or blowing your nose.

1

Why Does the Hole Cause Symptoms?

The inner ear normally has two "windows" - the oval window where sound enters, and the round window that provides pressure equalization. When a hole develops in the semicircular canal, an abnormal "third window" is created that changes how sound and pressure affect the balance organ.

This third window causes:

  • Air-conducted sound is diverted away from the cochlea, causing hearing loss in low frequencies
  • Bone-conducted sound gets enhanced access to the inner ear fluid, explaining hypersensitivity to internal body sounds
  • Sound and pressure changes create abnormal fluid movement that stimulates balance cells
2

Who Is Affected?

SCDS typically debuts at 40-60 years of age (average 50 years) and affects women slightly more often than men with a ratio of approximately 1.2:1. Anatomical dehiscence is bilateral (both sides) in up to 50% of patients, but usually one ear is more symptomatic than the other.

In approximately half of patients who undergo surgery, a triggering trauma or pressure-increasing event is reported before symptoms started - with an average of 5.6 years between the event and symptom onset.

0.5% of population has dehiscence
40-60 yrs typical age of onset
50% have bilateral dehiscence
3

Symptoms

Vestibular Symptoms (Balance Disturbances)

Sound-Induced Dizziness (Tullio Phenomenon)

Occurs in 42-43% of patients. Loud sounds (typically 500 Hz-2 kHz at 100-110 dB) trigger spinning dizziness or unsteadiness. Examples include loud music, barking dogs, or construction noise.

Pressure-Induced Dizziness (Hennebert Sign)

Affects 37% and is triggered by Valsalva maneuver (straining), coughing, sneezing, heavy lifting or nose blowing.

Chronic unsteadiness and balance problems affect many, and up to 50% report mental fatigue and brain fog.

Auditory Symptoms (Hearing Disturbances)

Autophony

The most characteristic symptom, affecting 42-60% of patients. Patients describe their own voice as sounding distorted or "metallic," and many hear their own body sounds abnormally loud - such as eye movements, footsteps, heartbeat and even bowel sounds. This is almost diagnostic for SCDS when present.

  • Pulsatile Tinnitus: Occurs in 20-50% and is experienced as a "whooshing" sound synchronous with pulse
  • Hearing Loss in Low Frequencies: Affects approximately 40%, typically in the 250-1000 Hz range
  • Fullness Sensation in Ear: Reported by over 60%
4

Differential Diagnoses - What Can It Resemble?

SCDS is called "the great imitator of the ear" because symptoms can resemble several other conditions:

Meniere's Disease

Also causes hearing loss and dizziness, but attacks come spontaneously and last 20 minutes to several hours. In SCDS, symptoms are temporally related to specific sound or pressure triggers.

BPPV (Crystal Disease)

Triggered by head movements and causes brief spinning dizziness that diminishes with repeated movements. SCDS is triggered by sound and pressure, not head position.

Otosclerosis

Also causes hearing loss in low frequencies, but in otosclerosis the stapedius reflex is absent - in SCDS it is normal. This difference is important because stapedectomy (surgery for otosclerosis) is contraindicated in SCDS.

5

Diagnosis

Clinical Questions That Can Reveal SCDS

When SCDS is suspected, the following questions can be revealing:

  • "Can you hear your eyes moving?"
  • "Do you get dizzy when straining or having a bowel movement?"
  • "Does blowing your nose give you a spinning sensation?"
  • "Do you hear your own voice abnormally loud in one ear?"

Diagnostic Criteria (Barany Society, 2021)

For the diagnosis of SCDS, all three of the following are required:

A. At Least One Characteristic Symptom

Bone conduction hypersensitivity (autophony for internal body sounds), sound-induced dizziness/oscillopsia, pressure-induced dizziness/oscillopsia, or pulsatile tinnitus.

B. At Least One Physiological Finding

Nystagmus in the plane of the superior canal triggered by sound or pressure, negative bone conduction thresholds on audiometry in low frequencies, or enhanced VEMP responses.

C. High-Resolution CT

Showing dehiscence with 0.5-0.63 mm slice thickness or less and reformatted images in Poschl and Stenvers planes.

Red Flags - When to Contact an ENT Specialist

  • Dizziness triggered by loud sounds
  • You hear your own eye movements, footsteps or heartbeat abnormally clearly
  • Dizziness when straining, coughing or blowing your nose
  • Pulsatile tinnitus combined with balance problems
  • Hearing loss with preserved acoustic reflexes
  • Position-triggered dizziness that doesn't respond to Epley maneuver
6

Treatment

Conservative Treatment

For patients with mild to moderate symptoms, conservative treatment is often first choice. Approximately 80% of diagnosed patients do not undergo surgery. Conservative measures include:

Conservative Measures

  • Avoid triggering factors (loud sounds, straining, heavy lifting)
  • Hearing protection in noisy environments
  • Reduced salt and caffeine intake
  • Acetazolamide (diuretic) which showed 78% symptom reduction in one study

Surgical Treatment

For debilitating symptoms that significantly affect quality of life, surgery may be considered. Transmastoid approach (TMA) has significantly lower revision rate (3.8% vs 33.3%), shorter hospital stay and better results for autophony (95% vs 80%) compared to middle fossa craniotomy.

Total surgical success rate is 94%. At long-term follow-up (average 5.3 years), 95% of patients reported improvement.

7

The Chiropractor's Role in SCDS

SCDS is a structural condition requiring specialist evaluation and possible surgical treatment. The chiropractor cannot treat the condition itself, but has an important role in identification and referral to an ENT specialist.

After SCDS surgery, 39-80% develop transient vestibular weakness, and 4.5-24% get BPPV. Here a chiropractor with vestibular competence can contribute with:

Vestibular Rehabilitation After Surgery

  • Gaze stabilization exercises
  • Balance training
  • Epley maneuver for postoperative BPPV

Important Precautions

When SCDS is suspected, the following should be avoided or approached with caution:

  • Valsalva maneuvers
  • Heavy lifting or straining exercises
  • Cervical manipulation with thrust
  • Breathing exercises with breath holding
  • Inverted positions
8

Prognosis and Quality of Life

Quality of life in SCDS is significantly reduced before treatment. Health Utility Values in preoperative patients is 0.68 versus 0.80 in the normal population. Dizziness Handicap Inventory shows an average of 44 points, equivalent to moderate handicap.

Surgical treatment provides lasting improvement for most. At long-term follow-up, 95% of operated patients report sustained improvement. Some symptoms, particularly brain fog and mental fatigue, show less improvement.

94% surgical success rate
95% sustained improvement
80% choose not to have surgery
