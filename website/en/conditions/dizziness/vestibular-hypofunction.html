Vestibular Hypofunction: The Complete Guide

From acute balance nerve virus to permanent loss of balance function | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Vestibular hypofunction means that the balance organ in the inner ear is not functioning properly. The balance organ, also called the vestibular system, normally sends information to the brain about the head's movements and position. This information is crucial for two things: maintaining balance and having stable vision during movement. Dizziness can have many causes, and vestibular hypofunction is one of the most common.

When this function is weakened or lost, a mismatch occurs between the signals the brain expects and what it actually receives. The result is dizziness, imbalance and visual disturbances - especially when you move.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Reduced or lost function in the balance organ in the inner ear - either on one side (unilateral) or both sides (bilateral)
  • Most common causes: Vestibular neuritis/virus (most common in unilateral), ototoxic medications (most common in bilateral), Meniere's disease - in 20-51% the cause remains unknown
  • Treatment: Vestibular rehabilitation has strong evidence (APTA guidelines 2022). Unilateral: 4-6 weeks. Bilateral: 8-12 weeks or longer
  • Red flags: Acute dizziness with headache/neck pain, speech difficulties, paralysis, double vision or numbness - call emergency services

When should you seek immediate help?

For acute dizziness combined with headache, neck pain, speech difficulties, paralysis, double vision or numbness - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

How Common is Vestibular Hypofunction?

Vestibular hypofunction is more common than many realize. Studies show an age-adjusted prevalence of approximately 5-9% in the population. Among people who see a doctor for dizziness, around 6-10% have documented vestibular hypofunction. The condition becomes more common with age - in people over 79 years, the prevalence is over 30%.

5-9% prevalence in population
30%+ in people over 79 years
6-10% of dizziness consultations
2

Unilateral vs. Bilateral - What's the Difference?

Vestibular hypofunction is divided into two main types, and the differences are significant - both regarding symptoms, causes and prognosis.

Unilateral Vestibular Hypofunction (One-sided)

In unilateral hypofunction, the balance organ is damaged on only one side. This is the most common form and is most often caused by vestibular neuritis (virus on the balance nerve).

How does it work? The balance organs in the right and left ear normally function as a "push-pull" system. When you turn your head to the right, the signal from the right ear increases while it decreases from the left. With one-sided damage, an imbalance occurs that the brain interprets as constant rotation. The good news is that the brain is remarkably good at compensating for one-sided damage.

Bilateral Vestibular Hypofunction (Two-sided)

In bilateral hypofunction, the balance organs function poorly or not at all on both sides. This is a rarer and more serious condition.

How does it work? When both sides are "silent," the brain receives no information about the head's movements. It's like driving a car without a speedometer and without windows - you have no idea how you're moving.

Prevalence: Approximately 28 per 100,000 adults. The average age at diagnosis is 58-64 years, and men are somewhat more often affected than women.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Causes of Unilateral Vestibular Hypofunction

Most common:

  • Vestibular neuritis/labyrinthitis - Viral infection that attacks the balance nerve or inner ear. The most common cause of acute unilateral vestibular hypofunction
  • Age-related weakening - Gradual loss of function due to natural aging

Less common:

  • Meniere's disease - Chronic condition with episodic vertigo, hearing loss and tinnitus
  • Head injuries - Can damage the balance organ or balance nerve, as in post-concussion dizziness or whiplash-related dizziness
  • Vestibular schwannoma - Benign tumor on the balance nerve

Causes of Bilateral Vestibular Hypofunction

1. Unknown Cause (Idiopathic) - 31-51%

In approximately half of cases, no clear cause is found. Interestingly, around 50% of these patients have migraine, which can also cause vestibular migraine.

2. Ototoxic Medications - 12-35%

Aminoglycosides (especially gentamicin) are the most common identifiable cause. Damage can occur with any dose.

3. Hereditary Conditions - 17-31%

DFNA9 (mutation in the COCH gene) is most common in Europe. CANVAS syndrome accounts for 2-5% of cases.

4. Meniere's Disease (Bilateral) - 2-17%

When Meniere's disease affects both ears over time.

5. Meningitis - 5-11%

Meningitis can spread to both inner ears.

Symptoms

Symptoms in Unilateral Hypofunction

Symptom Prevalence
Chronic dizziness 98%
Imbalance/unsteadiness 81%
Worsening with head movements 75%
Oscillopsia (unstable vision during movement) 22-70%
Fatigue/exhaustion 68%

Symptoms in Bilateral Hypofunction

Bilateral hypofunction gives a characteristic symptom triad:

1. Unsteadiness When Walking and Standing

The main complaint, especially on uneven surfaces, in darkness or with head movements. Many describe feeling like they're "walking on a boat."

2. Oscillopsia - When the World Jumps

Often the most bothersome symptom. The visual image "jumps" with head movements. About 70% experience this.

3. Symptom-Free at Rest

Symptoms disappear when you sit or lie still. This distinguishes bilateral hypofunction from other causes.

4. No Spinning Dizziness

Rarely classic vertigo because both sides are equally weak - there is no imbalance creating the sensation of rotation.

Red Flags - When You Must Seek Medical Help Immediately

Call emergency services or go to the emergency room immediately for:

  • Acute dizziness combined with headache, neck pain or recent trauma
  • Speech difficulties, swallowing difficulties or unclear speech
  • Paralysis, numbness or weakness in face, arm or leg
  • Double vision or visual field loss
  • Altered consciousness or confusion

Up to 25% of acute dizziness cases at the emergency room may be caused by stroke in the posterior brain circulation.

5

Treatment

Vestibular rehabilitation is the main treatment for both unilateral and bilateral hypofunction. The APTA guidelines from 2022 give a strong recommendation based on high-quality evidence.

What Does Vestibular Rehabilitation Involve?

Treatment is not about "repairing" the balance organs - they can rarely be restored. The goal is to train the brain to compensate.

Treatment Methods

  • Gaze stabilization exercises (VOR training): Trains the eyes to maintain focus while the head moves
  • Habituation: Gradual exposure to movements that trigger dizziness
  • Balance training: Exercises on uneven surfaces, with closed eyes, with head movements
  • Sensory substitution: Learns to use vision and sensation in the feet more actively for balance

Treatment Course

Type of Hypofunction Supervised Treatment Home Exercises
Acute/subacute unilateral 2-3 weeks weekly Min. 12 min/day
Chronic unilateral 4-6 weeks weekly Min. 20 min/day
Bilateral 8-12 weeks or longer 20-40 min/day + lifelong
6

Prognosis

In Unilateral Hypofunction

Most experience significant improvement. Average handicap score decreases from around 52 to 27 points after treatment - a clinically meaningful improvement. However, 29-66% have persistent symptoms with rapid head movements. In some, incomplete compensation can lead to development of PPPD (chronic dizziness).

In Bilateral Hypofunction

The prognosis for regaining vestibular function is limited - only about 4% improve enough to no longer meet diagnostic criteria. However, quality of life can improve significantly with rehabilitation and adaptations.

Fall Risk

Patients with bilateral hypofunction have 31 times higher fall risk than the normal population.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is vestibular hypofunction the same as crystal disease?
No. Crystal disease (BPPV) causes short episodes of intense spinning dizziness triggered by specific head positions. Vestibular hypofunction is a persistent weakening of balance function. Treatment is also different.
Can vestibular hypofunction fully recover?
In unilateral cases, the brain often compensates well, and many experience near-full function. In bilateral cases, full recovery is rare (about 4%), but quality of life can improve significantly with proper treatment.
Should I avoid activity when I'm dizzy?
No, quite the opposite! Early activity promotes brain compensation. Prolonged bed rest can delay recovery. Activity should be adapted to your abilities and increased gradually.
Can a chiropractor help with vestibular hypofunction?
Yes. Chiropractors with expertise in vestibular disorders can evaluate, diagnose and treat with evidence-based vestibular rehabilitation. In Norway, chiropractors have primary contact status, so you don't need a referral.
