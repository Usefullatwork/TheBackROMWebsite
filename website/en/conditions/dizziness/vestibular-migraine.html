Vestibular Migraine: When Dizziness Takes Over Your Life

The most common cause of episodic dizziness | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You wake up one morning and the room is spinning. Not the brief dizziness you know from getting up too fast, but a prolonged, nauseating rotation lasting hours. Doctors find nothing wrong with your ear. MRI scans are normal. Maybe you're told it's "just stress" or that you must "learn to live with it." As described in our main article about dizziness, there are many causes of balance disturbances - and vestibular migraine is one of the most underdiagnosed.

If this sounds familiar, you may be among the many who have vestibular migraine without knowing it. This is actually the most common cause of episodic dizziness, but the condition is so underdiagnosed that patients wait an average of 7-8 years before getting the right diagnosis.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Migraine that primarily affects the balance system, not necessarily with headache
  • Prevalence: 1-3% of the population, most common cause of episodic dizziness
  • Treatment: Preventive medications, CGRP inhibitors for refractory cases, vestibular rehabilitation
  • Red flags: Acute headache with neck stiffness, vision disturbances, unilateral hearing loss

When should you seek immediate help?

With sudden, intense headache ("the worst headache of my life"), headache with fever and neck stiffness, or unilateral hearing loss - call 113 or go to the emergency room.

1

What Is Vestibular Migraine?

Vestibular migraine is a neurological condition where migraine mechanisms in the brain affect balance centers in the brainstem and cerebellum. Contrary to what many believe, you don't need to have headache during an attack. In fact, only about 25% of patients experience headache simultaneously with dizziness.

The condition affects 1-3% of the population and is far more common than Meniere's disease, which many are mistakenly diagnosed with. In specialized dizziness clinics, vestibular migraine accounts for up to 25% of all newly referred patients. Women are affected more often than men, with a ratio of approximately 3-5:1.

Why Is the Condition So Often Missed?

Headache and dizziness rarely occur simultaneously. The patient may have had migraine in youth that "disappeared," only to develop unexplained dizziness 10-20 years later. In an American survey, only 10% of patients who met criteria for vestibular migraine had been told that migraine was the cause.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Pathophysiology - What Happens in the Brain?

During an attack, CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide) is released - a signaling substance that plays a key role in migraine. CGRP is found both in the trigeminal system and in vestibular ganglia. When released, it disrupts normal signal processing in balance centers.

Risk Factors (from most common to rarest):

Previous Migraine

With or without aura - the most important risk factor

Female Sex

3-5 times higher risk than men

Family History

40-70% have family history of migraine or vertigo

Anxiety and Depression

Often co-existing conditions that can worsen symptoms

Previous Head Injury

Increases risk significantly, see also post-concussion dizziness

Triggers That Can Provoke Attacks:

  • Stress: Both acute stress and "let-down" stress (when you finally relax)
  • Sleep: Too little, too much, or irregular sleep schedule
  • Hormones: Menstrual cycle and menopause
  • Food and drink: Red wine, aged cheese, chocolate, caffeine
  • Sensory overstimulation: Bright light, loud sounds, strong smells
3

Symptoms - More Than Just Dizziness

Main Dizziness Symptoms:

Spontaneous Rotational Vertigo (45%)

The room spins, typically lasting 1-4 hours. So intense you cannot continue activities.

Position-Triggered Dizziness (25%)

Triggered by head movement, but lasts minutes to hours (not seconds as in crystal disease).

Visually Triggered Dizziness (40%)

Worsens in busy visual environments (shopping centers, escalators). Scrolling on screens triggers symptoms.

Accompanying Symptoms:

  • Nausea (72% of patients)
  • Light and sound sensitivity
  • Pressure in head or ears
  • Tinnitus - often high-frequency
  • Neck pain and stiffness - very common! See also cervicogenic dizziness
4

Diagnosis

Barany Society/IHS Diagnostic Criteria:

For the diagnosis "Definite Vestibular Migraine" the following must be met:

  • At least 5 episodes with vestibular symptoms of moderate to severe intensity
  • Duration from 5 minutes to 72 hours
  • Current or past history of migraine (with or without aura)
  • At least 50% of attacks must have at least one migraine feature: headache, light/sound sensitivity, or visual aura
  • Symptoms cannot be better explained by another diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis:

Condition Duration Distinctive Features
Vestibular Migraine Minutes to hours Migraine features, gradual onset
BPPV (Crystal Disease) Seconds Sudden onset, specific head position
Meniere's Disease Hours Progressive hearing loss, unilateral fullness
Vestibular Neuritis Days Single episode, gradual improvement

Red Flags - See a Doctor or Emergency Room for:

  • Sudden, intense headache ("the worst headache of my life")
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness
  • Unilateral hearing loss or tinnitus
  • Double vision or vision loss
  • Weakness or numbness in face, arm or leg
  • Speech or language difficulties
  • Fainting in connection with dizziness
5

Treatment

Preventive Medications

A systematic review ranked effectiveness as follows (Frontiers in Pharmacology, 2023):

First-Line Choice

  • Propranolol (beta blocker): 60% achieve complete symptom control
  • Topiramate (antiepileptic): Moderate evidence

Second-Line Choice

Venlafaxine, Valproate, Amitriptyline

CGRP Inhibitors - New Treatment Option

The new CGRP monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, galcanezumab, fremanezumab) show promising results. A prospective study showed that 90% achieved at least 50% reduction in vertigo frequency. Average number of dizziness days fell from 10.3 to 0.8 days per month after 12 months.

Vestibular Rehabilitation

Treatment Includes:

  • Eye movement exercises (VOR training)
  • Balance training
  • Habituation for visual hypersensitivity
  • Gradual exposure to trigger situations

Important: Training must be dosed carefully. Too hard training can actually trigger new attacks.

Manual Treatment and the Neck

Neck pain is very common in patients with vestibular migraine. A case series from 2024 showed that manual treatment of upper cervical vertebrae gave rapid and sustained improvement. The background is trigeminocervical convergence - nerve pathways from the upper neck (C1-C3) converge with pathways from the vestibular system.

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Sleep: Go to bed and get up at the same time every day. Aim for 7-8 hours
  • Diet: Eat regularly. Identify and reduce food triggers (keep a diary)
  • Stress: Regular physical activity. Mindfulness or breathing exercises
6

Prognosis

With proper treatment, most experience significant improvement. Patients on preventive treatment get reduction in both frequency, intensity and duration of attacks.

Realistic Expectations:

  • Relapse is common: Even with good treatment, many will experience periods with more attacks
  • Treatment takes time: It can take 2-3 months before preventive medications have full effect
  • Individual variation: What works for one person doesn't necessarily work for another

The positive thing is that many find attacks become less intense and less frequent over the years, especially with good treatment and trigger management. In some cases, prolonged vestibular migraine can develop into PPPD (chronic dizziness), which requires a different treatment approach.
