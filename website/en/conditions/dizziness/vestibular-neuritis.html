Vestibular Neuritis

From Acute Dizziness Crisis to Full Recovery | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Vestibular neuritis is a sudden malfunction of the balance nerve on one side of the head. The balance nerve continuously sends signals from the inner ear to the brain about the head's position and movements. When this nerve suddenly stops working, a dramatic imbalance occurs between the signals from both ears.

The brain interprets this difference as intense rotation - even though you are lying completely still. The result is severe vertigo (the feeling that the room is spinning), nausea and difficulty maintaining balance. See our main article on dizziness for a complete overview of all dizziness conditions.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Acute inflammation of the balance nerve (vestibular nerve) on one side
  • Most common cause: Reactivation of herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) in the nerve ganglion
  • Treatment: Vestibular rehabilitation has strong evidence (Cochrane 2015)
  • Prognosis: 50-70% achieve full or near-full recovery
  • Red flags: Double vision, speech difficulties, swallowing problems, severe headache, hearing loss - call 113

When should you seek immediate help?

For double vision, speech difficulties, swallowing problems, severe headache or sudden hearing loss - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

What is Vestibular Neuritis?

This condition is the third most common cause of peripheral dizziness, after BPPV (crystal disease) and Meniere's disease. It affects approximately 3.5-15.5 per 100,000 people annually, and most commonly occurs in adults between 30 and 60 years old.

3.5-15.5 per 100,000 annually
30-60 yrs most common age group
50-70% full recovery
2

Causes and Mechanisms

The exact cause is not fully understood, but several factors have been identified:

Viral Cause (Main Hypothesis)

Reactivation of herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) in the balance nerve ganglion is the most accepted theory. HSV-1 - the same virus that causes cold sores - has been found in approximately two-thirds of human vestibular ganglia in autopsy studies. The virus can lie dormant in the nerve for years, then reactivate and create inflammation and swelling.

Since the balance nerve passes through a narrow bony canal in the skull, even moderate swelling can compress the nerve fibers and stop signal transmission. Most patients have had a viral infection in the weeks before symptom onset.

3

Symptoms

Symptoms typically come on acutely over a few hours, reach near-maximum intensity within 24 hours, and gradually subside over days to weeks.

Typical Symptom Presentation:

  • Rotational vertigo: Intense feeling that the room is continuously spinning
  • Spontaneous nystagmus: Involuntary, rapid eye movements beating toward the healthy ear
  • Nausea and vomiting: Can be so severe that hospitalization is necessary
  • Balance problems: Tendency to fall toward the affected side
  • Oscillopsia: Feeling that the visual field "jumps" during head movements

Important Diagnostic Point

Vestibular neuritis does not cause hearing loss. If you have dizziness combined with reduced hearing, it may indicate labyrinthitis or other conditions requiring different evaluation.

Time Course:

  • Acute phase (0-3 days): Intense dizziness, often bedridden
  • Subacute phase (1-2 weeks): Gradual improvement, can walk upright but becomes dizzy with rapid movements
  • Compensation phase (3-12 weeks): The brain adapts and learns to compensate

Most experience significant improvement within 10 weeks.

4

Diagnosis

Vestibular neuritis is primarily a clinical diagnosis. The most important thing is to distinguish it from stroke in the cerebellum or brainstem, which can cause very similar symptoms. Approximately 25% of patients with acute vestibular syndrome may have central nervous system causes.

The HINTS Examination

HINTS (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) is a three-step eye examination that, in cases of acute dizziness, has proven more sensitive than MRI for detecting stroke in the first 24-48 hours. Performed by trained clinicians, HINTS is 96.5% sensitive and 84-96% specific.

Head Impulse Test

Rapid passive head rotation while the patient focuses. In vestibular neuritis, the gaze will "slip" and must be corrected.

Nystagmus

In vestibular neuritis, nystagmus is unidirectional - the eyes beat the same direction regardless of gaze direction.

Test of Skew

Alternating covering of the eyes. Vertical readjustment indicates brainstem lesion.

5

Red Flags - When to Seek Acute Help

Call Emergency Services for:

  • The 5 D's: Dizziness + Diplopia (double vision), Dysarthria (speech difficulties), Dysphagia (swallowing problems), Drop attacks (sudden falls)
  • Sudden severe headache or neck pain
  • New-onset hearing loss
  • Unsteadiness out of proportion to the dizziness
  • Neurological deficits (weakness, numbness in face/extremities)
6

Treatment

Acute Phase (0-3 days)

In the first days, the goal is to relieve symptoms and ensure fluid balance. Anti-nausea medications and vestibular suppressants can be used, but should only be used for the first 1-3 days. Prolonged use "sedates" the brain and delays the natural compensation process.

Vestibular Rehabilitation - Core Treatment

Vestibular rehabilitation is the most important treatment and has strong evidence (Cochrane 2015). A systematic review of 39 studies with over 2400 participants showed that vestibular rehabilitation was significantly better than no treatment.

The brain compensates for the functional loss in three ways:

  • Adaptation: The brain adjusts the strength of signals from the healthy ear
  • Substitution: The brain begins to rely more on vision and body sense (proprioception)
  • Habituation: The brain learns to ignore the disturbing signals

Exercises - Start Day 3-4

1. Gaze stabilization (VOR x1): Hold a finger in front of you. Focus sharply on the target. Rotate your head calmly from side to side while gaze is locked. Start slowly, increase speed gradually. 1-2 minutes, 3-5 times daily.

2. Balance training: Stand with feet together, hold onto a chair back if needed. Try closing your eyes. Progression: Stand on one leg, walk on soft surface.

3. Movement provocation: Perform movements that make you dizzy. Repeat in a controlled manner until dizziness decreases.

It should make you slightly dizzy - that's how the brain learns.

Early mobilization is crucial. Lying still in a dark room prolongs the course of illness. As soon as nausea allows (often day 2-3), you should try to sit up and move carefully.

What Can the Chiropractor Contribute?

Chiropractor's Role

  • Diagnostics: Perform HINTS examination to distinguish vestibular neuritis from stroke and crystal disease (BPPV)
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: Guide in individually tailored exercises, especially important with persistent vestibular hypofunction
  • Treatment of secondary complaints: Many patients become very stiff in the neck as a compensatory mechanism, which can cause cervicogenic dizziness. Treatment of these muscular tensions can reduce secondary headache
7

Prognosis

Vestibular neuritis is a self-limiting condition with generally good prognosis:

2-3 weeks significant improvement
3-6 mo full compensation
50-70% full recovery
  • Most experience significant improvement within 2-3 weeks
  • Full compensation (can run, turn quickly without problems) can take 3-6 months
  • Approximately 50-70% achieve full or near-full recovery
  • 30-50% develop persistent, milder dizziness

Interesting Finding

Long-term prognosis does not depend on how much nerve function is restored. Even if caloric testing still shows significant asymmetry, patients can become completely symptom-free because the brain compensates effectively.

Factors Predicting Poorer Recovery:

  • High degree of anxiety at disease onset
  • Strong visual dependency (relying too much on vision for balance)
  • Avoidance behavior (lying still for too long)
  • Prolonged use of vestibular suppressants

In some cases, persistent symptoms can develop into PPPD (persistent postural-perceptual dizziness), especially with strong anxiety or avoidance behavior. Patients with vestibular migraine may also have increased risk for poorer outcomes.

Risk of Recurrence

  • Recurrence in the same ear is rare - the nerve is often permanently damaged
  • Risk of getting it in the other ear is approximately 2-4% over a lifetime
