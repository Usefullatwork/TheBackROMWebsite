Whiplash and Dizziness

When the neck is causing your world to sway | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Whiplash describes the injury mechanism - not the diagnosis itself. The head is rapidly thrown forward and backward (or vice versa) in a "whip-like" movement. This happens so fast that the muscles cannot protect the neck in time.

In Norway, approximately 30,000 people annually are affected by whiplash. Scandinavian studies show an incidence of 235 per 100,000 inhabitants. Women make up almost two-thirds of those affected, likely due to anatomical differences in neck musculature.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Soft tissue injury in the neck from rapid acceleration-deceleration, most often from car accidents
  • Dizziness: 40-70% of those with persistent whiplash symptoms experience dizziness
  • Treatment: Multimodal approach (manual therapy + exercises + education) has strong evidence
  • Prognosis: 50% improve within 3-6 months. 25-40% have persistent symptoms after one year

When should you seek immediate help?

With neurological deficits, altered consciousness, severe headache or bladder/bowel disturbances after whiplash - call 113 or go to the emergency room.

1

WAD Classification (Whiplash Associated Disorders)

90% of whiplash injuries are classified as WAD grade I-II, meaning neck complaints with or without musculoskeletal findings, but without neurological signs.

Grade 0

No complaints, no findings

Grade I

Neck pain/stiffness, but normal findings on examination

Grade II (most common)

Neck pain AND reduced mobility/tender points. Most patients fall into this category.

Grade III

Neck pain AND neurological deficits (weakened reflexes, numbness in arm)

Grade IV

Fracture or dislocation - requires hospital treatment

2

Causes and Risk Factors

From most common to rarest:

1. Car Accidents

Especially rear-end collisions - the most common cause

2. Sports Injuries

Ice hockey, wrestling, contact sports

3. Falls

From height or on slippery surfaces

4. Violence/Assault

Direct trauma to the neck

5. Recreational Activities

Rare cases from roller coasters etc.

3

Why Does Whiplash Cause Dizziness?

The connection between whiplash and dizziness is stronger than many expect. Studies show that 40-70% of those with persistent whiplash symptoms report dizziness.

40-70% experience dizziness
242 muscle spindles/gram in suboccipital
C1-C3 most proprioceptive sensors

To maintain balance, the brain depends on three systems: the eyes (vision), the inner ear (vestibular system) and proprioception (body sense). The neck - especially the upper part (C1-C3) - is packed with proprioceptive sensors that tell the brain where the head is located.

With whiplash, these sensors can be damaged. The suboccipital muscles have extremely high muscle spindle density - 242 spindles per gram compared to only 2.2 per gram in the trapezius. When these are damaged, the neck sends error signals to the brain.

Sensory Mismatch

The eyes say "you're standing still", the ear says "you're standing still", but the neck says "the head is moving!". The brain interprets this as dizziness - like being seasick on land.

4

Is It the Neck, Ear, or Brain?

This is perhaps the most important question to answer. Cervicogenic dizziness (neck dizziness) is a diagnosis of exclusion - other causes must first be ruled out.

Signs of Cervicogenic Dizziness (neck)

  • Dizziness increases with neck movements or when sitting still for long periods
  • Feels like "swaying" or "unsteadiness" - not spinning
  • You have concurrent neck pain
  • Dizziness lasts minutes to hours (not seconds)
  • Negative Dix-Hallpike test

Signs of BPPV (Crystal Disease)

  • Spinning vertigo lasting seconds to under one minute
  • Triggered by specific head positions (turning in bed, looking up)
  • Positive Dix-Hallpike test
  • Not necessarily neck pain

Signs of Concussion

  • Problems with memory or concentration
  • Light or sound sensitivity
  • "Foggy" feeling in the head
  • Becomes dizzy from screens or busy environments

Important

In car accidents, up to 40% may have both whiplash and concussion simultaneously. Then both must be treated.

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek emergency care or hospital for:

  • Midline tenderness in the neck combined with risk of fracture
  • Neurological deficits: Weakness, numbness in arms/legs, walking difficulties
  • Altered consciousness or confusion
  • Severe, sudden headache - especially days after the injury
  • Visual disturbances, speech problems or coordination issues
  • Bladder or bowel disturbances

Vertebral artery dissection can occur days to weeks after injury. Be alert to sudden severe headache with neurological symptoms.

6

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Systematic reviews show that multimodal treatment - the combination of manual therapy, exercises and patient education - has the strongest evidence base.

Treatment Methods

  • Manual therapy: Gentle joint mobilization, soft tissue treatment of tight muscles. A meta-analysis found significant pain relief in the medium term.
  • Deep neck muscle training: The small muscles that stabilize the neck often "switch off" after injury. Motor control training has strong evidence.
  • Sensorimotor training: Exercises that train the coordination between eyes and neck. Approximately 75% improve with this approach.
  • Information and reassurance: Explanation of the condition and realistic expectations for recovery.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Self-Help

  • Stay active: Early mobilization is important. Take walks - arms swing naturally and help loosen the neck.
  • Avoid static positions: Don't sit with your phone in your lap for long periods, especially in the first weeks.
  • Move your neck gently: Move your neck within pain-free range several times daily.
  • Avoid neck collar: Studies show that neck collars are not only ineffective but can hinder recovery.

Treatment Course

In the acute phase (0-2 weeks) more frequent treatment may be useful, with focus on early mobilization. In the subacute phase (2-12 weeks) it typically decreases to 1-2 treatments per week. Improvement should be seen within 4 weeks; without improvement, diagnosis and treatment should be reconsidered.

When Chiropractic Helps - and When It Doesn't

Chiropractic treatment is often effective for mechanical neck dizziness - when dizziness comes because the joints in the neck aren't moving as they should, or when muscle pain triggers symptoms.

Chiropractic alone is not sufficient if:

  • You have an active concussion (need rest/pacing first)
  • You have BPPV/crystal disease (need Epley maneuver, not neck treatment)
  • You have PPPD (need cognitive approach and gradual exposure)

This is why a thorough examination is crucial before I start treatment.

7

Prognosis

The first three months are critical. Most who will recover do so during this period - then the recovery curve flattens out.

50% improve within 3-6 months
64% report improvement at 1 year
25-40% persistent symptoms at 1 year

Important to Know

A study showed that 86% of patients who still had symptoms at 3 months still had complaints at 2 years - while 93% of those symptom-free at 3 months remained healthy. This underscores the importance of early and effective treatment.

Risk Factors for Chronic Symptoms:

  • High initial pain intensity (over 5.5/10)
  • High functional disability at start
  • Post-traumatic stress symptoms
  • Catastrophizing thoughts and passive coping strategies
  • Poor expectations for recovery
  • Presence of dizziness - which can develop into PPPD or vestibular hypofunction
8

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I use a neck collar?
No. Systematic reviews conclude that neck collars are not only ineffective but can actually hinder recovery. Both Norwegian and Nordic guidelines recommend against use.
How long before the dizziness goes away?
This varies significantly. Many experience improvement within 6-12 weeks with proper treatment. With cervicogenic dizziness, approximately 75% improve with conservative neck treatment. For some, it may take longer to rebuild fine motor control in the neck.
Can I have both whiplash and concussion?
Yes, and this is actually quite common. In car accidents, up to 40% may have both at the same time. Treatment must then be adapted to both conditions.
Do I need X-ray or MRI?
Not necessarily. For most whiplash injuries (grade I-II), imaging is not necessary. Clinical examination is often more important. MRI may be relevant with neurological deficits or lack of improvement.
Can I exercise with whiplash?
Yes, movement is medicine - but it must be the right movement. Start with pain-free movements and increase gradually. Avoid activities that worsen symptoms. Walking is often a good starting point.
