Carpal Tunnel Syndrome: Complete Guide

Why your hands go numb at night, and what actually helps | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You wake up in the middle of the night with tingling, numb fingers. You shake your hand to get the feeling back. In the morning your hand is stiff, and perhaps you've started dropping things because your grip is weaker than before.

Carpal tunnel syndrome is the most common nerve entrapment condition in the body, affecting especially women aged 40-60. It is one of several common elbow and arm conditions. Fortunately, there are effective treatment options - and most people don't need surgery.

Let's look at what research shows about symptoms, causes and treatment - including the latest AAOS guidelines from 2024.

  • 2.7% affected, women 3x more often
  • Night splint: 50-70% improvement
  • Manual therapy = surgery short-term
  • Affects thumb, index, middle, half ring finger
  • Little finger NOT affected

For constant numbness that doesn't go away, visible muscle wasting at thumb base, or sudden weakness in the hand.

What is carpal tunnel syndrome?

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) occurs when the median nerve is compressed in the carpal tunnel - a narrow passage on the inside of the wrist. This tunnel is surrounded by the wrist bones on three sides and a tight band (the carpal ligament) on top.

When pressure in the tunnel increases, the nerve is compressed. This causes the characteristic symptoms: tingling, numbness and pain in the fingers supplied by the nerve - the thumb, index finger, middle finger and half of the ring finger.

The little finger is the key

If the little finger is also affected, it is not carpal tunnel syndrome - it's supplied by the ulnar nerve (see cubital tunnel syndrome). This is an important diagnostic sign.

2.7% of population affected
3x more common in women
40-60 years most common age group

Causes and risk factors

Carpal tunnel syndrome occurs when pressure in the tunnel becomes too high. This can be caused by:

Swelling of the tendons (most common)

Repetitive strain or overuse can cause swelling in the tendon sheaths passing through the tunnel, reducing space for the nerve.

Hormonal factors

Pregnancy, menopause, hypothyroidism and diabetes increase risk through fluid retention and tissue swelling.

Anatomical factors

Some people naturally have narrower carpal tunnels. Previous wrist fractures can also narrow the tunnel.

Repetitive work

Office work, cashier work, production work - anything with repeated hand movements over time. See also mouse arm.

Important risk factors

  • Gender: Women are affected 3 times more often than men
  • Pregnancy: Common in the last trimester (fluid retention)
  • Diabetes: Increased risk of nerve disease in general
  • Obesity: BMI over 30 significantly increases risk
  • Arthritis: Inflammation can affect the tendon sheaths
  • Hereditary: Tends to run in families
3

Symptoms

The symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome are often characteristic:

Typical symptoms

  • Tingling and numbness in thumb, index finger, middle finger and half of ring finger
  • Night symptoms - waking up with "sleeping" hand
  • Pain that can radiate up into the forearm
  • Weakness - dropping things, problems with buttons
  • "Electric shocks" in the fingers

Why is it worst at night?

Most people sleep with bent wrists, either under the head or curled toward the chest. When the wrist is bent, pressure in the carpal tunnel increases significantly - up to 10 times normal. This compresses the nerve and causes symptoms.

Symptoms that worsen over time

Without treatment, carpal tunnel syndrome can lead to:

  • Constant numbness (not just at night)
  • Muscle wasting at thumb base (thenar atrophy)
  • Permanent nerve damage

The earlier treatment starts, the better the prognosis.

Where are your symptoms?

Carpal tunnel syndrome Thumb, index, middle, half ring finger

Cervical radiculopathy Varies with nerve root, often entire arm
4

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily based on medical history and clinical examination. Provocation tests can confirm suspicion:

Phalen's test

Hold wrists flexed for 60 seconds. Positive test: symptoms in the median nerve area are triggered.

Tinel's sign

Tapping over the carpal tunnel causes "electric shock" in the fingers.

Nerve conduction studies (EMG/NCV)

Nerve conduction velocity studies. Can confirm diagnosis and grade severity. Particularly important for surgical evaluation.

Important differential diagnosis

Symptoms in the hand can also come from:

  • Neck: C6-C7 radiculopathy can give similar symptoms
  • Thoracic outlet syndrome: Nerve entrapment at ribs/shoulders
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Diabetes-related nerve disease

A thorough examination rules out other causes.

5

Evidence-based treatment

AAOS (American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons) updated their guidelines in 2024. Here's what the research shows:

First choice: Night splint

  • 50-70% experience improvement with nightly use of wrist splint
  • Keeps the wrist in neutral position during sleep
  • Significantly reduces night symptoms
  • Recommended as first-line treatment (AAOS 2024)

Manual therapy matches surgery short-term

An important study (Fernandez-de-las-Penas et al.) showed that manual treatment gave equally good results as surgery at 1 and 3 month follow-up. This included:

  • Nerve gliding exercises
  • Myofascial release of forearm and hand
  • Mobilization of wrist bones

Treatment plan at Chiropractor Mads Finstad

1. Night splint (first choice)

Guidance on proper use of wrist splint. Must be used every night for at least 3-4 weeks to evaluate effect.

2. Nerve gliding exercises

Structured exercises that mobilize the median nerve through its path, to reduce adhesions and improve nerve mobility.

3. Manual treatment

Soft tissue treatment of forearm, mobilization of wrist bones, and nerve gliding techniques.

4. Ergonomic guidance

Assessment of workstation, mouse use and keyboard height. Small changes can make a big difference.

When is surgery considered?

  • No effect from conservative treatment after 3-6 months
  • Severe symptoms with constant numbness
  • Visible muscle wasting (thenar atrophy)
  • Significantly reduced nerve function on neurography

Surgical treatment (carpal tunnel release) has very high success rate (>90%), but most don't need it with proper conservative treatment.

6

When should you seek help?

See a doctor for the following

  • Constant numbness that doesn't go away
  • Visible muscle wasting at thumb base
  • Weakness causing you to drop things
  • Symptoms in both hands simultaneously with other systemic symptoms
  • Rapid worsening of symptoms

These symptoms may indicate more serious nerve involvement requiring faster investigation.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do my hands go numb at night?
When you sleep, the wrist is often bent, which significantly increases pressure in the carpal tunnel. This compresses the median nerve and causes tingling and numbness. A night splint that keeps the wrist in neutral position can help 50-70% of patients.
Do I need surgery?
Not necessarily. Many recover with conservative treatment such as night splint and manual therapy. AAOS 2024 recommends trying conservative treatment first. Surgery is considered for severe symptoms or no effect after 3-6 months.
Can carpal tunnel syndrome go away on its own?
Mild cases can improve spontaneously, especially if there's a temporary cause (like pregnancy). But without treatment, most cases worsen over time. The earlier you start treatment, the better the prognosis.
Is it dangerous to wait with treatment?
Long-term, untreated carpal tunnel syndrome can cause permanent nerve damage with constant numbness and muscle wasting. Early treatment prevents this.
Can I continue working at a computer?
Yes, but you should consider ergonomic adjustments. Keyboard height, mouse type, and frequent breaks are important. An ergonomic assessment can identify problems.
What's the difference between carpal tunnel and cubital tunnel?
Carpal tunnel syndrome affects the median nerve in the wrist and causes symptoms in thumb, index, middle and ring finger. Cubital tunnel syndrome affects the ulnar nerve at the elbow and causes symptoms in little finger and ring finger.
