Cubital Tunnel Syndrome: Everything You Need to Know

Why your little finger falls asleep, and the simple trick that helps | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You know that feeling - the intense, electric sensation when you hit your elbow against something hard. What people call the "funny bone" is actually the ulnar nerve, and when this nerve becomes chronically irritated, you develop cubital tunnel syndrome. This condition is one of several common elbow and arm conditions.

Perhaps you've started to notice that your little finger and ring finger are tingling and falling asleep, especially at night or when talking on the phone with a bent elbow. This is the second most common nerve entrapment syndrome after carpal tunnel syndrome.

Fortunately, treatment is often simpler than you might think - and most people recover without surgery.

Quick Summary

  • 50-90% recover without surgery
  • Towel trick: 82% improvement
  • Bent elbow worsens symptoms
  • Little finger + ring finger affected
  • Worst at night
1

What is Cubital Tunnel Syndrome?

Cubital tunnel syndrome occurs when the ulnar nerve becomes compressed or irritated at the elbow. The nerve passes through a narrow tunnel (the cubital tunnel) on the inside and back of the elbow - exactly where you feel it when you hit your "funny bone".

The ulnar nerve supplies the little finger, half of the ring finger, and several small muscles in the hand that control fine motor skills. When the nerve is compressed, you get symptoms in exactly these areas.

Second Most Common After Carpal Tunnel

Cubital tunnel syndrome is the second most common peripheral nerve entrapment syndrome. It affects about 25 per 100,000 people annually, but is likely underdiagnosed.

Causes

The ulnar nerve is vulnerable at the elbow for several reasons:

Bent ElbowMost Common

When the elbow is bent, the ulnar nerve is stretched and compressed. Prolonged elbow flexion (sleeping, phone, keyboard) is the main cause.

Leaning on the Elbow

Leaning your elbow on armrests, tables, or car door armrests over time puts direct pressure on the nerve.

Anatomical Factors

Some people naturally have a narrow cubital tunnel or a nerve that "jumps" out of its groove when the elbow bends.

Previous Injuries

Elbow fractures, dislocations, or arthritis can narrow the tunnel and irritate the nerve.

Important Risk Factors

  • Office work: Prolonged keyboard use with bent elbows (see also mouse arm)
  • Sleeping habits: Sleeping with arms under head/pillow
  • Phone use: Long conversations with bent elbow
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Can cause swelling around the nerve
  • Diabetes: Increases the risk of nerve problems in general
3

Symptoms

Typical Symptoms

  • Tingling/numbness in little finger and half of ring finger
  • Worst at night or after prolonged elbow flexion
  • Weakness in the hand - difficulty spreading fingers, holding things
  • Pain on the inside of the elbow that can radiate down the forearm
  • "Electric shocks" when pressing on the inside of the elbow

Typical Triggers

Activity Why It Makes It Worse
Sleeping with bent arms Prolonged nerve stretch and compression
Talking on the phone Bent elbow + pressure against ear
Leaning on elbow Direct pressure on the nerve
Working at keyboard Static elbow flexion over time

Symptoms of More Serious Nerve Involvement

With prolonged compression, you may experience:

  • Constant numbness (not just intermittent)
  • Visible muscle wasting between fingers
  • "Claw fingers" - difficulty straightening ring finger and little finger
4

The Towel Trick and Treatment

Most people with cubital tunnel syndrome recover without surgery. The key is to keep the elbow straight, especially at night.

The Towel Trick - Simple and Effective

  1. Roll a towel into a cylinder
  2. Place it on the inside of the elbow (in the elbow crease)
  3. Secure loosely with tape, bandage, or stretch wrap
  4. Use every night during sleep

Result: Studies show 82% improvement with this simple method combined with activity modification.

Treatment Plan with Chiropractor Mads Finstad

1. Activity ModificationMost Important

Identify and modify activities that worsen symptoms. Avoid prolonged elbow flexion and leaning on the elbow.

2. Night Splint / Towel Trick

Keeps the elbow in a straight position during sleep. Often all that's needed for mild to moderate symptoms.

3. Nerve Glide Exercises

Gentle exercises that mobilize the ulnar nerve through its path from neck to hand.

4. Manual Treatment

Soft tissue treatment of surrounding muscles, mobilization of elbow and neck.

When is Surgery Considered?

  • Symptoms that persist despite 3-6 months of conservative treatment
  • Constant numbness
  • Visible muscle wasting
  • Significant weakness in the hand
5

Prognosis

Good Prognosis with Proper Treatment

  • 50-90% of patients recover without surgery
  • Mild symptoms: often improvement within weeks with activity modification
  • Moderate symptoms: typically 2-3 months with consistent treatment
  • The earlier treatment starts, the better the prognosis

Important to Know

Prolonged nerve compression can cause permanent damage. If you have symptoms that have lasted a long time without improvement, or you notice weakness/muscle wasting, you should seek help quickly.

6

When to Seek Help?

See a Doctor for the Following

  • Constant numbness that doesn't go away
  • Visible muscle wasting in the hand
  • Increasing weakness - dropping things, difficulty with buttons
  • Claw fingers (can't straighten ring finger/little finger)
  • Symptoms in both arms simultaneously

These symptoms may indicate more serious nerve involvement that requires prompt evaluation.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my little finger fall asleep at night?
When you sleep with bent elbows, the ulnar nerve is stretched and compressed. This causes tingling and numbness in the little finger and ring finger. Keeping the elbow straight at night (e.g., with a towel) can help significantly.
What's the difference between cubital and carpal tunnel syndrome?
Cubital tunnel syndrome affects the ulnar nerve at the ELBOW and causes symptoms in the little finger and ring finger. Carpal tunnel syndrome affects the median nerve at the WRIST and causes symptoms in the thumb, index, middle and ring fingers.
Do I need surgery?
Most people don't. 50-90% recover with conservative treatment such as the towel trick and activity modification. Surgery is only considered for severe or prolonged symptoms without improvement.
How long does it take to get better?
Mild symptoms can improve within weeks. Moderate symptoms typically take 2-3 months with consistent treatment. The earlier you start, the faster the improvement.
Can symptoms come from the neck?
Yes, C8 nerve root irritation from the neck can cause similar symptoms. A thorough examination can distinguish between these. Some have both conditions simultaneously ("double crush").
