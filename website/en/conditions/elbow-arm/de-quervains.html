De Quervain's Tenosynovitis: Complete Guide

"Texting thumb" and pain on the thumb side of the wrist | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

You feel a sharp pain on the thumb side of the wrist when you grip, twist, or lift something. Perhaps you have small children and lift them often, or you use your phone a lot with your thumb. The wrist may be swollen, and you feel it especially when you move the thumb. This is one of several common elbow and arm conditions.

De Quervain's tenosynovitis affects the tendon sheath on the thumb side of the wrist. Modern research shows that this is not classic inflammation, but a thickening and degeneration of the tendon sheaths - similar to what we see in trigger finger.

Quick Summary

  • Cortisone + splint: Gold standard (JAMA 2023)
  • Not classic inflammation - thickening
  • Common in new mothers
  • Texting thumb increasing prevalence
  • Good prognosis with treatment
1

What is De Quervain's tenosynovitis?

De Quervain's tenosynovitis is a painful condition affecting the tendon sheath on the thumb side of the wrist. Two tendons (abductor pollicis longus and extensor pollicis brevis) pass through a narrow tunnel, and when this tunnel becomes tight or irritated, you get symptoms.

Despite the name "-itis" (meaning inflammation), modern research shows the condition is characterized by degenerative changes and thickening of the tendon sheaths, not classic inflammation.

Causes and risk factors

Repetitive thumb use (most common)

Repeated gripping and thumb movements over time - texting, gaming, handwork. Similar mechanisms as in mouse arm.

Newborns/small children

Repetitive lifting with "L-grip" significantly loads the tendons. Common in new parents.

Hormonal factors

Pregnancy and breastfeeding increase risk. Hormonal changes affect tendon tissue.

Direct trauma

A blow or fall to the thumb side of the wrist can trigger the condition.

3

Symptoms and diagnosis

  • Pain on the thumb side of wrist, at "anatomical snuffbox"
  • Worsens with gripping - especially "L-grip" and thumb movements
  • Swelling over tendon sheath
  • Crepitus - grinding sensation with thumb movement
  • Pain that radiates up into forearm or down into thumb (can be confused with thumb arthritis)

Finkelstein's test

The classic diagnostic test: Place thumb in palm, make a fist around it, and bend wrist toward little finger side. If this reproduces the pain, the test is positive.

4

Treatment

Gold standard: Cortisone + splint (JAMA 2023)

  • The combination of cortisone injection + thumb splint has best evidence
  • Splint offloads tendons and allows rest
  • Cortisone reduces swelling in tendon sheath
  • Most recover with 1-2 injections

Treatment at Chiropractor Mads Finstad

1. Offloading

Thumb-offloading splint, activity modification, ergonomic guidance.

2. Manual treatment

Soft tissue treatment of forearm and thumb muscles, gentle mobilization.

3. Exercise and rehabilitation

Gradual strengthening when acute symptoms have settled.

4. Referral when needed

If no improvement, referral to doctor for cortisone injection or surgical evaluation.

5

FAQ

How long does it take to recover?
With proper treatment, most experience significant improvement within 4-6 weeks. Complete healing may take 3-6 months.
Does cortisone help?
Yes, for De Quervain's, cortisone has good effect (unlike tennis elbow). Combined with splinting, this is gold standard according to JAMA 2023.
Can I continue using my phone?
You should significantly reduce thumb use on phone during treatment period. Consider using index finger or voice functions.
Do I need surgery?
Rarely. Most recover with conservative treatment. Surgery is only considered for prolonged symptoms without improvement from 1-2 cortisone injections.
