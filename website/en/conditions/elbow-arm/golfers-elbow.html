Golfer's Elbow: Everything You Need to Know

90% caused by work - not golf. This is how it's effectively treated. | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You feel an aching pain on the inside of your elbow. It hurts when you grip things, when you turn door handles, and maybe you've even noticed that your little finger and ring finger have started to tingle or go numb.

Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) is tennis elbow's "relative" - but on the opposite side of the elbow. And ironically, over 90% of cases have nothing to do with golf.

What makes golfer's elbow a bit more complicated than tennis elbow is that the nerve going to the little finger (the ulnar nerve) passes right next to the tender area - and in up to 60% of cases, this nerve is also involved.

Quick Summary

  • >90% caused by work, not golf
  • 20-60% have ulnar nerve symptoms
  • 85-96% recover without surgery
  • Eccentric training is the gold standard
  • Tendinosis - not inflammation

What is golfer's elbow?

Golfer's elbow, or medial epicondylitis, is a painful condition that affects the tendon attachment on the inside of the elbow. This is where the muscles that flex the wrist and fingers attach - the muscles you use when gripping, squeezing, and twisting.

Just like tennis elbow, golfer's elbow is not an inflammation, but a degenerative condition (tendinosis). The tendon gradually breaks down over time due to overuse, without sufficient time to repair itself.

Golfer's elbow vs. tennis elbow

Property Golfer's Elbow Tennis Elbow
Location Inside of elbow Outside of elbow
Muscles Flexors (flex wrist) Extensors (extend wrist)
Nerve involvement Ulnar nerve (20-60%) Rare
Prevalence Less common (3-10x) More common
0.4-0.6% of population affected
40-60 years most common age group
>90% work-related

Causes and risk factors

Over 90% of golfer's elbow cases are caused by work-related factors - not golf. The most common causes are:

Repetitive manual work Most common

Manual labor like carpentry, plumbing, painting. Repetitive gripping and twisting movements over time.

Office work

Prolonged mouse and keyboard use, especially with poor ergonomics. Often combined with tennis elbow. See our article on mouse arm.

Throwing sports

Baseball, handball, javelin throwing. Repetitive throwing motions strain the inside of the elbow.

Golf (5-10% of cases)

Actually only a small proportion. Most common in beginners with incorrect technique.

Important risk factors

  • Repetitive gripping movements: Same movement thousands of times
  • Heavy lifting with bent wrist: Increases strain on the inside
  • Vibration: Use of vibrating tools (drill, grinder)
  • Age 40-60 years: Natural aging of tendon tissue
  • Smokers: Poorer blood supply to tendons

The ulnar nerve - the hidden factor

What distinguishes golfer's elbow from tennis elbow is its proximity to the ulnar nerve - the nerve that goes to the little finger and ring finger. This nerve passes right behind the bony prominence on the inside of the elbow (where it hurts when you hit your "funny bone").

Important to know

Studies show that 20-60% of patients with golfer's elbow also have symptoms from the ulnar nerve. This can affect treatment choices and prognosis.

Signs of ulnar nerve involvement

  • Tingling or numbness in little finger and ring finger
  • Weakness in grip - things "slip out of your hand"
  • Difficulty spreading fingers apart
  • Symptoms that worsen with bent elbow (e.g., during sleep)

If you have these symptoms in addition to elbow pain, it's important to get a thorough examination that includes nerve tests. Treatment must then also address the nerve irritation.

Cubital tunnel syndrome

When the ulnar nerve is significantly involved, it's called cubital tunnel syndrome. This is the second most common nerve entrapment syndrome after carpal tunnel. Read more in our article on cubital tunnel syndrome.

Symptoms

Typical symptoms of golfer's elbow include:

  • Pain on the inside of the elbow: At the bony prominence (medial epicondyle)
  • Worsens with gripping: Especially when squeezing or twisting
  • Pain with wrist flexion: When actively flexing the wrist against resistance
  • Morning stiffness: Often worst in the first hour
  • Weakness: Reduced grip strength

Typical provoking activities

Activity Why it causes pain
Handshake Forceful grip activates the flexors
Wringing cloths Combination of grip and rotation
Unscrewing lids Rotation with loaded grip
Lifting heavy with bent wrist Maximum flexor strain
Golf swing (impact) Acceleration with grip

Evidence-based treatment

The good news: 85-96% of patients with golfer's elbow recover with conservative treatment. Surgery is rarely necessary.

Gold standard: Eccentric training

  • Gradual strengthening of the flexor tendons
  • Stimulates collagen production and tendon building
  • Eccentric "wrist curls" with light weight
  • 3 sets x 15 repetitions, 2 times daily

Treatment plan at Chiropractor Mads Finstad

1. Thorough examination

Assess whether the ulnar nerve is involved, rule out differential diagnoses, and map provoking factors.

2. Manual treatment

Soft tissue treatment of trigger points in the flexor muscles, mobilization of elbow and wrist, possibly nerve gliding for the ulnar nerve.

3. Eccentric training Most Important

Structured training program focusing on eccentric loading of the flexors. Reverse wrist curls with gradually increased resistance.

4. Ergonomic guidance

Identify and modify provoking activities at work and leisure.

Eccentric wrist curl for golfer's elbow

  1. Sit with your forearm on a table, palm up, wrist over the edge of the table
  2. Hold a light weight (0.5-1 kg) in your hand
  3. Start with wrist flexed up
  4. Lower the weight slowly (3-4 seconds) until wrist is extended
  5. Use the other hand to lift back to start
  6. Repeat 15 times, 3 sets, 2 times daily

Tip: Some discomfort is OK, but stop at sharp pain.

Prognosis and duration

Golfer's elbow generally has a good prognosis, but requires patience and active effort.

Expected course

  • 85-96% recover without surgery
  • Noticeable improvement: often after 6-8 weeks of active rehabilitation
  • Full healing: typically 3-6 months
  • Without treatment: can last 1-2 years or become chronic

Factors affecting prognosis

Positive Negative
Early treatment start Long symptom duration
Ability to modify work Continued overuse
Active participation in training Significant ulnar nerve involvement
Good general health Smoking, diabetes
7

When should you seek help?

See a doctor if you have

  • Increasing numbness or weakness in fingers
  • Visible muscle wasting in the hand
  • Sudden onset, intense pain after trauma
  • Visible swelling, redness and warmth (possible infection)
  • Night pain that wakes you up
  • Unable to flex or extend the elbow

These symptoms may indicate nerve damage or other conditions that require prompt investigation.

Differential diagnoses

  • Cubital tunnel syndrome: Primary nerve entrapment, can resemble golfer's elbow
  • Cervical radiculopathy (C8): Nerve root irritation from the neck
  • MCL injury: Damage to the medial collateral ligament
  • Medial elbow osteoarthritis: Osteoarthritis in the elbow joint

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between golfer's elbow and tennis elbow?
Golfer's elbow causes pain on the INSIDE of the elbow and affects the flexor muscles (that flex the wrist). Tennis elbow causes pain on the OUTSIDE and affects the extensor muscles (that extend the wrist). The treatment principles are similar.
Can I have both golfer's and tennis elbow at the same time?
Yes, this is not uncommon, especially with office work. Both flexors and extensors can become overloaded simultaneously. Treatment must then address both sides.
Why are my fingers tingling?
Tingling in the little finger and ring finger indicates that the ulnar nerve is involved. This occurs in 20-60% of patients with golfer's elbow. The nerve passes right by the tender area and can become irritated.
Should I use an elbow brace?
A golfer's elbow brace can provide symptomatic relief. It should be placed about 2-3 cm below the elbow on the inside. Use it as a supplement to training, not as the only treatment.
Can I continue to exercise?
Yes, but avoid exercises that provoke pain. Bicep curls and pulling exercises can be problematic. Focus on rehabilitation exercises and temporarily modify strength training.
Do I need surgery?
Rarely. 85-96% recover without surgery. Surgery is only considered for prolonged problems (over 12 months) without effect from thorough conservative treatment.
