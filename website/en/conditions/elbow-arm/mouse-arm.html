Mouse Arm and Office Injuries: What Actually Works

Why ergonomics alone isn't enough - and what you need to do in addition | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You sit at the computer day after day, and gradually it builds up: aching pain in the forearm, stiffness in the wrist, maybe tingling in the fingers. Your colleagues have the same problems. You've tried an ergonomic mouse, a sit-stand desk, and keyboard support - but the symptoms keep coming back. Mouse arm is one of the most common elbow and arm conditions we see.

Mouse arm (WRULD/RSI) affects over half of all people who work with computers. But here's the surprising thing: ergonomics alone is not enough. Research shows that strength training is the only intervention with strong evidence for effectiveness.

What is Mouse Arm?

Mouse arm is a collective term for pain and problems in the arm, wrist, and hand related to computer work. Medical terms include:

  • WRULD: Work-Related Upper Limb Disorders
  • RSI: Repetitive Strain Injury
  • Overuse injuries: Overuse problems in the upper extremities

The condition can include everything from diffuse pain without a specific diagnosis, to defined conditions such as tennis elbow, golfer's elbow, carpal tunnel syndrome, or De Quervain's tenosynovitis.

50-57% of office workers affected
#1 occupational disease in Norway
Women affected more often than men

Causes

Mouse arm develops gradually through a combination of factors:

Static Muscle Work (Main Cause)

Holding the arm in the same position hour after hour creates constant low-intensity strain on the muscles - this is more damaging than previously thought.

Repetitive Movements

Thousands of small movements daily - mouse clicks, keystrokes - accumulate strain over time.

Poor Ergonomics

Wrong height of chair, screen, or keyboard increases the strain. But ergonomics alone doesn't solve the problem.

Lack of Breaks

Working for long periods without breaks doesn't give the tissue time to rest and repair itself.

3

Symptoms

Symptoms of mouse arm vary from mild to severe:

  • Pain and aching in forearm, wrist, fingers
  • Stiffness especially in the morning or after long work sessions
  • Fatigue in the arm - "used up" feeling
  • Tingling/numbness in the fingers (can resemble carpal tunnel syndrome or cubital tunnel syndrome)
  • Weakness in grip
  • Heat/cold sensation in the hand

Important to Take Seriously

Mouse arm can develop into chronic problems if not treated. The earlier you address the problem, the easier it is to overcome.

4

The Myth About Ergonomics

Here comes the surprising truth: ergonomics alone does not have strong evidence for preventing or treating mouse arm.

What the Research Shows

Systematic reviews (Cochrane) have examined all available research and conclude:

  • Ergonomic adjustments alone have insufficient evidence for effect
  • Ergonomic equipment (mouse, keyboard, chairs) provides no documented improvement in symptoms
  • The combination of ergonomics + training gives better results than ergonomics alone

This doesn't mean ergonomics is unimportant - it means you need more than just ergonomics.

5

What Actually Works

Strength Training - Only Strong Evidence

  • Strength training for neck, shoulders, and arms is the only intervention with strong evidence
  • Increases muscle capacity to tolerate static strain
  • Reduces pain and increases function
  • Recommended 2-3 times weekly

Effective Combination Treatment

1. Strength Training (Most Important)

Specific exercises for neck, shoulders, forearm, and hand. Gradual increase in load.

2. Micro-breaks

Short breaks (30-60 seconds) every 20-30 minutes. Shake out your arms, move your fingers, look away from the screen.

3. Variation

Alternate between sitting and standing work, switch between mouse and keyboard, use both hands.

4. Ergonomics (as a Supplement)

Correct screen height, keyboard position, and chair settings - as an addition to training, not instead of it.

5. Manual Treatment

Soft tissue treatment of trigger points, joint mobilization, nerve glide techniques.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does an ergonomic mouse help?
An ergonomic mouse can reduce strain on the wrist, but research shows that it alone doesn't prevent or treat mouse arm. It should be combined with strength training and breaks.
How often should I take breaks?
Ideally a short micro-break (30-60 seconds) every 20-30 minutes, and a longer break (5-10 minutes) every hour. Move around and shake out your arms during the breaks.
Which exercises help?
Strength exercises for neck, shoulders, and arms are most important. Examples: shoulder shrugs, rowing exercises, grip exercises, and eccentric exercises for the forearms.
Can I continue working?
Yes, with adjustments. More frequent breaks, variation in work tasks, and an exercise program can allow you to work without worsening the symptoms.
When should I seek help?
If the symptoms persist for more than 2-3 weeks despite breaks and adjustments, or if you experience numbness, weakness, or increasing symptoms.
