Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)

Complete guide to treatment - Why cortisone often fails, and what actually works | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You feel it when you lift your coffee cup, push open a car door, or just try to wring out a dishcloth. A sharp, aching pain on the outside of the elbow that won't go away. Maybe you've already tried cortisone and experienced the pain returning after a few weeks.

Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) is one of the most common elbow and arm injuries - but also one of the most misunderstood. It's not an inflammation, and only 5-10% of cases are actually caused by tennis.

Let's look at what research actually shows about this condition, and why traditional treatments often fall short.

  • Tyler Twist: 81% improvement in studies
  • 89% get better within 12 months
  • Not inflammation - degeneration (tendinosis)
  • Only 5-10% from tennis

What is tennis elbow?

Tennis elbow, or lateral epicondylitis, is a painful condition that affects the tendon attachment on the outside of the elbow. This is where the muscles that extend the wrist and fingers attach - the same muscles you use when lifting, gripping, and twisting.

But here's the important thing: tennis elbow is not an inflammation. Modern research has shown that the condition is a degeneration (wear) of the tendon, not an inflammatory process. That's why the term "tendinosis" has replaced "tendinitis" in professional circles (Coombes et al., 2015).

Who is affected?

Despite the name, ironically only 5-10% of cases are caused by tennis. Most cases arise from:

  • Repetitive work with mouse and keyboard (mouse arm)
  • Manual labor (carpentry, painting, plumbing)
  • Housework and gardening
  • Other sports (strength training, golf, badminton)
1-3% of the population affected
35-54 years most common age group
90% dominant arm

Causes and risk factors

Tennis elbow develops when the strain on the tendon exceeds its ability to repair itself. Over time, this leads to:

Micro-tears in the tendon

Small tears in the tendon tissue that accumulate over time from repetitive strain.

Neovascularization

Abnormal growth of blood vessels and nerve fibers - this explains why the condition is so painful.

Collagen degeneration

The structural fibers in the tendon break down and are replaced by disorganized, weaker tissue.

Important risk factors

  • Repetitive strain: Same movement many times a day
  • Forceful gripping: Work requiring hard gripping over time
  • Poor ergonomics: Wrong work position or tools
  • Smokers: Have worse prognosis and longer healing time
  • Age 35-54 years: Natural aging of tendon tissue
3

Symptoms

The symptoms of tennis elbow are often characteristic and easily recognizable:

  • Pain on the outside of the elbow: Especially at the bony prominence (lateral epicondyle)
  • Worsens with gripping: Coffee cup, door handle, handshake
  • Radiates down the forearm: Can extend toward the wrist
  • Weakness in grip: Things "slip out of your hand"
  • Morning stiffness: Often worst in the first hour after waking

Typical provoking activities

Activity Why it causes pain
Lifting a coffee cup Combination of grip and wrist extension
Opening door handles Twisting with loaded grip
Handshake Forceful grip activates the extensors
Computer mouse use Static tension over time
Wringing cloths Forceful rotation and grip

The cortisone myth

Cortisone injections have long been a popular treatment for tennis elbow. But what does the research actually say?

The research is clear

A landmark study from Bisset et al. (2006) showed surprising results:

  • 72% recurrence rate within 12 months after cortisone injection
  • Those who received cortisone had worse long-term results than those who waited
  • Cortisone provides short-term relief (2-6 weeks), but worsens the prognosis

Why does cortisone fail?

When we understand that tennis elbow is degeneration, not inflammation, it becomes clear why anti-inflammatory treatment doesn't work:

  • Cortisone inhibits collagen synthesis - the tendon doesn't build up
  • Pain relief creates false security and increased strain
  • The tissue becomes weaker, not stronger
  • Recurrence is almost inevitable

What if you've already had cortisone?

Don't despair. Many get better anyway, but it's important to start active rehabilitation as soon as possible. Avoid repeated injections - each new injection weakens the tendon further.

5

Evidence-based treatment

Fortunately, there are treatment methods that actually work - and they have nothing to do with needles.

Gold standard: Eccentric training

  • Stimulates collagen production and tendon building
  • Gradually increased load tolerance
  • Long-term results without recurrence
  • Tyler Twist exercise: 81% improvement in controlled studies

Treatment plan at Chiropractor Mads Finstad

1. Manual treatment

Soft tissue treatment of trigger points in the extensor muscles, as well as mobilization of the elbow and wrist to optimize biomechanics.

2. Eccentric training Most Important

Structured training program focusing on eccentric loading. Tyler Twist with FlexBar is first choice, alternatively "reverse wrist curl".

3. Ergonomic guidance

Identify and modify provoking activities at work and leisure. Often small adjustments have a big effect.

4. Follow-up and progression

Gradual increase in load and return to full activity when pain allows.

The Tyler Twist Exercise

The Tyler Twist is the most studied and effective exercise for tennis elbow. It's performed with a FlexBar (rubber bar) and focuses on eccentric loading of the extensor muscles.

How to do the Tyler Twist

  1. Hold the FlexBar vertically in front of you with the affected hand up (overhand grip)
  2. Grip around the top with the healthy hand
  3. Twist the FlexBar with the healthy hand while the affected hand holds against it
  4. Extend both arms in front of you (90 degrees)
  5. Slowly release with the affected hand - this is the eccentric phase
  6. Repeat 15 times, 3 sets, 1-2 times daily

Tip: Some discomfort is normal, but sharp pain means you need to reduce the intensity.

81% experienced improvement
6-8 weeks for noticeable improvement
3x15 daily dose

Don't have a FlexBar?

You can start with "reverse wrist curls" with light weight (0.5-1 kg). Sit with your forearm on your thigh, wrist outside the knee, and slowly lower the weight down. This provides similar eccentric loading.

Duration and prognosis

The good news: tennis elbow generally has a good prognosis with proper treatment.

Expected course

  • 89% experience significant improvement within 12 months
  • With active rehabilitation: noticeable improvement from 6-8 weeks
  • Full healing: often 3-6 months
  • Without treatment: can last 1-2 years or become chronic

Factors affecting prognosis

Positive Negative
Early treatment start Long symptom duration before treatment

When should you seek help?

See a doctor if you have

  • Sudden onset, intense pain after trauma
  • Visible swelling, redness and warmth (possible infection)
  • Numbness or tingling in the fingers
  • Unable to extend the elbow
  • Night pain that wakes you up
  • Unexplained weight loss or general feeling of illness

These symptoms may indicate other conditions that require further investigation.

Differential diagnoses to be aware of

  • Radial tunnel syndrome: Nerve entrapment of the radial nerve - gives similar pain location
  • Cervical radiculopathy: Nerve root irritation from the neck - referred pain
  • Elbow osteoarthritis: Osteoarthritis in the elbow joint
  • Plica syndrome: Irritation of the joint lining

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for tennis elbow to heal?
Most people (89%) experience significant improvement within 12 months with conservative treatment. With active rehabilitation and eccentric exercises, improvement can be seen as early as 6-8 weeks.
Should I use an elbow brace/bandage?
A tennis elbow brace can provide symptomatic relief and make it easier to function in daily life. It should be placed about 2-3 cm below the elbow, not over the tender spot. Use it as a supplement to training, not as the only treatment.
Does cortisone help with tennis elbow?
Cortisone often provides short-term relief (2-6 weeks), but studies show a 72% recurrence rate. Patients who receive cortisone often have worse long-term results than those who choose active rehabilitation. I recommend avoiding cortisone.
Can I continue to exercise?
Yes, but avoid exercises that provoke pain. Pull exercises and gripping can be problematic. Focus on eccentric rehabilitation exercises and temporarily modify strength training.
What's the difference between tennis elbow and golfer's elbow?
Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) causes pain on the outside of the elbow and affects the extensor muscles. Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) causes pain on the inside and affects the flexor muscles. The treatment principles are similar.
Do I need surgery?
Rarely. Only 5-10% with prolonged problems (over 12 months) without effect from conservative treatment are considered for surgery. The vast majority get better without surgery.
