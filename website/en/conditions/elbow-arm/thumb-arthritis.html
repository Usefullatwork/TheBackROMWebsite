Thumb Arthritis (CMC-1): Complete Guide

Osteoarthritis in the thumb - and why X-rays don't always tell the truth | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You notice it when you open a jar, turn a key, or squeeze hard. A deep, aching pain at the base of the thumb, right where the thumb meets the palm. Perhaps the doctor has taken an X-ray and said you have "wear" - but does that mean you have to live with the pain? As part of elbow and arm conditions, thumb arthritis is common but often overdiagnosed.

Thumb arthritis (CMC-1 arthritis) is the most common form of hand arthritis. But here's the surprising thing: 72% of those with X-ray findings have no symptoms. Wear on the image doesn't necessarily mean pain.

What is thumb arthritis?

Thumb arthritis (CMC-1 arthritis, basal arthritis) is osteoarthritis in the joint at the base of the thumb - where the thumb meets the first metacarpal bone (carpometacarpal joint). This joint is particularly vulnerable because it gives the thumb its unique mobility, but also exposes it to great strain. The condition is sometimes confused with De Quervain's tenosynovitis or trigger finger, which also cause thumb problems.

The joint has a "saddle shape" that allows movement in multiple planes. This enables gripping and squeezing powerfully, but also makes the joint unstable and vulnerable to wear over time.

Causes and risk factors

Genetics (86% hereditary)

Heredity is by far the most important factor. If your mother has thumb arthritis, your risk is significantly increased.

Gender

Women are affected 10-15 times more often than men. Hormones and joint laxity play a role.

Age

Most common after 50, but X-ray findings can be found as early as the 40s.

Load

Heavy manual work and repetitive gripping movements over time increase risk somewhat. This is similar to the causation pattern for mouse arm and other overuse conditions in the arm.

3

Symptoms

  • Pain at thumb base - especially with gripping and pinching
  • Stiffness in the morning or after inactivity
  • Weakness in grip - difficulty opening jars
  • Swelling at thumb base
  • Crepitus - grinding sensation with movement
  • Changed shape - "squared" appearance at thumb base in advanced cases

X-ray findings don't always mean pain

Research shows that 72% of those with X-ray changes have no symptoms. Wear on the image is normal with age and doesn't always explain the pain you experience. Treatment should therefore focus on symptoms, not X-ray images.

4

Treatment

Conservative treatment is first choice

  • Most people get good relief without surgery
  • Hand exercises and strengthening of stabilizing muscles
  • Thumb splint for relief
  • Activity modification

Treatment plan

1. Splinting

Thumb splint stabilizes the joint and reduces load. Can be used as needed or continuously.

2. Hand exercises

Strengthening exercises for thumb stabilizing muscles. Increases stability and reduces pain.

3. Activity modification

Ergonomic aids, changing grip technique, and break strategies.

4. Manual treatment

Soft tissue treatment of overloaded musculature, gentle mobilization. Treatment of trigger points in thumb muscles can also be helpful.

Cortisone for thumb arthritis

Unlike some other conditions, cortisone has limited effect for thumb arthritis. The effect is often short-lived (2-3 weeks), and repeated injections are not recommended. Hand exercises provide better long-term results.

5

Frequently Asked Questions

Does wear on X-ray mean I have to live with pain?
No. 72% of those with X-ray findings actually have no symptoms. Wear is normal with age. Treatment focuses on symptoms, not images.
Does exercise help?
Yes! Strength training for thumb muscles increases stability and reduces pain. This is one of the most important treatment forms.
Should I use a thumb splint?
A splint can provide good relief, especially during activities that provoke pain. It can be used as needed or more continuously during periods of significant problems.
Do I need surgery?
Most people don't. Surgery is only considered for severe symptoms that don't respond to thorough conservative treatment over 6-12 months.
Is it hereditary?
Yes, genetics explain about 86% of risk. If your mother or grandmother has thumb arthritis, you have increased risk.
