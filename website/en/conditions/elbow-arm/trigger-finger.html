Trigger Finger: Complete Guide

When your finger "locks" in place - and what helps | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You feel it in the morning - your finger locks in a bent position, and you have to use your other hand to straighten it out. It may pop and click, and sometimes gets completely stuck. This is trigger finger, one of several common elbow and arm conditions, and it's far more common than you might think.

The good news? Over half of cases resolve on their own, and most people recover with simple treatment.

  • Cortisone: 63-80% success

What is trigger finger?

Trigger finger (stenosing tenosynovitis) occurs when the flexor tendon in the finger becomes too thick to glide freely through the tendon sheath. The tendon "catches" at the A1 pulley at the finger base, and the finger locks in a bent position. A similar mechanism is seen in De Quervain's tenosynovitis in the thumb.

When you try to straighten the finger, the tendon must pass the narrowing - and this causes the characteristic "triggering" or clicking sound.

2-3% of population affected
6x more common in women
Thumb most commonly affected
2

Causes and risk factors

Repetitive gripping (Most common)

Repeated gripping movements over time irritate the tendon sheath and cause thickening. Same mechanism as in mouse arm and other overuse conditions.

Diabetes

Significantly increased risk - up to 10% of diabetics develop trigger finger. Diabetes also increases risk of carpal tunnel syndrome.

Rheumatoid arthritis

Inflammatory joint diseases increase risk of tendon disease.

Hormonal factors

Women are affected 6 times more often than men, especially during menopause.

3

Symptoms

  • Clicking/locking when bending and straightening finger
  • Morning stiffness - often worst first hour after waking
  • Tenderness at finger base (A1 pulley)
  • Nodule that can be felt where tendon is thickened
  • Locked finger in severe cases - must be straightened with other hand

KANAVEL'S SIGNS - EMERGENCY

  • Swollen, red, warm finger
  • Intense pain on passive extension
  • Finger held in semi-flexed position
  • Tenderness along entire tendon

These signs may indicate infectious tenosynovitis (pyogenic flexor tenosynovitis) - a surgical emergency. Call emergency services immediately.

4

Treatment

Prognosis is good

  • 52% of mild cases resolve spontaneously
  • 63-80% recover with cortisone
  • Surgery has >90% success rate for the rest

Treatment ladder

1. Rest and splinting

Finger splint that keeps finger straight at night. Often sufficient for mild cases.

2. Cortisone (Effective)

Cortisone injection into tendon sheath has 63-80% success rate. Can be repeated 1-2 times.

3. Surgery

A1 pulley release - a simple outpatient procedure with >90% success.

Shockwave therapy (ESWT)

A systematic review from Brancaccio et al. (2024) concludes that ESWT has no proven effect for trigger finger. Not recommended as treatment.

5

Frequently Asked Questions

Does trigger finger go away on its own?
Yes, studies show that 52% of mild cases resolve spontaneously, especially with rest and time.
Does cortisone help?
Yes, cortisone injection has 63-80% success rate. This is often the first choice when rest alone is not enough.
Is shockwave treatment effective?
No. A new systematic review from 2024 (Brancaccio et al.) shows ESWT has no proven effect for trigger finger. This is in contrast to conditions like tennis elbow where there is some support.
Do I need surgery?
Most people don't. But for those who don't respond to cortisone, surgery (A1 release) has over 90% success rate.
