Trigger Points in Arm and Forearm

The hidden cause behind many arm pains | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You may have received a diagnosis of tennis elbow or carpal tunnel syndrome, but treatment isn't working. The pain moves around, comes and goes, and you feel tender knots in the muscles when you press. Could the cause be trigger points?

Trigger points are one of the most overlooked causes of arm pain. Data from pain clinics shows that 85-95% of patients with chronic pain have trigger points as a primary or contributing cause.

Let's look at what trigger points are, how they develop, and why they're so often misdiagnosed as other conditions.

Quick Summary

  • 85-95% of chronic pain involves trigger points
  • 70% of tennis elbow patients have active trigger points
  • Referred pain: Pain felt elsewhere
  • Treated effectively with manual therapy
  • Often misdiagnosed as nerve entrapment

What are trigger points?

Trigger points are hypersensitive areas in muscle fibers - often described as "knots" or "tightness." When you press on a trigger point, it causes characteristic pain, and often also referred pain - pain that radiates to other areas.

There are two main types:

Active trigger points Cause symptoms

Cause spontaneous pain and referred pain. Can disturb sleep and affect daily function.

Latent trigger points

Only tender when pressed. Can limit mobility and weaken the muscle, but don't cause spontaneous pain.

Why do trigger points develop?

Trigger points develop when muscle fibers become overloaded or damaged. The most common causes in arm and forearm:

  • Repetitive movements: Mouse use, keyboard, manual work
  • Static loading: Holding arm in same position over time
  • Acute overload: Lifting, unaccustomed activity
  • Poor ergonomics: Wrong work position at computer
  • Stress and tension: Elevated muscle tension over time
  • Cold: Drafts or cold exposure

What happens in the muscle?

Trigger points develop when motor end plates (where nerve meets muscle) become overactive. This leads to sustained contraction of muscle fibers, reduced blood flow, and accumulation of waste products. The result is a tender, tight knot.

3

Referred pain - pain in the wrong place

The most confusing thing about trigger points is referred pain: pain is felt in a completely different place from where the problem is. This explains why many get the wrong diagnosis.

Common referred pain patterns in the arm

  • Extensors (top of forearm): Referred pain to elbow and back of hand - resembles tennis elbow
  • Flexors (underside of forearm): Referred pain to wrist and fingers - resembles carpal tunnel
  • Brachioradialis: Referred pain to thumb base and wrist
  • Scalene muscles (neck): Referred pain down into arm and fingers - resembles radiculopathy
  • Subscapularis (shoulder): Referred pain to wrist and back of upper arm

Common misdiagnoses

Because of referred pain, trigger points are often misdiagnosed as:

  • Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis)
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Cervical radiculopathy (neck nerve root)
  • De Quervain's tenosynovitis
  • Frozen shoulder

Symptoms of trigger points

How to recognize trigger points in arm and forearm:

  • Tender knots: Palpable, tender points in muscles
  • Referred pain: Pain that radiates to other areas
  • Tightness: Reduced mobility in the muscle
  • Weakness: Reduced strength without muscle damage
  • Autonomic symptoms: Local sweating, goosebumps, redness
  • Tingling/numbness: Can resemble nerve irritation

Treatment of trigger points

Trigger points respond well to manual treatment. The most important methods:

Effective treatment methods

  • Ischemic compression: Sustained pressure on trigger point until it releases
  • Dry needling: Thin needle into trigger point to release it
  • Soft tissue treatment: Massage and friction to increase circulation
  • Stretching: Muscle stretches to normalize length
  • Heat: Improves circulation and eases tension

What can you do yourself?

You can treat many trigger points yourself with simple techniques:

Self-massage technique

  • Find the tender point by palpating the muscles
  • Press with moderate force (5-7 on a scale to 10)
  • Hold pressure for 30-90 seconds until pain subsides
  • Gently stretch the muscle afterward
  • Repeat 2-3 times daily

Tip: Use a tennis ball against the wall for hard-to-reach points in the upper arm.

Preventive measures

  • Take breaks: Regular micro-breaks during repetitive work
  • Stretch: Daily stretching exercises for forearm muscles
  • Ergonomics: Correct work position at computer
  • Warm up: Before physical activity involving arms
  • Stress management: Stress increases muscle tension

Frequently Asked Questions

What are trigger points?
Trigger points are tender knots in muscle tissue that can cause local pain and referred pain - pain felt in a completely different location from where the problem is. They occur in 85-95% of patients with chronic pain.
Can trigger points be confused with tennis elbow?
Yes, trigger points in the forearm muscles can cause pain resembling tennis elbow. Studies show that 70% of patients with tennis elbow have active trigger points contributing to their pain. Both conditions can also occur simultaneously.
How long does it take to get rid of trigger points?
Fresh trigger points can resolve after 1-3 treatments. Chronic trigger points that have been present for months or years often require 4-8 treatments over several weeks, combined with self-treatment.
Is treatment painful?
Treatment of trigger points involves pressure on tender points, so some discomfort is normal. The pain should be "good pain" - uncomfortable but tolerable. Afterward there may be some soreness for 1-2 days before improvement occurs.
Can trigger points come back?
Yes, if the causative factors aren't corrected. That's why it's important to identify what triggered the trigger points - repetitive movements, poor ergonomics, stress - and make changes to prevent recurrence.
