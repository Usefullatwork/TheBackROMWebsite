Foot and Ankle Problems

Overview, treatment and when you should seek help | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Every day your feet carry your entire body weight through thousands of steps. With 26 bones, 33 joints and over 100 muscles in each foot, it's impressive how rarely they actually fail. But when problems do arise, they can affect everything from work performance to sleep quality.

This overview article gives you an evidence-based review of the most common foot and ankle conditions I see at the clinic.

Quick Summary

  • Approx. 24% of adults over 45 have frequent foot problems (Thomas et al. 2011)
  • Most common causes: Plantar fasciopathy, Achilles tendinopathy, ankle sprain, metatarsalgia
  • 80-90% improve with conservative treatment without surgery
  • Red flags: Fever with swelling, night pain, numbness, inability to bear weight after injury

How Common Are Foot Problems?

A systematic review from 2011 analysed 31 studies with over 75,000 participants and found that 24% of adults over 45 report frequent foot problems, and 15% have ankle problems. Prevalence increases with age, and women are affected more often than men.

A large population study from North England found that foot problems in people over 55 double the risk of difficulties with standing and walking, and increase the risk of problems with stairs by 71%.

Common Symptoms

  • Morning stiffness: Pain and stiffness with first steps after rest
  • Load-related pain: Worsening with standing, walking or running
  • Swelling: Localised or diffuse around foot or ankle
  • Instability: Feeling the ankle "gives way"
  • Numbness or tingling: In toes or forefoot

Most Common Causes

Plantar Fasciopathy (Heel Pain)

10% lifetime risk

Go to full article on plantar fasciopathy

The most common cause of heel pain. Affects approx. 10% of the population during their lifetime. Most common between 40-60 years. Women have 2.5 times higher risk than men.

What is it? Degenerative changes in the plantar fascia - the connective tissue band that stretches from the heel to the toes. Despite the name "fasciitis", it's not inflammation but breakdown of collagen fibres. Therefore the term "fasciopathy" is now used.

Characteristics: Intense stabbing heel pain with the first steps in the morning ("start pain"). The pain often improves after a few minutes, but can return after prolonged activity.

Most important risk factors:

  • Limited ankle mobility (23x increased risk at zero degrees dorsiflexion)
  • Overweight (BMI >30 gives 5-6x increased risk)
  • Occupations with lots of standing

Prognosis: 80-90% improve with conservative treatment within 12 months. Shockwave treatment has the strongest long-term evidence for persistent cases.

The Heel Spur Myth

Many think the heel spur "pokes" and causes the pain. This is wrong.

  • 55% of adults over 60 have heel spurs, but 61% of these have no symptoms
  • Only 31% of people with visible spurs have pain
  • Treatment is identical whether there's a spur or not

Ankle Sprains and Chronic Instability

19-27 per 1000/year

Go to full article on ankle sprains

One of the most common musculoskeletal injuries. Annual incidence of 19-27 per 1000 people. 85% affect the lateral (outer) ligaments. Among athletes, the incidence is even higher - up to 11.55 per 1000 training sessions.

Serious complication: 40% develop chronic ankle instability within 12 months after first-time sprain if rehabilitation isn't done properly. Among athletes in high-risk sports, the numbers are even higher - up to 75% in gymnastics and trampoline.

Achilles Tendinopathy

6% of active people

Go to full article on Achilles tendinopathy

The body's thickest and strongest tendon. Affects approx. 6% of physically active people. Lifetime risk for elite runners can be as high as 52%. Important: 65% of cases occur independently of sports activity.

Treatment with best evidence: Eccentric training (Alfredson protocol: 180 repetitions daily for 12 weeks) or Heavy Slow Resistance (3 sessions/week). Both give equally good results, but HSR has higher patient satisfaction with 92% completing vs 78% for Alfredson.

Prognosis: 6-12 months is a realistic timeframe. Cortisone injections should be avoided - increase risk of tendon rupture.

Evidence for Manual Treatment

A systematic review from 2014 found that manual treatment combined with exercises gives significantly better results than exercises alone for ankle sprains - with improvement in dorsiflexion of 6.8 degrees that persists at 6-month follow-up. For plantar fasciopathy, a 2012 review showed moderate evidence that manual therapy combined with stretching gives better short-term results.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Activity and targeted exercises are often the most important treatment for foot problems. Here are evidence-based exercises for common conditions:

1. Calf Stretching (Plantar Fasciopathy)

Stand with your forefoot on a step, letting the heel hang over the edge. Lower slowly until you feel a stretch in the calf and Achilles. Hold 30-60 seconds. Repeat 3 times on each side. Do twice daily.

2. Eccentric Heel Raises (Achilles Tendinopathy)

Stand on tiptoes with both feet. Lift one foot and lower slowly on the affected side until the heel is below the step. Use the healthy leg to push up again. 3 sets of 15 repetitions, twice daily for 12 weeks.

3. Balance Training (Ankle Instability)

Stand on one leg with eyes open. Progress to: eyes closed, unstable surface, catching/throwing a ball. 3 sets of 30 seconds on each leg, daily. Reduces re-injury risk by 35%.

4. Towel Scrunches (General Foot Strength)

Sit with a towel under your foot. Use your toes to scrunch the towel towards you. Repeat 10-15 times, 2-3 sets. Strengthens the intrinsic muscles of the foot.

The Key: Optimal Loading

The goal is "optimal loading": enough load to stimulate healing, but not so much that you worsen the injury. Complete rest is rarely the solution. I provide specific advice tailored to your situation.

Red Flags - When You Must See a Doctor

Seek Medical Attention IMMEDIATELY If:

  • Fever combined with warm, red and swollen foot (signs of infection)
  • Inability to bear weight on the foot after injury (possible fracture - Ottawa rules)
  • Sudden severe pain with discolouration of the foot (vascular)
  • Numbness in the lower extremities combined with bladder/bowel problems

Seek Professional Help If:

  • Pain that wakes you at night without load
  • Unexplained weight loss together with foot pain
  • Pain persisting over 2-3 weeks without improvement
  • Recurring sprains or instability
  • Visible deformity or change in foot position

Other Common Conditions

Metatarsalgia (Forefoot Pain)

5-36% prevalence

Go to full article on metatarsalgia

Collective term for pain in the forefoot. Accounts for 85% of all foot pain in middle-aged women.

Most common causes: Mechanical overload (most common), wrong footwear (high heels shift 70-80% of weight to forefoot), foot deformities like hallux valgus (5.4x increased risk), high arch.

Prognosis: 80-85% improve with conservative treatment within 6-8 weeks.

Hallux Valgus ("Bunion")

19% prevalence

Go to full article on hallux valgus

The big toe angles towards the other toes, and the joint protrudes on the inside. Affects approx. 19% of the population, increasing to 36% over 65 years. Women are affected 2-3 times more often.

Treatment: Conservative treatment focuses on pain relief: shoes with wide toe box, toe spreaders, strength exercises for foot muscles. Surgery is only considered with significant pain and functional impairment.

Morton's Neuroma

5-8x women

Go to full article on Morton's neuroma

Thickening of the interdigital nerve, typically between 3rd and 4th toe. Women are affected 5-8 times more often. Accounts for approx. 40% of metatarsalgia diagnoses.

Treatment: Stepwise: first conservative (wide toe box, metatarsal pads) for 3-6 months. Cortisone injections if conservative treatment doesn't help (82% satisfied). Surgery as last resort.

Hallux Rigidus (Stiff Big Toe)

2.5% over 50

Go to full article on hallux rigidus

Osteoarthritis in the big toe's base joint leading to stiffness, pain and reduced mobility. Affects approx. 2.5% of the population over 50, and up to 35-60% over 65 have some degree of stiffness.

Treatment: Conservative treatment with stiff insoles or "rocker bottom" shoes that reduce the need for toe bend during walking. Joint mobilisation and exercises.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a referral?

No. Chiropractors have primary contact status in Norway. You can book an appointment directly without a referral from a doctor.

Can you refer me onwards?

Yes. I can refer to X-ray, MRI, CT and ultrasound covered by National Insurance. I can also refer to physiotherapy and specialists (orthopaedic surgeon, rheumatologist, neurologist).

Can I get a sick note?

Yes, chiropractors with documented competence can issue sick notes for up to 12 weeks.

How long does it take to recover?

It varies by condition: Ankle sprain grade I-II: 1-6 weeks. Plantar fasciopathy: 6-12 weeks active treatment (80-90% better within 12 months). Metatarsalgia: 6-8 weeks. Achilles tendinopathy: 6-12 months. Morton's neuroma: 3-6 months with conservative treatment.

Can I train while in treatment?

Often yes - The goal is "optimal loading": enough load to stimulate healing, but not so much that you worsen the injury. Complete rest is rarely the solution. I give specific advice tailored to your situation.

Do insoles help?

Can provide relief for some, but evidence is moderate. Important: prefabricated insoles work just as well as expensive custom-made ones for plantar fasciopathy. Stretching and strength training have better documentation.
