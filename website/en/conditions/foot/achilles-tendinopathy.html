Achilles Tendinopathy

Everything you need to know about Achilles tendon pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

The Achilles tendon is the body's strongest and thickest tendon. It connects the calf muscles to the heel bone and is subjected to enormous forces - up to 12 times body weight during running. This is one of the most common foot and ankle conditions we treat.

Previously, the condition was often called "Achilles tendinitis" (with -itis meaning inflammation), but modern research shows that it is primarily degenerative changes in the tendon tissue. Therefore, the more precise term tendinopathy is now used - the same terminology is used for plantar fasciopathy for the same reason.

Summary

  • What it is: Degenerative condition in the Achilles tendon - not inflammation
  • Incidence: Affects about 6% of physically active people, most common 30-60 years
  • Prognosis: 75-80% improve with exercise-based treatment
  • Red flags: Sudden snap, inability to stand on toes, visible dent in tendon
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

When must you seek help immediately?

With a sudden snap in the tendon, inability to stand on toes, or visible dent/gap in the tendon - seek medical attention immediately.

1

What is Achilles Tendinopathy?

The condition is divided into two main types based on where in the tendon the problem is located:

Mid-portion tendinopathy

2-6 cm above the heel attachment. Accounts for about 64% of cases and is most common in runners and athletes.

Insertional tendinopathy

At the attachment to the heel bone. Accounts for about 28% of cases and is more often associated with heel spur (Haglund's deformity). Can be confused with plantar fasciopathy which also causes heel pain.

6% of physically active people
52% lifetime risk for elite runners
65% occurs outside of sports
30-60 most common age group

Important to know

65% of cases occur independently of sports activity (de Jonge 2011). This means it's not just a "sports injury". Prevalence increases from 2% under 18 years to 8% over 45 years.

2

Symptoms

The classic symptom picture in Achilles tendinopathy is quite characteristic:

  • Morning stiffness - stiffness and discomfort in the tendon for the first few minutes after getting up, which gradually decreases with movement
  • "Warm-up phenomenon" - pain when starting activity that decreases after a few minutes, then returns after rest
  • Localized tenderness - when palpating the tendon, either 2-6 cm above the heel (mid-portion) or at the attachment
  • Pain with loading - especially with heel raises, stair climbing, running or jumping
  • Possible swelling - visible thickening of the tendon in some cases

Important distinction

Symptoms typically develop gradually over weeks to months. Sudden onset of pain with an audible "snap" indicates rupture and requires acute evaluation.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Achilles tendinopathy occurs when the load on the tendon exceeds its adaptation capacity over time.

Risk factors ranked by strength of evidence

  • Sudden increase in training load - the clearly most common triggering factor. Classic example: From couch potato to half marathon in 8 weeks
  • Age over 30 years - prevalence increases from 2% under 18 years to 8% over 45 years
  • Previous tendon injury - significantly increased risk of new episodes
  • Overweight (BMI > 25) - moderate evidence for association. Overweight is also a risk factor for other foot problems like metatarsalgia
  • Metabolic conditions - diabetes and high cholesterol negatively affect tendon health
  • Fluoroquinolone antibiotics - increase the risk of tendon problems by a factor of 3.14. Especially important in patients over 60 years

Gender differences

Research shows no significant difference between men and women when it comes to tendinopathy. This contrasts with Achilles tendon rupture, where men dominate with a ratio of 3.5-5.5:1.

4

Red Flags - When You Must See a Doctor

ACUTE WARNING SIGNS - See doctor/emergency room:

  • Sudden, severe pain with audible "snap" - classic sign of Achilles tendon rupture
  • Unable to stand on toes or walk normally on the affected side
  • Visible dent or gap in the tendon - indicates partial or complete rupture
  • Severe swelling and bruising that develops rapidly
  • Recent use of fluoroquinolone antibiotics (Ciprofloxacin, etc.) - increased rupture risk

The Thompson test (squeezing the calf while lying on stomach) has 96% sensitivity for detecting complete rupture. 20-25% of Achilles ruptures are initially misdiagnosed as ankle sprain.

5

Treatment

The cornerstone of Achilles tendinopathy treatment is progressive loading training. This may sound counterintuitive - why load a painful tendon? However, the research is clear: tendons need controlled loading to adapt and become stronger.

What can the chiropractor do?

1
Exercise program guidance

Correct execution of eccentric training or Heavy Slow Resistance is crucial

2
Load management

Finding the right balance between activity and rest, with gradual progression

3
Soft tissue treatment

Treatment of calf muscles and surrounding structures as supplement

4
Joint mobilization

Assessment and treatment of ankle joint with reduced mobility

5
Shockwave therapy (ESWT)

May be considered for chronic cases, especially in combination with exercises

The two gold standard protocols

1. Alfredson Eccentric Protocol (Sweden, 1998)

Eccentric heel drops from a stair edge:

  • 3 sets x 15 repetitions, with both straight and bent knee
  • Twice daily (total 180 repetitions)
  • Exercises performed in the pain zone (0-5/10)
  • Weight increased gradually

60% success after 4 months vs 24% for "wait and see" (Rompe 2007)

2. Heavy Slow Resistance (Denmark, 2015)

Three exercises with heavy loading:

  • Seated heel raise, leg press heel raise, standing heel raise
  • 3 times per week
  • Slow movements (3 sec up, 3 sec down)
  • Progression from 15RM to 6RM over 12 weeks

Equally good results as eccentric, but with better compliance (92% vs 78%) due to fewer training sessions.

What can you do yourself?

  • Start exercise program early: The sooner you begin structured training, the better
  • Train through moderate pain: Pain up to 5/10 during exercises is acceptable and actually desirable. Avoid pain that worsens the next day
  • Be patient: Full recovery often takes 3-6 months, sometimes longer
  • Adapt activity level: You rarely need complete rest, but may need to temporarily reduce intensity

Avoid cortisone injections

Cortisone injections are not recommended for chronic Achilles tendinopathy due to lack of effect and increased risk of tendon rupture.

6

Prognosis and Expected Course

The good news: Conservative treatment works for most people. 75-80% of patients with Achilles tendinopathy achieve good improvement without surgery.

84% return to full activity level
94% symptom-free or only mild symptoms

Typical time course

Acute cases (< 6 weeks)

Faster response, often improvement within 6-12 weeks

Subacute cases (6-12 weeks)

Expect 3-4 months for significant improvement

Chronic cases (> 3 months)

3-6 months with consistent training, sometimes up to 1 year

About recurrence

Among athletes who return early to activity, 27-44% experience new episodes. 41% also develop symptoms in the originally healthy tendon within 8 years - which underscores the importance of preventive training on both sides.

For the approximately 25-29% who don't respond adequately to conservative treatment, surgery may be considered after a minimum of 6 months. Surgical results are generally good, with 80-90% success rate.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I continue to exercise with Achilles tendinopathy?
Yes, in most cases. Silbernagel's pain monitoring model (2007) shows that continued activity is safe as long as pain stays below 5/10 during exercise and doesn't worsen the next day. You may need to adjust type, duration or intensity temporarily.
Is there inflammation in my tendon?
No, despite many calling it "tendinitis". Modern research shows that Achilles tendinopathy is primarily a degenerative process with changes in tendon structure, not classic inflammation. This is also why anti-inflammatory medications have limited effect.
Do I need an MRI or ultrasound?
Rarely. The diagnosis is made clinically, and imaging usually doesn't change the treatment plan. Important to know: 46% of symptom-free runners have tendon changes on ultrasound. Imaging is primarily done when rupture is suspected, for uncertain diagnoses, or for surgical evaluation.
Do heel lifts or orthotic insoles help?
The evidence is limited. Heel lifts can provide temporary pain relief by reducing the stretch on the tendon, but should be viewed as a supplement to - not a replacement for - exercise-based treatment.
How long until I recover?
It depends on duration and severity. Acute cases (< 6 weeks) can improve in 6-12 weeks, while chronic cases often require 3-6 months - sometimes up to a year. The key is patience and consistent completion of the exercise program.
Is shockwave therapy effective?
The evidence is mixed. ESWT appears to be most effective in combination with exercises, especially for insertional tendinopathy and chronic cases that haven't responded to exercises alone.
