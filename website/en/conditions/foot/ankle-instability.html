Chronic Ankle Instability

When the ankle keeps giving way | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You may have rolled your ankle several times, and each time it takes longer to recover. The ankle feels unstable, and you don't trust it on uneven terrain or during quick direction changes. This is chronic ankle instability - a common but often undertreated condition that falls under foot and ankle conditions.

20-40% of everyone who gets an ankle sprain develops chronic instability. The good news is that most people improve significantly with proper rehabilitation.

Summary

  • Incidence: 20-40% develop chronic instability after ankle sprain
  • Main symptom: Ankle "gives way" repeatedly
  • Cause: Damaged proprioception + weak stabilizers
  • Treatment: Balance training is the gold standard - 85% improve
  • Surgery: Only considered after failed rehabilitation (6+ months)

What is chronic ankle instability?

Chronic ankle instability (CAI) is defined as repeated episodes where the ankle "gives way," persistent feeling of instability, and/or repeated sprains - at least 12 months after the original injury.

Mechanical Instability

Loose ligaments that allow abnormal movement in the ankle joint. Detected by clinical tests such as anterior drawer and talar tilt.

Functional Instability (most common)

Damaged proprioception (position sense), delayed muscle reaction and reduced neuromuscular control. Can occur without mechanical laxity.

20-40% develop CAI
3x increased risk of new injury
85% improve with training
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Chronic ankle instability usually develops after one or more ankle sprains that have not healed optimally. The condition can also occur in connection with other foot problems such as Achilles tendinopathy or tibialis posterior tendinopathy.

What happens in the ankle?

  • Damaged proprioception: The receptors in ligaments and joint capsule that register position and movement function poorly
  • Delayed muscle reaction: The peroneal muscles (on the outside of the lower leg) don't react quickly enough when off balance
  • Weak muscles: Reduced strength in stabilizing muscles
  • Altered movement patterns: Compensatory gait patterns and posture

Risk Factors for Developing CAI

  • Inadequate rehabilitation after first sprain
  • Too early return to activity
  • Previous sprains (risk increases with each injury)
  • High BMI
  • General hypermobility (overly flexible joints)
  • Sports with quick direction changes
3

Symptoms

The most common symptoms of chronic ankle instability:

  • "Giving way": The ankle suddenly gives out, especially on uneven surfaces
  • Feeling of instability: You don't trust the ankle and avoid certain activities
  • Repeated sprains: New rolling incidents even with little provocation
  • Persistent pain: Often diffuse pain on the outside of the ankle (see also stress fracture in the foot as a differential diagnosis)
  • Swelling: Episodic swelling after activity
  • Stiffness: Especially in the morning or after rest

When should you seek help?

  • The ankle gives way more than 2 times per month
  • You avoid activities you previously enjoyed
  • Pain or swelling that doesn't resolve
  • Locking or clicking in the ankle joint
  • Numbness or weakness in the foot
4

Treatment

Conservative treatment is the first choice and gives good results in most people.

Evidence-Based Treatment

  • Balance training: Gold standard - rebuilds proprioception and neuromuscular control
  • Strength training: Focus on peroneal and calf muscles
  • Functional training: Sport-specific exercises with increasing difficulty
  • Manual therapy: Mobilization of stiff joints, soft tissue treatment
  • Taping/orthoses: Can provide extra support during the rehabilitation phase

Home Exercise: Single-leg Balance

  1. Stand on the affected leg with a slight bend in the knee
  2. Hold balance for 30 seconds
  3. Progression: Close your eyes, stand on a pillow, throw a ball
  4. Do 3 sets, 2-3 times daily

Tip: Start near a wall for safety. The goal is to make it challenging but achievable.

When is surgery considered?

Surgery is only considered after at least 6 months of failed conservative treatment. The most common procedures are:

  • Brostrom procedure: Reconstruction of lateral ligaments
  • Anatomical reconstruction: For severe instability
5

Prognosis and Prevention

With proper rehabilitation, most people have a good prognosis:

  • 85% improve significantly with structured training over 6-12 weeks
  • Many can return to full activity including sports
  • Maintenance training is important to prevent recurrence

Prevention of New Sprains

  • Continue balance training as maintenance (2-3x per week)
  • Use ankle support for high-risk sports for the first 6-12 months
  • Warm up thoroughly before activity
  • Strengthen the entire lower extremity
  • Use proper footwear for the activity - wrong footwear can contribute to plantar fasciopathy or metatarsalgia
6

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I exercise with chronic ankle instability?
Yes, in fact exercise is the main treatment! Avoid activities that provoke "giving way" initially, but balance training and strength training are essential for improvement.
Do I need an MRI or X-ray?
Usually not. The diagnosis is primarily clinical. Imaging may be relevant if other injury is suspected (cartilage damage, bone marrow edema) or before possible surgery.
Does ankle support/orthosis help?
Ankle support can provide security and reduce the risk of new sprains, especially during sports. But it doesn't replace training - use it as a supplement during rehabilitation.
How long does rehabilitation take?
Most people notice improvement after 4-6 weeks of structured training. Full rehabilitation often takes 3-6 months. Maintenance training is recommended long-term.
