Ankle Sprains

Everything you need to know about treatment and rehabilitation | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

An ankle sprain (also called a twisted ankle or ankle distortion) occurs when the ligaments in the ankle are stretched or torn. This typically happens when the foot rolls inward, damaging the lateral (outer) ligaments. This is one of the most common foot and ankle conditions we treat.

With an annual incidence of 19-27 per 1000 people, ankle sprains are one of the most common musculoskeletal injuries in the population. Among athletes, the incidence is even higher - up to 11.55 per 1000 training sessions. Without proper rehabilitation, this can lead to chronic ankle instability.

Summary

  • What it is: Damage to the ligaments in the ankle, most often on the outside
  • Incidence: 19-27 per 1000 people annually - one of the most common injuries
  • Treatment: POLICE protocol + functional rehabilitation
  • Critical: 40% develop chronic instability without proper rehabilitation
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

Anatomy and Incidence

The ankle has three main ligaments on the outside:

  • ATFL (anterior talofibular ligament) - injured in 65-85% of cases, the weakest ligament
  • CFL (calcaneofibular ligament) - involved in 20-40% of injuries
  • PTFL (posterior talofibular ligament) - strongest ligament, rarely injured

Injury to the medial deltoid ligament accounts for only 3% of all ankle sprains.

Who is most affected?

  • Young adults (15-24 years): Highest incidence with 7.2 per 1000 in the 15-19 age group
  • Basketball players: Highest risk among all sports - accounts for 41% of sports-related sprains
  • People with previous ankle injury: 3.5 times increased risk of new injury

Recurrence is common

12-47% of all ankle sprains are recurrent injuries. In volleyball, recurrence is seen in up to 46% of athletes.

2

Injury Grading

Ankle sprains are classified into three grades based on severity:

Grade I (mild)

Mild stretching, minimal swelling, can bear weight
1-3 weeks healing

Grade II (moderate)

Partial tear, moderate swelling, reduced stability
3-6 weeks healing

Grade III (severe)

Complete tear, significant instability
6-12 weeks or more

Diagnosis

Clinical examination is the cornerstone. Ottawa Ankle Rules have 97.6% sensitivity for detecting fractures and are used to determine if X-ray is necessary. This approach reduces unnecessary X-rays by 30-40%.

X-ray is only indicated with bone tenderness along the posterior edge of the ankle bones or inability to take four steps. With persistent pain after a sprain, stress fracture should be ruled out.

3

Treatment: The POLICE Protocol

In 2012, Bleakley and colleagues published a paradigm shift in acute treatment: The POLICE protocol replaces traditional RICE. The main difference is that "rest" is replaced with "protection" and "optimal loading".

A randomized study from 2023 showed that POLICE provides faster and better functional recovery than PRICE.

The POLICE Protocol

P

Protection

Use elastic bandage or aircast for support

OL

Optimal Loading

Gentle movement within pain limits from day 1

I
Ice

15-20 minutes with ice pack in towel, 3-4 times daily

C
Compression

Elastic bandage (not too tight)

E
Elevation

Keep the foot higher than heart level when resting

What can the chiropractor do?

Manual therapy and joint mobilization have moderate evidence for ankle sprains. Systematic reviews show improved dorsiflexion and reduced pain perception.

  • Assess the severity of the injury
  • Rule out fractures using Ottawa Ankle Rules
  • Provide manual treatment to improve mobility
  • Guide through a tailored rehabilitation program
4

Rehabilitation: Three Phases

1 Mobility and Pain Control (week 1)

  • Ankle alphabet: Write the letters A-Z with your foot, 3-4 times daily
  • Ankle rotations: 10-15 circles in each direction
  • Isometric exercises: Press the foot against a wall without movement, hold 5-10 seconds

2 Strength and Proprioception (weeks 2-6)

  • Resistance band exercises: In all directions, 3 sets x 15 repetitions
  • Single-leg balance: Start with 20 seconds, gradually increase to 60 seconds
  • Heel raises: 3 sets x 15 repetitions

3 Functional Training (week 6+)

  • Balance on unstable surface (BOSU ball, balance pad)
  • Side movements and lateral shuffle
  • Gradual return to running and sport-specific training

Important: Balance training prevents recurrence

Proprioceptive training reduces the risk of new sprains by 35% according to a meta-analysis of 7 studies with 3726 participants. Continue with balance exercises even after the pain is gone.

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention for:

  • Unable to bear weight on the foot at all
  • Severe swelling that increases after 24-48 hours
  • Visible deformity or misalignment in the ankle
  • Numbness, tingling or cold sensation in the foot
  • Pain directly over bone (not just soft tissue)
  • Persistent instability or recurrent sprains
6

Prognosis

With proper rehabilitation, most ankle sprains have a good prognosis. Approximately 90% of grade III injuries achieve good results with conservative treatment. Surgery is reserved for persistent instability after 3-6 months of failed conservative treatment.

Chronic Ankle Instability (CAI)

Without adequate rehabilitation, 40% of patients develop chronic ankle instability within 12 months of the first sprain. CAI is characterized by recurrent sprains, giving way episodes, and persistent functional impairment.

Prevention of Recurrence

  • Balance training: 35% reduction in risk of new sprains
  • Ankle brace or taping: 69-71% reduction in previously injured athletes
  • Continue with strength and balance exercises even after the pain is gone

Long-term Perspective

At 1-4 year follow-up, 74% have at least one residual symptom. Post-traumatic ankle osteoarthritis is a serious long-term complication - lateral ankle sprains are the main cause of ligamentous ankle osteoarthritis. Long-term loading abnormalities can also lead to secondary problems such as tibialis posterior tendinopathy.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for an ankle to heal?
Grade I: 1-3 weeks. Grade II: 3-6 weeks. Grade III: 6-12 weeks or more. Healing time depends on the severity of the injury and how consistently you follow the rehabilitation program.
Should I get an X-ray or MRI?
In most cases, imaging is not necessary. Ottawa Ankle Rules have 97.6% sensitivity for detecting fractures. X-rays are only taken if there is bone tenderness over specific points or inability to walk four steps.
Can I exercise with a sprained ankle?
Modern treatment (POLICE protocol) recommends early, gentle movement within pain limits from day 1. Complete rest is no longer recommended. You can often continue with activities that don't load the ankle, such as cycling or swimming, relatively early in the process.
How do I prevent new sprains?
Balance training provides 35% reduction in risk of new sprains. Ankle braces or taping provide 69-71% reduction in previously injured athletes. Continue with strength and balance exercises even after the pain is gone.
Do I need a referral to see a chiropractor?
No, you don't need a referral. Chiropractors in Norway have had primary contact status since 2006, and you can book an appointment directly. If needed, the chiropractor can refer you for X-rays, specialist consultations, or other examinations.
When should I seek help?
There's no reason to wait. Early assessment and treatment can contribute to faster healing and reduced risk of chronic problems. If you're unsure about the severity of the injury, it's better to get an evaluation.
