Foot Exercises That Work: Exercises for Stronger Feet

Evidence-based protocols | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Your feet contain 26 bones, 33 joints, and over 100 muscles, tendons, and ligaments. Yet most exercise programs completely ignore the foot. The result? Weak feet compensated for with stiff shoes and orthotics.

The good news: Research shows that targeted foot training can increase foot strength by up to 57%, reduce injuries by more than half, and relieve pain better than traditional treatments. And the best part is that the exercises are simple and can be done at home.

Summary

  • Key message: Strong feet are the best prevention against foot problems
  • Evidence: 57% strength increase and 2.4x fewer injuries with foot training
  • Key exercise: Short foot is the foundation for all foot training
  • Time investment: 10-15 minutes daily yields noticeable results

Why Foot Training?

Foot training is not just for athletes or those with existing problems. Research shows impressive results for everyone who invests time in their feet:

57% strength increase in 6 months
2.4x fewer running injuries
60% pain reduction

Strong feet provide better balance, more efficient walking function, and reduce strain up the kinetic chain to the knees, hips, and back. The foot is the foundation for the entire body - and like any foundation, it functions best when it is strong and stable.

The Foot's Important Role

The arch functions as a natural spring that stores 8-17% of mechanical energy with each step. This energy is released at push-off and makes movement more efficient. Weak feet lose this function - like driving a car with flat tires.

Exercise 1: Short Foot

Short foot is the most fundamental exercise for foot health. It activates the intrinsic muscles that support the arch - muscles that are often "switched off" after years in supportive shoes.

How to Perform Short Foot

  1. Starting position: Sit on a chair with your foot flat on the floor
  2. Movement: Draw the forefoot toward the heel by "shortening" the foot
  3. Focus: Lift the arch without curling the toes
  4. Hold: 5 seconds
  5. Repetitions: 10-15 reps
  6. Sets: 2-3 sets per foot

Progression:

  1. Seated with both feet
  2. Standing with both feet
  3. Standing on one leg
  4. Standing on unstable surface (cushion, balance board)

Important Tip

Many try to "cheat" by curling their toes. The toes should lie flat on the floor the entire time - it's the arch muscles that should do the work. Imagine trying to grip the floor with the middle of your foot.

4

Exercise 2: Toe Spreading

After years in narrow shoes, many have lost the ability to actively spread their toes. Toe spreading reactivates the abductor muscles and improves forefoot stability.

How to Perform Toe Spreading

  1. Starting position: Sit or stand with your foot flat on the floor
  2. Movement: Spread the toes as far apart as possible
  3. Focus: Especially try to spread the big toe away from the others
  4. Hold: 5-10 seconds
  5. Repetitions: 10-15 reps
  6. Sets: 2-3 sets per foot

Tip: Use Toe Spacers

If you struggle to spread your toes, toe spacers can help. Use them passively while sitting or sleeping to gradually regain toe mobility. Start with 15-30 minutes and increase gradually.

5

Exercise 3: Towel Curls

A classic exercise that strengthens the flexor muscles under the foot. This exercise is particularly useful for those with plantar fasciitis.

How to Perform Towel Curls

  1. Starting position: Sit with your foot on a towel on the floor
  2. Movement: Use your toes to pull the towel toward you
  3. Focus: Grip with all toes, including the big toe
  4. Repetitions: Pull in the entire towel, 2-3 times
  5. Variation: Pick up marbles or small objects with your toes

Marble Variation

Place 10-20 marbles (or other small objects) on the floor and pick them up one by one with your toes. This trains both grip and precision. A good variation to keep training interesting.

6

Exercise 4: Focused Heel Raises

Heel raises (calf raises) are a well-known exercise, but with proper focus they become powerful foot training. The key is to keep the arch active throughout the movement.

How to Perform Focused Heel Raises

  1. Starting position: Stand with feet hip-width apart
  2. Preparation: Activate the short foot position first
  3. Movement: Slowly lift the heels while keeping the arch active
  4. Top position: Hold 2-3 seconds at the top
  5. Lowering: Lower down in a controlled manner (3-4 seconds)
  6. Repetitions: 10-15 reps, 2-3 sets

Progression:

  1. Two legs on flat floor
  2. Two legs on stair edge (full range)
  3. One leg on flat floor
  4. One leg on stair edge

Avoid This Mistake

Many "roll out" over the little toe at the top of the movement. Keep weight evenly distributed and focus on pressing up through the big toe. The ankle should be stable, not tilting in or out.

7

Exercise 5: Single-Leg Balance

Functional training that dynamically challenges the entire foot. This exercise bridges the gap between isolated foot exercises and daily function.

Four Levels of Single-Leg Balance

  1. Level 1: Stand on one leg with eyes open, 30-60 seconds per side
  2. Level 2: Stand on one leg with eyes closed, 30-60 seconds per side
  3. Level 3: Stand on one leg on unstable surface (cushion, foam mat), 30-60 seconds
  4. Level 4: Stand on one leg while performing movements with arms or the free leg

Functional Challenge

Try doing daily activities on one leg: brush your teeth, wait for the bus, stand in line. Every opportunity to challenge balance is free foot training.

8

Suggested Weekly Program

Here is a simple program you can follow. Adjust according to your level and available time.

Day Exercises
Monday, Wednesday, Friday Short foot: 3 x 15 reps
Toe spreading: 3 x 15 reps
Heel raises: 3 x 12 reps
Tuesday, Thursday Towel curls: 3 rounds
Single-leg balance: 3 x 30-60 sec per side
Every day 5-10 min barefoot walking indoors
Short foot activation when standing

Tips for Implementation

Link foot training to existing routines: do short foot while brushing your teeth, toe spreading while watching TV, balance while waiting for your coffee. Consistency beats intensity.

Barefoot Walking and Minimalist Shoes

The Liverpool study (Curtis 2021) showed that 6 months in minimalist shoes increased foot strength by 57% - without specific exercises. Simply by letting the foot work naturally.

Safe Transition to Minimalist Shoes

An abrupt transition can lead to injuries. Follow this protocol:

  • Week 1-2: 10-15 min per day in minimalist shoes, indoors
  • Week 3-4: 20-30 min per day, short walks outside
  • Week 5-8: Gradual increase to 50% of daily use
  • Week 9-12: Increase to 75% if the foot tolerates it
  • After 3 months: Full transition possible for most people

Important Warning

If you have diabetes, neuropathy, or open wounds, you should NOT walk barefoot without consulting a doctor first. Loss of sensation in the feet means you won't notice injuries.

When Can You Expect Results?

Foot training requires patience, but results come gradually:

Timeline for Results

Time Period Expected Results
2-4 weeks Better awareness and control of the foot. Less fatigue when standing. Noticeable activation of foot muscles.
4-8 weeks Measurable strength gains. Better balance. Pain reduction for many. Toes begin to spread naturally.
3-6 months Structural adaptations in the foot. Up to 57% strength increase documented. Many can reduce or eliminate orthotic use.

Remember

Foot training should be viewed as maintenance - just like brushing your teeth. You don't stop brushing your teeth because they're healthy. Strong feet require continuous stimulus to stay strong.

11

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately for:

  • Sudden, intense foot pain without obvious cause
  • Swelling, redness and warmth combined with fever
  • Numbness or tingling that doesn't go away
  • Foot ulcers that don't heal (especially with diabetes)
  • Significant worsening of pain during or after training
  • Cracking or crunching sounds followed by acute pain

Mild muscle soreness after foot training is normal, especially at the start. But sharp pain, numbness, or swelling are signs that you should take a break and possibly seek evaluation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I train my feet with plantar fasciitis?
Yes, research actually recommends it. Studies show that strength training of the foot is more effective than stretching alone for plantar fasciitis. Start gently and avoid exercises that cause acute pain. The short foot exercise is particularly well documented and recommended as a first choice.
Can I train my feet while using orthotics?
Yes, absolutely. Foot training can be done alongside orthotic use. Many choose to gradually reduce orthotic use as foot strength increases. Consult a professional about an individual plan for tapering. Read more about orthotic tapering here.
How long does it take to see results?
Most notice better control and less fatigue after 2-4 weeks. Measurable strength gains are typically seen after 4-8 weeks. Full structural adaptation and maximum strength gains take 3-6 months of consistent training.
Can foot training replace orthotics?
For many, yes. Research shows that foot training is more effective than orthotics for most non-specific foot problems. Exceptions include diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, and severe structural deformities where orthotics have clear indications.
Is it too late to start foot training?
No. Studies show that even elderly people who have never trained their feet can achieve significant improvements in strength and balance. Japanese studies have shown 40% reduction in fall risk in elderly who regularly train their toe muscles.
How often should I train my feet?
For best results, it's recommended to do something every day - even if it's just 5-10 minutes. Foot muscles are used to working all day with natural walking, so they tolerate high training frequency well.
