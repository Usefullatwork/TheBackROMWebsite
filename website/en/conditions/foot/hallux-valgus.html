Hallux Valgus (Bunion)

Causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Hallux valgus, often called "bunion", is a common deformity where the big toe gradually angles toward the other toes. At the same time, the joint at the base of the toe protrudes on the inside of the foot, forming a visible bump. The condition is one of the most common foot and ankle conditions we treat.

The condition typically develops over many years. It starts with a mild angle change that gradually worsens, especially if the causal factors are not addressed.

Summary

  • What: Big toe angles toward the other toes, joint protrudes on the inside
  • Who: 19% of the population, women 2-3x more often, increases with age
  • Cause: Genetics (63-90%) + wrong footwear over time (tight shoes, high heels)
  • Treatment: Wide toe box, toe spreaders, strengthening exercises, joint mobilization
  • Prognosis: 80-90% achieve good pain relief without surgery

Who is Affected by Hallux Valgus?

Hallux valgus is the most common deformity of the forefoot and affects approximately 19% of the population. Prevalence increases with age to about 36% in those over 65 years.

Women are affected 2-3 times more often than men, probably due to a combination of genetic factors and use of tight shoes and high heels.

19% of population affected
36% prevalence over 65 years
2-3x more common in women
63-90% heritability component
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Hallux valgus is caused by an interplay between genetic factors and external influences. Research shows that heredity plays a significant role, but footwear and loading affect the progression.

Genetics (63-90% heritability)

Twin studies show a strong hereditary component. If parents have hallux valgus, the risk is significantly higher.

Footwear

Tight, pointed shoes and high heels significantly increase risk. High heels shift 70-80% of body weight to the forefoot.

Ligament laxity (hypermobility)

Allows increased movement in the MTP joint that predisposes to deformity.

Flat foot (pes planus)

Overpronation leads to increased loading on the inside of the big toe. See also tibialis posterior tendinopathy which can cause acquired flat foot.

Why are women affected more often?

Women are affected 2-3 times more often than men. This is probably due to a combination of:

  • Higher use of tight shoes and high heels
  • More loose ligaments (hypermobility)
  • Hormonal factors affecting connective tissue
  • Anatomical differences in foot structure

Severity Grading

Hallux valgus is graded by the angle between the big toe and metatarsal bone axis (HVA):

Mild

HVA under 20 degrees
Minimal visible deformity

Moderate

HVA 20-40 degrees
Clear bump visible

Severe

HVA over 40 degrees
Big toe overlaps 2nd toe

3

Symptoms and Characteristics

Many with hallux valgus have no symptoms - it is only when pain or functional problems arise that treatment becomes relevant.

  • Pain over the joint: Typically worst when wearing shoes, better barefoot
  • Redness and swelling: Over the protruding joint, especially with friction from shoes
  • Stiffness in the big toe: Gradually reduced mobility in the MTP joint. With significant stiffness, also consider hallux rigidus
  • Metatarsalgia: Pain under the forefoot due to altered loading pattern
  • Hammer toes: Secondary deformity of 2nd-5th toes because the big toe presses against them
  • Gait changes: Altered foot mechanics during walking

Progressive Condition

Without intervention, hallux valgus tends to gradually worsen. Studies show the angle increases by about 1-2 degrees per year in untreated cases. Early intervention with conservative measures can slow or stop progression.

4

Evidence-Based Treatment

Conservative treatment is the first choice and is effective for most people. The goal is pain relief, slowing progression, and maintaining function.

Gold Standard: Conservative Treatment First

80-90% of patients with hallux valgus achieve satisfactory pain relief with conservative measures. Surgery is reserved for cases with significant pain and functional impairment after failed conservative treatment.

Conservative Measures

1

Proper Footwear

Shoes with wide toe box and low heel. Avoid pointed shoes and heels over 2-3 cm. This is the most important single measure.

2
Toe Spreaders

Provide pain relief and better toe separation during use. Start gradually with short periods. Does not permanently change the deformity but can slow progression.

3
Strengthening Exercises for Foot Muscles

Strengthening of abductor hallucis and intrinsic foot muscles. "Toe yoga", towel gathering with toes, and foot arch activation.

4
Manual Treatment

Joint mobilization of the MTP joint and midfoot. Soft tissue techniques for tight muscles. Can improve mobility and reduce pain.

5
Protective Padding

Gel padding over the protruding joint to reduce friction and pain in shoes.

Exercises for Hallux Valgus

Regular exercises can strengthen the supporting muscles and potentially slow progression:

  • Big toe abduction: Spread the big toe away from the other toes, hold 5 seconds, 10-15 repetitions
  • Towel grip: Grip a towel with the toes and pull it toward you, 3 sets x 10
  • Toe lift: Lift the big toe while keeping the others down, and vice versa
  • Foot arch activation: "Short foot" exercise - draw the forefoot toward the heel without curling the toes
  • Toe spreading: Spread all toes, hold 5 seconds, repeat 15-20 times

When is Surgery Considered?

Surgery is appropriate only after 6-12 months of failed conservative treatment, and only for:

  • Persistent pain affecting daily activities
  • Significant functional impairment (walking difficulties, problems with footwear)
  • Progressive worsening despite conservative measures
  • Secondary problems such as hammer toe or overlapping toes

Surgical Results

With proper indication, 85-90% of operated patients achieve good pain relief and improved function. There are over 150 different surgical techniques - the choice depends on severity and individual factors. Recovery typically takes 6-12 weeks.

5

Warning Signs - When Should You Seek Help?

Seek healthcare for:

  • Rapid worsening of the deformity over a short time
  • Signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, pus or fever
  • Numbness or tingling in the toes that persists
  • Pain that does not respond to rest and pain medication
  • Open sores over the joint (especially important with diabetes)
  • Significant walking difficulties or tendency to fall

With diabetes or reduced blood circulation to the feet, regular follow-up is especially important.

6

Prognosis and Expectations

The prognosis for hallux valgus depends on timing of intervention and consistent implementation of conservative measures.

80-90% achieve good pain relief
1-2 degrees worsening per year without measures
85-90% good relief after surgery
10-15% recurrence after surgery

Key to Success

Consistent use of proper footwear and regular exercises is more important than one-off treatments. Early intervention before the angle becomes too large gives the best results for slowing progression.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can hallux valgus be corrected without surgery?
Conservative treatment cannot permanently correct the deformity itself, but can stop progression and significantly reduce pain. Toe spreaders, proper footwear and strengthening exercises provide 80-90% good relief. Surgery is only necessary for significant pain and functional impairment.
Is hallux valgus hereditary?
Yes, genetics plays a significant role. Studies show 63-90% heritability. If parents have hallux valgus, the risk is significantly higher. But genetic predisposition + wrong footwear over time is the typical pattern - genetics predisposes, but environmental factors trigger.
Do toe spreaders help with hallux valgus?
Toe spreaders can provide pain relief and better toe separation during use, but do not permanently change the underlying deformity. They are most useful combined with strengthening exercises and proper footwear. Use them gradually - start with short periods and increase over time.
When should I consider surgery for hallux valgus?
Surgery is considered only after 6-12 months of failed conservative treatment, and only for persistent pain affecting daily activities, significant functional impairment, progressive worsening, or secondary problems such as hammer toe. 85-90% achieve good relief after surgery.
Can I continue to exercise with hallux valgus?
Yes, exercise is often beneficial. Choose shoes with a wide toe box. Avoid high heels when running. Balance training and foot arch exercises can strengthen the supporting muscles. For acute pain, modify the activity temporarily, but avoid prolonged inactivity.
