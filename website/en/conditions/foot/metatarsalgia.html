Metatarsalgia

Forefoot pain - causes and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Do you feel a burning, aching pain under the forefoot when walking? Does it feel like you're walking on pebbles? These are typical signs of metatarsalgia - one of many foot and ankle conditions we treat.

Metatarsalgia is one of the most common causes of forefoot pain and affects everyone from runners to those who work standing for long periods. The good news is that most people improve significantly with conservative treatment.

Summary

  • What it is: Pain under the ball of the foot (metatarsal heads)
  • Most common cause: Overuse from improper footwear or biomechanics
  • Typical pain: Burning, aching - worse when walking
  • Treatment: Offloading, insoles, exercises - 90%+ improve
  • Prevention: Proper footwear, gradual increase in activity

What is metatarsalgia?

Metatarsalgia is not a specific diagnosis, but a collective term for pain located in the forefoot - the area under the metatarsal heads (ball of the foot). This is where the long midfoot bones (metatarsals) meet the toes.

The pain usually occurs when this area becomes overloaded. Over time, this can lead to soft tissue irritation, joint capsule inflammation, or stress reactions in the bone.

2nd & 3rd metatarsal most often affected
Women affected more often than men
90%+ improve conservatively
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Metatarsalgia develops when the forefoot is exposed to abnormally high or prolonged loading.

Common Causes

Improper FootwearMost common cause

High-heeled shoes, too tight shoes, or shoes without adequate cushioning significantly increase the load on the forefoot.

Biomechanical Factors

High arch (pes cavus), stiff ankle, long 2nd toe (Morton's toe), or hypermobile joints can lead to uneven load distribution.

Overuse

Too rapid increase in activity, prolonged standing work, or intensive running training can overload the forefoot.

Hallux ValgusOften concurrent

A crooked big toe causes weight to shift to the 2nd and 3rd metatarsal, which become overloaded. Hallux rigidus can cause similar loading changes.

Risk Factors

  • Overweight (increased load per step)
  • Age over 50 (thinner fat pads under the foot)
  • High heels (shift weight forward)
  • Diabetes (altered sensation and blood flow)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Sports with lots of running and jumping - can also lead to Achilles tendinopathy or stress fracture
3

Symptoms

Symptoms of metatarsalgia are often characteristic:

  • Burning or aching pain: Localized to the area under the ball of the foot
  • Pebble in shoe feeling: Like you're walking on something hard
  • Worsening with activity: Worse when walking, running, standing
  • Improvement with rest: Better when sitting or lying down
  • Swelling: May have mild swelling in the forefoot
  • Numbness or tingling: With concurrent nerve irritation

Differential Diagnoses

Condition Characteristics
Morton's Neuroma Burning pain between 3rd and 4th toe, often with numbness
Stress Fracture Point tenderness over bone, worse with percussion
Freiberg's Disease Avascular necrosis, most common in young girls
Plantar Plate Rupture Pain at MTP joint, often after hyperextension

Treatment

Conservative treatment is very effective for most people with metatarsalgia.

First-Line Treatment

  • Footwear: Shoes with wide toe box, low heel and good cushioning
  • Insoles: Metatarsal pad that offloads the forefoot
  • Activity modification: Reduce provoking activities temporarily
  • Ice: For swelling or after activity

Treatment at Chiropractor

1. Biomechanical Assessment

Examination of foot biomechanics, gait pattern and possible contributing factors higher up in the kinetic chain. Tibialis posterior tendinopathy or ankle instability can affect forefoot loading.

2. Manual Treatment

Mobilization of stiff joints in the forefoot, soft tissue treatment of tight muscles and fascia.

3. ExercisesImportant

Strengthening of intrinsic foot muscles, calf stretching, and balance training.

Home Exercise: Toe Spreading

  1. Sit with your foot flat on the floor
  2. Try to spread your toes apart like a fan
  3. Hold for 5 seconds, release, repeat
  4. Do 10-15 repetitions, 2-3 times daily

Tip: This strengthens the small muscles in the foot that help support the forefoot arch.

When Should You Seek Help?

  • Pain persists more than 2-3 weeks
  • Numbness or burning sensations
  • Visible swelling or discoloration
  • Pain that wakes you at night
  • Difficulty walking normally
5

Prognosis and Prevention

With proper treatment, the vast majority with metatarsalgia have a good prognosis. Over 90% improve with conservative treatment - similar success rates are seen with plantar fasciopathy.

Expected Course

  • Mild cases: Improvement within 2-4 weeks with offloading
  • Moderate cases: 6-12 weeks with structured treatment
  • Long-standing cases: May take 3-6 months, but usually improves

Prevention

  • Proper footwear: Wide toe box, low heel, good cushioning
  • Gradual progression: Don't increase activity too quickly
  • Maintain weight: Less load per step
  • Strengthen feet: Regular exercises for foot muscles
  • Variety: Avoid the same loading day after day
6

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I exercise with metatarsalgia?
Yes, but avoid activities that provoke the pain. Swimming and cycling are often well tolerated. For running: consider surface, shoes, and reduce distance until pain subsides.
Do insoles help?
Yes, insoles with a metatarsal pad can significantly offload the forefoot. The pad is placed behind (proximal to) the metatarsal heads to lift them and reduce pressure.
Do I have to stop wearing high heels?
High heels shift weight forward and dramatically increase the load on the forefoot. Limit use, especially during prolonged standing or walking. Consider wider heels rather than stilettos.
Do I need X-ray or MRI?
Usually not for typical metatarsalgia. Imaging may be relevant if stress fracture, neuroma, or if treatment doesn't help after 6-8 weeks.
Can metatarsalgia come back?
Yes, if the causative factors are not corrected. Continue with good shoes, maintain foot muscles with exercises, and avoid sudden increases in activity.
