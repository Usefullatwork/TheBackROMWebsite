Minimalist Shoes and Barefoot Training: What Does the Research Say?

Evidence-based review of footwear and foot health | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Humans have walked barefoot or in minimal sandals for millions of years. Modern cushioned shoes with high heels and narrow toes are an entirely new phenomenon - only 50-60 years old. What happens when we return to more natural footwear?

Research provides surprisingly clear answers: Minimalist shoes can increase foot strength by up to 57% in 6 months - without specific exercises. But the transition must be done correctly to avoid injuries.

Summary

  • Key message: Minimalist shoes strengthen the feet and improve natural gait patterns
  • Evidence: 57% strength increase, 5mm higher arch, 2.4x fewer running injuries
  • Risk: Too rapid transition can cause stress fractures and tendon injuries
  • The key: Gradual adaptation over 2-6 months is crucial

What Are Minimalist Shoes?

Minimalist shoes are designed to mimic barefoot walking as much as possible while providing basic protection. They are characterized by several key features:

Characteristics of Minimalist Shoes

  • Low or zero drop: No height difference between heel and forefoot
  • Thin, flexible sole: Typically 3-8mm, allowing the foot to feel the ground
  • Wide toe box: Room for toes to spread naturally
  • Minimal cushioning: No thick foam layers blocking sensory feedback
  • No motion control: No "stabilizing" elements restricting foot movement

Popular Brands

The market for minimalist shoes has grown significantly. Some of the most recognized brands include:

  • Vivobarefoot: Wide selection for daily use, running and hiking
  • Merrell Vapor Glove: Classic for running and training
  • Xero Shoes: Affordable entry into minimalist footwear
  • Lems: Comfortable everyday shoes with wide fit
  • Altra: Zero drop with some more cushioning, popular among ultrarunners

What Does the Research Say?

The Liverpool Study (Curtis 2021)

The most comprehensive study on minimalist shoes and foot strength was published in Scientific Reports (Nature) in 2021. Researchers followed participants who transitioned to minimalist shoes for everyday use:

57.4% strength increase
d=0.84 Cohen's d (large effect)
5mm higher foot arch

Key Findings from the Liverpool Study

After 6 months of daily use of minimalist shoes, participants showed 57.4% increase in foot strength - without any specific training exercises. Cohen's d of 0.84 indicates a large and clinically significant effect. Additionally, arch height increased by an average of 5mm.

Barefoot Populations

Studies of populations that traditionally walk barefoot or in minimal sandals provide valuable insight:

Study Finding
India Study (Sachithanandam & Joseph) Half as many cases of flat feet among those who walked barefoot
D'Aout et al. (2009) Indian barefoot walkers had more even pressure distribution and stronger feet
Hollander et al. (2017) Children who walked barefoot had better balance and motor skills
Tarahumara People Run ultramarathons in thin sandals with very few injuries

Running Injuries

A study by Taddei et al. (2020) in the American Journal of Sports Medicine examined runners with strengthened foot muscles:

2.4 Times Fewer Injuries

Runners who trained their foot muscles (which are also strengthened by minimalist shoes) had less than half as many running-related injuries as the control group.

Important Warning

Studies also show that too rapid transition to minimalist shoes significantly increases injury risk. Benefits only come with gradual adaptation over weeks and months.

Why Do Minimalist Shoes Strengthen the Feet?

There are four main mechanisms that explain why minimalist shoes result in stronger feet:

1. The Arch Must Stabilize Itself

Without artificial support from shoes or orthotics, the foot's own muscles must activate to maintain the arch with each step.

2. Natural Shock Absorption

Without thick cushioning, the body learns to land softly on the forefoot/midfoot, which activates foot muscles as natural shock absorbers.

3. Adaptation to Terrain

A flexible sole allows the foot to adapt to irregularities, stimulating small muscles and proprioceptors.

4. Toes Get to Work

Wide toe box allows toes to spread and grip, activating toe muscles that otherwise remain passive in narrow shoes.

Comparison: Shoes vs. Cast

Modern shoes with stiff soles, narrow toe boxes, and high drop do much of the same as a cast: they immobilize structures and allow muscles to atrophy. Minimalist shoes do the opposite - they allow the foot to move freely and build strength.

4

Benefits of Barefoot Walking and Minimalist Shoes

Documented Benefits

  • Stronger foot muscles: 57% strength increase documented in controlled studies
  • Better proprioception: Improved balance and body awareness
  • Higher arch: Natural strengthening of the arch structure
  • Wider forefoot: Toes get room to spread naturally
  • Fewer running injuries: 2.4x fewer injuries with strengthened foot musculature

Potential Benefits (More Research Needed)

  • Reduced knee load: Changed gait pattern may reduce stress on the knee
  • More natural gait pattern: Shorter steps, higher cadence
  • Better posture: Zero drop may positively affect pelvis and spine

Ridge et al. (2019)

The study published in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise showed that minimalist shoe use led to significant strength increases in the foot's intrinsic muscles, measured with MRI.

5

Risks of Too Rapid Transition

The greatest danger with minimalist shoes is not the shoes themselves, but too rapid transition. Feet that have been "protected" in cushioned shoes for decades have weak muscles and are not prepared for the increased load.

Common Injuries from Too Rapid Transition

  • Stress fractures: Especially in metatarsal bones
  • Achilles tendinopathy: Overload of the Achilles tendon
  • Plantar fasciitis: Overload of the arch's connective tissue
  • Muscle soreness: Intense stiffness in calves and foot muscles

Why Does This Happen?

Years of cushioned shoes have led to:

  • Weak foot musculature (up to 17% atrophy from orthotics alone)
  • Shortened Achilles tendon (from high heels)
  • Reduced bone mineral density in the foot
  • Loss of proprioceptive sensitivity

Suddenly removing all support is like going from a cast straight to running a marathon.

6

Safe Transition Protocol

For Daily Use

Gradual Adaptation to Minimalist Shoes for Everyday Life

  • Week 1-2: 30-60 minutes per day indoors. Walk barefoot at home as much as possible.
  • Week 3-4: 2-3 hours per day. Short walks outside on even surfaces.
  • Week 5-8: Half the day. Gradual increase to 4-6 hours.
  • Month 2-3: Full day possible for most. Continue listening to your body.

For Running

Conservative Approach for Runners

  • Month 1-2: ONLY walking in minimalist shoes. No running. Build base strength.
  • Month 3-4: 5-10 minutes easy jogging on soft surfaces, max 2x/week.
  • Month 5-6: Gradual increase to 20-30 minutes, 2-3x/week.
  • 6+ months: Continue gradual progression. Listen to your body.

The 10% Rule

Never increase minimalist shoe use by more than 10% per week. If you run 10 minutes in week 1, increase to maximum 11 minutes in week 2.

Who Should Be Careful?

Although minimalist shoes can be beneficial for most people, some groups should be extra careful or avoid them altogether:

Groups That Should Be Extra Careful

  • Over 50 years old: Reduced adaptability, lower bone density, longer recovery time
  • Previous stress fractures: Increased risk of new fractures
  • Diabetes: Reduced sensitivity may mask warning signals
  • Significant overweight: Higher load requires longer adaptation
  • Ongoing injuries: Treat existing problems before transition
  • Severe deformities: Some conditions require continued support

These groups can still benefit from minimalist shoes, but should get guidance from a professional and take even longer with the transition.

8

Combination: Minimalist Shoes + Foot Training

The most effective approach combines minimalist shoes with targeted foot training:

Synergistic Effect

Minimalist shoes provide passive stimulation throughout the day, while foot exercises provide active, targeted strengthening. Together they give faster and better results than either alone.

Recommended Combination

  • Morning: 5-10 minutes foot exercises (short foot, toe spreading, heel raises)
  • All day: Minimalist shoes or barefoot as much as possible
  • Evening: Light stretching of calves and arch

Red Flags - When to Stop

Stop and Seek Help If You Experience:

  • Sharp, localized pain that worsens with activity
  • Swelling that doesn't go down with rest
  • Pain lasting more than 2-3 days after activity
  • Numbness or tingling in the foot
  • Pain with first steps in the morning that worsens
  • Visible change in foot shape

These symptoms may indicate stress fractures, tendon injuries, or other conditions requiring professional assessment. Do not "train through" pain - it only prolongs healing time.

10

Frequently Asked Questions

Are minimalist shoes for everyone?
No. Minimalist shoes are suitable for most healthy adults, but require gradual adaptation. People over 50, those with diabetes, previous stress fractures, or significant overweight should be extra careful and possibly seek guidance from a professional.
Can children use minimalist shoes?
Yes, children are actually ideal candidates for minimalist shoes. Children's feet are naturally strong and adaptable. The earlier they get to walk in flexible shoes with a wide toe box, the better for natural foot development. Several studies (Hollander 2017) show that children who walk barefoot have better motor skills and balance.
Do I have to run barefoot to get the benefits?
No. Daily use of minimalist shoes provides significant strength gains without running barefoot. The Liverpool study showed 57% strength increase just from walking in minimalist shoes daily. Barefoot running is optional and requires extra caution.
What about winter conditions?
Several minimalist shoe brands offer winter variants with insulation and waterproof membrane. Vivobarefoot, Xero Shoes and Lems all have winter models. Alternatively, you can use minimalist shoes indoors and more traditional winter shoes outdoors in the cold - even partial use provides benefits.
How long does the transition take?
For daily use: 2-3 months for most people. For running: 6 months or more. Older individuals and those with previous injuries should take even longer. The key is to listen to your body and not rush.
Can I combine minimalist shoes with regular shoes?
Yes, this is actually recommended during the transition phase. Use minimalist shoes a few hours a day and regular shoes the rest of the time. Gradually increase the proportion of minimalist shoes. Many end up using minimalist shoes for most things but keep regular shoes for special occasions.
