Morton's Neuroma

Nerve compression between the toes | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Despite its name, Morton's neuroma is not a true neuroma (nerve tumor). It is a thickening and fibrosis of the common plantar digital nerve between the toes - one of many foot and ankle conditions we treat. The condition most often affects the space between the 3rd and 4th toe (66-85% of cases).

When the nerve is subjected to prolonged pressure and irritation, scar tissue develops around the nerve (perineural fibrosis). Many describe the classic symptom: "like having a stone under the foot" or "something in the shoe."

Summary

  • What it is: Thickening and irritation of nerve between toes - not a tumor
  • Most common cause: Tight shoes with narrow toe box, often combined with hallux valgus
  • Manual treatment: Strong evidence - 79% achieve pain relief
  • Red flags: Night pain, visible swelling, or symptoms in multiple areas
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

When should you seek help immediately?

If night pain is the main symptom, visible swelling/redness, or symptoms in multiple areas - contact a doctor for evaluation.

1

Who is Affected?

Women are significantly more often affected than men - studies show a ratio of 4-8:1. This difference is probably due to footwear use rather than anatomical differences.

4-8x more common in women
~4% prevalence in population
40-60 most common age group
66-85% between 3rd and 4th toe

Important to know

30-33% of people without symptoms have findings consistent with neuroma on MRI - which underscores that imaging findings must be correlated with clinical presentation.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Tight shoes with narrow toe box - main cause

Repeated compression of the nerve between the metatarsal bones during walking causes microtrauma, subsequent fibrosis and demyelination. Biomechanical studies show that high heels shift 28% of body weight to the forefoot in flat shoes, versus 66% in high heels.

Other risk factors

  • Hallux valgus (bunion): Up to 80% of patients with Morton's neuroma have concurrent hallux valgus
  • Hammer toes and other toe deformities: Create back pressure against the forefoot
  • Flat foot and high arch: Associated with altered forefoot mechanics (see also tibialis posterior tendinopathy)
  • Sports and activity: Running, ballet, tennis, skiing and climbing - these activities can also cause Achilles tendinopathy or stress fracture
3

Symptoms

Typical symptoms include burning, stinging or electric pain in the forefoot - most often between the 3rd and 4th toe. Similar symptoms can be seen with metatarsalgia, but with a different cause.

Common symptoms

  • "Stone under the foot" feeling - classic symptom (43-53%)
  • Numbness or tingling radiating into the toes (33-60%)
  • Pain worsened by tight shoes and relieved when shoes are removed (81-89%)
  • Pain during walking has high sensitivity (91%)
  • Symptoms can be intermittent or constant

Important

Night pain occurs in only about 25% with Morton's neuroma. If night pain is prominent, other diagnoses should be considered.

4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made primarily clinically. Two tests are particularly useful:

Mulder's sign

Lateral compression of the forefoot while palpating the interspace. A positive test produces a palpable "click" sensation. 62% sensitivity and 100% specificity - very reliable when positive.

Thumb-index finger squeeze test

Shows superior diagnostic accuracy with 96% sensitivity and 100% specificity. A negative test practically rules out Morton's neuroma.

Imaging

Ultrasound is the first choice when imaging is needed, with 90-91% sensitivity. Neuromas >3 mm are considered significant, and lesions >5 mm are more often symptomatic.

MRI is preferred when there is a need to rule out other pathology or for surgical planning.

5

Treatment

Strong evidence for manual treatment

Manual treatment represents one of only two interventions with strong evidence for Morton's neuroma, according to Matthews' systematic review from 2019.

79%

achieved complete pain relief (VAS 0) within 6 treatments

What can the chiropractor do?

1
Mobilization of midfoot and toe joints

Restores normal joint movement and reduces compression

2
Soft tissue treatment of the forefoot

Relieves muscle tension and improves circulation

3
Guidance on proper footwear

Wide toe box, low heel (under 3 cm), soft material

4
Metatarsal pads

Placed behind the toe balls to spread the metatarsal bones and relieve the nerve

What can you do yourself?

  • Footwear changes - most important: Shoes with wide toe box that don't squeeze the toes together. Remove all shoes with narrow front
  • Metatarsal pads: Placed just behind the toe balls (retrocapital position)
  • Avoid high heels: Maximum 3 cm heel height

Realistic expectations

Pure conservative treatment (footwear + orthotics without injections) succeeds in about 30-50%. With symptom duration over 4.5 months and neuroma >5-6 mm, orthotics and footwear changes alone are rarely sufficient.

Injection treatment

Ultrasound-guided cortisone injection can be considered if conservative treatment does not provide sufficient effect after 8-12 weeks. However, the long-term effect is limited - at 5-year follow-up, injections were still effective in only 36%.

Surgery

Considered after at least 6-9 months of failed conservative treatment. Neurectomy provides 74% complete pain relief. 72% experience numbness in the supplied toes - this is expected and generally does not affect satisfaction.

6

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

See a doctor/emergency room for:

  • Night pain/rest pain as main symptom (rare in Morton's - only 25%)
  • Visible swelling, redness or deformity (Morton's causes no visible signs)
  • General symptoms such as fever or weight loss
  • Bilateral symmetrical symptoms (suggests systemic neuropathy)
  • Symptoms in 1st or 4th interspace (very rare location for Morton's)
7

Prognosis

With the right approach, most improve significantly. Shoe change alone can provide noticeable relief for many, especially with short symptom duration and small neuromas. Proper footwear is also important for preventing other conditions like plantar fasciopathy.

Recurrence

Is common if the cause (tight shoes) is not eliminated. Surgical recurrence rate is 10-30%, and 5-20% need further treatment.

An honest conclusion: Conservative treatment is most effective early in the course. With prolonged symptoms and larger neuromas, surgery often becomes necessary for lasting improvement.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Morton's neuroma go away on its own?
The condition develops gradually and rarely resolves completely without intervention. With early intervention (footwear change, adapted pads), however, many can achieve symptom control without further treatment.
How long does it take for treatment to work?
With manual treatment, many see improvement within 4-6 treatments. 79% achieved complete pain relief within 6 treatments. Footwear changes and orthotics often need 4-6 weeks for full effect.
Do I need an MRI or ultrasound?
Not necessarily. Clinical examination with thumb-index finger squeeze test has 96% sensitivity and can often confirm the diagnosis. Imaging is most relevant for uncertain diagnoses, injection planning, or possible surgery.
Can I continue to exercise?
Activities that don't provoke pain can often be continued. Running and activities with high forefoot loading may need to be modified temporarily. Shoes with wide toe box are especially important during exercise.
Does cortisone help?
Ultrasound-guided cortisone injection can provide short-term relief (1-3 months), but long-term effectiveness is limited. About 30% eventually need surgery despite injections.
What about surgery?
Surgery is considered after 6-12 months without sufficient effect from conservative treatment. The success rate is high (74% complete pain relief), but involves expected numbness in the supplied toes.
