Do You Really Need Orthotics?

A Critical Review of the Evidence | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Every year, people spend hundreds of millions on orthotics and insoles. Many are told they have "overpronation," "flat feet," or "malalignments" that require orthopedic support. But what does the research actually say?

The answer surprises many: Meta-analyses repeatedly show that standard orthotics are no better than placebo for most foot complaints (Whittaker 2018, BJSM). And Norwegian guidelines from "Choosing Wisely" actually recommend avoiding treatment of symptom-free, flexible flat feet - because it is a normal variant, not a disease.

Summary

  • Key message: Most people do not need orthotics - strong feet provide better support
  • Evidence: Meta-analyses show orthotics are rarely better than placebo
  • Risk: 12 weeks of use can cause up to 17% muscle atrophy in the foot
  • Exceptions: Diabetic neuropathy, severe deformities, and acute injuries have clear indications

What Does the Research Say?

Meta-analyses: Orthotics vs. placebo

The most comprehensive systematic review (Whittaker et al. 2018, British Journal of Sports Medicine) analyzed 19 studies with 1660 participants with heel pain/plantar fasciitis:

Timepoint Finding
Short-term (0-6 weeks) "Very low quality evidence that foot orthotics DO NOT reduce pain"
Medium-term (7-12 weeks) Only small effect compared to sham insoles (SMD = -0.27)
Long-term (13-52 weeks) No significant difference from placebo
Custom vs. prefabricated No difference at any timepoint - despite enormous price difference

APTA Guidelines 2023

American Physical Therapy Association updated their guidelines in 2023 to Grade C recommendation: "Clinicians should NOT use orthotics as isolated treatment for short-term pain relief in plantar fasciitis."

2

Risk: Muscle Atrophy from Orthotics

A 2020 study (Protopapas & Perry, Clinical Biomechanics) measured what happens to foot muscles after just 12 weeks with custom-made orthotics:

Foot Muscle Reduction in Muscle Size
Flexor Digitorum Brevis -9.6%
Abductor Digiti Minimi -17.1%
Abductor Hallucis -17.4%

The Vicious Cycle

This creates a self-reinforcing cycle:

Weak feet -> need for support -> support weakens muscles -> even weaker feet -> even more need for support

3

Clinical Guidelines

"Choosing Wisely" (an international initiative), via Norwegian Physiotherapy Association's pediatric and adolescent physiotherapy group, published in October 2023:

"Avoid treatment of the normal variant symptom-free soft/flexible flat foot."
- Choosing Wisely, Norwegian Medical Association 2023

The Norwegian Health Library (2022) summarizes: "Knowledge in this area is limited. Therefore, there is still no international consensus on when insoles or foot orthotics should be used."

Norwegian social insurance does not cover orthotics for flat feet - not even when combined with other conditions. Patients pay everything themselves.

4

The Alternative: Train Your Feet Stronger

While orthotics show weak results, foot training has surprisingly strong effects:

57% strength increase in 6 months
2.4x fewer running injuries
8-17% energy storage in the arch

Liverpool Study (Curtis 2021)

Showed that 6 months of minimalist shoes increased foot strength by 57.4% - without specific exercises.

Taddei et al. (2020)

Found that runners who trained their feet had less than half as many injuries.

The foot arch functions as a spring - it stores 8-17% of mechanical energy with each running step (Stearne 2016). Orthotics that limit arch movement increase metabolic cost by up to 6%.

When Orthotics Actually Help

There are clear indications where orthotics have good evidence:

Condition Evidence
Diabetic neuropathy 71% reduction in ulcer formation (Lancet Digital Health 2019)
Rheumatoid arthritis ~20% pain reduction, ~30% function improvement (Cochrane)
Pes cavus (high arch) ~75% pain reduction in symptomatic condition (Cochrane)
Post-operative/acute injury Temporary offloading during healing

For these conditions, orthotics are a valuable tool - but they represent a small proportion of everyone who is prescribed orthotics today.

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately for:

  • Sudden, intense foot pain without obvious cause
  • Swelling, redness, and warmth combined with fever
  • Numbness or tingling that does not resolve
  • Foot ulcers that do not heal (especially with diabetes)
  • Significant change in foot shape over a short time
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with foot pain

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a chiropractor help with foot problems?
Yes. Chiropractors assess the entire musculoskeletal system and can identify whether foot complaints are due to local conditions or compensations from hips, pelvis, or back. Treatment often includes joint mobilization, soft tissue work, and targeted foot training.
Should I stop using my orthotics?
It depends on why you use them. Do you have diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, or severe deformities? Then you should continue. Are you using them for non-specific pain or "overpronation"? Then gradual weaning combined with foot training may be a good alternative. Consult a professional.
Are expensive orthotics better than cheap ones?
Research suggests not. Several meta-analyses (Whittaker 2018, CADTH 2022) find no significant difference between custom-made and prefabricated orthotics - despite custom orthotics costing 5-10 times more.
How long does it take to strengthen the feet?
Noticeable improvements are typically seen after 4-8 weeks of consistent training. The Liverpool study showed 57% strength increase after 6 months. Foot training should be viewed as maintenance - just like other training.
Does everyone with flat feet need treatment?
No. Up to 26% of adults have some degree of flat feet, and the vast majority are asymptomatic. Guidelines ("Choosing Wisely") recommend avoiding treatment of symptom-free, flexible flat feet. Many elite runners, including Usain Bolt, have documented flat feet.
