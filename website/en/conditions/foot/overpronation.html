Overpronation: Myth or Medical Fact?

The story behind a dubious diagnosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Have you been told you have overpronation? You are far from alone. Millions of people worldwide have been told that their feet move incorrectly and that they need special shoes or orthotics to correct the problem.

But what if the entire concept of overpronation lacks scientific foundation? Research actually shows that runners who pronate have fewer injuries than those with neutral feet (Nielsen 2013). Let us examine how this myth originated - and why it persists.

Summary

  • Key message: Overpronation is not a validated medical diagnosis
  • Origin: The concept stems from an unvalidated theory from 1954
  • Research: Pronators have fewer injuries than neutral runners
  • Wet test: Debunked by three large military studies
  • Elite runners: Usain Bolt and many East African runners have flat feet

1

A Diagnosis Born in the Shower

The story of overpronation begins in 1954, in a shower in California. Dr. Merton Root, a podiatrist, observed his own feet and developed a theory that there is an ideal foot position - what he called subtalar neutral.

Root believed that deviations from this neutral position were the cause of foot problems, and that correction with orthotics could prevent injuries. The theory sounded logical and was quickly adopted by the shoe industry and healthcare system.

The Problem: Never Scientifically Validated

Root's theory was never tested in controlled studies before it was put into practice. Over 70 years later, research has consistently failed to confirm that:

  • There is a measurable neutral foot position
  • Deviations from this position predict injuries
  • Correcting deviations prevents injuries

Dr. Kevin Kirby, himself a podiatrist and former student of Root, has said: "Subtalar neutral has never been scientifically validated. It was an idea that sounded good, but the research doesn't support it."

2

Dr. Benno Nigg: From Believer to Skeptic

Dr. Benno Nigg is perhaps the world's most respected researcher in biomechanics and running shoes. He founded the Human Performance Laboratory at the University of Calgary and has studied running movements for over 40 years.

Nigg started his career believing in the overpronation theory. But after decades of research, he completely changed his mind.

Nigg's Conclusion

"There is no evidence that overpronation causes injuries. And there is no evidence that motion control shoes reduce injuries."
- Dr. Benno Nigg, 2015

Nigg's research showed that:

  • The body naturally adapts to the surface
  • Forcing the foot into a certain position can actually increase stress
  • Comfort is a better indicator for the right shoe than pronation measurement
3

Pronators Have Fewer Injuries

In 2013, Nielsen and colleagues published a study in the British Journal of Sports Medicine that turned the overpronation myth on its head.

972 novice runners
1 year follow-up period
Fewer injuries in pronators

All participants received identical neutral running shoes, regardless of foot type. After one year of follow-up, the results showed:

Study Findings

Runners with pronated feet had FEWER injuries than those with neutral feet.

This is the opposite of what the overpronation theory predicts. The study concluded: "Foot pronation is not associated with increased injury risk in novice runners."

Why? A possible explanation is that pronation is actually the body's natural shock absorption mechanism, and restricting this movement can increase stress on other structures.

4

The Wet Test is Debunked

The wet test - where you stand on wet paper to see your footprint - has been the basis for assigning running shoes for decades. Flat footprint? Then you need motion control shoes. High arch? Then you need cushioning.

But does this approach work? The U.S. military tested this thoroughly.

Study Participants Findings
Knapik 2009 1,400 recruits No difference in injuries between foot type-matched and standard shoes
Knapik 2010 (Marine) 840 recruits No difference in injuries
Knapik 2010 (Air Force) 3,000+ recruits No difference in injuries

The Conclusion

Three large military studies with over 5,000 participants combined showed that assigning shoes based on footprint (wet test) does not reduce injuries compared to giving everyone standard shoes.

Despite this, the wet test is still used in running stores worldwide.

5

What is Pronation Really?

Pronation is a natural rotational movement in the foot that occurs when you land during walking or running. The foot rolls inward, and the arch flattens somewhat. This is not a flaw - it is the body's built-in shock absorption system.

Professor Gert-Peter Bruggemann

Former head of research at Adidas and one of the world's leading experts on foot biomechanics:

"There are no normal values for pronation. We have never been able to define what is too much pronation, because it varies enormously from person to person."

The Function of Pronation

  • Shock absorption: Absorbs impact upon landing
  • Adaptation: Allows the foot to adapt to uneven terrain
  • Energy storage: The arch functions as a spring that stores and releases energy

When we restrict pronation with stiff shoes or orthotics, the energy must be absorbed elsewhere - often in the knees, hips, or back.

6

Elite Athletes with "Problematic" Feet

If overpronation and flat feet were really so harmful, we would expect elite athletes to have perfect feet. But reality is quite different.

Usain Bolt

The world's fastest man has documented flat feet. He also has a significant leg length difference and scoliosis. Yet he dominated sprinting for over a decade.

East African Runners

Kenya and Ethiopia dominate long-distance running. Studies show that many of these runners have what we in the West would call flat feet or overpronation - often after a lifetime of barefoot running.

What This Tells Us

The foot's shape and movement vary extremely between individuals. What looks abnormal on a scanner or in a running store may be perfectly functional for that person.

7

Why Does the Myth Persist?

Despite lack of scientific support, the overpronation concept continues to dominate. Why?

$3.7-4.8 billion - global orthotics market

Economic Incentives

The orthotics industry is enormous. Manufacturers, retailers, and practitioners have significant economic interests in maintaining the belief that many people need foot correction.

Tradition and Education

The overpronation theory has been taught in healthcare education for decades. Many practitioners learned this as established science and have not updated themselves on newer research.

Visual Logic

It looks wrong when the foot rolls inward. We are programmed to think that what looks abnormal must be harmful. But the body is more robust and adaptable than we often think.

Placebo Effect

Many experience improvement with orthotics. But studies show that this effect is often placebo - sham insoles provide the same effect as expensive custom-made orthotics.

8

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately for:

  • Sudden, intense foot pain without apparent cause
  • Swelling, redness, and warmth combined with fever
  • Numbness or tingling that doesn't go away
  • Foot sores that don't heal (especially with diabetes)
  • Significant change in foot shape over a short time
  • Pain that worsens significantly with activity and doesn't improve with rest
  • Sudden development of flat foot in an adult (may indicate tendon damage)

Even though overpronation is not a valid diagnosis, there are real foot problems that require medical evaluation. Persistent pain should always be examined by a professional.

9

Frequently Asked Questions

Is all pronation normal?
Yes, pronation is a completely natural movement. The foot is designed to pronate upon landing - it is the body's built-in shock absorption. The problem arises when normal variation is pathologized as overpronation. There is no scientifically defined limit for what is too much pronation.
Do children with flat feet need orthotics?
No, in the vast majority of cases. Research concludes that there is no evidence base for orthotics in children with flat feet. Flexible flat feet in children is a normal variant that usually develops normally. Unnecessary treatment can actually hinder natural foot development.
Why do practitioners still prescribe for overpronation?
Several factors are at play: tradition from education, economic incentives (the orthotics market is worth billions), visual logic (it looks wrong), and lack of updated knowledge on newer research. Many practitioners are genuinely convinced because they learned this as established science.
Should I stop using my motion control shoes?
If they are comfortable and you have no problems, there is no reason to switch immediately. But if you are considering new shoes, research suggests that comfort is a better guide than pronation measurement. A gradual transition to more neutral shoes may be appropriate for many.
Can a chiropractor help with foot problems?
Yes. Chiropractors assess the entire musculoskeletal system and can identify whether foot complaints are due to local conditions or compensations from hips, pelvis, or back. Treatment often includes joint mobilization, soft tissue work, and targeted foot exercises - without unnecessary pathologization of normal foot variation.
