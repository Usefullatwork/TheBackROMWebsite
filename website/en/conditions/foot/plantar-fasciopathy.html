Plantar Fasciopathy

Everything you need to know about heel pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Do you recognize that intense, stabbing heel pain when you take your first steps in the morning? You are far from alone. Plantar fasciopathy is the most common cause of heel pain in adults, affecting approximately 10% of the population during their lifetime. This is one of the most common foot and ankle conditions we treat.

The plantar fascia is a thick band of connective tissue that runs along the sole of the foot from the heel bone to the toe balls. The tissue functions as a natural shock absorber and helps maintain the foot's arch. When this tissue becomes overloaded over time, degenerative changes can develop - a process similar to what we see in Achilles tendinopathy.

Summary

  • What it is: Degenerative condition in the plantar fascia (not inflammation)
  • Incidence: Affects about 10% during lifetime, most common between 40-60 years
  • Prognosis: 80-90% improve with conservative treatment within 12 months
  • Red flags: Night pain, numbness, fever, severe swelling
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

What is Plantar Fasciopathy?

Why fasciopathy and not fasciitis?

The suffix -itis means inflammation, but histological studies actually show absence of inflammatory cells in the tissue. What happens is degenerative changes: breakdown of collagen fibers, thickening of the fascia, and reduced blood supply. Therefore, many professionals now use the term plantar fasciopathy - a more precise description of what is actually happening in the tissue (Riel et al., BJSM 2017).

10% lifetime risk
40-60 most common age group
2.5x higher risk for women
83% in working adults 25-65 years

The condition primarily affects people between 40-60 years, and 83% of cases occur in working adults between 25-65 years (StatPearls 2024). Women have 2.5 times higher incidence than men. Among runners, prevalence is 5-10%, and some studies report up to 22%.

2

Symptoms

The most characteristic symptom is intense heel pain with the first steps in the morning - so-called start-up pain. The pain occurs because the plantar fascia contracts during the night while the foot rests, and the sudden loading when standing up causes stretching pain.

Typical characteristics

  • Pain is located on the underside of the heel, often slightly toward the inside
  • Tenderness when pressing on the heel attachment (medial calcaneal tuberosity)
  • Pain typically improves after a few minutes of walking
  • Can worsen again with prolonged activity or standing
  • About one third have symptoms in both feet

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on medical history and physical examination. Typical localized tenderness over the heel attachment and positive Windlass test (pain when toes are bent backward) confirms the diagnosis in most cases. It is important to distinguish the condition from other causes of foot pain such as Morton's neuroma or metatarsalgia.

Imaging is normally not necessary at the start. Ultrasound is considered if there is no response after 3 months.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Research has identified several risk factors, ranked by how strongly they affect the chance of developing the condition:

Limited ankle mobility

23x increased risk with zero degrees dorsiflexion or less (Riddle et al., JBJS 2003)

Obesity (BMI > 30)

5-6x increased risk compared to normal weight

Occupations with lots of standing

4x increased risk when standing most of the workday

Foot structure

Both flat foot and high arch can increase load on the plantar fascia

Sudden increase in activity - like starting to run after a long break, or increasing training volume too quickly - is a classic triggering factor. This type of overload can also lead to stress fractures in the foot.

4

The Heel Spur Myth

The myth: The heel spur pokes and causes the pain

Fact: This is not correct. Studies show:

  • 10-16% of symptom-free people have heel spurs
  • Up to 55% of adults over 60 have heel spurs without symptoms
  • Only about 5% of people with heel spurs have symptoms
  • The spurs are a result of chronic loading - not the cause of the problem
  • They don't disappear even if the pain goes away

Treatment is identical regardless of whether a heel spur is present or not. Surgical removal of the heel spur alone does not improve the outcome.

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately for:

  • Night pain that wakes you up (may indicate other pathology)
  • Numbness or tingling in the foot (possible nerve involvement)
  • Severe swelling and redness in the heel (infection?)
  • Fever combined with foot pain
  • Recent trauma or injury
  • Symptoms in both heels combined with joint pain elsewhere (rheumatic disease?)
6

Treatment

Conservative treatment is the first choice, and over 80-90% respond well to this (StatPearls 2024). Only 5-10% eventually need surgical treatment.

Exercise guidance

Stretching and strength training with documented effect

Evidence for manual treatment

A systematic review from 2012 found moderate evidence that manual therapy combined with stretching and strength exercises gives better short-term results than exercises alone (Brantingham et al. 2012).

What can you do yourself?

Plantar fascia-specific stretching (strongest evidence)

One study showed 52% pain relief with this technique versus only 22% with general calf stretching.

  1. Sit down, cross the affected leg over the other
  2. Grip the toes and pull them backward toward the shin
  3. Hold 15-30 seconds
  4. Do this 3-4 times daily, especially before getting up in the morning

High-load training (Rathleff protocol)

Danish study showed better results than stretching at 3 months.

  1. Single-leg heel raises with a towel under the toes
  2. 3x12 repetitions with slow tempo (3 sec up, 2 sec hold, 3 sec down)
  3. Every other day

Other practical advice

  • Avoid walking barefoot on hard floors
  • Wear shoes with good support, also indoors
  • Roll the foot over a frozen water bottle for pain relief
  • Increase activity level gradually (max 10% per week)

Prognosis and Expected Course

Plantar fasciopathy generally has a good prognosis. 80-90% improve significantly with conservative treatment within 12 months.

Typical timeline

2-6 weeks

First improvement often seen with proper treatment

3-6 months

Most experience significant improvement

Up to 12 months

Full recovery for some cases

Average about 2 years

For prolonged cases (Hansen et al. 2018)

About recurrence

30-50% experience recurrence without preventive measures. Risk factors for recurrence include:

  • Too rapid return to activity
  • Lack of maintenance exercises
  • Unaddressed biomechanical factors

Women and people with symptoms in both feet have statistically longer courses.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I exercise with plantar fasciopathy?
Yes, but adapt the activity. Low-impact activities like cycling and swimming are often well tolerated. When running or walking: reduce distance and intensity, increase gradually, and listen to your body. Pain that worsens during or after activity is a signal to reduce the load.
Do heel spur insoles help?
Insoles can help somewhat in the short term. A meta-analysis from 2017 found moderate effect at 6-12 weeks. Interestingly, one study showed that ready-made prefabricated insoles combined with stretching were actually more effective than custom-made insoles plus stretching.
Should I have the heel spur surgically removed?
No. The heel spur is not the cause of the pain, and removing the spur alone does not improve the outcome. Surgery is only considered in the 5-10% of cases where conservative treatment over 6-12 months has not helped, and then usually involves partial release of the plantar fascia - not removal of the spur.
How long does it take to recover?
Most experience significant improvement within 3-6 months with proper treatment. For some, it takes up to 12 months. Early treatment is associated with faster improvement. Remember that recurrence is common (30-50%), so maintenance exercises are important even after recovery.
What about cortisone injections?
Cortisone injections can provide short-term relief (up to 1 month), but have limited long-term effects according to Cochrane reviews. There is also a risk of complications: 2.4-6.7% experience rupture of the plantar fascia, and fat tissue under the heel can be weakened. Shockwave therapy is often recommended as a safer and more effective alternative.
Do I need MRI or X-ray?
Usually not. The diagnosis is made clinically based on medical history and examination. Imaging is only considered if symptoms don't respond to 3 months of treatment, or if there is suspicion of another cause of the pain.
