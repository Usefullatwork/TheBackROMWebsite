Sever's Disease

Heel pain in children and young athletes | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Does your child have heel pain after soccer practice or running? Sever's disease is the most common cause of heel pain in active children and adolescents. This type of foot pain is completely harmless and always resolves on its own - but that doesn't mean the pain should be ignored.

Sever's disease (also called calcaneal apophysitis) occurs when the growth plate in the heel bone becomes irritated from repeated stress. It is especially common in children who participate in a lot of sports, and is most often seen during periods of rapid growth. In adults with similar symptoms, plantar fasciopathy or Achilles tendinopathy are more common diagnoses.

Summary

  • What it is: Growth-related heel pain in children (calcaneal apophysitis)
  • Age group: Most common in children 8-14 years, especially during growth spurts
  • Prognosis: Self-limiting - always resolves when the growth plate closes
  • Important: Not dangerous, but pain should be managed to maintain activity
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up
1

What is Sever's Disease?

Sever's disease is an overuse condition that affects the growth plate (apophysis) in the heel bone of children. The Achilles tendon attaches here, and with repeated stress, the area can become irritated and painful. This is different from stress fracture in the foot, which involves an actual bone break.

The condition is named after American physician James Warren Sever who first described it in 1912. Today, the more precise name calcaneal apophysitis is often preferred.

8-14 most common age group
60% have it in both feet
2-3x more common in boys
100% fully recover

Why does it occur?

During growth spurts, bones grow faster than muscles and tendons. This creates tension where the Achilles tendon attaches to the heel bone. The growth plate (apophysis) is weaker than surrounding tissue and therefore becomes irritated with repeated stress.

2

Symptoms

Symptoms typically develop gradually and worsen with activity.

Common symptoms

  • Heel pain - typically behind and under the heel
  • Worsens with activity - especially running and jumping
  • Limping - especially after training
  • Morning stiffness or after rest
  • Tenderness when pressed on the sides of the heel
  • Walking on toes - the child avoids loading the heel

Typical pattern

Pain is often worst:

  • During and after training
  • When running on hard surfaces
  • When wearing soccer cleats or other sports shoes with low heel height
  • During periods of rapid growth

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made clinically based on medical history and examination. The classic "squeeze test" (pain when squeezing the sides of the heel) is typically positive.

X-ray is usually not necessary, but may be taken to rule out other conditions.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Sever's disease typically occurs when several factors are present at the same time. Many of these risk factors can also contribute to ankle sprains and other foot problems in young athletes.

Growth spurt

Rapid growth makes the growth plate vulnerable to overload

Lots of sports

Especially running, soccer, handball and basketball

Hard surfaces

Training on asphalt, concrete or hard gym floors

Tight calf muscles

Increases the pull on the growth plate

Overweight

Increased load on the growth plate

Flat foot

Can increase the pull on the Achilles tendon

Typical sports

Sever's disease is especially common in:

  • Soccer and handball
  • Basketball and volleyball
  • Track and field (running and jumping)
  • Gymnastics and dance
4

Is Sever's Disease Dangerous?

No, Sever's disease is completely harmless and causes no permanent damage. It is a common, benign growth pain that resolves on its own when the growth plate in the heel closes.

The good news

  • The condition always resolves on its own
  • No permanent damage or deformities
  • Does not affect the child's growth or development
  • The child can usually continue with sports (with modifications)

The most important thing is to manage the pain so that the child can stay active and avoid long periods of inactivity. Complete rest is rarely necessary.

5

Red Flags - When You Must See a Doctor

See a doctor if the child has:

  • Pain that wakes them at night
  • Fever together with heel pain
  • Swelling and redness over the heel
  • Pain after a fall or injury
  • Pain that doesn't improve with rest
  • Severe pain in children under 7 years or over 15 years
  • Weight loss, fatigue or general feeling of illness

These symptoms may indicate other conditions that require further investigation.

6

Treatment

The goal of treatment is to reduce pain so that the child can maintain activity. Complete rest is rarely necessary.

What can the chiropractor do?

Soft tissue treatment

Loosen tight muscles in the calf and foot

Joint mobilization

Ensure good movement in ankle and foot joints

Taping

Can provide support and relief during acute periods

Exercise guidance

Stretching and strengthening exercises adapted to the child

What can you do at home?

Calf stretching

Important for reducing the pull on the growth plate:

  1. Stand with hands against a wall
  2. Place the affected leg behind, keep the heel on the floor
  3. Bend the front knee until you feel a stretch in the calf
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3 times
  5. Do this 2-3 times daily

Other measures

  • Heel cups/insoles: Cushion impact and reduce load
  • Ice after activity: 15-20 minutes as needed
  • Good shoes: Shock-absorbing shoes with some heel height
  • Activity modification: Reduce volume rather than stopping completely

Activity modification

Instead of complete rest, modify the activity:

  • Reduce training volume and intensity
  • Avoid hard surfaces when possible
  • Switch to low-impact activities during periods (swimming, cycling)
  • Let the child take breaks during training when in pain
7

Prognosis and Expected Course

The prognosis is excellent. All children with Sever's disease fully recover when the growth plate closes.

Acute phase

Pain with activity, need for modification and treatment

Treatment phase (weeks-months)

Gradual improvement with stretching, insoles and activity modification

Variable phase

Symptoms may come and go during periods of growth or increased activity

Full recovery

The condition resolves when the growth plate closes (12-16 years)

When does the growth plate close?

  • Girls: Usually around 12-14 years
  • Boys: Usually around 14-16 years

When the growth plate is closed, the condition can no longer occur.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my child have to stop training with Sever's disease?
Not necessarily. Activity should be adapted to the pain level. The child can often continue training with reduced intensity and volume. Pain-free activities like swimming and cycling are good alternatives during periods with significant symptoms. Complete rest is rarely recommended.
How long does Sever's disease last?
The condition is self-limiting and resolves when the growth plate closes, usually around 12-14 years for girls and 14-16 years for boys. Symptoms can last from a few months to 2 years, depending on activity level and treatment. Many have periods of improvement and worsening.
Is Sever's disease dangerous?
No, Sever's disease is not dangerous and causes no permanent damage. It is a common, benign condition caused by growth-related pain. The condition always resolves on its own when the child finishes the growth spurt, and there is no risk of long-term problems.
Do heel insoles help with Sever's disease?
Yes, heel cups or cushioning insoles can help by reducing the load on the growth plate. They should be used in both sports shoes and everyday shoes. Prefabricated insoles from the pharmacy or sports store often work as well as custom-made ones.
Can Sever's disease come back?
Yes, symptoms can return with increased activity or a new growth spurt. This is normal and does not mean the condition has worsened. When the growth plate is closed (usually in the teenage years), the condition can no longer occur.
Should we get an X-ray?
Usually not. The diagnosis is made based on medical history and clinical examination. X-ray may be considered if symptoms are unusual, don't improve as expected, or to rule out other conditions. X-ray doesn't show Sever's disease itself, but can rule out other causes.
Can other heel problems be confused with Sever's disease?
Yes, in adults with similar symptoms, plantar fasciopathy and Achilles tendinopathy are common diagnoses. In children, stress fracture and infection must also be ruled out. A thorough examination ensures the correct diagnosis.
