Stress Fracture in the Foot

Overuse fractures in runners and athletes | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Do you have gradually increasing foot pain that worsens during exercise and improves with rest? Stress fractures are one of the most common overuse injuries in runners and active people. This type of foot pain requires proper diagnosis and treatment for optimal healing.

A stress fracture (also called a fatigue fracture) is a small crack in the bone that occurs when the load on the bone exceeds its ability to repair itself. Unlike regular fractures, stress fractures are not caused by a single injury, but by repeated microstress over time.

Summary

  • What it is: Microscopic fractures in bone caused by repeated overuse
  • Common in: Runners, military personnel, dancers and active athletes
  • Healing time: 6-8 weeks for most, longer for high-risk fractures
  • Important: Early diagnosis is crucial - continued loading can lead to complete fracture
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

What is a stress fracture?

Bone is living tissue that is constantly being broken down and rebuilt. When the load is greater than the bone's capacity to repair itself, a "stress reaction" - a precursor to fracture - first occurs. If loading continues, a stress fracture develops. This differs from acute injuries like ankle sprains.

2-5% of all sports injuries
95% in lower extremities
2-3x more common in women
6-8 weeks typical healing time

Most Common Locations in the Foot

Location Proportion Risk Level Healing
2nd-3rd metatarsal Approx. 50% Low risk 6-8 weeks
5th metatarsal (proximal) Approx. 10% High risk 12-20 weeks
Navicular Approx. 15% High risk 12-20 weeks
Calcaneus (heel bone) Approx. 10% Low risk 6-8 weeks

High-risk vs. Low-risk

High-risk fractures are located in areas with poor blood supply and high loading. They require stricter offloading and have higher risk of complications.

2

Symptoms

Symptoms typically develop gradually over days to weeks.

Typical Course

  • Early: Vague pain that comes toward the end of activity
  • Progression: Pain comes earlier and earlier during activity
  • Late: Pain when walking and eventually at rest

Classic Symptoms

  • Localized pain - point tenderness over the fracture
  • Pain that worsens with loading
  • Improvement with rest
  • Possible swelling over the area
  • Pain with percussion (tapping on the bone)
  • Pain with vibration (tuning fork test)

Diagnosis

X-ray is often negative in the first 2-3 weeks. With clinical suspicion, MRI should be taken, which is the gold standard and shows stress fractures early. MRI can also distinguish between stress reaction and fracture.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Stress fractures occur when the load on the bone exceeds its capacity for repair. Many of the same risk factors can also contribute to other overuse conditions like Achilles tendinopathy and plantar fasciopathy.

Rapid Increase in Training (High Risk)

The most important risk factor. "Too much, too fast, too soon"

Female Athlete Triad/RED-S (High Risk)

Energy deficiency, menstrual disturbances, low bone density

Low Energy Intake (Moderate Risk)

Insufficient calories relative to activity level

Vitamin D/Calcium Deficiency (Moderate Risk)

Important for bone health. Common in Norway due to limited sun

Previous Stress Fracture (Low Risk)

Increased risk of new fractures

Hard Surface (Low Risk)

Running on asphalt/concrete vs. dirt trails

The 10% Rule

An important rule of thumb for preventing stress fractures: Never increase training volume (distance, intensity or frequency) by more than 10% per week.

4

RED-S and Female Athlete Triad

Women have 2-3 times higher risk of stress fractures. An important cause is RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport), previously called the female athlete triad.

The Three Components

  • Energy deficiency: Too few calories relative to activity level
  • Menstrual disturbances: Irregular or absent menstruation
  • Low bone density: Increased risk of stress fractures

RED-S can also affect men, especially in weight-sensitive sports. With repeated stress fractures, underlying causes should always be evaluated.

Important to Know

Absent menstruation in female athletes is not normal, even with hard training. It is a sign of energy deficiency and requires follow-up to prevent serious consequences such as osteoporosis and stress fractures.

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention promptly for:

  • Pain that doesn't go away with rest
  • Night pain
  • Visible swelling or deformity
  • Fever or general symptoms
  • Suspected high-risk fracture (5th metatarsal, navicular)
  • Repeated stress fractures - may indicate underlying disease

High-risk fractures require specialist evaluation and may need surgical treatment.

6

Treatment

Treatment depends on the fracture's location (low- vs. high-risk) and severity.

Basic Principles

Offloading

Stop the activity that caused the fracture. Duration: 6-8 weeks for low-risk fractures

Supportive Footwear

Stiff shoes or walker boot to reduce loading

Alternative Training

Swimming, cycling, pool running - activities without loading

Gradual Return

Slow progression of loading when pain-free

High-Risk Fractures

For fractures in the 5th metatarsal (proximal) or navicular:

  • Often need complete offloading with crutches
  • Cast or walker boot for 6-12 weeks
  • Surgery may be necessary if healing fails
  • Close follow-up with control X-ray/MRI

When Can I Start Running Again?

Week 0-6

No weight-bearing activity. Alternative training like swimming/cycling

Week 6-8

Gradual increase in walking. Pain-free walking before further progression

Week 8-10

Start with light jogging (walk-jog intervals)

Week 10+

Gradual increase in distance and intensity (max 10%/week)

7

Prevention

Stress fractures can often be prevented with the right measures.

Most Important Preventive Measures

  1. Follow the 10% rule - never increase training volume more than 10%/week
  2. Ensure adequate energy intake - eat enough to support training
  3. Take vitamin D - especially important in Norway (1000-2000 IU daily)
  4. Ensure calcium intake - dairy products or supplements (1000 mg/day)
  5. Vary surfaces - mix asphalt with dirt trails
  6. Replace shoes regularly - every 500-800 km for running shoes
  7. Listen to your body - take breaks when experiencing pain

For Female Athletes

  • Monitor menstrual cycle
  • Seek help for absent menstruation
  • Consider bone density measurement if risk factors present
  • Work with sports dietitian if needed
8

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for a stress fracture to heal?
Most stress fractures in the foot heal in 6-8 weeks with proper rest. Some locations (like proximal 5th metatarsal and navicular) are "high-risk" and can take 12-20 weeks. Full return to sport usually takes 8-16 weeks depending on location and severity.
Can I walk with a stress fracture?
For low-risk fractures (e.g., 2nd-3rd metatarsal), you can often walk with supportive footwear or a walker boot. For high-risk fractures (5th metatarsal, navicular), complete offloading with crutches may be necessary. Always follow your doctor's or chiropractor's recommendations.
Does a stress fracture show on X-ray?
Often not in the first 2-3 weeks. Early stress fractures and stress reactions are best seen on MRI, which is the gold standard. X-rays may show the fracture after 2-6 weeks when callus (new bone tissue) forms. If stress fracture is clinically suspected, MRI should be requested.
How do I prevent stress fractures?
Follow the 10% rule (don't increase training volume more than 10% per week), ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D, wear good shoes that are replaced regularly, vary surfaces, and listen to your body. For women, regular menstruation is important for bone health.
Can I exercise with a stress fracture?
You can train upper body and do non-weight-bearing activities like swimming, pool running and cycling (if pain-free). All weight-bearing activity on the foot must stop until the fracture has healed. Returning to loading too early can lead to complete fracture or delayed healing.
Can stress fractures occur without training?
Yes, in conditions that affect bone density (osteoporosis, RED-S, certain medications), stress fractures can occur with normal activity. If you have a stress fracture without clear overuse, you should be evaluated for underlying causes.
