Tibialis Posterior Tendinopathy

Pain on the inside of the ankle and acquired flat foot | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Do you have pain on the inside of the ankle that worsens with walking? Have you noticed that your foot arch has become flatter? Tibialis posterior tendinopathy (PTTD) is the most common cause of acquired flat foot in adults. This is one of the most common foot and ankle conditions we treat.

The tibialis posterior tendon is one of the most important structures for maintaining the foot arch. When this tendon becomes damaged or weakened, the foot gradually loses its natural arch, which can lead to pain, instability and walking difficulties. The condition can also affect the plantar fascia and cause heel pain.

Summary

  • What it is: Injury to the tendon that supports the foot arch (tibialis posterior)
  • Consequence: Most common cause of acquired flat foot in adults
  • Prognosis: Good with early treatment (stage 1-2)
  • Important: Progressive condition - early treatment is crucial
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up
1

What is Tibialis Posterior Tendinopathy?

Tibialis posterior is a muscle that runs along the inside of the lower leg and attaches under the foot. The tendon has an important task: it supports the foot arch and helps turn the foot inward (inversion).

In tibialis posterior tendinopathy (also called PTTD - Posterior Tibial Tendon Dysfunction), the tendon gradually becomes weakened, lengthened or torn. This causes the foot arch to collapse and the foot to turn outward.

3-10% of population affected
4x more common in women
40-60 most common age group
90% respond to conservative treatment

The tendon's important function

The tibialis posterior tendon works as a "winch" that holds the foot arch up. It is activated with every step to stabilize the foot during loading. When the tendon fails, other structures (ligaments, joint capsule) take over, which gradually become stretched.

2

Symptoms

Symptoms typically develop gradually over time, but can also occur more acutely after injury.

Common symptoms

  • Pain on the inside of the ankle - along the tendon's course
  • Swelling along the inside of the ankle
  • Pain with walking - especially during push-off
  • Difficulty standing on toes on one leg
  • Gradual flattening of the foot arch
  • Foot turns outward - "too many toes" sign when viewed from behind

"Too many toes" sign

When looking at the patient from behind, with advanced PTTD you will see more toes on the affected side because the foot has turned outward. Normally you only see the little toe and parts of the 4th toe.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made clinically by examining the tendon, foot arch and ability to raise onto toes on one leg. Ultrasound or MRI can confirm the diagnosis and show the degree of tendon damage.

3

Stages of PTTD

Tibialis posterior tendinopathy is classified into four stages based on severity:

Stage Tendon Foot Treatment
Stage 1 Inflammation, normal length Normal foot arch, flexible Conservative
Stage 2 Lengthened, degenerated Flat foot, still flexible Conservative/orthotics
Stage 3 Significantly damaged Rigid flat foot, not correctable Often surgery
Stage 4 Ruptured/non-functional Rigid flat foot + ankle arthritis Surgery

Why early treatment is important

Stage 1-2 respond well to conservative treatment. When the condition progresses to stage 3-4, the foot deformity becomes permanent and surgery is often necessary. Therefore, early diagnosis and treatment is crucial.

4

Causes and Risk Factors

The condition most often develops gradually as a result of overload and degeneration.

Overweight

Increased load on the tendon, especially with BMI over 25

Female gender

4 times more common in women, possible hormonal factor

Age over 40

The tendon naturally degenerates with age

Diabetes

Affects blood supply and tendon quality

Hypertension

Associated with reduced blood supply to the tendon

Previous injury

Ankle injuries and ankle instability can predispose to PTTD

Athletes who engage in running, basketball or tennis can also develop the condition due to repeated loading. Load injuries can also affect the Achilles tendon and lead to stress fractures in the foot.

5

Red Flags - When You Must See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately for:

  • Sudden, severe pain with audible "snap" (possible tendon rupture)
  • Severe swelling and bruising
  • Unable to stand on toes at all
  • Fever combined with swelling (infection?)
  • Redness and warmth over the area
  • Rapidly progressing symptoms

An acute rupture of the tibialis posterior tendon requires prompt evaluation for possible surgical treatment.

6

Treatment

Treatment depends on the stage. In stage 1-2, conservative treatment is the first choice.

What can the chiropractor do?

1
Joint mobilization

Of ankle and foot joints to optimize biomechanics

2
Soft tissue treatment

Of tibialis posterior and surrounding muscles

3
Exercise guidance

Strength training for tibialis posterior and foot arch muscles

4
Footwear and insole advice

Guidance on proper footwear and orthotic insoles

Orthotic insoles

Insoles that support the foot arch are often an important part of treatment:

  • Stage 1: Prefabricated insoles may be sufficient
  • Stage 2: Custom insoles are often recommended
  • Stage 2-3: AFO (ankle-foot orthosis) may be necessary

What can you do yourself?

Strength exercise for tibialis posterior

Eccentric strength training has shown good results:

  1. Sit with a resistance band around the forefoot
  2. Keep the band taut and turn the foot inward (inversion)
  3. Slowly release back (3-4 seconds)
  4. 3 sets of 15 repetitions
  5. Do this daily

Single-leg heel raise

To strengthen the entire foot arch complex:

  1. Stand on one leg by a wall for balance
  2. Slowly raise up onto toes
  3. Hold 2 seconds at the top
  4. Slowly lower down (3-4 seconds)
  5. 3 sets of 10-15 repetitions on each side

When is surgery considered?

Surgery is considered for:

  • Lack of response to 3-6 months of conservative treatment
  • Stage 3-4 with rigid deformity
  • Acute tendon rupture in younger, active individuals
7

Prognosis and Expected Course

Prognosis depends strongly on the stage at treatment start.

Stage 1

Good prognosis. Most improve with 2-3 months of conservative treatment

Stage 2

Good to moderate prognosis. May take 6-12 months. Insoles often necessary

Stage 3-4

Permanent deformity. Surgery often needed for functional improvement

Factors that affect outcome

  • Early treatment is the most important factor
  • Weight loss in overweight individuals improves prognosis
  • Consistent use of insoles is important
  • Ongoing strength training prevents recurrence
8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can tibialis posterior tendinopathy heal without surgery?
Yes, especially in early stages (stage 1-2). Conservative treatment with strength training, orthotic insoles and activity modification gives good results for about 90% of patients in early stages. Early treatment is crucial to prevent progression to more serious stages.
What is the difference between tibialis posterior tendinopathy and flat foot?
Tibialis posterior tendinopathy is an injury to the tendon that supports the foot arch. When the tendon weakens, the foot arch can gradually collapse leading to acquired flat foot. Flat foot can also be congenital. PTTD is the most common cause of acquired flat foot in adults.
How long does it take to recover from tibialis posterior tendinopathy?
Recovery varies depending on stage. Stage 1 can improve in 2-3 months with proper treatment. Stage 2 can take 6-12 months and often requires continuous use of insoles. Ongoing strength training is important to prevent recurrence.
Do I need special insoles for tibialis posterior tendinopathy?
Insoles that support the foot arch are often an important part of treatment. In early stages, prefabricated insoles may be sufficient. With more advanced disease, custom orthotics or support braces (AFO) may be necessary. The chiropractor can guide you.
Can I exercise with tibialis posterior tendinopathy?
Yes, but activity must be adapted. Low-impact activities like swimming and cycling are often well tolerated. Avoid activities that stress the tendon significantly (running, jumping) during acute phases. Specific strength training of tibialis posterior and foot arch muscles is actually an important part of treatment.
Will my foot arch return to normal?
It depends on the stage. In stage 1-2 where the foot is still flexible, strength training and insoles can help maintain or improve the foot arch. In stage 3-4, the deformity is often permanent, but treatment can still improve function and reduce pain.
