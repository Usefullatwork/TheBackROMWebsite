When Orthotics Actually Help

The right indications | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Orthotics are not always wrong. While the evidence for general use is weak, there are specific conditions where orthotics have documented effect. This article reviews when orthotics actually help - and when they don't.

The problem is not orthotics themselves, but overprescription to patients who don't benefit from them. A study showed that 72-93% of podiatrists prescribe orthotics for knee, hip and back pain - despite lacking evidence.

Summary

  • Strong evidence: Diabetic neuropathy, rheumatoid arthritis, symptomatic pes cavus
  • Moderate evidence: Acute injury offloading, postoperative rehabilitation
  • Weak evidence: Overpronation, flat foot, nonspecific foot/heel pain
  • Custom vs. prefabricated: No documented difference in effect

Orthotics Are Not Always Wrong

While many articles focus on the overuse of orthotics, it's important to acknowledge that there are legitimate indications. For certain patient groups, orthotics can be a valuable - sometimes lifesaving - aid.

The key is to distinguish between:

  • Conditions with strong evidence - where orthotics have documented effect in randomized controlled trials
  • Conditions with moderate evidence - where orthotics may have a temporary role
  • Conditions with weak/no evidence - where orthotics are often prescribed without scientific basis

The problem is overprescription

A survey showed that 72-93% of podiatrists prescribe orthotics for knee, hip and back pain - despite no evidence for effect in these conditions. This undermines trust in the entire field.

2

Conditions With Strong Evidence

Diabetic Neuropathy

Why it works

In diabetic neuropathy, the patient loses sensation in their feet and can develop foot ulcers that in the worst case lead to amputation. Custom pressure-relieving orthotics can prevent ulcer formation.

71% fewer ulcers (Lancet 2019)
IWGDF recommends in 2023
Medicare covers the cost

Lancet Digital Health (2019) showed 71% reduction in ulcer formation with custom orthotics. International Working Group on the Diabetic Foot (IWGDF 2023) recommends pressure-relieving orthotics as standard treatment for high-risk patients.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Why it works

In rheumatoid arthritis, the immune system attacks the joints, including those in the feet. Orthotics can offload inflamed joints and reduce pain.

~20% pain reduction
~30% function improvement

Woodburn et al. (2002) in Journal of Rheumatology showed significant pain reduction and improved function. Cochrane reviews give good/moderate quality assessment of the evidence.

Symptomatic Pes Cavus (High Arch)

Why it works

In pes cavus, the foot arch is abnormally high, causing increased pressure on the heel and forefoot. Orthotics can redistribute pressure and reduce pain.

~75% pain reduction

Cochrane reviews show good effect for symptomatic pes cavus. Note: this only applies when the condition causes symptoms - asymptomatic high arch does not require treatment.

3

Conditions With Moderate Evidence

Acute Injury Offloading

Temporary use during healing

For acute injuries such as stress fractures, tendinitis or joint injuries, orthotics can be used temporarily to offload the injured area while it heals.

Important: Orthotics should be weaned after healing. Long-term use can lead to muscle atrophy and dependency.

Postoperative Rehabilitation

After surgery

After certain foot surgeries, orthotics may be indicated during the rehabilitation period:

  • Hallux valgus surgery (bunion surgery)
  • Joint replacements in the foot
  • Reconstructive procedures

The surgeon will usually provide specific recommendations based on the procedure.

4

Conditions Where Evidence Is Weak

For the following conditions, evidence is weak or absent - despite orthotics being frequently prescribed:

"Overpronation"

Nielsen et al. (2013) randomized 927 novice runners to shoes based on foot type versus neutral shoes. Result: No difference in injury rates. Overpronation as a diagnosis lacks scientific basis.

Read more about the overpronation myth

Flexible Flat Foot Without Symptoms

Choosing Wisely (2023): "Avoid treatment of the normal variant asymptomatic flexible flat foot." Up to 26% of adults have flat feet - most without complaints. Treatment is unnecessary and potentially harmful.

Nonspecific Foot and Heel Pain

Whittaker et al. (2018) meta-analysis: Orthotics are no better than placebo in the long term. APTA (2023) does NOT recommend orthotics as isolated treatment for plantar fasciitis.

Knee, Hip or Back Pain

72-93% of podiatrists prescribe orthotics for these conditions - despite no evidence for effect. This represents significant overtreatment.

5

Checklist: Are Orthotics Right for You?

Orthotics may be right if:

  • You have diabetic neuropathy with foot ulcer risk
  • You have rheumatoid arthritis with foot joint involvement
  • You have symptomatic pes cavus (painful high arch)
  • You are rehabilitating after foot surgery
  • You have an acute injury that needs temporary offloading

Orthotics are probably NOT right if:

  • You have been diagnosed with "overpronation" without other symptoms
  • You have flexible flat feet without pain
  • You have nonspecific heel pain/plantar fasciitis
  • You have knee, hip or back pain
  • You have used orthotics for years without improvement
  • You were recommended orthotics after a quick assessment in a shoe store

If you're unsure, you should get a thorough evaluation from a professional who can provide an objective recommendation based on your individual situation - not based on what they sell.

Custom vs. Prefabricated?

A common argument for expensive custom orthotics is that they are "better" than cheap prefabricated ones. What does the research say?

Study Finding
Whittaker et al. (2018) No significant difference between custom and prefabricated
Guimaraes et al. (2023) Prefabricated as effective as custom-made
CADTH (2022) No evidence that custom orthotics are better

The price difference

Custom orthotics can cost 3,000-8,000 NOK, while prefabricated cost 300-800 NOK. Despite 5-10 times higher price, research shows no difference in effect. For most patients, prefabricated orthotics work just as well.

Exception: For complex deformities or special medical needs (e.g., diabetic neuropathy), custom orthotics may be necessary for optimal pressure relief.

7

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately for:

  • Sudden, intense foot pain without obvious cause
  • Swelling, redness and warmth combined with fever
  • Numbness or tingling that doesn't resolve
  • Foot ulcers that don't heal (especially with diabetes)
  • Significant change in foot shape over a short time
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with foot pain

These symptoms may indicate serious conditions such as infection, nerve damage, or systemic disease and require prompt medical evaluation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who benefits from orthotics?
Patients with diabetic neuropathy, rheumatoid arthritis and symptomatic pes cavus have strong evidence for benefit. Additionally, orthotics can be used temporarily for acute injuries and postoperative rehabilitation.
Are custom orthotics worth the price?
For most conditions, research shows no difference between custom and prefabricated orthotics. The exception is complex deformities or special medical needs where precise customization is critical.
Do orthotics work for overpronation?
Evidence is weak. Large studies (Nielsen 2013) showed no difference in injury rates when runners got shoes matched to foot type versus neutral shoes. "Overpronation" as a diagnosis lacks scientific basis.
Can I wean off orthotics?
Yes, for many people gradual weaning combined with foot exercises is a good alternative. But it should be done gradually over several months. If you have diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis or severe deformities, you should continue with orthotics.
Why do so many prescribe orthotics without evidence?
Several factors are at play: tradition, commercial interests, and genuine misunderstandings about foot biomechanics. Many practitioners learned that orthotics were standard treatment - research has since shown this is often not the case.
