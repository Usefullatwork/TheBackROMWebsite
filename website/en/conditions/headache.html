Headaches and Migraines – Causes, Types and Treatment

Everything you need to know about headaches | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua Oslo

Headache is pain in the head or upper neck. It's a symptom, not a disease in itself. According to studies, 75% of men and almost 90% of women report headache during the past year. On any given day, nearly 16% of the world's population has a headache.

Headaches are divided into two main categories: Primary headache (the headache is the problem itself – migraine, tension headache) and secondary headache (the headache is caused by something else – neck problems, muscle tension, jaw problems).

In fewer than 1% of people seeking help for headache, serious underlying disease is found.

Key Facts

  • 75-90% of the population has headache annually
  • Tension headache and migraine are most common
  • Neck headache responds well to chiropractic care
  • Trigger points cause headache in 90%+ with chronic tension headache

Most Common Headache Types

Tension Headache

78% experience it in their lifetime - Tension headache is by far the most common headache type. For most, it comes and goes, perhaps after a long day at the screen. But for some, it becomes chronic.

  • Feeling: Pressing or tight pain – like a tight band around the head
  • Intensity: Mild to moderate
  • Location: Both sides of the head
  • Often worse throughout the day
  • Does NOT worsen with normal physical activity

Common causes: Muscle tension in neck/shoulders/jaw, poor posture, stress, sleep deprivation, teeth grinding

Migraine

About 15% of women, 6-7% of men - Migraine is a neurological condition. Women are affected more often.

  • Feeling: Intense, throbbing or pulsating pain
  • Often on one side of the head
  • Nausea and/or vomiting
  • Extreme sensitivity to light and sound
  • Physical activity worsens the pain
  • Duration: 4–72 hours

Migraine with aura: 20-30% experience visual disturbances like flashing lights or zigzag patterns before the headache.

Cervicogenic Headache (Neck Headache)

15-20% of chronic headaches - Cervicogenic headache is caused by dysfunction in cervical vertebrae (C1-C3) or soft tissue in the neck. This is the headache type that responds best to chiropractic treatment.

  • Pain that starts in the neck and radiates upward toward forehead, temple or around the eye
  • Typically one-sided – rarely changes sides
  • Gets worse with certain neck movements or positions
  • Often accompanied by neck stiffness
  • Responds poorly to regular painkillers

Cluster Headache

0.1% of the population - Rare but extremely painful. Intense attacks lasting 15 minutes to 3 hours, often several times daily in "clusters" over weeks. Requires specialized treatment.

Muscles and Headache – Trigger Points

Muscle tension in neck, shoulder and jaw can cause referred pain to the head. Research shows trigger points (tender knots in muscles) reproduce headache in over 90% of patients with chronic tension headache.

Most Common Muscles That Cause Headache

  • Suboccipital muscles (92%): Pain inside head, forehead and behind eyes
  • Upper trapezius (44-85%): Temple, back of head and eyebrow
  • SCM – neck muscle (60-80%): Forehead, around eye, can also cause dizziness
  • Masseter and temporalis (36-60%): Temple, cheek, "sinus-like" pain
  • Splenius capitis (30-50%): Top of head, behind eye

Where Does It Hurt? Possible Causes:

  • Back of head: Often neck-related, suboccipital trigger points, occipital neuralgia
  • Temple: Tension headache, trapezius trigger points, TMJ/jaw joint
  • Top of head: Tension headache, splenius and semispinalis muscles
  • Around eye/forehead: Migraine, cluster headache, sinuses, suboccipital muscles
  • Whole head: Tension headache ("band around head")

Red Flags – When You MUST Contact a Doctor

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES IMMEDIATELY for:

  • "Thunderclap headache" – maximum pain within seconds
  • Headache + fever + neck stiffness – could be meningitis
  • Neurological symptoms: paralysis, speech difficulties, vision loss, confusion
  • Headache after head trauma

Book a regular doctor appointment for:

  • New headache after age 50 without previous headache history
  • Headache that gradually worsens over weeks
  • Headache that changes character drastically

Treatment of Headache

Chiropractic Treatment

Chiropractic care has documented effect for cervicogenic headache and tension headache with muscular component. A systematic review and meta-analysis showed moderate evidence for spinal manipulation in neck headache.

What I Do

  • Thorough examination of neck, jaw and shoulder
  • Joint mobilization and correction of upper cervical vertebrae
  • Soft tissue treatment of trigger points and tight muscles
  • Exercises for neck and posture
  • Ergonomic advice and preventive strategies

Medical Treatment of Migraine

Migraine often requires medical treatment from a doctor or neurologist.

  • Acute treatment: Triptans (like sumatriptan) are standard treatment for moderate to severe attacks
  • Preventive: Beta-blockers, candesartan, topiramate, amitriptyline
  • For chronic migraine: Botox or CGRP inhibitors

What You Can Do Yourself

Headache Diary

The most important tool for understanding your headache. Note when headache comes, intensity (1-10), symptoms, and possible triggers. After 1-2 months you often see patterns you didn't notice before.

Sleep

Sleep deprivation is the most commonly reported trigger for headache. Same bedtime and wake time every day, 7-9 hours of sleep, dark and quiet bedroom.

Physical Activity

Moderate aerobic exercise 3-5 times a week has documented preventive effect against headache – as good as several preventive medications.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a chiropractor treat headaches?

Yes, chiropractic care has documented effect for neck headache (cervicogenic headache) and tension headache with muscular component.

What is cervicogenic headache?

Cervicogenic headache is headache caused by dysfunction in the cervical vertebrae (C1-C3) or soft tissue in the neck. Pain starts in the neck and radiates toward forehead, temple or around the eye.

Can muscle tension cause headaches?

Yes, trigger points in neck, shoulder and jaw can cause referred pain to the head. Research shows trigger points reproduce headache in over 90% of patients with chronic tension headache.

Can dehydration cause headaches?

Yes, dehydration is a common headache trigger. When the body lacks sufficient fluids, the brain can temporarily shrink and pull away from the skull, causing pain. Drinking adequate water throughout the day can help prevent dehydration-related headaches.
