Cervicogenic Headache - When the Headache Comes from the Neck

Written by Mads Finstad, Chiropractor

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Headache caused by dysfunction in the neck - joints, muscles, or nerves in the upper cervical vertebrae
  • Most common causes: Stiffness in upper neck joints, trigger points in neck muscles, prolonged screen work
  • Chiropractic treatment: Good evidence - 71-81% achieve significant improvement with manual treatment and exercises
  • Red flags: Sudden intense headache, neurological deficits, fever with neck stiffness
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up
1

What is Cervicogenic Headache?

Cervicogenic headache is a collective term for headache that originates in the neck. The most common form is called cervicogenic headache, but tension headache can also be neck-related.

The pain occurs when structures in the upper neck - particularly the joints between C1, C2, and C3, as well as muscles and nerves in this area - send pain signals to the brain. Through what is called the trigeminocervical nucleus, nerve signals from the neck converge with signals from the facial nerve (trigeminal). This explains why pain from the neck can be felt in the forehead, temple, or around the eye.

How common is cervicogenic headache?

Studies show that 15-20% of all chronic headaches originate in the neck. Among people with extensive screen work, up to 65% report cervicogenic headache.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Cervicogenic headache can have several underlying causes:

Most Common Causes

  • Dysfunction in upper neck joints: Stiffness or malalignment in C1-C3 joints is the most common cause
  • Trigger points: Tender knots in neck and shoulder muscles that refer pain to the head
  • Prolonged screen work: Static posture in front of a computer increases the load on the neck
  • Whiplash: As many as 53% of people with whiplash injury develop cervicogenic headache
  • Sleep position: Wrong pillow height or sleeping position can trigger morning headache

Risk Factors

  • Sedentary work with extensive computer use
  • Previous neck injury
  • Stress and muscle tension
  • Poor ergonomics at the workplace
  • Lack of physical activity
3

Symptoms

Cervicogenic headache has characteristic features that distinguish it from other headache types:

Typical Characteristics

  • Starts in the neck: The pain often begins in the neck or back of the head and radiates forward
  • One-sided pain: The headache usually stays on the same side
  • Triggered by neck movements: Turning the head or holding it in a certain position provokes the headache
  • Tenderness on pressure: Pressure on upper cervical vertebrae or neck muscles worsens the headache
  • Reduced mobility: Especially rotation (turning the head) is often limited

The Radiation Pattern

The pain often follows a "ram's horn pattern" - from the neck via the back of the head, over the crown and temple to the forehead or the area around the eye.

4

Diagnosis

Cervicogenic headache is diagnosed primarily through medical history and clinical examination. There is no blood test or imaging that confirms the diagnosis.

Clinical examination includes:

  • Palpation of neck muscles and joints to identify tenderness and trigger points
  • Assessment of neck mobility
  • The flexion-rotation test - the most reliable test for cervicogenic headache
  • Neurological examination to rule out other causes

The Flexion-Rotation Test

This test has a sensitivity of 91% and specificity of 90% for detecting dysfunction in upper neck joints. A positive test shows reduced rotation below 30-32 degrees to the affected side.

5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Manual treatment has good evidence for cervicogenic headache. The treatment targets the underlying causes in the neck.

Expected Results

  • Combined manipulation + exercises: 81% achieve at least 50% reduction in headache frequency
  • Manipulation alone: 71% achieve significant improvement
  • Exercises alone: 76% achieve significant improvement

Treatment Methods

  • Joint manipulation: Restores normal mobility in stiff neck joints
  • Soft tissue treatment: Releases tight muscles and trigger points
  • Mobilization: Gentle techniques to improve joint mobility
  • Exercises: Specific exercises for neck and shoulders
  • Ergonomic advice: Optimization of workplace and habits

Treatment Course

Typical treatment course: 2-3 treatments per week over 4-6 weeks, totaling 8-12 treatments. Many notice improvement after just 2-4 treatments.

6

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Contact a doctor/emergency room immediately if:

  • Thunderclap headache - Sudden, violent headache that reaches maximum intensity within seconds
  • Fever and neck stiffness - May be a sign of meningitis
  • Neurological deficits - Weakness, numbness, speech difficulties, or visual disturbances
  • New headache type after age 50 - Increased risk of underlying disease
  • Headache that worsens over time - Progressive headache requires investigation

Prognosis

With proper treatment, 71-81% of patients achieve significant improvement. Many experience lasting relief after a treatment series.

Relapse risk: Cervicogenic headache tends to return, especially in people with persistently stressful work positions. Preventive measures and regular exercises are important for long-term results.

When Treatment Doesn't Work

About 20-25% do not respond satisfactorily to manual treatment. Alternatives include nerve blocks, acupuncture, or multidisciplinary pain management.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my headache comes from the neck?

Typical signs are that the pain starts in the neck and radiates forward toward the forehead or temple, that the headache is triggered by neck movements or certain positions, and that you have stiffness or reduced mobility in the neck.

Can a chiropractor help with cervicogenic headache?

Yes, manual treatment has good evidence for cervicogenic headache. Studies show that 71-81% achieve significant improvement with a combination of manipulation and exercises.

What is the difference between cervicogenic headache and migraine?

Cervicogenic headache is triggered by neck movements and often stays on one side. Migraine is typically pulsating, can change sides, and comes with nausea, light and sound sensitivity.

Do I need an MRI or X-ray?

Usually not. The diagnosis is made clinically through physical examination. Imaging often shows normal findings that do not explain the pain, and is primarily relevant for red flags.

How many treatments do I need?

Typically 8-12 treatments over 4-6 weeks. Many notice improvement after 2-4 treatments, but full effect often requires a longer course.
