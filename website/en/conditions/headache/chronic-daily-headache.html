Chronic Daily Headache

When the headache never stops - causes, types and ways out of the vicious cycle

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

You wake up with headache. Again. It has been like this for weeks, months - maybe years. Some days are worse than others, but the pain is always there like a heavy cloud. You've tried everything: painkillers, rest, massage. Maybe it helps a little in the moment, but the headache always comes back.

If this describes you, you are not alone. Chronic daily headache (CDH) affects between 3-5% of the adult population. It is a condition that steals quality of life, work capacity and joy - but which can often improve with the right approach.

Here you'll learn what chronic daily headache actually is, the most common causes, and what can actually help.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 3-5% of the population affected
  • Definition: Headache 15+ days/month for 3+ months
  • Medication overuse: Up to 80% of CDH patients
  • Four main types: Chronic migraine, tension, MOH, new daily persistent
  • Many get significantly better with proper treatment

What is Chronic Daily Headache?

Chronic daily headache (CDH) is not one diagnosis, but an umbrella term for all headache types that occur 15 or more days per month for more than 3 months. It is thus a description of frequency, not cause.

The condition is classified by the duration of each individual attack:

3-5% of population affected
15+ headache days per month
3+ months duration
2-3x more common in women

Chronic daily headache is one of the most disabling neurological conditions, significantly affecting work, family and quality of life. But there is hope - many get better with proper treatment.

2

The Four Main Types

ICHD-3 (International Classification of Headache Disorders) defines four main types of chronic daily headache with attack duration over 4 hours:

1. Chronic Migraine

Most common

Headache 15+ days/month, with at least 8 days having migraine features (one-sided, pulsating, nausea, light/sound sensitivity). Develops gradually from episodic migraine - about 2.5-3% progress annually.

  • Often aura or prodromal symptoms
  • Worsens with physical activity
  • Can respond to migraine preventive medications

2. Chronic Tension Headache

Bilateral, pressing/tightening pain that does not worsen with activity. Mild to moderate intensity. No nausea, at most one of light or sound sensitivity.

  • Can feel like a tight band around the head
  • Often associated with muscle tension in neck/shoulders and trigger points
  • Stress is an important trigger

3. Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

78-80% of CDH

The elephant in the room. Regular use of painkillers can paradoxically lead to more headache. Found in up to 78-80% of all chronic daily headache patients.

  • Triptans: 10+ days/month
  • Common painkillers: 15+ days/month
  • Combination preparations/opioids: 10+ days/month

4. New Daily Persistent Headache (NDPH)

Rare

Sudden onset with daily, unrelenting headache from day one. The patient often remembers exactly when it started. Can resemble tension headache or migraine, but is distinguished by the acute onset.

Important to Remember

Most with chronic daily headache do not have just one type - many have overlapping diagnoses. For example, a person may have chronic migraine complicated by medication overuse.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Chronic daily headache often develops gradually from episodic headache. Several factors increase the risk of chronification:

Modifiable Risk Factors

  • Medication overuse: The most important risk factor - use of acute painkillers more than 2-3 days per week
  • High attack frequency: 10+ headache days per month significantly increases the risk
  • Overweight: Correlation between BMI and headache frequency
  • Sleep disturbances: Snoring, sleep apnea and poor sleep quality
  • High caffeine intake: More than 200 mg/day
  • Stress and psychological factors: Depression and anxiety increase the risk

Non-modifiable Factors

  • Sex: Women are affected 2-3 times more often
  • Genetics: Family predisposition for headache
  • Previous head injury: Can trigger chronic headache

Central Sensitization

With chronic headache, changes occur in the brain's pain system - a "sensitization" that makes the nervous system react more strongly to stimuli that would not normally cause pain. This process can be reversed, but it takes time.

4

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Although chronic daily headache usually is caused by primary headache disorders, it is important to rule out serious causes:

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION PROMPTLY FOR:

  • New headache after age 50 - risk of serious causes increases
  • Gradually worsening headache over weeks/months
  • Headache that wakes you at night
  • Changed character in known headache patient
  • Neurological symptoms: visual disturbances, numbness, weakness
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, weight loss, general symptoms
  • Headache triggered by coughing, exertion or sexual activity

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

  • Thunderclap headache - "worst headache of my life" with acute onset
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness
  • Altered consciousness or confusion

With known chronic headache, it is change in the pattern that is most important to be aware of

5

Treatment

Step 1: Identify and Treat Medication Overuse

This is often the most important first step. In up to 80% of patients with chronic daily headache, medication overuse is present. Without addressing this, other treatment will have limited effect.

Discontinuation of Painkillers

Tapering or abrupt discontinuation of painkillers must occur under doctor supervision. Expect headache to worsen the first 1-2 weeks ("rebound headache") before improvement sets in. Most experience significant improvement after 6-8 weeks.

Step 2: Lifestyle Interventions

Lifestyle Measures With Documented Effect

  • Regular sleep: Fixed bedtimes and wake times, even on weekends
  • Regular meals: Don't skip meals
  • Physical activity: Regular aerobic exercise has preventive effect
  • Stress management: Mindfulness, relaxation, cognitive therapy
  • Limit caffeine: Max 200 mg/day, keep intake stable
  • Headache diary: Map triggers and medication use

Step 3: Preventive Treatment

With insufficient response to lifestyle measures, preventive medications may be relevant. This must be discussed with a doctor. Relevant medication groups include:

  • Beta-blockers (propranolol, metoprolol)
  • Antidepressants (amitriptyline, venlafaxine)
  • Antiepileptics (topiramate, valproate)
  • CGRP inhibitors (for chronic migraine)
  • Botulinum toxin (for chronic migraine)

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Manual treatment can be part of a holistic approach, especially for:

  • Neck-related headache (cervicogenic component)
  • Muscle tension in neck and shoulders
  • Desire for non-drug treatment

Honest About Evidence

Evidence for manual treatment of chronic daily headache is limited. What works best is a multimodal approach that combines lifestyle changes, medication discontinuation when needed, and possibly preventive treatment. Manual therapy can be a useful supplement for some patients.

6

Prognosis - Can It Get Better?

Yes, there is hope. Many with chronic daily headache experience significant improvement, especially with the right treatment approach:

26-27% with chronic migraine revert to episodic
75% improve after successful MOH discontinuation

Factors That Improve Prognosis

  • Successful discontinuation of medication overuse
  • Lower baseline frequency before chronification
  • Absence of depression and anxiety
  • Good sleep quality
  • Regular physical activity
  • Active participation in own treatment

It Takes Time

Improvement doesn't happen overnight. Expect gradual improvement over weeks to months. The most important thing is consistency in lifestyle changes and patience with the process.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

What is defined as chronic daily headache?
Chronic daily headache is defined as headache 15 or more days per month for more than 3 months. It is an umbrella term that encompasses several underlying diagnoses.
Can chronic daily headache be cured?
Many with chronic daily headache can improve significantly or revert to episodic form. Studies show that 26-27% with chronic migraine revert to episodic within 2 years. Success rates increase by addressing medication overuse and lifestyle factors.
Is medication overuse a common cause?
Yes, medication overuse headache (MOH) is found in up to 78-80% of patients with chronic daily headache. Regular use of painkillers 10-15+ days per month can paradoxically worsen the headache.
Does a chiropractor help with chronic headache?
Manual treatment can be part of a holistic approach, especially for neck-related components. Evidence is limited, but many experience improvement as part of multimodal treatment combined with lifestyle changes.
Do I need to stop all painkillers?
Not necessarily, but use must be limited. General recommendation is max 2-3 days per week for acute painkillers. With established medication overuse, a period of discontinuation or significant reduction may be necessary under doctor supervision.
