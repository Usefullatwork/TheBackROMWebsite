Cluster Headache: The Most Intense Pain

Symptoms, treatment and what the chiropractor can contribute

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Cluster headache is described as the worst pain a human can experience. On a pain scale from 1-10, most patients report 10+ during attacks. Some compare it to a red-hot knife through the eye.

The condition affects about 1 in 1000 Norwegians - approximately 5,500 people. The name comes from the fact that attacks typically come in "clusters" - intensive periods of weeks or months, followed by pain-free periods. This distinguishes it from other headache types.

With an average diagnostic delay of 5-7 years for many, this is a condition that requires awareness from all healthcare workers.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Extreme pain around one eye, 15-180 min
  • 1 in 1000 Norwegians affected
  • Treatment: Oxygen + triptans
  • Chiropractic role: Recognize and refer
  • Diagnostic delay: 5-7 years

What is Cluster Headache?

Cluster headache belongs to a group of headaches called trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias (TACs). The condition was first described by the Norwegian neurologist Ottar Sjaastad at St. Olavs Hospital in Trondheim.

The pain is always on one side (strictly unilateral) and localized around or behind the eye, in the temple or forehead. It is described as boring, burning or stabbing.

0.12% lifetime prevalence
5,500 Norwegians affected
15-180 minutes per attack
5-7 years diagnostic delay

Gender Distribution

A Norwegian registry study from 2024 showed a female:male ratio of 1.2:1. This differs from older studies showing 4:1 male predominance. The difference is likely because women were previously misdiagnosed with migraine.

2

Symptoms

Main Symptom: One-Sided, Extreme Pain

Attacks typically last 15-180 minutes if untreated, with an average of 45-90 minutes (ICHD-3, 2018).

Autonomic Accompanying Symptoms

During attacks, you will almost always see one or more of these signs on the same side as the pain:

  • Eye watering (lacrimation) - occurs in 73-91%
  • Eye becomes red (conjunctival injection) - 60-84%
  • Nose becomes blocked or runs - 42%
  • Eyelid droops or swells (ptosis) - 16-84%
  • Pupil becomes small (miosis)
  • Sweating on forehead or face

The Most Important Sign: Restlessness and Agitation

Here cluster headache differs dramatically from migraine. While migraine patients seek darkness and rest, cluster headache patients become extremely restless during attacks.

They pace restlessly, rock back and forth, hold their eye, or bang their head against the wall. This agitation is seen in up to 90% of patients.

Time Pattern

Attacks follow a striking circadian rhythm. A full 82% of patients experience attacks at exactly the same time each day, often between 9 PM and 3 AM. Many wake from the pain 1-2 hours after falling asleep.

The disease also has a seasonal rhythm - cluster periods often come in spring and fall, around the vernal and autumnal equinoxes.

3

Episodic vs. Chronic Form

Episodic cluster headache (80-90% of cases): Attacks come in periods ("clusters") of 2 weeks to 3 months, followed by at least 3 months of pain-free remission.

Chronic cluster headache (10-20% of cases): Attacks persist for over one year without sufficient breaks, or with remissions under 3 months.

Progression Between Forms

About 4-13% of episodic patients develop chronic form over time. Fortunately, 25-33% of chronic patients go the other way - back to episodic form.

4

Why Does Cluster Headache Occur?

Research points to the hypothalamus - the brain's biological clock - as the main cause. A groundbreaking PET study from 1998 (May et al., The Lancet) showed activation of the hypothalamus during attacks. This explains why attacks follow circadian and seasonal patterns so precisely.

During an attack, the trigeminovascular system is activated, and large amounts of CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide) - a pain signaling substance - are released.

Why Don't Regular Painkillers Work?

Attacks reach maximum intensity in 5-10 minutes and last only 15-180 minutes. Paracetamol and ibuprofen need 30-60 minutes to work. By the time they could have effect, the attack is often over by itself.

Even morphine has no effect. Therefore, specific, fast-acting treatments are required.

5

Diagnosis

Cluster headache is diagnosed primarily through thorough medical history. The diagnostic criteria from ICHD-3 (2018) require:

  • At least 5 attacks with severe one-sided pain around the eye
  • Duration 15-180 minutes untreated
  • At least one autonomic symptom on the same side (lacrimation, red eye, blocked nose, etc.)
  • Frequency from every other day to 8 times daily

The Problem: Long Diagnostic Delay

On average, it takes 5-7 years from first attack to correct diagnosis. A full 77% receive the wrong diagnosis first.

Common Misdiagnoses

  • Trigeminal neuralgia - 22% - see occipital neuralgia for similar neuralgia
  • Migraine - 19%
  • Sinusitis - 15% - see sinus migraine
  • Dental problems - significant proportion
6

Treatment

Acute Treatment - Break the Attack

1. High-Flow Oxygen (The Gold Standard)

100% pure oxygen at least 12-15 liters/minute through a non-rebreathing mask for 15-20 minutes.

  • 78% became pain-free after 15 minutes (vs 20% with placebo)
  • No side effects or cardiovascular contraindications
  • Can be used unlimited

2. Subcutaneous Sumatriptan (Imigran)

An injection (6 mg) in the thigh gives the fastest medicinal relief.

  • 75% achieved no or mild pain
  • Effect in 5-10 minutes
  • Tablets work too slowly for cluster headache

Preventive Treatment

Verapamil (heart medication) is first choice for preventing attacks, with response rates of 70-80%. ECG monitoring is necessary.

Galcanezumab (Emgality) - a CGRP inhibitor - was FDA-approved in 2019 for episodic cluster headache. Patients got 8.7 fewer attacks per week versus 5.2 with placebo.

7

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Let me be honest: Chiropractic does not treat cluster headache. This is a neurological disease in the hypothalamus that requires specific medical treatment.

But chiropractors have two important roles:

1. Recognize and Refer

With an average diagnostic delay of 5-7 years, rapid recognition can be life-changing. When you see a patient with:

  • Short, intense pain attacks around one eye
  • Red eye and lacrimation during attacks
  • Extreme restlessness (cannot lie still)
  • Attacks at the same time each day

...think cluster headache and refer to neurologist immediately.

2. Relieve Secondary Muscle Pain

A study by Solomon and Lipton (Headache Journal, 1990) found that among 100 cluster headache patients:

  • 47% had pain in or radiating to the neck - see cervicogenic headache
  • 40% had neck stiffness during attacks
  • 29% had neck tenderness

Many patients develop secondary muscle tension from tensing through brutal pain attacks, with trigger points that amplify the headache. These secondary issues the chiropractor can help with - but only as supplement to neurological treatment, never as replacement.

8

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

CONTACT DOCTOR/EMERGENCY IMMEDIATELY FOR:

  • Sudden, violent headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds ("thunderclap headache")
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness
  • Neurological deficits - weakness, speech difficulties, visual disturbances
  • Headache after head injury
  • New headache in people over 50

Frequently Asked Questions

Can cluster headache be confused with migraine?
Yes, 19% are first misdiagnosed with migraine. The main difference is behavior: migraine patients seek rest in dark rooms, while cluster headache patients pace restlessly during attacks.
Can stress trigger attacks?
No, stress is not a trigger for cluster headache. However, alcohol can trigger attacks within minutes during an active cluster period - but not in the remission phase.
Why do attacks come at night?
The hypothalamus controls circadian rhythm, and cluster headache is linked to this biological clock. Attacks often come 1-2 hours after falling asleep, possibly related to REM sleep.
Can I get oxygen at home in Norway?
Yes, but it requires a prescription and coordination with home healthcare or medical equipment supplier. Contact Headache Norway for practical guidance on proper equipment.
Does massage or chiropractic help?
No studies document effect of manual therapy on cluster headache itself. Such treatments can only help with secondary muscle tension, not replace medical treatment.
Can cluster headache go away on its own?
Rarely permanently, but remission periods can become longer with age. Some few experience spontaneous long-term remission. About 80% of episodic patients retain that form over 10 years.
