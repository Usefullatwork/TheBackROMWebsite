Headache in Back of Head

Causes, symptoms, and treatment of pain in the back of the head

Headache in the back of the head describes pain felt in the posterior part of the head - from the neck line up toward the crown. The pain can range from dull, pressing discomfort to sharp, stabbing pain.

Globally, approximately 2.9 billion people are affected by headache each year. Most cases are due to benign causes such as muscle tension or neck problems, but there are serious conditions that require prompt evaluation.

Anatomically, the back of the head is a complex area. The suboccipital muscles play a central role in many forms of headache. The upper neck (C1-C3) and the occipital nerve are frequent sources of pain.

Quick Summary

  • Tension headache most common (26-38%)
  • Often related to neck and muscles
  • 50-70% experience improvement with treatment
  • Moderate evidence for chiropractic treatment
  • Red flags: sudden intense headache, fever
1

Causes and Risk Factors

Tension Headache

Most Common - 26-38%

Tension headache is by far the most common type of headache. The pain is typically described as a band around the head that extends into the back of the head.

A study found that all patients with chronic tension headache had trigger points in the suboccipital muscles - 65% were active and 35% latent.

Risk factors: Sedentary work, stress, poor sleep, forward head posture during screen work.

Migraine

14% of population

Migraine affects approximately 14.4% of the global population. Although migraine is often associated with pain in the temple or forehead, the pain starts in the back of the head in about 18% of patients.

Typical signs: Unilateral, pulsating pain, nausea, light and sound sensitivity, worsening with physical activity.

Cervicogenic Headache (from the neck)

2.5-4% of population

Cervicogenic headache has a special place in Norwegian medical history. The Norwegian neurologist Ottar Sjaastad was among the first to systematically describe the condition.

Characteristics: The headache always starts in the neck and radiates forward toward the eye or forehead. The pain stays on one side (never switches sides). Neck mobility is often reduced.

Occipital Neuralgia

Rare

Occipital neuralgia is relatively rare, with an incidence of only 3.2 per 100,000 people. The condition is characterized by sharp, electric shock-like pain in the back of the head along the occipital nerve.

Typical: Intense, shooting pains lasting from seconds to minutes, tenderness over the occipital nerve behind the ear.

TMJ Disorders (TMD)

Problems in the jaw joint can cause referred pain to the back of the head. Many patients with TMD also have neck problems and tension headache.

Temporal Arteritis - Red Flag in People Over 50

Temporal arteritis is an inflammatory condition of blood vessels that requires urgent evaluation. Scandinavian populations have higher prevalence than the global average.

Warning signs: New-onset headache in people over 50, tenderness in the temple, jaw pain when chewing, visual disturbances, general feeling of illness.

2

Symptoms

The symptom picture varies depending on the cause:

Tension Headache

  • Dull, pressing pain on both sides
  • Often described as a tight band around the head
  • Usually worsens throughout the day

Cervicogenic Headache

  • Starts in the neck and radiates forward
  • Always on the same side
  • Limited neck mobility
  • Triggered by neck movements or positions

Occipital Neuralgia

  • Sharp, electric shock-like pain in the back of the head
  • Episodic character with pain-free intervals
  • Extreme tenderness along the nerve path

Migraine

  • Pulsating, often unilateral
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Light and sound sensitivity
  • Worsens with activity
3

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

A thorough clinical examination is the cornerstone of diagnosis. The chiropractor assesses neck mobility, palpates muscles and joints in the neck and head, and performs neurological tests.

For cervicogenic headache, the CHISG criteria are used: unilateral headache that doesn't switch sides, pain triggered by neck movements or pressure on the upper neck, reduced neck mobility, and pain that starts in the neck and radiates forward.

When is Imaging Necessary?

X-ray and MRI are rarely necessary for common headache. The American Headache Society does not recommend routine imaging for typical tension headache or migraine with normal neurological examination.

It's important to know that MRI and X-ray often show "normal findings" such as wear and degeneration - these are common age-related changes that rarely explain the headache.

4

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

SEEK MEDICAL HELP IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • Sudden, intense headache ("the worst headache of my life")
  • Fever combined with neck stiffness (may indicate meningitis)
  • Visual disturbances, double vision, or vision loss
  • Neurological deficits such as weakness, numbness, speech difficulties
  • New-onset headache after age 50 - evaluate for temporal arteritis
  • Headache after head injury
  • Headache that worsens with coughing, sneezing, or exertion
  • Personality changes or confusion

Guidelines use the SNOOP mnemonic: Systemic symptoms, Neurological signs, Onset sudden, Older age >50, Pattern change

5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Chiropractic treatment of headache focuses on the neck and upper back. Treatment may include:

  • Joint mobilization or manipulation
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight muscles
  • Trigger point treatment in suboccipital muscles
  • Exercises to improve function

Evidence for Treatment

Cervicogenic headache: The evidence is strongest here. A meta-analysis analyzed 20 randomized studies with 1,439 patients. Findings showed moderate to large short-term effect on pain intensity and headache frequency. Effects persisted beyond 3 months, but to a lesser degree.

Tension headache: The evidence is more nuanced. A meta-analysis found that high-velocity manipulation alone was not effective for tension headache, but soft tissue treatment showed good effect. Trigger point treatment in the suboccipital muscles may be useful for chronic tension headache.

Realistic Expectations

Approximately 50-70% of patients with cervicogenic headache experience clinically meaningful improvement. It's important to be aware that relapse is common - several guidelines point out that cervicogenic headache tends to recur.

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Chin tucks: Pull your chin backward as if making a "double chin," hold for 5 seconds, repeat 10 times. Do this 5-7 times daily.
  • Deep neck flexor exercises: Lie on your back with slightly bent knees. Make a gentle nodding motion. Hold for 10-15 seconds, repeat 10 times.
  • Ergonomic measures: Screen at eye level, arm's length away. Follow the 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 meters away for 20 seconds.

Treatment Course

For cervicogenic headache, research recommends a structured approach: first 4 weeks with 2 times per week focusing on upper neck joint treatment (C1-C3), then weeks 5-8 with 1 time per week and deep neck flexor exercises, followed by maintenance as needed with home exercise programs.

6

Prognosis

Most people with headache in the back of the head have a good prognosis. Tension headache and cervicogenic headache often respond well to treatment, especially when patients also take self-care measures.

Realistic Expectations:

  • 50-70% with cervicogenic headache experience clinically meaningful improvement with treatment
  • Short-term effects are often greater than long-term effects
  • Relapse is common - maintenance strategies are important
  • Combination of treatment and self-care gives the best results

For chronic headache (lasting over 12 weeks), it may take longer to achieve improvement. Early treatment often leads to faster recovery - there's no reason to "wait and see" with new headache complaints.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can stress cause headache in the back of the head?
Yes, stress is one of the most important triggers for tension headache. Stress often leads to increased tension in neck and shoulder muscles, which in turn can cause pain in the back of the head.
When should I see a chiropractor for headache?
You can see a chiropractor for headache that you suspect comes from the neck or muscles - that is, when the pain worsens with neck movements, sedentary work, or muscle tension. For sudden, severe headache or neurological symptoms, you should contact a doctor first.
How long does it take for treatment to help?
Most people notice some improvement within the first 2-4 weeks. For cervicogenic headache, research shows that optimal effect is achieved after 12-18 treatments over 3-6 months. The effect is best maintained with home exercises.
Is headache in the back of the head dangerous?
The vast majority of cases are due to benign causes such as muscle tension or neck problems. Sudden, intense headache, headache with fever and neck stiffness, or new-onset headache in people over 50 should always be evaluated by a doctor.
Does exercise help headache in the back of the head?
Yes, specific exercises for neck muscles have good documentation. Chin tucks and deep neck flexor training can reduce both the frequency and intensity of headache over time.
Do I need an MRI or X-ray?
Rarely for typical headache. Imaging is primarily relevant for alarm symptoms (red flags) or abnormal findings on examination. It's important to know that MRI often shows age-related changes that don't explain the pain.
