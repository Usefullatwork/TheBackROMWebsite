Headache and Dizziness

When they occur together - causes and treatment

Quick Summary

  • Over 51% of headache patients also have dizziness
  • Most common: Vestibular migraine or neck problems
  • Moderate evidence for manual treatment for neck-related dizziness
  • Red flags: Sudden headache, balance problems, double vision
  • Thorough assessment is crucial

Headache and dizziness are each among the most common complaints people seek healthcare for. When they occur simultaneously, it is rarely coincidental.

A 2010 study showed that 51.1% of people with recurring headaches also experience dizziness as an accompanying symptom. The combination occurs three times more often than expected if the conditions were independent of each other.

The problem is that the combination has many possible causes - from completely harmless muscular tension as in tension headache to serious pathology requiring immediate attention. Therefore, thorough assessment is essential.

1

What is the combination of headache and dizziness?

Headache and dizziness are each among the most common complaints people seek healthcare for. When they occur simultaneously, it is rarely coincidental.

51% of headache patients have dizziness
3x more often together than by chance
8.4 years average diagnostic delay

Why do they occur together?

Headache and dizziness share many common nerve pathways and structures. Both migraine and the balance organ are closely connected to the brainstem. The neck contains many proprioceptors that communicate with both headache and balance centers in the brain.

2

Causes - from most to least common

1. Vestibular Migraine

Vestibular migraine is the most common cause of episodic dizziness in adults, affecting 0.9-2.7% of the population. Among migraine patients, 10.3% have vestibular migraine.

What is unusual about this condition is that headache and dizziness often do not occur simultaneously. On average, 8 years pass from the first migraine until vestibular symptoms develop.

  • Dizziness episodes lasting from minutes to hours
  • Light and sound sensitivity during attacks
  • Worsening with head movements
  • Often motion sickness throughout life
  • Women are affected 3-5 times more often than men

Note: Vestibular migraine is highly underdiagnosed. Only 10-20% of patients receive the correct diagnosis.

2. Cervicogenic Dizziness (neck-related)

Dizziness caused by neck problems affects 40% of patients with neck pain. Of these, 59.7% were related to myofascial pain syndrome (muscle tension with trigger points). This often overlaps with cervicogenic headache.

The neck contains extremely many proprioceptors (position sensors) - especially in the small muscles at the top toward the skull. When these send incorrect signals, a conflict arises between what the neck "tells" the brain and what the balance organ in the ear registers.

  • Dizziness with head movements
  • Neck pain or stiffness
  • Often triggered by positions or activities
  • SCM trigger points can cause both headache and dizziness

3. BPPV with Secondary Headache

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) is the most common single cause of dizziness in general - responsible for about 50% of all dizziness consultations.

34.8% of BPPV patients also report headache. Migraine patients have twice the risk of developing BPPV compared to the general population.

4. Tension Headache with Dizziness

Tension headache is the most common headache type overall. Many experience a nonspecific dizziness or "foggy head" along with the headache - especially with tight neck and shoulder muscles.

5. Medication Side Effects

Over 1000 medications have dizziness as a known side effect, and over 2000 have headache on the list. Common culprits include:

  • Blood pressure medication (especially amlodipine)
  • Pain relievers when overused - see medication overuse headache
  • Antidepressants (especially when starting or stopping)
  • Antiepileptic medications

6. Less Common Causes

Meniere's disease (0.2% of population), vestibular neuritis, and persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD) are less common causes of the combination.

3

Red Flags - when you MUST see a doctor

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES IF:

  • Thunderclap headache: Severe headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds ("the worst headache of my life")
  • Problems walking or standing: Unsteadiness or inability to maintain balance
  • Neurological deficits: Double vision, speech difficulties, swallowing problems, numbness, weakness
  • Fever combined with neck stiffness: May indicate meningitis
  • Sudden neck/back of head pain after neck movement: May indicate vertebral artery dissection

Posterior fossa stroke (stroke in brainstem/cerebellum) is misdiagnosed in 35-37% of cases. The classic warning signs are the "5 D's": Dizziness, Diplopia, Dysarthria, Dysphagia, and Dysmetria.

4

Diagnosis

The combination of headache and dizziness requires thorough assessment to distinguish between the various causes:

Vestibular Migraine - Diagnostic Criteria

  • At least 5 episodes of vestibular symptoms of moderate to severe intensity
  • Duration 5 minutes to 72 hours
  • Current or previous migraine
  • At least one migraine feature during 50% of episodes

Cervicogenic Dizziness

Diagnosis is made by ruling out other causes and demonstrating a connection between neck problems and dizziness:

  • Dizziness triggered by neck positions or movements
  • Neck pain or stiffness present
  • Other vestibular causes ruled out
  • Positive response to neck treatment

Special Examinations

When vestibular causes are suspected, VNG examination (videonystagmography) and TRV chair can be useful for mapping balance function. Chiropractor Mads Finstad has access to this equipment for thorough diagnostics.

5

Treatment

For Cervicogenic Dizziness (moderate evidence)

What can the chiropractor do?

A systematic review of 4 randomized studies shows that manual treatment is effective for neck-related dizziness. Studies showed significant improvement in dizziness intensity and frequency that persisted at 12-month follow-up.

  • Specific neck techniques targeting the upper cervical spine
  • Trigger point treatment of SCM and suboccipital muscles
  • Mobilization of stiff neck joints
  • Exercises to normalize proprioceptive function

For Vestibular Migraine (limited evidence)

There are only case reports and small case series on manual treatment for vestibular migraine. However, many patients have a neck component that can be addressed. Medication prophylaxis may be relevant in collaboration with a doctor.

For BPPV (strong evidence)

Repositioning Maneuvers

BPPV is treated with specific repositioning maneuvers - most often the Epley maneuver. Most people become symptom-free after 1-3 treatments.

Treatment Course

  • Cervicogenic dizziness: Typically 2-6 treatments over 6 weeks
  • BPPV: Often 1-3 treatments
  • Vestibular migraine: Requires multidisciplinary approach
6

Prognosis

Cervicogenic Dizziness

With proper treatment, most experience significant improvement. Studies show significant improvement in dizziness frequency and intensity at 12-month follow-up. Relapses can occur, especially with repeated strain on the neck.

Vestibular Migraine

Vestibular migraine is a chronic condition that often requires long-term follow-up. Episode frequency can be significantly reduced with proper treatment and lifestyle changes, but the condition rarely disappears completely.

BPPV

Excellent prognosis - most become symptom-free with repositioning treatment. Relapses occur in 30-50% within 5 years, but the condition responds equally well to repeated treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do headache and dizziness often occur together?

Headache and dizziness occur together three times more often than by chance. Over 51% of people with recurring headaches also experience dizziness. The most common causes are vestibular migraine and cervicogenic dizziness from the neck.

What is vestibular migraine?

Vestibular migraine is the most common cause of episodic dizziness in adults and affects 0.9-2.7% of the population. The condition causes dizziness episodes that can last from minutes to hours, often with light and sound sensitivity. Headache and dizziness often do not occur simultaneously.

Can a chiropractor help with dizziness?

For cervicogenic dizziness (neck-related), there is moderate evidence that manual treatment is effective. Studies show significant improvement in dizziness intensity and frequency that persists at 12-month follow-up. For BPPV, repositioning maneuvers are highly effective.

What is cervicogenic dizziness?

Cervicogenic dizziness is caused by neck problems and affects about 40% of patients with neck pain. The neck contains many proprioceptors, and when these send incorrect signals, a conflict arises between the neck and the balance organ in the ear.

When should I see a doctor?

Seek medical attention immediately for sudden severe headache, problems with walking/balance, double vision, speech difficulties, numbness, fever with neck stiffness, or sudden neck/back of head pain after neck movement.
