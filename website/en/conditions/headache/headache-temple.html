Temple Headache

Causes, symptoms and treatment of pain in the temple region

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Tension headache most common (26-33%)
  • Migraine affects 11-15%
  • TMJ disorders 5-10%
  • Moderate evidence for manual treatment
  • Red flag: new headache after age 50
Book assessment

Temple headache is pain localized to the lateral part of the head, where the temporalis muscle covers the skull. This fan-shaped muscle extends from the temple region down to the lower jaw and is involved in chewing and jaw movements.

Temple headache is not a single diagnosis, but a symptom that can have many different causes. The most common cause is tension headache, which affects about one in four adults annually.

The temporalis muscle is particularly prone to developing trigger points - tender nodules in the musculature that can cause referred pain to the temple, eyebrow and even the teeth. Studies show that up to 87% of patients with chronic tension headache have active trigger points in the temporalis.

1

Causes and Risk Factors

Tension Headache

Most common - 26-33%

Tension headache is the most frequent cause of temple pain. The pain is typically described as a pressing band around the head, often bilateral. It peaks in the 30-39 age range and is somewhat more common in women.

Triggering factors: Stress, poor sleep, prolonged screen work and muscular tension in neck and shoulders. Trigger points in the upper trapezius can cause referred pain in a characteristic "ram's horn" pattern.

Migraine

11-15%

Migraine affects 11-15% of the population annually. Women are affected about three times as often as men. Migraine pain is typically pulsating and often unilateral, commonly localized to the temple.

Migraine differs from tension headache in that it is often accompanied by nausea, light and sound sensitivity, and worsens with physical activity. Many also experience warning signs (aura) with visual disturbances before the headache. Some also experience vestibular migraine with dizziness.

TMJ Disorders (TMD)

5-10%

Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) cause clinically significant symptoms in 5-10% of the population. Women are affected 2-4 times more often than men, with highest prevalence in the 20-40 age range.

The relationship between TMD and headache is bidirectional: approximately 62% of TMD patients report headache, and 59% of headache patients have signs of TMD involvement.

Typical symptoms: Jaw pain, clicking sounds when opening mouth wide, limited mouth opening, and headache that worsens with chewing or jaw use.

Cervicogenic Headache

0.4-4%

Cervicogenic headache originates from structures in the neck and refers pain to the head, including the temple. Population prevalence is 0.4-4%, but accounts for 15-20% of chronic headache cases in clinical practice.

Characteristics: Unilateral headache starting in the neck, reduced neck movement, and worsening with certain neck positions. The headache usually does not switch sides.

Temporal Arteritis - rare but serious

Temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis) is rare but requires immediate attention. Scandinavian populations have the world's highest prevalence: 28-33 per 100,000. Untreated, temporal arteritis can lead to permanent vision loss in up to 20% of patients.

2

Symptoms

Symptoms of temple headache vary depending on the underlying cause:

Tension Headache

  • Pressing, tightening pain
  • Often bilateral like a band around the head
  • No nausea
  • Mild to moderate intensity

Migraine

  • Pulsating, throbbing pain
  • Often unilateral
  • Nausea, light and sound sensitivity
  • Moderate to severe intensity
  • Lasts 4-72 hours

TMD/Jaw Problems

  • Dull pain in the temple and jaw
  • Worsens with chewing
  • Clicking sounds in jaw joint
  • Jaw stiffness

Cervicogenic Headache

  • Unilateral, starts in the neck
  • Reduced neck movement
  • Triggered by neck positions
  • Does not switch sides

Overlapping Causes

Many patients have overlapping causes. Studies show that trigger points in the temporalis muscle can refer pain to the temple, eyebrow and even the teeth - which can be confused with dental problems or sinus infections.

3

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of temple headache is primarily based on medical history and clinical examination. The chiropractor will map the pain's character, duration, triggering factors and any accompanying symptoms.

Clinical Examination

The examination includes assessment of neck movement, palpation of musculature in neck, jaw and head, as well as neurological screening. The Flexion-Rotation Test (FRT) and neck flexion strength can help distinguish cervicogenic headache from migraine.

For TMD-related headache, diagnostic criteria (DC/TMD) have shown 89% sensitivity and 87% specificity: headache in the temple region that is affected by jaw movement, and which can be reproduced by palpation of the chewing musculature.

When is imaging appropriate?

MRI or CT of the head is not indicated for typical primary headache with normal neurological examination and stable pattern. Only 0.3% of headache patients with normal screening have significant findings on imaging.

Imaging is appropriate for thunderclap headache, new headache after age 50, neurological deficits, or progressive worsening.

4

Red Flags - when you should see a doctor

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • New headache after age 50 - risk of temporal arteritis
  • Visual disturbances - blurred vision, double vision, transient vision loss
  • Jaw claudication - jaw pain that comes after 2-3 minutes of chewing (unlike TMD which causes immediate pain)
  • Tenderness over the temporal artery - visible, pulsating blood vessel in the temple that is tender to touch
  • Headache with fever, neck stiffness - possible infection
  • Thunderclap headache - sudden, severe headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds

Temporal arteritis can cause permanent vision loss in up to 20% of untreated patients. Scandinavia has the world's highest prevalence.

5

Treatment

Treatment approach depends on the underlying cause. Early treatment can often provide faster improvement than waiting.

For Cervicogenic Headache

Evidence is strongest here. A systematic review of 20 RCT studies found moderate evidence that manipulation provides significant short-term effect on pain intensity. One important study showed that 72% of patients achieved at least 50% reduction in headache frequency at 12-month follow-up.

For Tension Headache

Evidence is weaker for manipulation alone. A meta-analysis found that soft tissue techniques were better than no treatment (low evidence), while manipulation did not show significant effect compared to control. Dry needling showed moderate evidence for effect.

For TMD-Related Headache

Research suggests that combined treatment of jaw and neck is more effective than neck treatment alone. Trigger point treatment of temporalis and masseter can reduce both local tenderness and referred temple pain.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Self-Examination of Temporalis

Place your fingertips on the temple, above and in front of the ear. Clench your teeth - you should feel the muscle tighten. Press with moderate force for 2 seconds on the front, middle and back parts. Assess tenderness. Then massage with circular movements for 30-60 seconds.

Jaw Exercises

Controlled mouth opening with tongue relaxed against the palate, sideways jaw movements, and relaxation exercises ("lips together, teeth apart") have moderate evidence from international consensus.

Neck Exercises

Craniocervical flexion exercise (deep neck flexion) has moderate to high evidence for cervicogenic headache. Lie on your back, perform gentle chin tuck, hold 10 seconds, 10 repetitions.

Treatment Course

For cervicogenic headache, research suggests that 8-10 treatments over 6 weeks is effective. More frequent treatment (3-4 times per week) shows greater improvement than weekly treatment. Combined manual therapy and exercises provide approximately 10% additional effect compared to each treatment alone.

6

Prognosis

Prognosis for temple headache is generally good, but varies depending on cause and duration.

Tension Headache

A long-term study showed that almost half of patients with frequent or chronic tension headache were pain-free after 3 years. However, relapses are common, and many experience periods of worsening.

Cervicogenic Headache

Without treatment, the condition tends to persist. With treatment, most achieve significant improvement, but long-term maintenance strategies are often necessary.

Important to Remember

Long-term results are often comparable between different treatment methods after 6 months. The most important thing is to find an approach that works for you and that you can follow up over time.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do I have pain on only one side of my temple?
Unilateral temple pain is typical for migraine (60-70% of cases), cervicogenic headache and TMD. Migraine can switch sides between attacks, while cervicogenic headache usually stays on the same side. With TMD, pain is often on the side with the most jaw joint problems.
Can temple headache come from the teeth?
Yes, trigger points in the temporalis muscle can refer pain to the teeth, especially the upper front teeth and molars. This can be confused with dental problems. At the same time, dental problems like teeth grinding (bruxism) can strain the temporalis and contribute to headache.
Do I need MRI or X-ray?
Usually not. For typical primary headache without alarm symptoms, imaging rarely provides useful information. Clinical examination is often more important than images. Imaging is appropriate for red flags or atypical symptoms.
What is the difference between temporal arteritis and TMD?
Both can cause temple pain, but there are important differences: TMD causes pain immediately when chewing, while temporal arteritis causes pain after 2-3 minutes of chewing (jaw claudication). Temporal arteritis almost exclusively affects people over 50 years old and may be accompanied by visual disturbances.
Can stress cause temple headache?
Absolutely. Stress is one of the most important triggering factors for tension headache. Stress increases tension in neck and jaw musculature, including the temporalis. This can lead to trigger point formation and referred pain to the temple.
How long does treatment last?
For cervicogenic headache, research suggests that 8-10 treatments over 6 weeks is effective. Many notice improvement already after 2-3 treatments. For persistent problems, maintenance treatment may be appropriate.
