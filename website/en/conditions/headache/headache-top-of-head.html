Headache on Top of the Head

Causes, symptoms and treatment of pain on the crown

Headache localized to the top of the head - called the vertex - is a common symptom that most people experience during their lifetime. Globally, 2.9 billion people are affected by headache disorders, and vertex-localized pain is one of the most common presentations.

Many describe this type of headache as "pressure on top of the head," "something pressing down on the crown" or a feeling of tightness around the head. The pain can be constant and aching, or come and go in periods.

What often surprises patients is that the pain on top of the head rarely originates from structures in the scalp itself. In most cases, the pain comes from the neck - either from muscle tension, trigger points or dysfunction in the upper cervical vertebrae.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Tension headache most common (46% lifetime)
  • Pain often comes from the neck
  • Splenius capitis refers pain to the crown
  • Moderate effect with treatment + exercises
  • Red flag: sudden intense headache

Causes and Risk Factors

Tension Headache

Most common - 46% lifetime

Tension headache is by far the most common cause of headache on top of the head. Lifetime prevalence is 46% globally, and Danish studies show up to 78%. Women are affected twice as often as men.

Typical presentation is bilateral, pressing or tightening pain - often described as a "band around the head" that can focus on the top. The pain is mild to moderate and does not worsen with physical activity.

Risk factors: Stress, poor sleep, prolonged screen work and static work postures.

Trigger Points in Neck Musculature

Referred pain

Muscles in the neck can refer pain to the top of the head through a phenomenon called "referred pain." Read more about trigger points and headache.

Splenius capitis is the primary muscle that sends pain specifically to the crown. Trigger points in this muscle - located approximately at the C3 level - create a characteristic pain sensation in a 3-5 cm zone in the middle of the top of the head. Patients often describe this as "internal head pain" or "pressure on the crown."

Suboccipital muscles (the small muscles at the very top of the neck) are found to have active trigger points in 65% of patients with chronic tension headache. These muscles have a direct anatomical connection to the meninges.

Cervicogenic Headache

2.5-4.1% of population

Cervicogenic headache is caused by dysfunction in the upper cervical vertebrae (C1-C3) and surrounding structures. The Norwegian Vaga study found 4.1% prevalence.

This type of headache typically starts in the neck and radiates upward toward the top of the head on one side. The pain worsens with neck movements and sustained postures.

Occipital Neuralgia

Rare

Occipital neuralgia causes sharp, shooting pain from the back of the head that can radiate to the top of the head. The pain follows the course of the occipital nerve and is often unilateral with periods of intense "lightning-like" pain.

Medication Overuse Headache

0.5-7.2%

With frequent use of pain relievers - especially paracetamol, NSAIDs or triptans more than 10-15 days per month - headache can paradoxically worsen and become daily. Read more about medication overuse headache.

Symptoms

Headache on top of the head can vary significantly depending on the underlying cause:

With Tension Headache

  • Pressing, tightening feeling - like a band around the head or "helmet" on top
  • Mild to moderate intensity
  • Little affected by physical activity
  • Can last hours to days

With Neck-Related Causes

  • Pain often starts in the neck and radiates upward
  • Worsens with neck movements or sustained positions
  • May be accompanied by neck stiffness

Splenius Capitis - pain to the crown

Common causes of splenius trigger points are working with multiple monitors (requires head rotation), forward head posture and sleeping on the stomach with a thick pillow.

3

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination is Crucial

Diagnosis of headache on top of the head is based primarily on medical history and clinical examination. A chiropractor will assess neck mobility, palpate the musculature for trigger points, and examine the upper cervical joints for dysfunction.

Imaging

MRI or X-ray of the neck is rarely necessary for typical presentations. Images often show normal findings like wear or degenerative changes - findings that are equally common in people without pain and therefore rarely explain the symptoms.

4

Red Flags - when you should see a doctor

CONTACT A DOCTOR OR EMERGENCY ROOM IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • Sudden, intense headache ("the worst headache of my life") - may be brain hemorrhage
  • Neurological symptoms: Visual disturbances, double vision, numbness, weakness, speech difficulties
  • Fever and stiff neck - may indicate meningitis
  • New headache after age 50 - increased risk of serious causes
  • Increasing headache over days/weeks
  • Headache that worsens with coughing, sneezing or exertion

These red flags are part of the internationally recognized SNNOOP10 criteria for identifying serious secondary causes of headache.

5

Treatment

Treatment of headache on top of the head focuses on the underlying causes - especially neck structures and musculature.

For Cervicogenic Headache

Manual treatment has moderate evidence for short-term effect. A 2022 systematic review with 20 studies and 1439 patients found large effect on pain intensity in the short term compared to placebo.

Important to note: At 12-month follow-up, manual treatment alone did not show better effect than placebo. Neck exercises, however, showed sustained effect.

For Tension Headache

Evidence for manual treatment is more limited. 2011 guidelines concluded that manipulation cannot be recommended for episodic tension headache, but that low-intensity mobilization may be useful for chronic problems.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Posture Correction

Avoid forward head posture during screen work. Make sure the screen is at eye level and take regular breaks.

Neck Exercises

Gentle stretches and strengthening exercises for the neck have documented effect. Especially exercises that strengthen the deep neck flexors can be useful.

Stress Management

For tension headache, relaxation, mindfulness or other stress management techniques can help.

Limit Pain Medication

Avoid daily use of paracetamol or NSAIDs to prevent medication overuse headache.

Treatment Course

Dose-response studies for cervicogenic headache found that 8-10 treatments over 6 weeks was effective. For most patients, you can expect immediate relief after the first treatment, significant improvement after 4-8 weeks, and full effect after 6-8 weeks of treatment and exercises.

6

Prognosis

Most people with headache on top of the head have a good prognosis. Tension headache and neck-related causes often respond well to treatment.

Short-term

Good probability of improvement with treatment - one study found that 72% of patients with cervicogenic headache maintained at least 50% reduction in headache frequency after 12 months.

Relapses are Common

Headache tends to return, especially with persistent risk factors like stress and static work. This does not mean treatment has failed - it means prevention and self-management are important.

Long-term

Evidence suggests that different treatments often give similar results over time. The most important factor for sustained improvement appears to be regular exercises and lifestyle changes.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do I have pain on top of my head?
The most common causes are tension headache and pain referred from neck musculature - especially the splenius capitis muscle which sends pain directly to the crown. Even though it feels like the pain comes from the head, it often starts in the neck.
Can a chiropractor help with headache?
Yes, especially for headache related to the neck. Systematic reviews show moderate effect in the short term for cervicogenic headache. Treatment combined with exercises gives the best long-term results.
Do I need MRI or X-ray?
Rarely. For typical presentation without red flags, imaging rarely provides useful information. Findings like wear and degeneration are equally common in people without pain.
How many treatments do I need?
It varies, but studies show that 8-10 treatments over 6 weeks is typical for cervicogenic headache. Many notice improvement after 2-4 treatments.
Can stress cause headache on top of the head?
Absolutely. Stress is one of the most important risk factors for tension headache. Stress leads to muscle tension in neck and shoulder, which in turn can cause headache.
What is the difference between migraine and tension headache?
Tension headache is typically bilateral (both sides), pressing/tightening, mild-moderate, and does not worsen with physical activity. Migraine is often unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, and worsens with activity - often with nausea and light/sound sensitivity.
