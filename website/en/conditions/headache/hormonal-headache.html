Hormonal Headache and Menstrual Migraine

When hormones trigger headache - understanding and treatment

Conditions / Headache / Hormonal Headache

You know it's coming. A few days before your period starts, the headache arrives - perhaps with full migraine, perhaps an aching tension headache. It happens every month, so predictable you could mark it on your calendar.

If this sounds familiar, you are far from alone. Approximately half of all women with migraine experience a clear connection between attacks and their menstrual cycle. Hormonal fluctuations are one of the most important headache triggers in women.

Here you will learn why hormones affect headaches, how to recognize the pattern, and what can actually help.

1

What is hormonal headache?

Hormonal headache is a collective term for headaches triggered or worsened by hormonal fluctuations. The most common form is menstrual migraine, but hormones can also affect tension headache and other headache types.

Why hormones trigger headache

The main culprit is estrogen - or more precisely: the rapid drop in estrogen levels just before menstruation. This hormonal shift affects neurotransmitters in the brain, particularly serotonin, and can trigger migraine attacks in women who are genetically predisposed.

It's not the absolute levels that are the problem, but the fluctuations. That's why stable hormone levels (as in pregnancy or after menopause) can often bring improvement.

50%+ of migraine women have hormonal connection
3x more women than men have migraine
7-14% have pure menstrual migraine
60%+ improvement during pregnancy
2

Types of hormone-related headache

Pure menstrual migraine

7-14% of migraine women

Migraine attacks that only occur in connection with menstruation (day -2 to +3 of the cycle). No attacks at other times. Often characterized by longer duration and poorer response to triptans than regular migraine.

Menstrually-related migraine

Most common - approx. 50%

Migraine attacks that often occur around menstruation, but also occur at other times in the cycle. There must be a clear increase in attack frequency around the period to qualify.

Hormonal headache from birth control

Headache that occurs during the pill-free week when estrogen levels drop. Can also be experienced when starting or switching contraception.

Hormonal headache in menopause

Unpredictable hormonal fluctuations in perimenopause can cause irregular headaches. Many experience worsening during this phase, but improvement after menopause. Some develop chronic daily headache during this period.

How to know if you have menstrual migraine?

Keep a headache diary for at least 3 months and note attack days as well as menstruation dates. A clear pattern where attacks consistently occur on days -2 to +3 of the cycle supports the diagnosis.

3

Headache through the lifespan

Hormonal headache changes throughout life in step with the major hormonal shifts:

Menarche (first period)

Migraine often debuts in puberty. Before puberty, migraine is equally common in boys and girls - afterward it becomes 3x more common in women.

Fertile years

Menstrual migraine is most common. Many experience that attacks are worst during the first days of the period. Birth control can both help and worsen.

Pregnancy

60-70% experience improvement, especially in the 2nd and 3rd trimester when hormone levels are stable and high. Some experience onset or worsening.

After birth

Rapid drop in estrogen can cause migraine relapse shortly after birth. Breastfeeding can provide protection due to stable hormones.

Perimenopause

Often a difficult period with unpredictable fluctuations. Many experience worsening of migraine during this phase.

After menopause

Many experience significant improvement or their migraine disappears when hormone levels stabilize at a lower level.

4

Treatment

Acute treatment

Menstrual migraine often responds poorly to regular attack treatment, but the following can help:

  • Triptans: Start early in the attack. Some need higher doses or longer-acting triptans
  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen - can also be used preventively around the period
  • Combination: Triptan + NSAID can be more effective

Short-term prevention ("mini-prophylaxis")

For predictable menstrual migraine, preventive medication can be taken only during the vulnerable window:

NSAIDs around the period

Start 2 days before expected menstruation and continue until day 3:

  • Naproxen 500 mg x 2 daily
  • Ibuprofen 400-600 mg x 3 daily

Triptans in the vulnerable window

Can be used preventively for very predictable migraine, but watch out for medication overuse:

  • Frovatriptan 2.5 mg x 2 from day -2 to day +3
  • Naratriptan or other long-acting triptans

Hormonal approach

Important about birth control and migraine

Combined birth control pills (with estrogen) are contraindicated in migraine with aura due to increased stroke risk. Always discuss with your doctor before starting or changing hormonal contraception.

Hormonal alternatives that can be discussed with your doctor:

  • Continuous pill: Skip the pill-free week to stabilize estrogen
  • Estrogen supplementation: Estrogen gel or patch during pill-free week/around period
  • Mini-pill or hormonal IUD: Progestins without estrogen

What can the chiropractor do?

Although hormonal headache is primarily caused by hormonal factors, manual treatment can be a useful supplement:

  • Reduce muscle tension that can amplify headache - see trigger points and headache
  • Improve neck mobility - relevant for cervicogenic headache component
  • Guidance on lifestyle factors
  • Contribute to a comprehensive treatment plan

Lifestyle measures

  • Regular sleep: Extra important around the period
  • Stable blood sugar: Frequent, small meals
  • Physical activity: Preventive effect on migraine in general
  • Stress management: Stress amplifies hormonal trigger
  • Headache diary: Map out the pattern

Frequently asked questions

What is menstrual migraine?
Menstrual migraine refers to migraine attacks that consistently occur in connection with menstruation, typically from day -2 to day +3 of the cycle. Pure menstrual migraine means attacks only occur during this period, while menstrually-related migraine means attacks also occur at other times.
Why do many women get headaches around their period?
The main cause is the rapid drop in estrogen levels just before and during menstruation. This hormonal shift affects neurotransmitters in the brain like serotonin and can trigger migraine attacks in women who are genetically predisposed.
Can birth control pills help with menstrual migraine?
It depends. Some women experience improvement with continuous pill use that stabilizes hormone levels. Others experience worsening, especially during pill-free weeks. Estrogen-containing pills are contraindicated in migraine with aura due to increased stroke risk.
Does hormonal headache improve in menopause?
Many women experience improvement or their migraine disappears after menopause when hormone levels stabilize. However, perimenopause itself can cause more headaches due to large hormonal fluctuations. Hormone therapy can both help and worsen headaches.
Is it safe to get pregnant with migraine?
Yes, migraine is not a contraindication for pregnancy. On the contrary, 60-70% experience improvement during pregnancy. Certain migraine medications should be avoided in pregnancy - talk to your doctor about adjusting treatment.
Can a chiropractor help with hormonal headache?
Manual treatment can be a useful supplement by reducing muscle tension that amplifies the headache. It will not change the underlying hormonal factors, but can contribute to a comprehensive approach together with medication and hormonal treatment.
