Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)

When painkillers make your headache worse - and how to break the cycle

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

It started as regular migraine or tension headache. You took a Paracetamol or Ibuprofen when the pain came, and it helped. But gradually you had to take it more often. Now you take painkillers almost every day - and the headache is still there, all the time.

If this sounds familiar, you may have developed medication overuse headache (MOH). This is one of the most common but least known forms of chronic headache - and the good news is that it is treatable.

Here you will learn how MOH develops, how to recognize it, and most importantly: how you can break the vicious cycle.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 78-80% of chronic headache has MOH
  • Risk: Painkillers 10-15+ days/month
  • The vicious cycle: Medicine -> more headache -> more medicine
  • 50-75% improve after discontinuation
  • Improvement: Typically after 6-8 weeks

What is Medication Overuse Headache?

Medication overuse headache (MOH) is a chronic headache that develops from regular use of acute pain medications. Paradoxically, the medications that are supposed to relieve the headache make it worse over time.

MOH is a global public health problem and is found in up to 78-80% of all patients with chronic daily headache. It is the third most common headache diagnosis after tension headache and migraine.

1-2% of the population is affected
78-80% of chronic headache has MOH
3-4x more common in women
40-50 years most common age group

The Vicious Cycle

Headache occurs
->
Takes painkillers
->
Temporary relief
->
Nervous system sensitization
->
Lower pain threshold
->
More headache

Which Medications Can Cause MOH?

All acute headache medications can lead to MOH with overuse. ICHD-3 defines the following limits:

Triptans (Sumatriptan, Zolmitriptan, etc.)

10+ days/month

Migraine-specific medications. Risk of MOH when used 10 or more days per month for 3+ months.

  • Can cause MOH fastest of all preparations
  • Increases attack frequency and lowers pain threshold

Combination Preparations and Opioids

10+ days/month

Combinations containing caffeine, codeine, or other opioids. High risk of dependence and MOH.

  • Co-codamol, Vicodin, etc.
  • Caffeine in combination with painkillers
  • Particularly difficult discontinuation

Common Painkillers (NSAIDs, Paracetamol)

15+ days/month

Ibuprofen, naproxen, paracetamol, and similar. Risk with use 15 or more days per month.

  • Often underestimated risk because they are over-the-counter
  • Lower risk than triptans and combination preparations
  • Easier discontinuation

Important to Know

MOH only develops in people who already have a primary headache diagnosis (migraine, tension headache). People who take painkillers regularly for other conditions (arthritis, back pain) do not develop MOH unless they also have headache to begin with.

3

Symptoms and Diagnosis

The MOH diagnosis is made when the following criteria are met (ICHD-3):

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Headache 15+ days per month in a patient with known primary headache
  • Regular overuse of acute headache medications for more than 3 months
  • The headache cannot be better explained by another diagnosis

Typical Characteristics

  • Daily or almost daily headache - often present upon waking
  • Mixed character - may resemble both migraine and tension headache
  • Fear of headache - takes medication "preventively" before the pain comes
  • Escalation need - must take higher doses for the same effect
  • The headache "breaks through" the medication faster than before
  • Medications work less well over time

Who is Most at Risk?

  • Women (3-4x higher risk)
  • People with frequent migraine or cervicogenic headache
  • Anxiety and depression
  • Low socioeconomic status
  • Smoking and obesity
  • Physical inactivity

Treatment - How to Break the Cycle

The most important treatment for MOH is discontinuation of the overused medications. This can be done abruptly or gradually, depending on which medications are being used.

Step 1: Recognition and Preparation

Many do not know they have MOH. The first step is to understand the connection between medication use and headache. Keep a headache diary for 4-8 weeks documenting:

  • Headache days and intensity
  • All medications (type, dose, timing)
  • Other factors (sleep, stress, menstruation)

Step 2: Discontinuation

Methods for Discontinuation

  • Abrupt discontinuation: Recommended for triptans and common painkillers. Safe to do at home with good planning
  • Gradual tapering: Recommended for opioids and combination preparations. Should be done under doctor supervision
  • Hospitalization: For severe dependence or previous failed attempts

Expect Rebound Headache

The first 1-2 weeks after discontinuation, the headache will typically worsen significantly. This is normal and temporary. Other symptoms may include:

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Sleep difficulties
  • Anxiety and restlessness
  • Agitation

These symptoms gradually subside and most notice clear improvement after 6-8 weeks.

Step 3: Support Treatment During Discontinuation

  • Fluids and rest: Drink plenty, rest when necessary
  • Limited "rescue" medications: The doctor may prescribe a limited number for the worst days
  • Preventive medication: Can be started at the same time as discontinuation
  • Non-pharmacological measures: Ice/heat, relaxation, light activity

Step 4: Relapse Prevention

Lifestyle Changes to Prevent Relapse

  • Headache diary: Continue keeping a diary to catch increased use early
  • The 10-day rule: Never use acute painkillers more than 10 days per month
  • The 2-3 day rule: Ideally max 2-3 days per week
  • Regular sleep and meals
  • Physical activity: Has preventive effect on headache
  • Stress management
5

Prognosis - Can It Get Better?

Yes, MOH has a good prognosis with successful discontinuation:

50-75% experience significant improvement
6-8 weeks to noticeable improvement
30-40% relapse rate in 1st year

Factors That Improve Prognosis

  • Shorter duration of overuse
  • Lower medication use at start
  • Absence of combination preparations/opioids
  • Good adherence to preventive treatment
  • Active lifestyle change

Risk of Relapse

30-40% experience relapse in the first year after discontinuation. The risk is highest in the first 6 months. Therefore, follow-up and preventive strategies are important.

The Underlying Headache

Remember that discontinuation does not cure the primary headache (migraine/tension headache). The goal is to return to the original state with episodic headache that can be effectively treated with limited medication use. Trigger point treatment can be a useful supplement for managing muscle tension during the discontinuation phase.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is medication overuse headache?
Medication overuse headache (MOH) is a chronic headache that develops from regular use of painkillers over time. Paradoxically, the medications you take to relieve the headache can make it worse and more frequent.
How much painkiller is too much?
The risk increases with use of triptans or combination preparations 10+ days per month, or regular painkillers (paracetamol, NSAIDs) 15+ days per month, over a period of 3 months or more.
Can MOH be treated?
Yes, MOH is treatable. The main treatment is discontinuation (tapering/stopping) of the overused medications. About 50-75% experience significant improvement within 2-3 months after successful discontinuation.
Does the headache get worse when I stop the medications?
Yes, expect temporary worsening (rebound) during the first 1-2 weeks after discontinuation. This is normal and temporary. Most notice clear improvement after 6-8 weeks, often with fewer and milder headache days.
Can I stop on my own?
When using common painkillers or triptans, many can manage abrupt discontinuation at home with good planning. When using opioids or combination preparations, you should have guidance from a doctor. Talk to your GP first.
How do I avoid relapse?
Stick to max 10 days of painkillers per month (ideally 2-3 days per week). Keep a headache diary to catch increased use early. Focus on preventive measures such as sleep, exercise, and stress management.
