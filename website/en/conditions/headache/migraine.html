Migraine: Everything You Need to Know

Types, triggers, phases and treatment - with honest information about evidence

You notice it coming. Maybe you see a flickering light show in your visual field, or feel a strange fatigue and food cravings. Then it hits: a throbbing, pulsating pain that makes it impossible to think clearly. The light from the window feels like knives, and the sound of the TV is unbearable.

If this sounds familiar, you are not alone. Migraine affects around 12-13% of all Norwegians - that means about one in eight adults experiences this (HUNT studies). Women are three times as likely to be affected as men.

But what exactly is migraine? And what can actually help? Here you get honest information based on research - including what is known and not known about treatment.

Quick Summary

  • 12-13% of Norwegians are affected annually
  • Women 3x more likely than men
  • Four phases: prodrome, aura, headache, postdrome
  • Evidence for chiropractic: low certainty
  • Red flags: Sudden "worst headache", fever + neck stiffness
1

What is Migraine?

Migraine is a neurological condition - not "just headache". It is a complex disturbance in brain function that causes repeated attacks of moderate to severe headache. The pain is often one-sided and pulsating, and typically worsens with physical activity.

Most people also experience nausea, vomiting, or increased sensitivity to light and sound during attacks. Migraine is actually the second most common cause of disability worldwide (GBD, Lancet Neurology 2024).

12-13% of Norwegians affected annually
3x more common in women
30-39 years most common age group
~25 years typical age of onset

Migraine typically debuts around age 25 and is most common in the 30-39 age group - in the middle of the most productive working years. For many, the condition is chronic with recurring attacks throughout much of life, although attack frequency often decreases with age.

2

Types of Migraine

Migraine is not one condition, but a group of related conditions. Here are the most common types, ranked from most to least common:

Migraine without aura

~75% of cases

The most common form. Headache without preceding warning symptoms. The diagnosis requires at least five attacks with headache lasting 4-72 hours (StatPearls, 2024).

Migraine with aura

25-30%

Temporary visual disturbances, numbness or speech difficulties 5-60 minutes before the headache. Typically one sees flickering zigzag lines, blind spots or light flashes (ICHD-3, 2018).

Chronic migraine

1-2% of population

Headache 15 or more days per month for more than 3 months, with at least 8 days having migraine features. Annually, 2.5-3% progress from episodic to chronic migraine. Read more about chronic daily headache.

Vestibular migraine

Dizziness and balance disturbances as the main symptom. Can occur with or without headache.

Menstrual migraine

Attacks linked to the menstrual cycle (day -2 to +3). Caused by hormonal fluctuations, especially estrogen levels. Read more about hormonal headache.

Risk of Chronification

Factors that increase the risk of episodic migraine developing into chronic: high attack frequency (10+ days/month), overuse of painkillers, and concurrent depression.

3

Triggers and Risk Factors

Most people with migraine have individual triggers that can provoke attacks. A large study (Kelman, Cephalalgia 2007) mapped the most common:

Stress 79.7%
Hormonal fluctuations 65%
Skipped meals 57%
Weather changes 53%
Sleep disturbances 50%
Strong odors 44%
Alcohol 35-38%

Triggers Often Work in Combination

A single exposure does not necessarily trigger an attack - it is often the sum of several factors that tips the balance. Strict avoidance of all possible triggers is rarely necessary or advisable.

4

The Four Phases of a Migraine Attack

A migraine attack typically progresses through four phases, although not everyone experiences all of them:

1. Prodromal Phase (hours to 1-2 days before)

29-66% experience warning symptoms: fatigue, mood swings, food cravings, neck stiffness, frequent yawning or concentration difficulties. These can warn of an impending attack 24-48 hours in advance. Some also experience morning headache as a warning sign.

2. Aura Phase (5-60 minutes)

25-33% get aura - typically visual disturbances such as flickering zigzag lines, blind spots or light flashes. Some experience numbness in the face or hand, or temporary speech difficulties. Symptoms develop gradually over at least 5 minutes and are completely reversible.

3. Headache Phase (4-72 hours)

The headache itself is often one-sided (about 60%) with pulsating character, moderate to severe intensity. The pain can be localized to the temple, top of head or back of head. Most have concurrent nausea (80-90%), light sensitivity (66-90%) and sound sensitivity (80%). The pain typically worsens with physical activity.

4. Postdromal Phase (up to 24-48 hours)

68-97% experience a "hangover phase" with fatigue, concentration difficulties and reduced energy. This can last up to two days after the headache is over.

5

How is the Diagnosis Made?

Migraine diagnosis is made clinically - there are no blood tests or imaging that confirm migraine. The diagnosis is based on medical history and symptoms meeting the criteria in the International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD-3, 2018).

Criteria for Migraine Without Aura

The diagnosis requires at least five attacks with:

  • Headache lasting 4-72 hours
  • At least two of four characteristics: one-sided, pulsating, moderate-severe intensity, worsening with activity
  • At least one accompanying symptom: nausea/vomiting or light and sound sensitivity

Do I Need an MRI?

Imaging is rarely necessary for typical migraine. MRI or CT is only relevant for alarm symptoms (see red flags), suddenly changed headache character, neurological deficits, or new headache in people over 50. Normal findings on images do not confirm migraine - they rule out other causes such as occipital neuralgia or sinus-related headache.

6

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

  • Thunderclap headache - sudden, "worst headache of my life" reaching maximum intensity within seconds
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness - may indicate meningitis
  • Neurological deficits - sudden paralysis, speech difficulties, vision loss or altered consciousness
  • Severe headache after head injury

SEE A DOCTOR THE SAME DAY FOR:

  • New headache in people over 50 - risk of serious cause increases 10-fold after 65
  • Temple tenderness + pain when chewing in elderly - may indicate giant cell arteritis
  • Gradually worsening headache over days/weeks
  • New headache with known cancer

Based on SNNOOP10 criteria (Do et al., Neurology 2019)

7

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

It is important to be honest about what research shows: The evidence for spinal manipulation treatment for migraine is classified as low to very low certainty according to GRADE criteria (Posadzki et al., Systematic Reviews 2024).

Important About Evidence

The best-designed Norwegian study (Chaibi et al., European Journal of Neurology 2017) from Akershus University Hospital found no significant difference between chiropractic treatment and placebo.

This does not mean manual therapy is without value - many patients experience improvement, but one cannot say for certain whether the effect is due to the treatment itself or other factors.

Manual treatment may be relevant as part of a holistic approach, especially for patients who:

  • Prefer non-drug treatment
  • Have significant neck stiffness or cervical components (neck headache)
  • Do not tolerate or respond to medications
  • Want a multimodal approach combined with lifestyle advice

What Can You Do Yourself?

Several self-help strategies actually have stronger evidence than manual treatment:

Lifestyle Measures With Documented Effect

  • Regular sleep: Keep fixed bedtimes and wake times, even on weekends. Two-thirds of migraine patients have poor sleep quality
  • Regular meals: Don't skip meals - fasting triggers attacks in 57%
  • Physical activity: Aerobic exercise reduces attack frequency. At least 30 minutes, 3 times per week has documented preventive effect
  • Stable caffeine intake: Sudden withdrawal can trigger attacks. Keep a steady, moderate intake (max 200 mg/day)
  • Stress management: Cognitive therapy, relaxation and mindfulness have documented effect

Supplements

Magnesium (400-500 mg/day) and riboflavin/vitamin B2 (400 mg/day) have moderate evidence for prevention according to AAN Guidelines (2012). Talk to your doctor before starting.

8

Prognosis - How Does It Go?

Migraine is a chronic condition - the goal is to reduce attack frequency and intensity, not necessarily to eliminate all attacks. Data from the American AMPP study (Lipton et al., 2007) shows that at 1-year follow-up:

84% still have migraine
10% achieve full remission
3% worsen to chronic

It is thus possible to get better - and chronic migraine can also be reversed. Studies show that 26-27% of patients with chronic migraine revert to episodic form within 2 years (Manack et al., Neurology 2011).

Factors That Improve Prognosis

  • Lower baseline frequency
  • Absence of medication overuse
  • Absence of depression
  • Good sleep quality

Attack frequency tends to decrease with age. From age 60, remission rates increase noticeably, especially in men.

Medication Overuse Headache

Regular use of triptans 10+ days/month or common painkillers 15+ days/month over 3 months can cause medication overuse headache (MOH). This affects up to 78-80% of patients with chronic daily headache.

9

Frequently Asked Questions

Can migraine be cured?
Migraine cannot be cured, but attack frequency and intensity can often be significantly reduced with proper treatment. Some experience long-term remission, and many have fewer attacks with age.
Does a chiropractor help with migraine?
Research shows limited evidence. Many patients experience improvement, but one cannot say for certain whether the effect is due to the treatment itself. Manual therapy can be part of a holistic approach, especially for neck problems. I am honest about what is known and not known.
What is the difference between migraine and tension headache?
Migraine typically causes one-sided, pulsating pain that worsens with activity, with nausea and light/sound sensitivity. Tension headache causes bilateral, pressing pain without worsening with activity and at most one sensitivity (not both).
Can I exercise with migraine?
During an attack, exercise is often impossible due to worsening. Between attacks, regular aerobic exercise is actually preventive - studies show that 30 minutes of moderate activity 3 times weekly can reduce attack frequency.
Can overuse of painkillers make migraine worse?
Yes. Medication overuse headache (MOH) affects up to 78-80% of patients with chronic daily headache. Regular use of triptans 10+ days/month or common painkillers 15+ days/month over 3 months significantly increases the risk.
Should I get an MRI?
For typical migraine with normal neurological examination, imaging is rarely necessary. MRI is relevant for atypical symptoms, neurological deficits, or suddenly changed headache character. Talk to your doctor.
