Morning Headache

Why you wake up with a headache - and what you can do about it

The alarm goes off, and before you can even open your eyes properly, you feel it: an aching pain in your head. What should be a rested start to the day begins with a headache. Again.

Morning headache is surprisingly common - and there are many possible causes. Everything from sleep disorders and poor sleeping position to medication overuse and underlying medical conditions can make you wake up with a sore head.

Here you get an overview of the most common causes of morning headache, when you should worry, and what you can do to wake up rested instead of in pain.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Sleep apnea is a common cause
  • Teeth grinding causes jaw tension and temple headache
  • Wrong sleep position may be the culprit
  • Medication overuse often causes morning headache
  • Regular morning headache should be evaluated

The most common causes

Sleep apnea

Very common cause

Obstructive sleep apnea causes repeated breathing stops at night. This leads to oxygen deficiency and carbon dioxide buildup that dilates blood vessels in the brain.

  • Typical: Pressing, bilateral headache upon awakening
  • Usually improves within 30-60 minutes
  • Often accompanied by snoring, daytime fatigue, dry mouth
  • Treatment of sleep apnea (e.g., CPAP) often eliminates the headache

Bruxism (teeth grinding)

Grinding or clenching teeth at night strains jaw muscles and can cause headache upon awakening.

  • Often tension headache character (pressing, bilateral)
  • Pain in jaw, temple and/or chewing muscles
  • Dentist can see wear on teeth
  • Bite guards and muscle relaxation can help

Poor sleep position and pillow

Wrong position at night can cause muscle tension in neck and head.

  • Especially stomach sleeping with head turned to the side
  • Pillow too high or too low
  • Neck in unnatural position all night
  • Often gives cervicogenic headache or tension headache character

Medication overuse headache (MOH)

Regular use of painkillers can paradoxically cause daily headache - often most pronounced in the morning.

  • Headache present upon awakening most days
  • Temporarily improves with painkillers, but returns
  • See separate article on medication overuse headache

Migraine and tension headache

Primary headache disorders can start at night or early morning.

  • Migraine often has morning/forenoon peak in attack frequency
  • REM sleep can trigger migraine attacks
  • Tension headache can worsen from tight muscles at night and trigger points

Other causes

  • Caffeine withdrawal: If you're used to lots of coffee, absence at night can cause headache
  • Alcohol: "Hangover" causes dehydration and headache
  • High blood pressure: Very high blood pressure can cause morning headache
  • Dehydration: Too little fluid can cause headache upon awakening
  • Depression and anxiety: Associated with increased incidence of morning headache
  • Chronic daily headache: Can be present upon awakening

Red flags - when to see a doctor

Most cases of morning headache are due to benign causes. But certain symptoms require prompt medical evaluation. Headache combined with dizziness may for example need extra attention:

SEE A DOCTOR FOR:

  • Headache that wakes you at night - may indicate increased intracranial pressure
  • Nausea and vomiting in the morning without other explanation
  • Visual disturbances - blurred vision, double vision (can also be seen in vestibular migraine)
  • Worsening over time - gradually increasing intensity or frequency
  • Neurological symptoms: Weakness, numbness, unsteadiness
  • New headache after age 50
  • Personality changes or cognitive impairment

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

  • Sudden, intense headache ("worst headache of my life")
  • Altered consciousness
  • Seizures

Headache with the above symptoms may be due to serious conditions requiring prompt evaluation

Brain tumors and intracranial pressure

It's important to know that morning headache can be a symptom of increased pressure in the brain (e.g., from a tumor), but this is very rare. Typical for this is:

  • Gradually worsening headache over weeks/months
  • Headache that wakes you at night
  • Nausea and vomiting, especially in the morning
  • Worsening with coughing, sneezing or straining
  • Neurological deficits

If you suspect this, you must see a doctor for evaluation.

3

Treatment

Treatment depends on the underlying cause. Here are some approaches:

For sleep-related causes

Sleep apnea

  • Evaluation with sleep study (polysomnography)
  • CPAP treatment is first choice for moderate to severe sleep apnea
  • Weight loss if relevant
  • Avoid alcohol before bedtime

Bruxism (teeth grinding)

  • Bite guards fitted by dentist
  • Stress management (stress often worsens grinding)
  • Jaw exercises and muscle relaxation techniques
  • Manual treatment of jaw muscles can help

Improve sleeping conditions

Sleep position and equipment

  • Avoid stomach sleeping: Causes neck torsion
  • Right pillow: Keep neck in neutral position
  • Side sleeping: Have pillow that fills the space between head and shoulder
  • Back sleeping: Moderately high pillow that supports the neck curve

Lifestyle measures

Sleep hygiene

  • Regular sleep pattern: Go to bed and get up at the same time
  • Sufficient sleep: Most need 7-9 hours
  • Limit caffeine: Avoid after 2-3 PM
  • Limit alcohol: Disrupts sleep quality
  • Drink enough water: Prevent dehydration

What can the chiropractor do?

For morning headache related to muscle tension or neck problems, manual treatment can be useful:

  • Treatment of tight neck and jaw muscles
  • Improve neck mobility
  • Guidance on sleep position and ergonomics
  • Exercises for neck and jaw
  • Address headache in back of head or top of head related to neck tension

When do you need to see a doctor?

If you suspect sleep apnea, hypertension, or other medical causes, it's important to get a medical evaluation. The chiropractor can help with muscle and joint-related components, but not treat underlying medical conditions.

Differential diagnoses

Morning headache can be confused with other headache types. Sinus headache can give pressing pain upon awakening, while cluster headache often comes early in the morning. Hormonal headache in women can also have a morning pattern. For sharp pain in the back of the head, occipital neuralgia should be considered.

Frequently asked questions

Why do I wake up with a headache every morning?
Morning headache can be caused by many things: sleep apnea, teeth grinding (bruxism), poor sleep position, medication overuse, high blood pressure, or primary headaches like migraine and tension headache. Regular morning headaches should be evaluated by a doctor.
Can sleep apnea cause headaches in the morning?
Yes, sleep apnea is one of the most common causes of morning headache. Repeated breathing stops at night cause oxygen deficiency and carbon dioxide buildup that dilates blood vessels in the brain. Typical is pressing, bilateral headache that improves during the morning.
When should I see a doctor for morning headache?
See a doctor for regular morning headaches without known cause, headaches that wake you at night, headaches accompanied by nausea/vomiting, visual disturbances, or other neurological symptoms. New headache after age 50 should always be evaluated.
Can a bad pillow cause morning headache?
Yes, wrong sleep position or unsuitable pillow can cause muscle tension in the neck and head resulting in headache upon awakening. Sleeping face down is particularly problematic. A pillow that keeps the neck in a neutral position is recommended.
Can caffeine withdrawal cause morning headache?
Yes, if you're used to drinking lots of coffee, the absence of caffeine at night can cause withdrawal headache upon awakening. This is typically pressing headache that improves after morning coffee. The solution may be to gradually reduce caffeine intake.
