Occipital Neuralgia

Stabbing pain from neck to back of head - causes and treatment

It feels like electric shocks or stabbing pain that starts in the neck and shoots upward toward the back of the head and crown. The scalp is tender to touch, and the pain can be so intense that it's difficult to find a comfortable position for your head.

If this sounds familiar, you may have occipital neuralgia - a condition caused by irritation of the occipital nerves in the upper neck. Although it's less well-known than migraine or tension headache, it's important to recognize because it often responds well to treatment.

Here you'll learn what occipital neuralgia is, why it occurs, and what can help.

Quick Summary

  • Stabbing/burning pain from neck to crown
  • Caused by irritation of the occipital nerve
  • Often related to muscle tension or neck injury
  • Often responds well to manual treatment
  • Nerve block can be diagnostic and therapeutic
1

What is occipital neuralgia?

Occipital neuralgia is a condition with intense, stabbing or burning pain in the back of the head and neck area. The pain is caused by irritation, inflammation, or damage to the occipital nerves - the nerves that supply the scalp at the back.

The condition can cause attacks of intense pain lasting from seconds to minutes, or more constant, aching pain with overlying shock-like episodes.

The Anatomy

There are three occipital nerves on each side:

  • Greater occipital nerve: Comes from the C2 nerve root. Passes through the neck muscles and supplies the largest part of the back of the head. Most commonly involved in occipital neuralgia.
  • Lesser occipital nerve: Comes from C2-C3. Supplies the area behind the ear and lateral part of the back of the head.
  • Third occipital nerve: Comes from C3. Supplies the lower part of the back of the head.

Prevalence

Occipital neuralgia is relatively rare as an isolated diagnosis - exact prevalence is unknown, but it's considered one of the less common headaches. The condition is somewhat more common in women and usually debuts between ages 30-50.

2

Symptoms

The typical symptoms of occipital neuralgia are characteristic and differ from other headache types:

Pain Character

  • Stabbing, shooting, or burning pain
  • Often described as "electric shocks"
  • Starts at the neck/back of head junction and radiates upward toward the back of the head
  • Can reach all the way to the forehead or behind the eye
  • Usually unilateral, but can be bilateral

Attack Pattern

  • Attacks of intense pain lasting seconds to minutes
  • May have underlying aching pain between attacks
  • Often triggered by head movements or touching the scalp

Accompanying Symptoms

  • Allodynia: Scalp becomes tender to touch - even light touch can hurt
  • Numbness or tingling in the scalp
  • Pain when brushing hair or laying head on pillow
  • Light sensitivity (less common than in migraine)

Diagnostic Test

In occipital neuralgia, there is typically tenderness on palpation over the occipital nerve's exit point (approximately midway between the ear and the cervical vertebrae, at the lower border of the back of the head). Pressure here can reproduce or intensify symptoms.

3

Causes

Occipital neuralgia can have many causes, but in many cases no clear underlying cause is found (idiopathic). The most common known causes include:

Muscle Tension and Trigger Points (Most Common)

Tight muscles in the upper neck can compress the occipital nerves where they pass through the musculature. Particularly semispinalis capitis, trapezius, and sternocleidomastoid. Read more about trigger points and headache.

Neck Injuries (Whiplash)

Trauma to the neck can damage or irritate the occipital nerves, either directly or through subsequent muscle spasms and scarring.

Osteoarthritis in Upper Cervical Vertebrae

Degenerative changes in C1-C3 can affect the nerves that form the occipital nerves. Osteoarthritis or bone spurs can compress the nerves.

Prolonged Poor Posture

Forward head posture (e.g., from office work) can over time lead to muscle tension and nerve involvement in the upper neck. This can also contribute to cervicogenic headache.

Other Causes

  • Inflammation (gout, diabetes, vasculitis)
  • Infections
  • Tumors (rare)
  • Complications after neck surgery
4

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

SEE A DOCTOR FOR:

  • Neurological deficits: Weakness, numbness in arms/legs, balance problems or dizziness
  • New headache after age 50
  • Fever and general symptoms together with headache
  • Gradually worsening symptoms over time
  • Visual disturbances not due to aura
  • Pain that wakes you at night (see also morning headache)

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

  • Sudden, very intense headache ("worst headache of my life")
  • Altered consciousness or confusion
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness

These symptoms may indicate more serious conditions requiring prompt evaluation

5

Diagnosis

Occipital neuralgia is diagnosed primarily clinically, based on history and examination:

ICHD-3 Diagnostic Criteria

  • Unilateral or bilateral pain in the distribution of the occipital nerves
  • The pain has at least two of three characteristics:
    • Recurring paroxysms (attacks) lasting seconds to minutes
    • Severe intensity
    • Stabbing, shooting, or sharp quality
  • Tenderness on palpation over the affected nerve
  • Pain temporarily relieved by local nerve block

Nerve Block as Diagnostic Test

An injection of local anesthetic at the occipital nerve's exit point that provides significant, albeit temporary, pain relief supports the diagnosis. This test can be performed by a doctor and is both diagnostic and therapeutic.

When is Imaging Necessary?

MRI of the neck may be appropriate to rule out structural causes such as:

  • Disc herniation or stenosis in the cervical spine
  • Tumors or other space-occupying lesions
  • Vascular malformations
6

Treatment

Treatment of occipital neuralgia is often stepwise, with conservative measures first:

Conservative Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Manual treatment can be effective for occipital neuralgia, especially when the condition is caused by muscle tension or dysfunction in the upper neck:

  • Soft tissue treatment: Soften tight muscles that may be compressing the nerve
  • Cervical mobilization: Improve mobility in the upper neck
  • Trigger point therapy: Address painful knots in neck muscles
  • Posture guidance: Prevent future strain
  • Exercises: Strengthening and stretching exercises for the neck

Self-Help

  • Heat treatment: Heat pack on the neck can loosen muscles
  • Ergonomics: Adjust your workstation to avoid forward head posture
  • Stretching: Gentle stretching of neck muscles
  • Avoid triggers: Identify and avoid positions/activities that trigger pain

Medication Treatment

If conservative treatment is insufficient, a doctor may consider:

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen or similar for mild to moderate pain
  • Neuropathic pain medications: Gabapentin, pregabalin, or amitriptyline
  • Muscle relaxants: For significant muscle spasm

With frequent use of painkillers (more than 10-15 days per month), be aware of the risk of medication overuse headache.

Invasive Treatments

For refractory cases, the following may be considered by a specialist:

  • Occipital nerve block: Injection of local anesthetic +/- corticosteroid
  • Botulinum toxin: Can reduce muscle tension and nerve activity
  • Pulsed radiofrequency treatment
  • Occipital nerve stimulator: For chronic, refractory cases
7

Prognosis

The prognosis for occipital neuralgia varies depending on the underlying cause:

  • Muscle-related: Often good prognosis with manual treatment and self-help
  • Traumatic: May take longer, but often improves over months
  • Idiopathic: Variable course - some experience spontaneous improvement, others develop chronic daily headache

Many Get Better

Most people with occipital neuralgia experience significant improvement with proper treatment. Conservative measures such as manual treatment, self-help, and medications often help. For those who don't respond, more invasive alternatives exist.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

What is occipital neuralgia?
Occipital neuralgia is a condition with intense, stabbing or burning pain in the back of the head, caused by irritation or damage to the occipital nerves that supply the scalp at the back.
What causes occipital neuralgia?
Common causes include muscle tension in the neck that compresses the nerves, neck injuries (whiplash), osteoarthritis in the upper cervical vertebrae, and prolonged poor posture. In many cases, no clear cause is found.
Can a chiropractor help with occipital neuralgia?
Manual treatment can be helpful for occipital neuralgia, especially when the condition is caused by muscle tension or dysfunction in the upper neck. Treatment aims to reduce muscle tension and improve neck mobility.
How does occipital neuralgia differ from other headaches?
Occipital neuralgia typically causes unilateral, stabbing/burning pain from the neck/back of head junction that radiates upward toward the crown. The pain often comes in attacks, and the scalp may be tender to touch. Migraine and tension headache have different characteristics.
Is occipital neuralgia dangerous?
Occipital neuralgia is not dangerous in itself, but the pain can be very bothersome and affect quality of life. It's important to get the correct diagnosis to rule out other causes of the symptoms.
