Sinus Headache or Migraine?

How to tell them apart - 88% get the wrong diagnosis

Have you had repeated episodes of headache, facial pressure, and nasal congestion - and assumed it must be your sinuses? You're not alone. But new research shows this assumption is often wrong.

A large study from 2004 examined nearly 3,000 people who thought they had sinus headache. The result was surprising: 88% actually met the criteria for migraine.

The misdiagnosis can persist for a long time. A 2013 study found that patients had been misdiagnosed for an average of nearly 8 years before getting the correct diagnosis - and over 12% had undergone unnecessary sinus surgery.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 88% with "sinus headache" have migraine
  • Migraine causes nausea, light sensitivity, clear discharge
  • Sinusitis causes fever, yellow-green mucus, reduced smell
  • The NLD check detects migraine with 93% accuracy
  • Correct diagnosis = correct treatment

The Great Confusion

88% have migraine, not sinusitis
8 years average misdiagnosis time
12% unnecessarily operated on

Similar findings came from the SAMS study in 2007, where only 3% of participants actually had headache from sinusitis. The rest had migraine or other headache types.

Why are they confused?

The trigeminal nerve supplies both the meninges (where migraine originates) and the sinuses. When migraine activates this nerve, it triggers a reflex causing nasal congestion, watery eyes, and facial pressure - symptoms we associate with a cold or sinusitis.

Studies show that 40-74% of migraine patients experience at least one such "sinus-like" symptom during attacks.

The Critical Difference

Migraine produces clear fluid from the nose (from nerve activation)

Bacterial sinusitis produces yellow-green, thick mucus (from infection)

How to Tell Them Apart: A Practical Checklist

Signs That Suggest MIGRAINE

Most Common

Pain quality: Pulsating or throbbing, often on one side of the head (see temple headache). Worsens with physical activity like climbing stairs.

Accompanying symptoms: Nausea or vomiting, sensitivity to light (photophobia) or sound (phonophobia). Many also have aura. Some experience dizziness as well.

Triggers: Stress, hormones, lack of sleep, alcohol, or certain foods often trigger attacks.

Nasal secretion: Clear fluid, not thick or discolored.

Duration: Attacks lasting 4-72 hours.

Signs That Suggest SINUSITIS

Pain quality: Aching, deep pressure over cheeks or forehead. Worsens when bending forward.

Accompanying symptoms: Fever, reduced sense of smell, upper jaw toothache, and a feeling that the face is "full."

Nasal secretion: Thick, yellow-green or greenish mucus - especially from one side.

Duration: Symptoms persist and worsen over 7-10 days. Often starts after a cold.

The NLD Check (ID-Migraine)

Three simple questions to identify migraine:

  1. Do you have Nausea during the headache?
  2. Are you Light-sensitive (bothered by bright light)?
  3. Do you feel Disabled (need to stop activities)?

If you answer yes to two of three, the probability of migraine is 93%.

If you answer yes to all three, the probability increases to 98%.

When Is It Actually Sinusitis?

True sinusitis is far less common than many believe. 90-95% of acute sinusitis is viral and resolves on its own without antibiotics.

Bacterial sinusitis requiring treatment is characterized by:

  • Symptoms that worsen after 5-7 days
  • High fever over 39C
  • Severe unilateral facial pain
  • Purulent (yellow-green) discharge, especially from one side
  • Symptoms lasting more than 10 days without improvement

If you have these signs, you should contact a doctor for evaluation of antibiotic treatment.

4

Red Flags - When to Seek Medical Help Immediately

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES OR GO TO THE ER IF:

  • Thunderclap headache: Acute, severe headache reaching maximum intensity in under one minute
  • Swelling or redness around the eye: May indicate spread of infection to the eye socket
  • Double vision or reduced vision: Sign of serious complication
  • High fever with neck stiffness: May indicate meningitis
  • Confusion or reduced consciousness: Requires immediate evaluation
  • New headache after age 50: Increased risk of serious underlying cause

Orbital cellulitis (infection around the eye) is a rare but serious complication of sinusitis that requires immediate treatment.

Treatment

How Can the Chiropractor Help?

Neck-Related Headache (Cervicogenic Headache)

Many people with headache and facial pain have a component from the neck. Cervicogenic headache arises from joints, muscles, or nerves in the upper neck and can cause pain radiating toward the eye, temple, or forehead.

For this type of headache, there is moderate evidence that manual treatment such as mobilization and manipulation can reduce pain intensity, attack frequency, and disability.

For Migraine

For migraine, the evidence for manual treatment is more uncertain. This doesn't mean treatment is useless - many experience relief, and neck treatment may be relevant if neck problems contribute to the migraine picture. But expectations should be realistic.

What Do We Do in Practice?

During consultation, we conduct a thorough examination to map:

  • Headache type (migraine, tension headache, cervicogenic, or mixed forms)
  • Neck and jaw joint function
  • Muscular trigger points
  • Need for referral to a doctor

Treatment is tailored to findings and may include joint mobilization, soft tissue treatment, dry needling, and exercises.

What Can You Do Yourself?

For Migraine

  • Headache diary: Record attacks, triggers, food, sleep, and menstruation. This helps you identify patterns.
  • Regularity: Keep fixed times for sleep, meals, and activity. Migraine brains like predictability. Sleep problems can also cause morning headache.
  • Trigger control: Avoid known triggers, but be aware that "trigger hunting" can become excessive.
  • Acute treatment: Pain relievers work best when taken early in the attack. Overuse (more than 10-15 days per month) can lead to medication overuse headache.

For Suspected Sinusitis

  • Saline rinse: Nasal irrigation with saline can relieve symptoms.
  • Humid air: Steam inhalation or a humidifier can help with dryness.
  • Nasal spray: Decongestant nasal spray for short periods (max 7-10 days for decongestant types).
  • Wait and see: Most viral sinusitis resolves in 7-10 days without treatment.

Prognosis

Migraine

Migraine is a chronic neurological condition that varies throughout life for most people. Many experience fewer attacks with age, especially women after menopause.

Correct diagnosis is crucial - when patients who were misdiagnosed with sinusitis received migraine treatment, nearly 70% experienced improvement.

Relapse is common and expected. The goal is to reduce attack frequency and severity, not necessarily to eliminate symptoms completely. For persistent daily symptoms, see chronic daily headache.

Sinusitis

Acute viral sinusitis resolves on its own. Bacterial sinusitis treated with antibiotics has a good prognosis. Some develop chronic sinusitis (over 12 weeks), which requires evaluation by an ENT specialist.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you have both migraine and sinusitis at the same time?
Yes, but it's rare for symptoms to coincide. If you have recurring episodes of "sinus headache" without fever and purulent secretion, it's far more likely you have migraine with autonomic symptoms.
Why don't antibiotics help my headache?
Because the headache probably isn't caused by a bacterial infection. Repeated courses of antibiotics for "sinusitis" that don't provide lasting relief is a strong signal that the diagnosis should be reconsidered.
Can CT or X-ray show if it's the sinuses?
Imaging can show swelling in the sinuses, but this is common even with migraine and colds. Findings on CT don't automatically mean the sinuses are causing the headache.
Do I need to see a neurologist to get a migraine diagnosis?
No, the diagnosis is usually made by a GP based on medical history and clinical examination. Referral to a neurologist is relevant for uncertain diagnosis, poor treatment response, or alarm symptoms.
Can a chiropractor treat migraine?
Chiropractors can assess whether the neck contributes to the headache picture and treat any findings. For migraine specifically, the evidence for manual treatment is uncertain, but many experience subjective benefit. The most important thing is to get the correct diagnosis.
How many treatments do I need?
It depends on the diagnosis. For cervicogenic headache, we often see response after 4-8 treatments. For migraine with a neck component, maintenance treatment every 4-6 weeks may be relevant for some.
