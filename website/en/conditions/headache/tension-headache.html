Tension Headache: Everything You Need to Know

Trigger points, causes, and treatment - with good evidence for manual therapy

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 52.7% of Norwegians are affected
  • Trigger points: average 3.9 per patient
  • Manual treatment: large effect (SMD -0.86)
  • Most: improvement after 4-6 treatments
  • Red flags: Sudden intense headache, fever
Book assessment

You feel it like a tight band around your head. A pressing, aching sensation on both sides that makes it difficult to concentrate. Not painful enough to make you lie down, but painful enough to affect you throughout the day. Some also experience dizziness along with the headache.

This is tension headache - the world's most common type of headache. Over half of all Norwegians experience this: 52.7% according to the recent PopHEAD study (Kristoffersen et al., 2025). Globally, around 1.89 billion people live with the condition.

The good news? This is the headache type where manual treatment has the best evidence. Here you will learn what causes tension headache and what actually helps.

1

What is Tension Headache?

Tension headache (tension-type headache, TTH) is characterized by a pressing or tightening sensation - like a tight band around the head. Unlike migraine, which is a neurological disease with often one-sided, pulsating pain, tension headache is bilateral and more aching.

Most people experience this sporadically, but for 2-4% of the population the headache becomes chronic and daily (Bendtsen & Jensen, Lancet Neurology 2006).

52.7% of Norwegians are affected
1.89 billion people globally
30-39 years peak occurrence
1.2-1.5:1 women:men ratio

Episodic vs. Chronic

  • Episodic tension headache: Less than 15 days per month. Most people have this form.
  • Chronic tension headache: 15 days or more per month for at least 3 months. Here the pain system has often become "self-sustaining".
2

Symptoms

Tension headache has characteristic features that distinguish it from other headache types. According to ICHD-3 criteria (International Headache Society 2018), at least two of the following must be present:

  • Bilateral location - the pain sits on both sides of the head, often in the back of the head or forehead
  • Pressing or tightening quality - not pulsating
  • Mild to moderate intensity - you can usually continue with daily activities
  • Not worsened by physical activity - walking stairs or light movement does not make it worse

Important difference from migraine

With tension headache, you typically do not experience nausea or vomiting. Light or sound sensitivity may occur, but never both at the same time.

Tension Headache vs. Migraine

Headache in the temple or top of the head can occur in both conditions, but the character is different.

Characteristic Tension Headache Migraine
Location Bilateral, both sides Often unilateral
Pain quality Pressing, tightening Pulsating, throbbing
Intensity Mild to moderate Moderate to severe
Physical activity Does not worsen Worsens
Nausea Rare Common (80-90%)
Light/sound sensitivity At most one Often both
3

Causes and Risk Factors

There is rarely just one cause. Tension headache most often develops through an interplay of several factors:

1. Muscular Tension and Trigger Points

Tender knots (trigger points) in neck and jaw muscles are the most consistent finding. A meta-analysis from 2022 (Pain Physician) found that chronic patients had significantly increased tenderness in pericranial muscles (SMD 1.57).

In people with chronic tension headache, research has found an average of 3.9 trigger points per patient, compared to 1.4 in healthy controls (Couppe et al., 2007).

2. Stress and Mental Strain

Stress activates the sympathetic nervous system and automatically increases muscle tension. One study found that headache frequency increased by 4.3% for every 10-point increase on the stress scale. Many recognize that shoulders rise and jaw clenches during demanding periods.

3. Sleep Disturbances

About 50% of patients with tension headache have sleep problems (Odegard et al., 2010). Poor sleep quality lowers the pain threshold and is an independent risk factor for chronification. Many therefore experience morning headache as a result of poor night sleep.

4. Central Sensitization

With prolonged tension headache, changes occur in the central nervous system. The pain system becomes "overactive" - the brain registers pain signals more strongly than normal. This explains why the headache can persist even after the original triggers are gone.

5. Posture and Ergonomics

Forward head posture ("text neck") is associated with increased occurrence of active trigger points and tension headache. Static work in front of a screen without variation increases the risk.

6. Genetic Factors and Hormones

The heritability for frequent tension headache is estimated at 44-48% in twin studies. First-degree relatives have a 3.1 times higher risk of developing the condition. In women, hormonal fluctuations can also play a role - read more about hormonal headache.

7. Medication Overuse

An often overlooked cause. Frequent use of painkillers can paradoxically worsen the headache. The PopHEAD study from 2023 found that 5.9% of Norwegians have probable medication overuse headache - among the highest figures reported globally.

4

How is the Diagnosis Made?

Tension headache is diagnosed primarily through medical history and clinical examination. There is no blood test or imaging that confirms the diagnosis.

Clinical examination includes:

  • Systematic palpation of neck and head muscles to identify trigger points and tenderness
  • Assessment of neck mobility
  • Neurological examination to rule out other causes
  • Mapping of headache character, frequency, and triggering factors

Do I need an MRI?

Imaging is rarely necessary. MRI or CT is only ordered when underlying disease is suspected - for example with red flags or atypical presentation. It's important to know that findings such as degenerative changes on MRI are very common even in pain-free individuals, and often do not explain the headache.

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention immediately if:

  • Sudden, intense headache - "the worst headache of my life"
  • Fever and neck stiffness - may indicate meningitis
  • Neurological symptoms - paralysis, visual disturbances, confusion
  • New headache type after age 50 - increased risk of underlying disease
  • Headache that worsens over time - progressive headache requires investigation
  • Headache after head injury - even mild head injury should be evaluated

These signs follow the SNNOOP10 guidelines (Do et al., Neurology 2019)

6

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Manual treatment has good evidence for tension headache. A systematic review and meta-analysis from 2020/2022 (Kamonseki et al., Disability and Rehabilitation) analyzed 15 randomized controlled trials with over 1100 participants:

Soft tissue treatment showed large effect:

  • Pain intensity: SMD -0.86 (large effect)
  • Headache frequency: SMD -1.45 (large effect)

Trigger point treatment had particularly good evidence:

  • Reduction of 3.5 attacks per month
  • Pain intensity reduced by 12.8 points (0-100 scale)

(Falsiroli Maistrello et al., Frontiers in Neurology 2018)

In practice, I often combine several approaches: soft tissue treatment of tight muscles, trigger point therapy (manual or with needles), mobilization of stiff joints in the neck and upper back, as well as guidance on ergonomics and exercises.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Self-help strategies with documented effect

  • Strength training for neck and shoulders: Moderate effect (SMD -0.84). Training makes the muscles more robust against static strain.
  • Stress management and relaxation: EMG biofeedback and relaxation exercises have Level A evidence (EFNS 2010), with 45-60% average reduction in headache frequency.
  • Regular breaks: Get up and move your neck regularly during screen work.
  • Jaw tension: Be aware - release the jaw, let the tongue rest lightly.
  • Prioritize sleep: Poor sleep lowers the pain threshold.

Limit Painkillers

Use of painkillers should be limited to max 2 days per week to avoid medication overuse headache. TTH patients with this complication have a 91% relapse rate after treatment.

Treatment Course

Most treatment studies use 1-2 treatments per week over 4-8 weeks, typically 6-12 treatments in total. Effect is usually assessed after 4-6 weeks. With episodic tension headache, many see improvement faster. With chronic form, you often need to work with multiple factors simultaneously.

7

Prognosis - How Does It Go?

Episodic tension headache generally has a good prognosis:

75% continue only episodically
~50% remission over 3 years
15-25% develop chronic form

Risk Factors for Chronification

  • Medication overuse (strongest predictor)
  • Concurrent migraine
  • Sleep problems
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Sedentary lifestyle

Realistic Expectations

Relapse is common, especially with the chronic form. Treatment can provide significant relief and fewer headache days, but preventive measures (exercise, stress management, good habits) often need to be maintained to keep complaints in check.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Is tension headache dangerous?

No, tension headache is not dangerous in itself. It is a primary headache - meaning a condition in itself, not a symptom of underlying disease. However, red flags should always be taken seriously.

What is the difference between tension headache and migraine?

Migraine is often one-sided and pulsating, worsens with physical activity, and typically comes with nausea and both light and sound sensitivity. Tension headache is bilateral, pressing/tightening, does not worsen with activity, and rarely causes nausea.

Can a chiropractor help with tension headache?

Yes, there is moderate to good evidence that manual treatment helps with tension headache. Soft tissue treatment and trigger point therapy have shown large effects in systematic reviews (SMD -0.86 to -1.45).

How often should I have treatment?

For active complaints, 1-2 times per week is common initially. Most notice improvement after 4-6 treatments. For chronic headache, it may take longer.

Should I get an MRI of my head?

Usually not. Tension headache is diagnosed clinically, and imaging is only relevant for atypical symptoms or red flags. MRI often shows "normal findings" that do not explain the headache.
