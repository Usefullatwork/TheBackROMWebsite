Muscles and Trigger Points That Cause Headache

How neck and shoulder muscles cause headache

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Tension headache is the world's most common headache diagnosis. The Global Burden of Disease study identified 961 million cases globally, making tension headache the third most prevalent condition of all diseases studied.

But what exactly is the connection between the muscles in your neck and shoulders and the headache you feel in your temple, forehead or back of head? The answer lies in trigger points - muscle knots that send pain to the head.

A double-blind study found trigger points in 85% of patients with chronic tension headache, compared to only 30% in the control group.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 85% of chronic tension headache involves trigger points
  • Pain is felt in the head, but the cause is in the muscles
  • Most common: Suboccipital, trapezius, SCM, temporalis
  • Moderate evidence for trigger point treatment
  • 3.5 fewer attacks/month with treatment

Tension Headache - The Most Common Headache Type

30-78% lifetime prevalence
1.5x more common in women
3.9 trigger points on average

In practice, this means that most of us will experience tension headache during our lifetime. Women are affected about 1.5 times more often than men (partly related to hormonal factors), with the highest occurrence in the 30-49 age group. For some, the symptoms can develop into chronic daily headache.

What is a Trigger Point?

A trigger point is an irritated area in a muscle that is hard and tender to touch. What is special about trigger points is that they often cause so-called referred pain - the pain is felt in a different location than where the trigger point actually is.

On average, patients with chronic tension headache have 3.9 trigger points. Of these, about half are active (cause spontaneous pain) and half are latent (only cause pain when pressed).

2

How Each Muscle Contributes to Headache

Suboccipital Muscles - The Most Important Source

65-92% prevalence

The suboccipital muscles lie deep at the base of the skull, right beneath the cranium. This muscle group has the highest prevalence of trigger points in headache patients. Irritation here can also contribute to occipital neuralgia.

Referral pattern: Pain spreads laterally across the back of the head and temple, often experienced as "pain inside the head" extending from the neck forward toward the eye and forehead. This is the largest referral area compared to other neck muscles.

Upper Trapezius - The Classic "Ram's Horn" Pattern

85% in headache patients

The upper trapezius runs from the shoulder up to the neck and skull. Trigger points here produce a characteristic pain pattern that begins at the base of the skull, curves around the ear to the temple and continues behind the eye - shaped like a ram's horn.

Important: 45% of patients recognized this referred pain as their usual headache, confirming that the trigger point is actually active and contributing to the symptoms.

SCM (Sternocleidomastoid) - The Neck Muscles That Can Cause Dizziness

Up to 7 trigger points

The SCM is the large muscle on the side of the neck that you can feel when you turn your head. This muscle can harbor up to seven trigger points - one of the highest densities in the body.

  • Sternal division (front): Pain deep in the eye socket, above the eyebrow, in the cheek/sinus region
  • Clavicular division (back): Frontal headache, deep ear pain

What is unique about SCM trigger points is that they can also cause dizziness and balance disturbances. This often overlaps with symptoms of vestibular migraine.

Temporalis - The Temple Muscle

70% in children with headache

The temporalis covers the temple and is active when you chew. Trigger points here cause pain that radiates upward across the forehead, around the ear and down to the eyebrow.

The most confusing pattern is referred pain to teeth and gums - both in the upper and lower jaw. This often leads to unnecessary dental visits before the muscular cause is discovered.

Masseter - The Jaw Muscle That Mimics Sinus Pain

55% with jaw problems

The masseter is the most powerful chewing muscle. Trigger points here cause pain that is often mistaken for sinus problems - you feel pressure and aching in the upper jaw, cheek region and molar teeth. Deeper fibers can cause discomfort in the jaw joint or ear.

Splenius Capitis and Levator Scapulae

82% on dominant side

Splenius capitis runs from the neck up to the skull. Trigger points here cause characteristic pain on top of the head (vertex) and behind the eye. Many describe it as "pressure from above".

Levator scapulae runs from the shoulder blade up to the upper cervical spine. Trigger points mainly cause pain in the neck and shoulder blade region, but can also contribute to a complex headache picture.

The Combination Recreates the Entire Headache Experience

A key study showed that the combination of referred pain from several active trigger points reproduces the entire headache experience in patients with chronic tension headache. This explains why many have "headache everywhere" - they have active trigger points in multiple muscles.

3

Forward Head Posture and Poor Posture

Studies have established a significant correlation between forward head posture and the presence of trigger points.

Patients with chronic tension headache had a craniovertebral angle of 45.3 degrees compared to 54.1 degrees in healthy controls. The lower the angle, the more forward the head position.

Interestingly, there is also a negative correlation between craniovertebral angle and headache frequency: More forward head position = more headache days.

Posture Awareness is Important

Since forward head posture correlates with both trigger point occurrence and headache frequency, conscious posture correction is important. Think about tucking your chin slightly in and bringing your head back ("chin tuck").

4

Diagnosis

Headache from muscular causes is diagnosed primarily through clinical examination. Imaging such as MRI or CT is rarely necessary for typical tension headache and often shows findings like wear or degeneration that do not explain the symptoms.

The Examination Includes:

  • Palpation of neck and facial muscles to locate trigger points
  • Assessment of whether pressure on the trigger point reproduces the patient's known headache
  • Posture assessment including forward head position
  • Neurological screening to rule out serious causes
  • Craniovertebral mobility and neck joint function

ICHD-3 Classification of Tension Headache:

  • Infrequent episodic: Less than 1 day/month
  • Frequent episodic: 1-14 days/month
  • Chronic: 15 days or more/month for over 3 months
5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • Sudden, explosive headache ("thunderclap") - worst headache ever
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness (suspected meningitis)
  • Headache after head injury
  • Headache with visual disturbances, speech problems or paralysis
  • New headache after age 50
  • Headache that gradually worsens over weeks
  • Headache that significantly changes character from before

These warning signs follow the SNNOOP10 criteria for secondary headache.

6

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

A systematic review analyzed 7 randomized controlled trials with 316 participants. Manual trigger point treatment resulted in:

  • Reduction in attack frequency: 3.5 fewer attacks/month
  • Lower pain intensity: 12.83 points on a 0-100 scale
  • Shorter attack duration: 0.51 hours per episode

A Norwegian RCT compared manual therapy with usual GP follow-up for chronic tension headache. Manual therapy produced 6.4 fewer headache days at 8 weeks, with effect persisting at 26-week follow-up.

Typical treatment protocol: 6-24 treatments over 4-12 weeks, with 1-3 treatments weekly depending on severity.

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Stretching neck muscles - especially upper trapezius and SCM
  • Self-massage of accessible trigger points with tennis ball or massage ball
  • Heat therapy over tense muscles
  • Ergonomic adjustment of workspace to reduce forward head position
  • Frequent breaks during screen work with neck and shoulder exercises
7

Prognosis

Most people with episodic tension headache get good relief with treatment. Manual treatment often produces effect within 4-8 weeks.

Realistic Expectations:

  • Treatment typically reduces headache frequency and intensity, but does not necessarily eliminate all headaches
  • Relapse is common, especially with persistent risk factors like poor posture or stress
  • Long-term results (6-12 months) are often comparable between different treatment methods
  • Active self-effort with exercises and posture awareness improves outcomes
  • Some experience morning headache as a result of nighttime teeth grinding and muscle tension

Chronic tension headache (15 days or more/month) often has a more persistent course and may require longer treatment periods and a multidisciplinary approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can tight muscles really cause headache?
Yes. Trigger points in neck and facial muscles produce documented referred pain to the head. In studies, 45% of headache patients recognize the pain from a trigger point as their usual headache.
What is the difference between tension headache and migraine?
Tension headache typically feels pressing/tightening on both sides, with mild to moderate intensity that does not worsen with normal physical activity. Migraine is often one-sided, pulsating, moderate to severe, and worsens with activity. Migraine usually has nausea and light/sound sensitivity.
How many treatments do I need?
Typically 6-24 treatments over 4-12 weeks are recommended depending on whether the headache is episodic or chronic. Many notice improvement after 4-6 treatments.
Can I treat trigger points myself?
Some trigger points can be treated with self-massage, especially in the upper trapezius and areas you can easily reach with a tennis ball against the wall. Deeper muscles like the suboccipitals often require professional treatment.
Why do I have headache even though the muscles are the problem?
Trigger points cause referred pain - the pain is registered in a different location than where the problem is. This is a well-known neurological phenomenon. The brain interprets signals from the irritated muscle fibers as pain in a larger area, often in the head.
Do painkillers help with muscle headache?
Common painkillers like paracetamol and ibuprofen can temporarily reduce the headache, but do not treat the underlying muscle problem. With frequent use (more than 2-3 times per week), you also risk medication overuse headache.
Should I get an MRI or X-ray of my neck?
For typical tension headache without red flags, imaging is rarely necessary. The images often show "normal findings" like wear and degeneration that do not explain the headache. Clinical examination is often more useful.
