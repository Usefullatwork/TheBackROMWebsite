Vestibular Migraine: When Dizziness Takes Over

The most common cause of episodic dizziness - symptoms, treatment, and prognosis

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Migraine primarily affecting the balance system, not necessarily with headache
  • Prevalence: 1-3% of the population, most common cause of episodic dizziness
  • Treatment: Preventive medications, CGRP inhibitors for refractory cases, vestibular rehabilitation
  • Red flags: Acute headache with neck stiffness, visual disturbances, unilateral hearing loss
Book assessment

You wake up one morning and the room is spinning. Not the brief dizziness you know from standing up too fast, but a prolonged, nauseating rotation that lasts for hours. Doctors find nothing wrong with your ear. MRI scans are normal. Perhaps you are told it is "just stress" or that you need to "learn to live with it."

If this sounds familiar, you may be among the many who have vestibular migraine without knowing it. This is actually the most common cause of episodic dizziness, but the condition is so underdiagnosed that patients wait an average of 7-8 years before receiving the correct diagnosis.

When to Seek Immediate Help

For sudden, intense headache ("the worst headache of my life"), headache with fever and neck stiffness, unilateral hearing loss, double vision, or weakness in face/arm/leg - call emergency services or go to the emergency room immediately.

1

What is Vestibular Migraine?

Vestibular migraine is a neurological condition where migraine mechanisms in the brain affect the balance centers in the brainstem and cerebellum. Contrary to what many believe, you do not need to have a headache during an attack. In fact, only about 25% of patients experience headache simultaneously with dizziness.

The condition affects 1-3% of the population and is far more common than Meniere's disease, which many are mistakenly diagnosed with. In specialized dizziness clinics, vestibular migraine accounts for up to 25% of all newly referred patients. Women are affected more often than men, at a ratio of approximately 3-5:1.

Why is the condition so often overlooked?

Headache and dizziness rarely occur simultaneously. The patient may have had migraine in their youth that "disappeared," only to develop unexplained dizziness 10-20 years later. In an American study, only 10% of patients who met the criteria for vestibular migraine had been told that migraine was the cause.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Pathophysiology - What Happens in the Brain?

During an attack, CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide) is released - a signaling molecule that plays a key role in migraine. CGRP is found both in the trigeminal system and in vestibular ganglia. When released, normal signal processing in the balance centers is disrupted.

Risk Factors (from most to least common)

Previous Migraine (most important)

With or without aura - the most important risk factor. Many have had migraine in their youth that "disappeared."

Female Gender

3-5 times higher risk than men.

Family History

40-70% have a family history of migraine or vertigo.

Anxiety and Depression

Often concurrent conditions that can worsen symptoms.

Previous Head Injury

Significantly increases the risk.

Triggers That Can Provoke Attacks

  • Stress: Both acute stress and "let-down" stress (when you finally relax)
  • Sleep: Too little, too much, or irregular sleep patterns
  • Hormones: Menstrual cycle and menopause
  • Food and drink: Red wine, aged cheese, chocolate, caffeine
  • Sensory overstimulation: Bright lights, loud sounds, strong smells
3

Symptoms - More Than Just Dizziness

Main Dizziness Symptoms

Spontaneous Rotational Vertigo (45%)

The room spins, typically lasting 1-4 hours. So intense that you cannot continue activities.

Position-Triggered Dizziness (25%)

Triggered by head movement, but lasts minutes to hours (not seconds as with BPPV/crystals in the ear).

Visually Triggered Dizziness (40%)

Worsens in busy visual environments (shopping centers, escalators). Scrolling on screens triggers symptoms.

Accompanying Symptoms

  • Nausea (72% of patients)
  • Light and sound sensitivity
  • Pressure in the head or ears
  • Tinnitus (ringing in the ears) - often high-frequency
  • Neck pain and stiffness - very common!
4

Diagnosis

Barany Society/IHS Diagnostic Criteria

For a diagnosis of "Definite Vestibular Migraine," the following must be met:

  • At least 5 episodes with vestibular symptoms of moderate to severe intensity
  • Duration from 5 minutes to 72 hours
  • Current or previous history of migraine (with or without aura)
  • At least 50% of attacks must have at least one migraine feature: headache, light/sound sensitivity, or visual aura
  • Symptoms cannot be better explained by another diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Duration Key Features
Vestibular migraine Minutes to hours Migraine features, gradual onset
BPPV (crystals) Seconds Abrupt onset, specific head position
Meniere's disease Hours Progressive hearing loss, unilateral fullness
Vestibular neuritis Days Single episode, gradual improvement

Red Flags - Seek Medical Attention For:

  • Sudden, intense headache ("the worst headache of my life")
  • Headache with fever and neck stiffness
  • Unilateral hearing loss or tinnitus
  • Double vision or vision loss
  • Weakness or numbness in face, arm, or leg
  • Speech or language difficulties
  • Fainting in connection with dizziness
5

Treatment

Preventive Medications

A systematic review ranked effectiveness as follows (Frontiers in Pharmacology, 2023):

First-Line Options

  • Propranolol (beta-blocker): 60% achieve complete symptom control
  • Topiramate (antiepileptic): Moderate evidence

Second-Line Options

Venlafaxine, Valproate, Amitriptyline

CGRP Inhibitors - New Treatment Option

The new CGRP monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, galcanezumab, fremanezumab) show promising results. A prospective study showed that 90% achieved at least 50% reduction in vertigo frequency. Average number of dizziness days fell from 10.3 to 0.8 days per month after 12 months.

Vestibular Rehabilitation

Vestibular rehabilitation provides significant improvement and includes:

  • Eye movement exercises (VOR training)
  • Balance training
  • Habituation for visual hypersensitivity
  • Gradual exposure to trigger situations

Important: Training must be dosed carefully. Too intense training can actually trigger new attacks.

Manual Treatment and the Neck

Neck pain is very common in patients with vestibular migraine. A case series from 2024 showed that manual treatment of upper cervical vertebrae provided rapid and lasting improvement. The basis is trigeminocervical convergence - nerve pathways from the upper neck (C1-C3) converge with pathways from the vestibular system.

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

  • Manual treatment: Mobilization and manipulation of the upper neck
  • Soft tissue techniques: For tight neck muscles
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: Customized exercises
  • Trigger management: Advice on lifestyle factors
6

What Can You Do Yourself?

Sleep

Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day. Aim for 7-8 hours.

Diet

Eat regularly. Identify and reduce food triggers (keep a diary).

Stress

Regular physical activity. Mindfulness or breathing exercises.

Trigger Diary

Keep a diary of attacks and possible triggers. This helps you and your practitioner identify patterns.

7

Prognosis - How Does It Progress?

With proper treatment, most experience significant improvement. Patients on preventive treatment see reduction in both frequency, intensity, and duration of attacks.

Realistic Expectations

  • Relapses are common: Even with good treatment, many will experience periods with more attacks
  • Treatment takes time: It may take 2-3 months before preventive medications have full effect
  • Individual variation: What works for one person does not necessarily work for another

The positive news is that many find that attacks become less intense and less frequent over the years, especially with good treatment and trigger management. In some cases, prolonged vestibular migraine can develop into PPPD (persistent postural-perceptual dizziness), which requires a different treatment approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is vestibular migraine?

Vestibular migraine is a neurological condition where migraine mechanisms in the brain affect the balance centers. Unlike regular migraine, you do not need to have a headache - only 25% experience headache during dizziness episodes.

How common is vestibular migraine?

Vestibular migraine affects 1-3% of the population and is the most common cause of episodic dizziness. In specialized dizziness clinics, the condition accounts for up to 25% of all newly referred patients.

How is vestibular migraine treated?

Treatment includes preventive medications (propranolol, topiramate), CGRP inhibitors for refractory cases, vestibular rehabilitation, and manual treatment of the neck. Lifestyle changes such as regular sleep and trigger management are also important.

Can a chiropractor help with vestibular migraine?

Yes, manual treatment of upper cervical vertebrae can provide improvement in many patients. This is due to trigeminocervical convergence - nerve pathways from the upper neck converge with pathways from the vestibular system.
