Hip Pain? - Complete Guide

Your complete guide to causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Hip pain is more common than many think. Around 10% of adults experience it (Picavet 2003), and in people over 60, 14-19% report persistent problems. Women are affected more often than men, especially after age 40.

But here's the important part: most have problems that come from muscles, tendons or joints - not from serious injury or disease. And even more important: most get better without surgery. The LEAP study from 2018 showed that 78% of patients with tendon pain on the outside of the hip improved with exercise and guidance - better than with cortisone injection.

The problem is that hip pain can be confusing. Groin pain can come from the hip joint, but also from the groin muscles or an irritated nerve. Pain on the outside of the hip is often misdiagnosed as "bursitis". And buttock pain? It can come from the hip, back or pelvis. This guide helps you sort it out.

Quick Summary

  • Hip pain affects 10% of adults - most get better without surgery
  • Most common causes: Tendinopathy (GTPS), osteoarthritis, FAI - all treated well conservatively
  • MRI findings often mislead: 69% of pain-free people have labral tears
  • Success rates: GTPS 78% with exercise, FAI up to 82% avoid surgery

What does "non-specific hip pain" mean?

For most people, the pain comes from muscles, tendons or joints - not from serious injury. Conservative treatment (exercise, manual therapy) is often as effective as surgery.

Where Does It Hurt? Find Your Pain Type

Instead of listing diagnoses, I'll start with what you actually notice: where is the pain located? This often gives the best clue to what's wrong.

Pain on the OUTSIDE of the hip

Typical signs: Pain over the large bony prominence on the outside of the hip. Painful to lie on your side at night. Worsens with stairs, longer walks or getting up from a chair.

Who is affected? GTPS occurs in 15% of women and 6-8% of men between 50-79 years. Women are affected over 3 times as often.

Prognosis: The LEAP study showed that exercise + education gave 78.6% success rate after one year. Read more about GTPS

Pain in the GROIN (front of the hip)

Typical signs: Deep pain in the front of the hip or groin. Worst when bending the hip far (putting on shoes, sitting long, going up stairs). May feel stiff in the morning.

  • Hip osteoarthritis: Most common in those over 55-60 years. Read more
  • FAI (hip impingement): Common in young, active people. Read more
  • Labral tear: 69% of pain-free adults have labral tears on MRI. Read more

Pain in the BUTTOCK or back

Typical signs: Pain in the buttock, lower back or back of the hip. May radiate down the thigh. Often worse when sitting or standing on one leg.

  • SI joint: If you can point to the "dimple" at the top of the buttock. Read more
  • Piriformis syndrome: Deep buttock pain mimicking sciatica. Read more

Common Causes of Hip Pain

GTPS (Gluteal Tendinopathy)

Previously misdiagnosed as "bursitis" - studies show that less than 5% actually have inflammation in the bursa. In 85-98%, the problem is in the gluteal tendons. This is the most common cause of pain on the outside of the hip.

Hip Osteoarthritis

Wear and tear in the hip joint, most common after 55-60. Causes gradually increasing stiffness, especially with internal rotation. Exercise is the first-choice treatment.

FAI (Femoroacetabular Impingement)

Pinching in the hip joint due to bone shape. Common in young, active people. 37% of pain-free people have FAI morphology on MRI - so images must be interpreted with caution.

The MRI Trap

MRI shows everything - including things that are normal age-related changes. 69% of pain-free people have labral tears on MRI. A "tear" doesn't necessarily mean it's the source of your pain. I treat people, not images.

Treatment: Conservative vs. Surgery

Research tells a clear story: for most hip and pelvic pain, exercise and manual treatment are as good or better than invasive measures.

Treatment at the Chiropractor

Treatment starts with a thorough examination to find out where your pain actually comes from - not just what an image shows. Then I create a plan tailored to you.

  • Manual therapy: Joint mobilization, soft tissue work
  • Shockwave therapy: Particularly effective for tendinopathies
  • Exercise guidance: Individualized program
  • Load management: Training modification advice

What about FAI surgery? The UK FASHIoN study compared hip surgery with physiotherapy in 348 patients. Both groups improved significantly. Conservative treatment should be tried first for most.

4

Acute Self-Help

How to sleep

Night pain is very common with hip pain, especially with GTPS. When you lie on your side, the tendons are compressed against the bone.

  • Avoid lying on the painful side
  • Use a pillow between your knees when lying on the healthy side
  • Lie on your back - often the best position

How to sit and stand

  • Don't sit with legs crossed - stretches the tendons
  • Avoid "hanging" on the hip when standing
  • For groin pain: Lower the chair slightly to open the hip angle
5

Red Flags: When You Must Seek Help Quickly

Contact emergency services IMMEDIATELY if:

  • Cannot bear weight on the leg after a fall or injury
  • Fever together with hip or back pain
  • Severe swelling, redness and warmth over the hip
  • Loss of control over bladder or bowel
  • Sudden, severe weakness or numbness in the leg

Contact a doctor or chiropractor within a few days if:

  • Persistent night pain that wakes you repeatedly
  • Morning stiffness lasting over 60 minutes
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with persistent pain

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it dangerous if my hip "clicks" or "snaps"?

Usually not. "Snapping hip" is very common - affecting 5-10% of the population - and is rarely dangerous as long as it doesn't hurt. It only becomes concerning if accompanied by pain or locking.

Does stretching the hip help?

Be careful. For GTPS or proximal hamstring tendinopathy, aggressive stretching can make pain worse. Focus instead on strength training and mobility within pain limits.

Does cortisone help?

Short-term yes, long-term doubtful. The LEAP study showed that after 1 year, cortisone results were no better than "wait and see". I usually recommend trying exercise first.

Can I exercise with hip pain?

Yes, and you should! Research shows that exercise is more effective than passive treatment for most hip pain. But train smart: avoid activities that cause severe pain.

Do I need an MRI or X-ray?

Not always - and often not initially. A thorough clinical examination often provides enough information. Imaging is relevant if symptoms don't improve as expected.

How long until I get better?

This varies greatly. Acute muscle problems: 2-4 weeks. GTPS: 6-9 months with proper treatment. Hip osteoarthritis: most notice effects from exercise within 8-12 weeks.
