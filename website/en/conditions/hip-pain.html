Hip Pain? - Complete Guide

Your complete guide to causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Hip pain is more common than many think. Around 10% of adults experience it (Picavet 2003), and in people over 60, 14-19% report persistent problems. Women are affected more often than men, especially after age 40.

But here's the important part: most have problems that come from muscles, tendons or joints - not from serious injury or disease. And even more important: most get better without surgery. The LEAP study from 2018 showed, for example, that 78% of patients with tendon pain on the outside of the hip improved with exercise and guidance - better than with cortisone injection.

The problem is that hip pain can be confusing. Groin pain can come from the hip joint, but also from the groin muscles or an irritated nerve. Pain on the outside of the hip is often misdiagnosed as "bursitis". And buttock pain? It can come from the hip, back and pelvis.

This guide helps you sort it out.

Quick Summary

  • Hip pain affects 10% of adults - most get better without surgery
  • Most common causes: Tendinopathy (GTPS), osteoarthritis, FAI - all treated well conservatively
  • MRI findings lie often: 69% of pain-free people have labral tears, 37% have FAI morphology
  • Success rates: GTPS 78% with exercise, FAI up to 82% avoid surgery

What does "non-specific hip pain" mean?

For most people, the pain comes from muscles, tendons or joints - not from serious injury. Conservative treatment (exercise, manual therapy) is often as effective as surgery.

Where Does It Hurt? Find Your Pain Type

Instead of listing diagnoses, I'll start with what you actually notice: where is the pain located? This often gives the best clue to what's wrong.

Pain on the OUTSIDE of the hip

Go to full article on GTPS →

Typical signs: Pain over the large bony prominence on the outside of the hip. Painful to lie on your side at night. Worsens with stairs, longer walks or getting up from a chair. May radiate down the outside of the thigh towards the knee.

Myth: "It's bursitis"

No, it almost never is. Studies show that less than 5% actually have inflammation in the bursa. In 85-98%, the problem is in the tendons of the gluteal muscles - gluteus medius and minimus. This is called gluteal tendinopathy or GTPS, and requires completely different treatment than cortisone.

Who is affected? GTPS occurs in 15% of women and 6-8% of men between 50-79 years. Women are affected over 3 times as often. The condition accounts for 10-20% of all hip pain at the GP.

Prognosis: The LEAP study showed that exercise + education gave 78.6% success rate after one year - better than cortisone (58%) and "wait and see" (52%). Improvement takes time (6-9 months), but most get better without injections.

Pain in the GROIN (front of the hip)

Typical signs: Deep pain in the front of the hip or groin. Worst when bending the hip far (putting on shoes, sitting long, going up stairs). May feel stiff in the morning. Some experience clicking or locking.

Groin pain can have several causes - and age and activity level give important clues:

  • Hip osteoarthritis: Most common in those over 55-60 years. 10-15% of people over 65 have symptomatic hip osteoarthritis. Causes gradually increasing stiffness, especially with internal rotation. Exercise is first choice. Read more →
  • FAI (femoroacetabular impingement): Pinching in the hip joint due to bone shape. Common in young, active people. Causes pain with deep flexion + internal rotation. 37% of pain-free people have FAI morphology on MRI - so images must be interpreted with caution. Read more →
  • Labral tear: Damage to the cartilage ring around the hip. Often causes clicking, locking or feeling that the hip "pops out". 69% of pain-free adults have labral tears on MRI - so not all tears need treatment. Read more →
  • Iliopsoas tendinopathy: Pain when lifting the leg, often with "snapping" in front of the hip. Common in runners, dancers and footballers. Read more →

Pain in the BUTTOCK or back

Typical signs: Pain in the buttock, lower back or back of the hip. May radiate down the thigh. Often worse when sitting, standing on one leg or turning in bed.

Buttock pain can come from the hip, back OR pelvis. Here are some clues:

  • SI joint ("pelvic dysfunction"): If you can point with one finger to where it hurts - right at the "dimple" at the top of the buttock - it's often the SI joint. SI joint problems account for 15-30% of all chronic low back pain. Read more →
  • Piriformis syndrome: Deep pain in the buttock that can radiate down the back of the thigh. Mimics sciatica, but comes from a tight muscle - not the back. Read more →
  • Sitting bone (proximal hamstring): Painful to sit, especially on hard chairs? Pain where "bottom meets thigh"? This may be tendinopathy in the hamstring attachment. Important: don't stretch hamstrings - it makes pain worse. Read more →
  • Referred pain from the back: Problems in the lower back can cause pain felt in the buttock or hip. Studies show that a full 71% of patients with hip problems actually have buttock pain as the dominant symptom.

Numbness, tingling or burning pain

Typical signs: Burning, tingling or numb feeling. May be on the outside of the thigh, or radiate from back/buttock down into the leg.

  • Meralgia paraesthetica: Compression of a nerve causing numbness or burning on the outside of the thigh. Common with overweight, pregnancy or tight trousers/belts. 85% get better without surgery. Read more →
  • Sciatica/lumbar radiculopathy: Radiation from back/buttock down the back or outside of the leg - often past the knee to the foot. Usually caused by an irritated nerve root in the back.

Do-It-Yourself Tests: Find Out More Before You Come

Before you book an appointment, you can try these simple tests at home. They often give a good indication of what's wrong - and help me get to the bottom of the problem faster.

Test 1: The sock test (checks the hip joint)

How to do it: Sit on a chair. Try to put on socks on the painful side without bending your back much - by pulling your foot up towards you and rotating the hip outward.

Result: Is it difficult or does it hurt deep in the groin? Studies show this is much more common with hip osteoarthritis than with tendon problems on the outside. It indicates stiffness in the hip joint itself.

Test 2: The single-leg test (checks gluteal tendons)

How to do it: Stand on the painful leg for 30 seconds. Hold lightly onto a wall for balance if needed.

Result: If this reproduces the familiar pain on the outside of the hip, it strongly suggests GTPS (gluteal tendinopathy). A recent meta-analysis showed this test has 94% sensitivity and 99% specificity for tendon problems on the outside of the hip.

Test 3: The pointing test (checks SI joint vs. hip)

How to do it: Where is the pain? Try to point to the most painful spot with one finger.

Result: Are you pointing right at the "dimple" or hollow at the lower back? Then the pain probably comes from the SI joint (pelvic joint), not the hip. If you point to the groin (often forming a C with your hand), it's more often the hip joint itself.

Hip, Back or Pelvis? How to Tell Them Apart

Many who come to me think they have "hip problems" - but the pain actually comes from the back or pelvis. And vice versa. Here are the rules of thumb:

Where? Typical pain pattern
Hip Pain in the groin or front of the thigh. May radiate to the knee, but rarely further. Stiffness with internal rotation. Patients with groin pain are 7x more likely to have a hip problem.
Back Pain radiating below the knee - often all the way to the foot. Numbness or tingling in specific areas. Burning, "electric" pain. Weakness in foot or toes.
Pelvis Localised pain at the back/buttock junction (SI joint). Often sharp with quick movements: turning in bed, getting up, walking stairs. May radiate to buttock/back of thigh, but stops at the knee.

The important thing:

This table is a simplification. Many have overlapping symptoms, and this is where a thorough clinical examination becomes crucial.

The MRI Trap: Why the Image Doesn't Always Match

"MRI shows I have a tear - do I need surgery?"

Probably not. This is one of the most important things you can learn about hip pain.

MRI is fantastic technology. But it shows everything - including things that are completely normal age-related changes. The problem is that many of the "findings" are found just as often in people without pain.

The point?

A "tear" or "wear" on MRI doesn't necessarily mean it's the source of your pain. Images must be interpreted in context with symptoms and examination findings. I treat people, not images.

Acute Self-Help: What You Can Do Now

While you wait for treatment, it's important not to irritate the tissue unnecessarily. Here are the "fire-fighting measures" I recommend:

How to sleep

Night pain is very common with hip pain, especially with tendon problems on the outside (GTPS). When you lie on your side, the tendons are compressed against the bone - like squeezing a tendon between two hard surfaces.

  • Avoid lying on the painful side: This increases compression on the tendons.
  • Use a pillow between your knees: If lying on the healthy side, a thick pillow from knee to ankle keeps the hip neutral.
  • Lie on your back: Often the best position. Optionally with a pillow under the knees.

How to sit and stand

  • Don't sit with legs crossed: This stretches the tendons on the outside of the hip.
  • Avoid "hanging" on the hip: When standing, distribute weight equally on both legs.
  • For groin pain: Lower the chair slightly or tilt the seat forward to open the hip angle.

Treatment: Conservative vs. Surgery

Many think that "something must be done" - preferably quickly, preferably an injection or surgery. But research tells a different story: for most hip and pelvic pain, exercise and manual treatment are as good or better than invasive measures.

What about FAI surgery?

The UK FASHIoN study compared hip surgery with physiotherapy in 348 patients. Both groups improved significantly. The physiotherapy group went from 35.6 to 49.7 points, the surgery group from 39.2 to 58.8 points. Conservative treatment should be tried first for most.

What about cortisone?

Cortisone injections can provide short-term pain relief, but long-term effects are poorly documented. The LEAP study showed that the cortisone group after 1 year was no better than the "wait and see" group. Systematic reviews have also shown negative effects on tendon tissue.

7

Red Flags: When You Must Seek Help Quickly

Contact emergency services IMMEDIATELY if:

  • Cannot bear weight on the leg after a fall or injury
  • Fever together with hip or back pain
  • Severe swelling, redness and warmth over the hip
  • Loss of control over bladder or bowel
  • Sudden, severe weakness or numbness in the leg

Contact a doctor or chiropractor within a few days if:

  • Persistent night pain that wakes you repeatedly
  • Morning stiffness lasting over 60 minutes (may indicate inflammatory disease)
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with persistent pain
  • Runners with gradually increasing groin pain and night waking - may be femoral neck stress fracture

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it dangerous if my hip "clicks" or "snaps"?

Usually not. "Snapping hip" is very common - affecting 5-10% of the population - and is rarely dangerous as long as it doesn't hurt. The sound is usually caused by a tendon sliding over a bony prominence. In ballet dancers, the prevalence is up to 90%. It only becomes concerning if the clicking is accompanied by pain, locking, or a feeling that the hip "gives way".

Does stretching the hip help?

Be careful. For tendon problems on the outside of the hip (GTPS) or high on the back of the thigh (proximal hamstring), aggressive stretching can actually make pain worse. Stretching compresses the tendon against the bone and increases compression. Focus instead on strength training and mobility within pain limits.

Does cortisone help?

Short-term yes, long-term doubtful. For GTPS, the LEAP study showed that cortisone gave rapid improvement in the first weeks, but after 1 year the results were no better than doing nothing. Systematic reviews have also shown potential negative effects on tendon tissue over time. I usually recommend trying exercise first.

Can I exercise with hip pain?

Yes, and you should! Research shows that exercise is more effective than passive treatment for most hip and pelvic pain. But you must train smart: avoid activities that cause severe pain, and start with exercises that don't load the joint in extreme positions. A chiropractor or physiotherapist can guide you to the right exercises.

Do I need an MRI or X-ray?

Not always - and often not initially. A thorough clinical examination often provides enough information to make a diagnosis and start treatment. Imaging is relevant if symptoms don't improve as expected. Remember: MRI often shows "findings" that don't explain your pain.

How long until I get better?

This varies greatly. Acute muscle problems can improve in 2-4 weeks. GTPS (tendon problems on the outside) often takes 6-9 months with proper treatment. Hip osteoarthritis requires a long-term approach, but most notice good effects from exercise within 8-12 weeks. Relapses are common and don't mean treatment has failed - it means your body needs maintenance.
