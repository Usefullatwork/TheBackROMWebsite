Athletic Pubalgia (Sports Hernia)

Symptoms, treatment, and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Athletic pubalgia - often called "sports hernia" - is a misleading term. The condition is not a classic inguinal hernia. There is no visible bulge or tissue protrusion. Instead, it involves an injury to the muscles and tendons that attach to the pubic bone (pubis).

The core of the problem is the so-called rectus abdominis-adductor aponeurosis plate - a shared tendon sheet where the rectus abdominis muscle and the long adductor muscle meet and attach to the front of the pubic bone. When you sprint, kick, or change direction, opposing forces occur through this attachment point.

Summary

  • What it is: Soft tissue injury in the groin region - tear or weakness in the muscle-tendon attachments at the pubic bone
  • Most common causes: Soccer (32.5% season prevalence), ice hockey, rapid direction changes
  • Treatment: Conservative treatment with structured exercise gives 79-87% success rate
  • Time to recovery: 9-18.5 weeks with conservative treatment, 6-12 weeks after surgery
  • Red flags: Visible lump in the groin (true hernia), signs of incarcerated hernia

When should you seek help immediately?

If you have a visible lump in the groin (true hernia), sudden intense pain with nausea (incarcerated hernia), or fever combined with groin pain - seek medical attention or emergency care.

1

Who is affected by athletic pubalgia?

Groin injuries account for 5-28% of all sports injuries. Athletic pubalgia specifically accounts for about 5% of all groin injuries in athletes. Among athletes with prolonged, unexplained groin pain, 50-80% have athletic pubalgia as the underlying cause.

Men account for over 90% of cases, with a male-to-female risk ratio of approximately 2.4:1. This difference is likely due to anatomical factors: women have larger and more robust attachment points for the rectus abdominis at the symphysis. Peak incidence is between ages 26-28.

2

Causes and risk factors

Sports with highest risk

Soccer (highest incidence)

A systematic review showed 32.5% season prevalence for groin pain, and up to 19% of professional players sustain injuries requiring absence from training or matches per season.

Ice Hockey

Groin strains represent 10-11% of all injuries and 43% of all muscle strains in the sport.

American Football

4.2% prevalence of athletic pubalgia repair among NFL Combine participants. Soccer, hockey, and American football together account for over 70% of all cases.

Other risk factors

  • Reduced hip mobility, especially internal rotation
  • Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) - found in about 32% of patients who undergo surgery
  • One-sided training without focus on core stability
  • Previous groin injury
3

Symptoms

The typical course is insidious onset of pain - not an acute event. The pain is felt deep in the groin or lower abdomen and worsens with activity. It improves with rest but returns when training resumes.

Characteristic features

  • Deep, aching pain in the groin that worsens with sprinting, kicking, direction changes
  • Pain with coughing or sneezing (increased abdominal pressure)
  • Vague, diffuse pain that can be difficult to localize precisely
  • Radiation to the inner thigh, perineum, or testicles in men
  • Morning stiffness after activity
  • Loss of function that forces the athlete to reduce or stop activity

The pain usually starts on one side but can develop on both sides in up to 43% of cases.

4

Diagnosis

Clinical examination

The diagnosis is primarily clinical. Five findings that support athletic pubalgia:

  1. Deep groin/lower abdominal pain that worsens with sports activity and improves with rest
  2. Palpation tenderness over the pubic ramus at the rectus abdominis attachment
  3. Pain with resisted hip adduction
  4. Pain with resisted sit-up
  5. No palpable inguinal hernia

Resisted adduction sit-up test (RASUT)

78% sensitivity and 88% negative predictive value. A valuable test to support the diagnosis in a clinical setting.

Imaging

MRI is the standard imaging modality when confirmation is needed. MRI has 68% sensitivity and 100% specificity for rectus abdominis injury, and 86% sensitivity and 89% specificity for adductor tendon injury.

Important to remember

MRI findings must be interpreted cautiously. Studies have shown that asymptomatic ice hockey players often have MRI changes resembling athletic pubalgia.

5

Treatment

Conservative treatment is the first choice for everyone with suspected athletic pubalgia. Success rates vary from 14-100% depending on protocol quality.

The critical distinction: active vs. passive approach. A landmark study showed 79% success with structured exercise therapy versus only 14% with passive physiotherapy (massage, stretching, electrotherapy).

What can a chiropractor do?

  • Thorough examination to distinguish athletic pubalgia from other causes of groin pain
  • Manual treatment of adjacent areas: hip, lower back, pelvis - which often have reduced mobility
  • Guidance in the exercise program with focus on correct execution
  • Soft tissue treatment as a supplement to active rehabilitation
  • Assessment of when further referral is necessary

What can you do yourself? (Modified Holmich protocol)

Phase 1 (weeks 0-4)

Rest from triggering activities, pain relief, gentle mobility exercises, core awareness, and transversus abdominis activation.

Phase 2 (weeks 4-8)

Progressive strength training of adductors, abdominal muscles, and hip muscles. Balance and coordination exercises. Gradual jogging.

Phase 3 (weeks 8-12)

Sport-specific training, rotational exercises, single-leg exercises, plyometrics and speed training. Gradual return to full activity.

Treatment timeline

Conservative treatment typically takes 9-18.5 weeks for return to sport. The Holmich protocol achieved 79% return to previous sport level after a median of 18.5 weeks. The modified protocol showed 86.6% success after an average of 12 weeks.

When is surgery appropriate?

Surgery is reserved for patients who do not respond to 2-6 months of adequate conservative treatment. Surgical results are good: 97% return to sport in the surgery group versus 50% in the conservative group at 12 months - in patients with confirmed defects.

6

Red flags - when to see a doctor

Seek medical or emergency care for:

  • Visible lump in the groin - may be a true inguinal hernia requiring different treatment
  • Sudden, intense pain with nausea - sign of possible incarcerated hernia
  • Unexplained weight loss or general symptoms
  • Mechanical symptoms in the hip (clicking, locking) - may indicate hip pathology
  • Fever combined with groin pain
  • No improvement after 3-6 months with proper conservative treatment
7

Prognosis - What to expect

Long-term outcomes are generally good.

79-87% success with conservative treatment
90%+ return to sport after surgery
43% develop symptoms on both sides

With conservative treatment: 79-87% return to normal sports activity with a structured exercise program. The traditional claim that only 20-40% recover conservatively comes from older studies with passive approaches.

After surgery: Over 90% return to sport without recurrence at 1-year follow-up.

Risk of recurrence: Low after successful surgery. With conservative treatment, symptoms may return with too rapid return to activity.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I continue training with athletic pubalgia?
It is not recommended to train through the pain. Continued activity worsens the condition and prolongs rehabilitation time. A modified training plan that avoids triggering movements (kicking, rapid turns) is necessary at first.
What is the difference between athletic pubalgia and a regular groin strain?
Groin strain (adductor strain) affects the muscle tissue and usually resolves within 3-4 weeks. Athletic pubalgia involves the tendon-bone attachment at the pubic bone and has a more prolonged course. Adductor strain accounts for 62% of groin injuries in soccer.
Do I need an MRI to get a diagnosis?
Not necessarily. Clinical examination is often sufficient to make the diagnosis. MRI is useful to confirm suspicion, rule out other causes, and plan any surgery - but changes on MRI are also found in asymptomatic athletes.
How long does it take to recover?
With structured conservative treatment: 9-18.5 weeks to return to sport. After surgery: 6-12 weeks. The time depends on the severity of the injury and how quickly proper treatment begins.
Can a chiropractor help, or do I need to go straight to a surgeon?
Conservative treatment with structured exercise is the first choice and succeeds in 79-87% of patients. A chiropractor can help with examination, guidance, and manual treatment. Surgery is appropriate only after 2-6 months without sufficient improvement.
