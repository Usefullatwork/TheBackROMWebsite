Back, Hip or Sciatica?

How to tell the difference and why correct diagnosis matters

Summary (TL;DR)

  • 44% of hip patients are initially misdiagnosed
  • Hip pain is typically felt in the groin and front of thigh
  • Back pain often radiates down the back of the leg
  • Up to 30% have both hip and back problems simultaneously
  • A thorough clinical examination is the key to correct diagnosis
1

Why Is This So Difficult?

44% of hip patients are initially given the wrong diagnosis

Back, hip and sciatica share overlapping symptoms and pain patterns. Leg pain can come from the hip joint, from the back via the nerves, or from both places simultaneously. Research shows that almost half of all patients with hip problems are first given a wrong diagnosis - often treated for back problems they don't have.

This article helps you understand the most important differences and why a thorough examination is so important.

2

Three Main Categories of Pain

Back Pain

  • Located in lower back/lumbar area
  • Can radiate to buttocks and back of thigh
  • Worsens with forward bending
  • Morning stiffness common
  • Coughing/sneezing can provoke

Hip Pain

  • Felt primarily in the groin
  • Can radiate to front of thigh
  • Worsens with hip rotation
  • Pain when putting on shoes/socks
  • Limping common

Nerve Pain (Sciatica)

  • Radiates down the leg
  • Burning, stabbing character
  • Numbness/tingling
  • Weakness in foot possible
  • Follows nerve pathway
3

Where Is the Pain Felt?

Pain location provides important clues, but is not always reliable. Here are typical patterns:

Pain Location Likely Cause Other Clues
Groin (C-sign) Hip joint, iliopsoas, inguinal hernia Worst with internal rotation, deep flexion
Outside of hip GTPS, gluteus medius, TFL Worst when lying on side, stairs
Back of hip/buttock SI joint, piriformis, referred from back Worst when sitting, rising from chair
Front of thigh Hip, L3-L4 nerve, quadriceps Check hip mobility and reflexes
Back of thigh/calf Sciatica, piriformis, trigger points Check SLR test, neurological status
Down to foot Nerve root (L4, L5, S1) Dermatome pattern, strength, reflexes

The C-Sign: A Classic Hip Problem Sign

When patients with hip problems try to point to where it hurts, they often form a "C" with their hand around the groin and front/lateral hip. This is called the C-sign and is characteristic of intra-articular hip disease (problems inside the hip joint itself).

What Makes the Pain Worse?

Provocation tests and functional movements can help distinguish between the different causes:

Typical for back/sciatica:

  • Bending forward (flexion)
  • Raising the leg straight up (SLR)
  • Coughing, sneezing, straining
  • Prolonged sitting
  • Rising from sitting

Typical for hip:

  • Rotating hip inward (FADIR)
  • Putting on shoes/socks
  • Crossing legs
  • Climbing stairs
  • Long walks
5

Hip-Spine Syndrome

A Common and Complex Picture

Up to 30% of patients with hip osteoarthritis have simultaneous significant spinal disease. This is called "hip-spine syndrome" and complicates both diagnosis and treatment.

Why Does This Occur?

  • Biomechanical coupling: A stiff hip increases load on the back
  • Compensatory patterns: Altered gait affects the entire kinetic chain
  • Common risk factors: Age, overweight, physical inactivity
  • Sensitization: Chronic pain can make the entire region hypersensitive

Treatment Implications:

When both back and hip are involved, both must be addressed to achieve improvement. A hip replacement, for example, cannot solve a simultaneous back problem, and vice versa.

6

Sciatica vs. Piriformis Syndrome

Both cause pain down the leg, but the causes are different:

Feature True Sciatica (Radiculopathy) Piriformis Syndrome
Cause Nerve root compression in the spine Nerve compression in the buttock
Neurological findings Often reduced strength, reflex, sensation Rarely neurological deficits
SLR test Positive (pain with leg raise) Often negative
Aggravating factors Bending forward, coughing, sneezing Prolonged sitting, driving
Relieving factors Lying, walking Standing, walking
MRI findings Often visible disc herniation/stenosis Usually normal
7

Important Clinical Tests

  • Straight Leg Raise (SLR): Lifting of straight leg while lying on back. Positive when pain radiates past the knee at 30-70 degree lift - suggests nerve root involvement.
  • FADIR test: Flexion, adduction and internal rotation of the hip. Positive with groin pain - suggests intra-articular hip pathology.
  • FABER test: Flexion, abduction and external rotation (figure-4 position). Pain in groin suggests hip, pain in back suggests SI joint.
  • Log Roll test: Passive rolling of the leg inward/outward while lying on back. The only isolated hip joint test - positive with groin pain.
  • Trendelenburg test: Standing on one leg. Pelvic drop on the opposite side indicates weak gluteus medius.

Red Flags - Seek Medical Help Immediately

  • Loss of bladder or bowel control (cauda equina)
  • Progressive weakness in the leg/foot
  • Numbness in the "saddle area"
  • Unexplained weight loss and night sweats
  • Fever combined with back/hip pain
8

Treatment Approach

Treatment depends entirely on correct diagnosis. This is why a thorough examination is so important:

For back pain/sciatica:

  • Manual treatment of the back
  • Exercises to reduce nerve pressure
  • Core muscle strengthening
  • Ergonomic adjustments
  • Possibly injections or surgery in severe cases

For hip pain:

  • Manual treatment of hip and pelvis
  • Strength training for hip muscles
  • Mobility exercises
  • Activity modification
  • Possibly injections or surgery for osteoarthritis/FAI

For combined hip-spine syndrome:

  • Treat both problems in parallel
  • Focus on global movement and function
  • Gradual progression of exercises
  • Often longer treatment course necessary

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if the pain is coming from my back or hip?
Hip pain is typically felt in the groin and front of the thigh, and worsens with hip rotation. Back pain often sits lower in the back and can radiate down the back of the leg. Bending forward often worsens back pain, while hip flexion worsens hip pain. A clinical examination is necessary for accurate diagnosis.
Can I have both back and hip problems at the same time?
Yes, this is actually common and is called "hip-spine syndrome". Studies show that up to 30% of patients with hip osteoarthritis also have significant spinal disease. The problems can also affect each other - hip problems can change gait and put more stress on the back, and vice versa.
What is the difference between sciatica and piriformis syndrome?
Sciatica is caused by compression of the nerve root in the spine (often from a disc herniation), while piriformis syndrome is caused by compression of the sciatic nerve where it passes through the buttock muscles. The symptoms can be similar, but piriformis syndrome rarely causes neurological deficits and often worsens with prolonged sitting.
Do I need an MRI to find out what's wrong?
Not necessarily. A thorough clinical examination can often distinguish between back and hip problems. MRI is indicated when there is suspicion of serious pathology, neurological deficits, or when conservative treatment doesn't improve symptoms after 6-8 weeks. Many MRI findings (such as degenerative changes) are also found in symptom-free individuals.
