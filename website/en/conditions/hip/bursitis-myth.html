Why Bursitis Is Rarely the Problem

Trochanteric bursitis is an outdated diagnosis - learn about GTPS | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

For decades, pain on the outside of the hip has been diagnosed as "trochanteric bursitis" or "bursitis." Patients have received cortisone injections, rest advice, and in some cases even surgery to remove the bursa.

But modern imaging has revealed an uncomfortable truth: The bursa is rarely the problem.

Summary

  • Only 8% actually have bursitis - most have tendon problems (tendinopathy)
  • The correct diagnosis is GTPS (Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome)
  • 77% improve with exercises vs. 58% with cortisone injection
  • Cortisone injections only provide short-term relief and can weaken tendons
  • 10-25% of adults experience this problem

Myth

Pain on the outside of the hip is caused by inflammation in the bursa, and is best treated with cortisone injection.

Fact

Research shows that pain on the outside of the hip is almost always caused by tendon changes (tendinopathy) - and exercises are superior treatment.

What MRI Studies Show

When researchers examine patients with "classic bursitis symptoms" using MRI, they find something surprising:

8% have bursitis as the main finding
50% have gluteal tendinopathy
30% have partial tendon tears

Most people diagnosed with "bursitis" actually have tendon problems - an entirely different condition that requires entirely different treatment.

2

GTPS - The Modern Understanding

Experts now use the term GTPS - Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome as an umbrella term for pain on the outside of the hip. This includes:

Gluteal tendinopathy

Degenerative changes in the tendons of the gluteus medius and/or minimus. This is the most common cause.

Gluteal tendon tears

Partial or complete tendon ruptures that can develop over time.

Bursitis

Inflammation in the bursa - but this is only a small proportion of cases.

External snapping hip

The IT band "clicks" over the trochanter during movement.

Why It Matters

Calling the condition "bursitis" leads to wrong treatment. Cortisone injections for inflammation don't make sense when the problem is degenerative tendons. In fact, cortisone can further weaken tendons and worsen the problem long-term.

3

The LEAP Study: Evidence for Exercises

The most important study in this area is the LEAP study from 2018, published in the prestigious BMJ journal. The study compared three treatments:

Treatment Success at 8 weeks Success at 52 weeks
Exercises + education 78% 77%
Cortisone injection 75% 58%
Wait-and-see 52% 68%

Key Findings

  • Exercises were clearly best in the long term (77% vs. 58% for cortisone)
  • Cortisone injection provided good short-term effect, but this diminished over time
  • Even "wait-and-see" was better than cortisone after 52 weeks
  • The cortisone group had higher risk of relapse
4

Why Cortisone Isn't the Solution

Problems with Cortisone Injections

  • Short-term effect: Often relieves for 4-8 weeks, then the pain returns
  • Tendon weakness: Cortisone can weaken tendons and increase risk of rupture
  • Not addressing the cause: Doesn't tackle underlying muscle weakness
  • Repeated injections: Many patients end up with serial injections
  • Fat tissue atrophy: Can cause sunken skin and altered tissue quality

This doesn't mean cortisone never has a place. For patients with very severe pain, a single injection can provide a "window" where exercises can be started. But cortisone alone is not the solution.

5

What Actually Helps

Evidence-Based Treatment of GTPS

  • Load management: Avoid provocations that worsen symptoms
  • Progressive strength training: Exercises for gluteal muscles
  • Patient education: Understanding the condition and prognosis
  • Manual therapy: Can help as a supplement to exercises
  • Patience: Tendons need time to heal (months, not weeks)

Load Management: Avoiding Provocations

The first step is to reduce loads that irritate the tendons:

  • Avoid lying on the painful side - use a pillow between your knees
  • Don't cross your legs when sitting
  • Avoid standing on one leg with the hip pushed out to the side
  • Be careful with stretching - many stretches compress the tendons
  • Limit stairs/hills in the acute phase

Progressive Strength Training

Phase 1: Isometric Exercises (weeks 1-3)

Static muscle contractions without movement. For example: Stand by a wall and press the outer leg out against the wall for 30-45 seconds. Minimal provocation, initiates tendon healing.

Phase 2: Isotonic Exercises (weeks 3-8)

Exercises with movement under load. Side-lying leg raises, standing hip abduction with resistance band, "clamshell" exercises. Gradual increase in resistance over time.

Phase 3: Functional Exercises (week 8+)

Exercises that mimic daily activities and sports. Single-leg exercises, lunges in various directions, stair walking, possibly running.

6

Risk Factors for GTPS

Some groups are more susceptible than others:

  • Women over 40: 4 times more common than in men
  • Post-menopausal women: Hormonal changes affect tendons
  • Overweight: Increased load on hip muscles
  • Runners and walkers: Repetitive loading
  • Sedentary lifestyle: Weak gluteal muscles
  • Previous hip injury: Altered biomechanics

The Chiropractor's Role

As a chiropractor, I can make the correct diagnosis (GTPS vs. other causes of hip pain), create an individualized exercise program, and provide manual therapy as a supplement. I can also identify underlying factors such as back stiffness or pelvic asymmetry that may contribute to the problem.

7

When Should You Seek Help?

Seek treatment if:

  • Pain has lasted more than 2-3 weeks without improvement
  • You have trouble sleeping due to the pain
  • Daily activities like walking and stairs are limited
  • You have tried rest and it doesn't help
  • You are unsure about the diagnosis

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is bursitis not the correct diagnosis?
MRI studies show that only 8% of patients with lateral hip pain have bursitis as the main finding. The vast majority (over 90%) have tendinopathy (tendon changes) in the gluteus medius or minimus. Therefore, the term GTPS (Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome) is now used instead of trochanteric bursitis.
Does cortisone injection help with pain on the outside of the hip?
Cortisone often provides short-term relief (4-8 weeks), but is no better than placebo in the long term. The LEAP study from 2018 showed that exercises gave 77% success after 52 weeks, compared to only 58% for cortisone injection. Cortisone can actually weaken tendons over time.
What exercises help with GTPS?
Progressive strength training for the gluteal muscles (gluteus medius and minimus) is most effective. Start with isometric exercises (static holds) and progress to concentric and eccentric exercises. Avoid stretching and external rotation in the beginning, as this can irritate the tendons.
How long does it take to recover from GTPS?
With proper treatment (exercises and load management), many see improvement within 6-12 weeks. Full recovery can take 3-6 months in chronic cases. The key is gradual progression of exercises and avoiding provocations like lying on your side and crossing your legs.
