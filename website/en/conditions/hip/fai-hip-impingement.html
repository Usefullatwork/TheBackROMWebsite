FAI - Hip Impingement

Femoroacetabular Impingement: Causes, symptoms, and treatment

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Abnormal shape of the femoral head and/or hip socket causing impingement
  • Types: Cam (bump on femoral head), Pincer (deep socket), or both
  • Main symptom: Groin pain with deep hip flexion and rotation
  • Who is affected: Common in young active people, especially athletes
  • Treatment: Exercise and physical therapy first - surgery is rarely the first choice
1

What is FAI?

FAI (Femoroacetabular Impingement), often called "hip impingement," is a common cause of hip pain. It is a condition where the shape of the hip joint leads to bone-on-bone contact in certain positions. This can irritate cartilage and the labrum over time.

FAI is not a disease, but an anatomical variant. Many people have FAI morphology without having pain. The problem arises when this shape is combined with activities requiring large hip movements.

10-15% of population
20-40 most common age
2:1 men vs women (cam)
60-70% better without surgery

The Three Types of FAI

Cam Type

A bump on the femoral head that impinges against the hip socket during flexion. Most common in men and athletes. Often develops during adolescence.

Pincer Type

The hip socket extends too far out and covers too much of the femoral head. More common in women. Causes the labrum to be pinched, which can develop into a labral tear.

Mixed Type

A combination of cam and pincer. The most common form. Approx. 70% of FAI patients have both.

2

Causes and Development

How Does FAI Develop?

FAI morphology typically develops during adolescence (12-18 years), during the period of rapid bone growth. Intensive sports during this period can affect how the hip forms.

  • Genetics - hereditary component
  • Sports in adolescence - especially soccer, hockey, dance
  • Repetitive hip movements during bone growth
  • Load before growth plates close

Why Do Some Get Pain?

Many have FAI shape without knowing it. Pain arises when:

  • Activity level increases suddenly
  • Participating in sports with large hip range of motion
  • Labrum or cartilage becomes irritated/damaged over time
  • Muscle strength and control are insufficient

Important to Understand

FAI shape on X-ray does not automatically mean you need treatment. Many live perfectly fine with FAI morphology. It is symptoms and function that matter, not images.

3

Symptoms

Typical Symptoms

  • Groin pain - often described as "deep" pain
  • Pain with deep hip flexion - squatting, sitting in a low chair
  • Hip stiffness - especially after rest
  • C-sign - patient grips around the groin with their hand
  • Clicking or locking - may indicate labral tear
  • Pain with prolonged sitting

Pain Worsens With:

  • Deep squats
  • Long car rides
  • Sitting in low chairs or sofas
  • Pivoting movements in sports
  • Getting in and out of a car
  • Tying shoes

Typical Pain Area

The pain is usually located in the front of the groin, but can also radiate to:

  • Front of thigh
  • Gluteal region
  • Lateral (outside) of the hip

When Should You Seek Help?

  • Persistent groin pain over 4-6 weeks
  • Pain limiting daily activities
  • Reduced hip movement
  • Clicking or locking that worsens
4

Diagnosis

FAI is diagnosed through a combination of history, clinical examination, and imaging.

Clinical Tests

  • FADIR test - flexion, adduction, internal rotation (highest sensitivity)
  • FABER test - flexion, abduction, external rotation
  • Movement assessment - often reduced internal rotation
  • Strength and control testing

Imaging

  • X-ray - shows cam and pincer morphology, alpha angle
  • MRI - shows labrum, cartilage, and bone marrow
  • CT - detailed 3D image of bone shape

Alpha Angle

On X-ray, the alpha angle is measured to assess cam morphology. Over 55-60 degrees is considered elevated. But remember: An elevated angle alone does not mean you need treatment - only if you have symptoms.

5

Treatment

Conservative Treatment (First Choice)

The important UK FASHIoN study (2018) compared surgery with physical therapy for FAI. After 12 months, the difference was minimal - both groups improved.

Conservative Treatment Includes:

  • Activity modification - avoid provocative movements temporarily
  • Strength training - hip muscles, core, pelvis
  • Mobility training - within pain-free ranges
  • Motor control - movement quality
  • Gradual return to activity

Chiropractic Treatment

As a chiropractor, I can help with:

  • Thorough hip examination
  • Mobilization of hip, pelvis, and lower back
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight hip muscles
  • Individually tailored strength program
  • Activity adaptation

Exercises to Avoid/Modify

  • Deep squats - limit depth
  • Wide lunges - keep narrower
  • Hip flexor stretches - avoid extreme positions
  • Kickbacks in end-range

Surgery (Hip Arthroscopy)

Surgery is considered when:

  • 3-6 months of conservative treatment does not provide sufficient improvement
  • Significant cam/pincer morphology
  • Labral tear requiring repair
  • High activity demands (elite sports)

Important on Surgery

  • Even after successful surgery, rehabilitation takes 6-12 months
  • Results are best in patients without osteoarthritis
  • Training and rehabilitation are equally important after surgery
6

Prognosis

With Conservative Treatment:

  • 60-70% achieve satisfactory function
  • Many return to full activity (with modifications)
  • Rehabilitation typically takes 3-6 months

With Surgery:

  • 80-90% report improvement
  • Return to sport: 6-12 months
  • Best results in patients under 40 without osteoarthritis

Long-term Perspective

It is still unclear whether FAI treatment prevents osteoarthritis in the long term. Studies are ongoing. The most important thing is to maintain good hip function and muscle strength.

FAI and Osteoarthritis

FAI is a risk factor for hip osteoarthritis, but most people with FAI do not develop osteoarthritis. Good muscle strength and control can reduce load on the joint and protect the cartilage.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between cam and pincer?
Cam FAI is caused by a bump on the femoral head impinging against the socket. Pincer FAI is caused by the socket extending too far out. Cam is most common in men, pincer in women. most have a combination (mixed type).
Does FAI need surgery?
No, most people with FAI do not need surgery. The UK FASHIoN study showed that physical therapy was almost as effective as surgery after 12 months. Surgery is considered only after 3-6 months without sufficient improvement from conservative treatment.
Can I continue to exercise?
Yes, but with modifications. Avoid exercises that cause pain, especially deep squats and extreme hip positions. Many activities can continue with modifications. Hip strength training is actually important for improvement.
Will FAI lead to osteoarthritis?
FAI is a risk factor for hip osteoarthritis, but most do not develop osteoarthritis. Good muscle strength and control can reduce risk. It is unclear if treatment prevents osteoarthritis long-term.
Why did I get FAI?
FAI morphology typically develops in adolescence during bone growth. Genetics play a role, and intensive sports in adolescence (especially soccer, hockey, dance) can affect hip shape. It's not something you "did wrong".
