GTPS - Pain on the Outside of the Hip

Gluteal tendinopathy: Causes, symptoms and treatment

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Tendon problems in the gluteal muscles (gluteus medius and minimus) at the greater trochanter
  • Prevalence: 10-25% of the population, most common in women aged 40-60
  • Main symptom: Pain on the outside of the hip, worst at night and when lying on the side
  • Treatment: Progressive strength training is most effective - 79% better after 1 year (LEAP study)
  • Duration: Typically 6-9 months with proper treatment
1

What is GTPS?

GTPS (Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome) is an umbrella term for pain on the outside of the hip around the greater trochanter - the bony prominence you can feel on the outside of your hip. It is one of the most common forms of hip pain.

Previously, this condition was often called "trochanteric bursitis", but research has shown that the pain is rarely caused by inflammation in the bursae. In 90% of cases, the pain is caused by tendinopathy (tendon problems) in the gluteus medius and/or gluteus minimus tendons.

10-25% of population affected
4:1 women vs men
40-60 years - most common age
79% better with exercise

Why does it affect women more often?

Women have wider pelvises, which creates a larger angle in the hip joint. This increases compression of the gluteal tendons against the greater trochanter, especially during activities like walking, standing on one leg, or lying on the side.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Compression is the Main Problem

The gluteal tendons become compressed against the greater trochanter in certain positions and movements:

  • Side-lying - especially on the affected side
  • Standing with hip pushed out to the side ("hanging on one hip")
  • Crossing legs when sitting
  • Walking up stairs or uphill
  • Sudden increase in activity - e.g., lots of walking on vacation

Risk Factors

  • Female sex - 4 times higher risk
  • Age 40-60 years - hormones and natural aging
  • Overweight - increases the load
  • Weak hip muscles - lack of stability
  • Runners - especially with rapid increase in training volume
  • Low back pain - often concurrent problems

Avoid These Habits

  • Lying on the painful side without a pillow between the knees
  • Standing with all weight on one leg ("sexy stand")
  • Sitting with legs crossed
  • Aggressive stretching of the hip
3

Symptoms

Main Symptoms

  • Local pain on the outside of the hip - over the greater trochanter
  • Night pain - especially when lying on the affected side
  • Pain when walking - especially on stairs and uphill
  • Tenderness to pressure over the trochanter
  • Morning stiffness that eases after movement

Pain worsens with:

  • Walking long distances
  • Standing on one leg for a long time
  • Sitting in low chairs
  • Walking up stairs
  • Lying on the side

Characteristics of GTPS vs other hip conditions

With GTPS, the pain is typically located on the outside of the hip. Pain in the groin more often indicates problems inside the hip joint (osteoarthritis, labrum tear, FAI). Pain in the buttock region may be sciatica or piriformis syndrome.

4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is primarily made clinically through history and physical examination.

Clinical Tests

  • Palpation of the greater trochanter - tenderness over the bony prominence
  • FABER test - reproduces lateral hip pain
  • Single leg stance - pain after 30 seconds
  • Resisted hip abduction - pain with resistance

Imaging

MRI can show tendon degeneration, partial tears, or bursitis, but is rarely necessary to make the diagnosis. Clinical examination is often sufficient.

X-ray can rule out osteoarthritis and other bone changes.

5

Treatment

First-line Treatment: Exercise

The most important study on GTPS treatment is the LEAP study (Mellor et al., BMJ 2018), which compared exercise, cortisone, and "wait and see".

Results after 1 year (LEAP study)

  • Exercise: 79% reported global improvement
  • Cortisone: 58% reported global improvement
  • Wait and see: 52% reported global improvement

Exercise was clearly superior - and the effect lasted!

Exercise Principles

  • Isometric exercises first - holding the position without movement
  • Gradual progression - from isometric to isotonic to functional
  • Focus on gluteus medius and minimus
  • Avoid compression positions during training
  • Train 3-4 times per week for at least 12 weeks

Chiropractic Treatment

I often combine:

  • Soft tissue treatment of gluteal muscles and ITB
  • Manual therapy of hip and pelvis
  • Individually tailored exercise program
  • Advice on load management

What About Cortisone?

Cortisone provides rapid pain relief in the first weeks, but the LEAP study showed that the effect does not last. After 1 year, the cortisone group was no better than the "wait and see" group. Additionally, repeated injections can damage tendon tissue.

When to consider cortisone?

Cortisone may be appropriate as a "breathing space" to get started with exercise if the pain is so severe that exercise is not possible. But it should never be the only treatment.

6

Prognosis

GTPS is a condition that takes time to treat - typically 6-9 months for full recovery. But the prognosis is good if you are patient and follow an exercise program.

What can you expect?

  • Weeks 1-4: Pain management and load reduction. Many experience it getting worse before it gets better.
  • Weeks 4-12: Gradual increase in exercise. Pain begins to subside.
  • 3-6 months: Continued progression. Most notice clear improvement.
  • 6-12 months: Full or near-full function. Maintenance exercise recommended.

Red flags - seek medical attention if:

  • Sudden severe pain after a fall or trauma
  • Night pain that does not improve with position changes
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fever or general feeling of illness
7

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between GTPS and trochanteric bursitis?
GTPS is an umbrella term that includes gluteal tendinopathy, bursitis, and muscle dysfunction. Research shows that most of the pain is caused by tendon problems, not bursitis. Treatment is similar regardless.
How long does it take to recover?
GTPS often takes 6-9 months to treat effectively. The LEAP study showed that 79% were better after 1 year with proper exercise treatment. Getting worse before better is not unusual.
Should I avoid lying on the painful side?
Yes, in the acute phase. Side-lying compresses the tendons against the trochanter. Use a pillow between your knees or lie on the healthy side. Gradual acclimatization as pain subsides.
Does stretching help?
Be careful with stretching. Many stretching exercises compress the tendons and can make things worse. Focus on strength training instead.
Can I continue to exercise?
Yes, but you need to adjust your training. Avoid exercises that cause a lot of pain, especially in end-range positions. Activities like cycling and swimming are often well tolerated.
