Hip Osteoarthritis

Symptoms, exercise and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Summary

  • What it is: Wear of the hip joint cartilage (coxarthrosis)
  • Prevalence: 5.5% of the population, increases with age
  • Main symptoms: Groin pain, stiffness, reduced mobility
  • Treatment: Exercise is the most important - 88% avoid surgery with proper treatment
  • Prognosis: Variable - many live well with osteoarthritis for years
1

What is Hip Osteoarthritis?

Hip osteoarthritis (coxarthrosis) is a condition where the cartilage in the hip joint gradually wears down. This leads to pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility. It is one of the most common causes of hip pain in adults over 50.

The cartilage acts as a shock absorber between the femoral head and the hip socket. When the cartilage thins, the bones can rub against each other, causing pain and inflammation.

Symptoms

Main Symptoms

  • Groin pain - the most common location
  • Stiffness - especially in the morning and after rest
  • Reduced mobility - difficulty with shoes and socks
  • Pain with activity - walking, stairs, getting up
  • Pain at night - in advanced cases

Pain worsens with:

  • Long walks
  • Stairs - especially downward
  • Getting up from low chairs
  • Long car rides
  • Rotation movements

Where does it hurt?

Hip osteoarthritis typically causes pain in the groin - not on the outside of the hip. Pain on the outside may indicate GTPS/gluteal tendinopathy instead.

Risk Factors

  • Age - risk increases after 50
  • Overweight - extra load on the joint
  • Previous injury - hip fracture, labral tear
  • FAI (femoroacetabular impingement) - bone abnormality
  • Family history - genetic factors
  • Heavy physical work - repeated loading
4

Treatment

Exercise is the Most Important Treatment

Research shows that exercise is as effective as pain medication for hip osteoarthritis - but without side effects. The GLA:D program has shown that 88% avoid surgery with proper exercise treatment.

Recommended exercises:

  • Strength training - hip and thigh muscles
  • Flexibility exercises - maintain mobility
  • Low-impact cardio - cycling, swimming, walking
  • Balance training - fall prevention

Chiropractic Treatment

  • Manual therapy - joint mobilization
  • Soft tissue treatment - tight muscles around the hip
  • Exercise prescription - individually tailored program
  • Advice - activity modification, weight management

Other treatments

  • Weight loss - every kg less reduces joint load
  • Pain medication - as needed (NSAIDs)
  • Cortisone injection - temporary relief
  • Hip replacement - when conservative treatment fails
5

Frequently Asked Questions

How long before exercise helps?
Most notice improvement after 4-6 weeks of regular exercise. Full effect typically comes after 8-12 weeks. The effect lasts as long as you continue exercising.
Can osteoarthritis get better?
The cartilage cannot grow back, but symptoms can definitely improve. Many experience significant pain relief and improved function with proper exercise and treatment. 88% of those with early hip osteoarthritis avoid surgery for at least 10 years.
When do I need a hip replacement?
Hip replacement is considered when pain and functional loss are significant despite 3-6 months of active conservative treatment. In Norway, about 10,000 hip replacements are performed annually, with 95.6% survival after 10 years.
Can I continue jogging?
It depends on how the hip reacts. If jogging does not cause increased pain the next day, you can continue. Cycling, swimming or brisk walking may be gentler alternatives.

Red flags - seek medical attention if:

  • Sudden severe pain after a fall
  • Unable to bear weight on the leg
  • Significant swelling or redness
  • Fever along with hip pain
  • Night pain that does not improve with position changes
