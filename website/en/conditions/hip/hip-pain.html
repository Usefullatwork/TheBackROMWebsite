Hip Pain?

Your complete guide to causes, symptoms and treatment

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

TL;DR - Summary

  • ✓ Hip pain affects 10% of adults - most get better without surgery
  • ✓ Most common causes: Tendinopathy (GTPS), osteoarthritis, FAI - all respond well to conservative treatment
  • ✓ MRI findings often lie: 69% of pain-free people have labral tears, 37% have FAI morphology
  • ✓ Success rates: GTPS 78% with exercise, FAI up to 82% avoid surgery
  • ⚠ Red flags: Fever, night pain, weight loss, unable to bear weight after a fall

Hip pain is more common than many think. About 10% of adults experience it (Picavet 2003), and among those over 60, 14-19% report persistent problems. Women are affected more often than men, especially after age 40.

But here's the important thing: the vast majority have problems coming from muscles, tendons or joints - not from serious injury or disease. And even more importantly: most get better without surgery. The LEAP study from 2018 showed, for example, that 78% of patients with tendon pain on the outside of the hip improved with exercise and education - better than with cortisone injections.

The problem is that hip pain can be confusing. Groin pain can come from the hip joint, but also from the groin muscles or an irritated nerve. Pain on the outside of the hip is often misdiagnosed as "bursitis." And pain in the buttock? That can come from the hip, back and pelvis.

This guide helps you sort it out. I start with the most important question:

1

Where does it hurt? Find your pain type

Instead of listing diagnoses, I start with what you actually feel: where is the pain? This often gives the best indication of what's wrong.

Pain on the OUTSIDE of the hip

Very common

Typical signs: Pain over the large bony prominence on the outside of the hip. Hurts to lie on that side at night. Gets worse with stairs, longer walks or getting up from a chair. Can radiate down the outside of the thigh toward the knee.

Myth: "It's bursitis"

No, it almost never is. Studies show that less than 5% actually have inflammation in the bursa. In 85-98%, the problem is in the tendons of the gluteal muscles - gluteus medius and minimus. This is called gluteal tendinopathy or GTPS, and requires completely different treatment than cortisone.

Who is affected? GTPS occurs in 15% of women and 6-8% of men between 50-79 years. Women are affected over 3 times as often. The condition accounts for 10-20% of all hip pain at the GP.

Prognosis: The LEAP study showed that exercise + education gave 78.6% success rate after one year - better than cortisone (58%) and "wait and see" (52%). Improvement takes time (6-9 months), but most get better without injections.

Pain in the GROIN (front of hip)

Complex

Typical signs: Deep pain at the front of the hip or groin. Worst when bending the hip far (putting on shoes, sitting for long periods, climbing stairs). May feel stiff in the morning. Some experience clicking or catching.

Groin pain can have several causes - and age and activity level provide important clues:

  • Hip osteoarthritis: Most common in those over 55-60 years. 10-15% of people over 65 have symptomatic hip osteoarthritis. Causes gradually increasing stiffness, especially with internal rotation. Exercise is the first choice.
  • FAI (femoroacetabular impingement): Pinching in the hip joint due to bone shape. Common in young, active people. Causes pain with deep flexion + internal rotation. 37% of pain-free people have FAI morphology on MRI - so images must be interpreted carefully.
  • Labral tear: Damage to the cartilage ring around the hip. Often causes clicking, catching or feeling that the hip "pops out." 69% of pain-free adults have labral tears on MRI - so not every tear needs treatment.
  • Iliopsoas tendinopathy: Pain when lifting the leg, often with "snapping" at the front of the hip. Common in runners, dancers and soccer players.

Pain in the BUTTOCK or back

Difficult to sort out

Typical signs: Pain in the buttock, lower back or behind the hip. Can radiate down the thigh. Often worse when sitting, standing on one leg or turning in bed.

Buttock pain can come from the hip, back OR pelvis. Here are some clues:

  • SI joint ("pelvic loosening"): If you can point with one finger to where it hurts - right at the "dimple" at the top of the buttock - it's often the SI joint. SI joint problems account for 15-30% of all chronic low back pain.
  • Piriformis syndrome: Deep buttock pain that can radiate down the back of the thigh. Mimics sciatica, but comes from a tight muscle - not the back.
  • Sit bone (proximal hamstring): Painful to sit, especially on hard chairs? Pain where "buttock meets thigh"? This could be tendinopathy at the hamstring attachment. Important: don't stretch the hamstrings - it makes it worse.
  • Referred pain from the back: Problems in the low back can cause pain that feels like it's in the buttock or hip. Studies show that 71% of patients with hip problems actually have buttock pain as their dominant symptom.

Numbness, tingling or burning pain

Nerve involvement

Typical signs: Burning, tingling or numb feeling. Can be on the outside of the thigh, or radiate from back/buttock down the leg.

  • Meralgia paresthetica: Compression of a nerve causing numbness or burning on the outside of the thigh. Common with overweight, pregnancy or tight pants/belts. 85% get better without surgery.
  • Sciatica/lumbar radiculopathy: Radiation from back/buttock down the back or outside of the leg - often past the knee to the foot. Usually caused by an irritated nerve root in the back.
2

Do-it-yourself tests: Find out more before you come

Before booking an appointment, you can try these simple tests at home. They often give a good indication of what's wrong - and help me get to the bottom of the problem faster.

1 Sock test (checks the hip joint)

How to do it: Sit in a chair. Try to put on socks on the painful side without bending your back much - by pulling your foot up toward you and rotating the hip outward.

Result: Is it difficult or does it cause deep groin pain? Studies show this is much more common with hip osteoarthritis than with tendon problems on the outside. It suggests stiffness in the hip joint itself.

2 Single-leg test (checks gluteal tendons)

How to do it: Stand on the painful leg for 30 seconds. Hold lightly against the wall for balance if necessary.

Result: If this reproduces the familiar pain on the outside of the hip, it strongly suggests GTPS (gluteal tendinopathy). A recent meta-analysis showed this test has 94% sensitivity and 99% specificity for tendon problems on the outside of the hip.

3 Pointing test (checks SI joint vs. hip)

How to do it: Where is the pain? Try to point to the most painful spot with one finger.

Result: Are you pointing right at the "dimple" or hollow at the low back? Then the pain probably comes from the SI joint (pelvic joint), not the hip. If you point to the groin (often shaped like a C with your hand), it's more often the hip joint itself.

3

Hip, back or pelvis? How to tell them apart

Many who come to me think they have "hip problems" - but the pain actually comes from the back or pelvis. And vice versa. Here are the rules of thumb:

Pain Source Typical Location Key Distinctive Features
True Hip Pain Groin (front) or "C-shape" above hip bone. Stiffness with internal rotation (putting on socks). Pain with deep flexion. Rarely goes below the knee.
Back (Sciatica) Buttock or back of thigh. Radiates past the knee to foot/toes. Electrical/shooting pain. Often worse with bending forward.
Pelvis (SI Joint) "Dimple" in low back (PSIS). Pinpoint pain at the belt line. Sharp pain when turning in bed or standing on one leg.
Bursitis/GTPS Outside of hip (Lateral). Tender to touch on the bone. Pain lying on that side. Not caused by the hip joint itself.

4

The MRI trap: Why the picture doesn't always match

"MRI shows I have a tear - do I need surgery?"

Probably not. This is one of the most important things you can learn about hip pain.

MRI is fantastic technology. But it shows everything - including things that are completely normal age-related changes. The problem is that many of these "findings" are found just as often in people without pain.

69% of pain-free people have labral tears on MRI
37% have FAI morphology without symptoms
44-57% have cartilage or labral defects
50%+ of 60-year-olds have "degenerative changes"

The point? A "tear" or "wear" on MRI doesn't necessarily mean that's the source of your pain. Images must be interpreted in the context of symptoms and examination findings. I treat people, not pictures.

5

Acute self-help: What you can do now

While waiting for treatment, it's important not to unnecessarily irritate the tissue. Here are the "fire-fighting measures" I recommend:

How you should sleep

Night pain is very common with hip pain, especially with tendon problems on the outside (GTPS). When you lie on your side, the tendons are compressed against the bone - like squeezing a tendon between two hard surfaces.

  • Avoid lying on the painful side: This increases compression on the tendons.
  • Use a pillow between your knees: If you lie on the healthy side, a thick pillow from knee to ankle keeps the hip neutral.
  • Lie on your back: Often the best position. Possibly with a pillow under your knees.

How you should sit and stand

  • Don't sit with crossed legs: This stretches the tendons on the outside of the hip.
  • Avoid "hanging" on the hip: When standing, distribute weight equally on both legs.
  • For groin pain: Lower the chair slightly or tilt the seat forward to open the hip angle.
6

Treatment: Conservative vs. surgery

Many think that "something must be done" - preferably quickly, preferably an injection or surgery. But research tells a different story: for most hip and pelvic pain, exercise and manual treatment are as good as or better than invasive measures.

Success rates for conservative treatment

78.6% GTPS success with exercise after 1 year
82% FAI avoid surgery
63% Snapping hip improvement without surgery
85% Meralgia paresthetica without surgery

What about FAI surgery?

The UK FASHIoN study compared hip surgery with physiotherapy in 348 patients. Both groups improved significantly. The physiotherapy group went from 35.6 to 49.7 points, the surgery group from 39.2 to 58.8 points. Conservative treatment should be tried first for most people.

What about cortisone?

Cortisone injections can give short-term pain relief, but long-term effects are poorly documented. The LEAP study showed that the cortisone group after 1 year was no better than the "wait and see" group. Systematic reviews have also shown potential negative effects on tendon tissue.

7

Red flags: When you need to seek help quickly

Contact emergency services IMMEDIATELY for:

  • Cannot bear weight on the leg after a fall or injury
  • Fever together with hip or back pain
  • Significant swelling, redness and warmth over the hip
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Sudden, severe weakness or numbness in the leg

Contact a doctor or chiropractor within a few days for:

  • Persistent night pain that wakes you repeatedly
  • Morning stiffness lasting over 60 minutes (may indicate inflammatory disease)
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with persistent pain
  • Runners with gradually increasing groin pain and night aching - could be femoral neck stress fracture
8

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it dangerous when my hip "clicks" or "pops"?
Usually not. "Snapping hip" is very common - affecting 5-10% of the population - and is rarely dangerous as long as it doesn't hurt. The sound is usually caused by a tendon sliding over a bony prominence. Among ballet dancers, the incidence is up to 90%. It only becomes concerning if the clicking is accompanied by pain, instability, or a feeling that the hip "gives way."
Does stretching help?
Be careful. With tendon problems on the outside of the hip (GTPS) or high on the back of the thigh (proximal hamstring), aggressive stretching can actually make things worse. Stretching compresses the tendon against the bone. Focus instead on strength training and mobility within pain-free limits.
Does cortisone help?
Short-term yes, long-term doubtful. For GTPS, the LEAP study showed that cortisone gave rapid improvement in the first weeks, but after 1 year the results were no better than doing nothing. Systematic reviews have also shown potential negative effects on tendon tissue over time. I usually recommend trying exercise first.
Can I exercise with hip pain?
Yes, and you should! Research shows that exercise is more effective than passive treatment for most hip and pelvic pain. But you need to train smart: avoid activities that cause severe pain, and start with exercises that don't stress the joint in extreme positions. A chiropractor or physical therapist can guide you to the right exercises.
Do I need MRI or X-ray?
Not always - and often not initially. A thorough clinical examination often provides enough information to make a diagnosis and start treatment. Imaging is relevant if symptoms don't improve as expected. Remember: MRI often shows "findings" that don't explain your pain.
How long until I get better?
This varies greatly. Acute muscle problems can improve in 2-4 weeks. GTPS (tendon problems on the outside) often takes 6-9 months with proper treatment. Hip osteoarthritis requires a long-term approach, but most people notice good effects from exercise within 8-12 weeks. Relapses are common and don't mean treatment has failed - it means your body needs maintenance.
