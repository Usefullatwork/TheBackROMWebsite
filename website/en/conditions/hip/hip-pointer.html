Hip Pointer (Iliac Crest Bruise)

Symptoms, treatment and recovery time | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Bruise/hematoma on the iliac crest after direct impact
  • Common causes: Direct trauma (>95%) - falls, tackles, collisions in contact sports
  • Treatment: PRICE protocol in acute phase, then gradual rehabilitation
  • Healing: Mild 1-2 weeks, moderate 2-4 weeks, severe 4-8 weeks
  • Red flags: Abdominal pain, thigh numbness, persistent swelling >2 weeks
1

What is a Hip Pointer?

A hip pointer is an injury where you get a bruise or blood collection (hematoma) on the iliac crest - the curved bony ridge you can feel on the side of your hip. The injury occurs when the iliac crest receives a direct blow, typically from a fall, tackle, or collision with an opponent.

Prevalence

Hip injuries account for 5-10% of all sports injuries, and hip pointer is a subset of these. In NFL data from 1997-2006, hip pointer accounted for 0.3% of all injuries and led to an average of 5.6 days away from training.

The injury is most common in contact sports such as football, ice hockey, rugby, and handball.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Over 95% of all hip pointer injuries are caused by direct trauma to the iliac crest. The most common causes ranked by frequency:

  1. Direct impact in contact sports - tackles in football, checking in ice hockey, collisions in handball or rugby
  2. Falls on the hip - cycling, skating, skiing, or general accidents
  3. Traffic accidents - rarer, but can cause more severe injuries

Important for Adolescents

In teenagers, the growth plate on the iliac crest (apophysis) is not fully developed until the 20s. This means what looks like a regular hip pointer in an adolescent could actually be an avulsion fracture where bone pulls away from the growth plate. Average age for such pelvic avulsion fractures is only 14.4 years.

3

Symptoms

Symptoms appear immediately after the impact and vary in intensity depending on severity:

  • Local pain - directly over the iliac crest, often sharp and intense
  • Tenderness to touch - point tenderness exactly where the impact occurred
  • Bruising - typically develops over hours to days
  • Swelling - locally around the injury site
  • Reduced mobility - pain with bending, extending, or rotating the hip
  • Limping - many alter their gait pattern to compensate
  • Pain with coughing/sneezing - because nerves in the area are irritated

Severity Grades

Grade Clinical Findings Expected Healing
Mild (Grade 1) Minimal swelling, normal walking, >2/3 range of motion 1-2 weeks
Moderate (Grade 2) Visible bruising, limping, 1/3-2/3 range of motion 2-4 weeks
Severe (Grade 3) Large hematoma, difficulty weight-bearing, <1 /3 range of motion 4-8 weeks
4

Treatment

What Can a Chiropractor Do?

The chiropractor can play a role in the subacute phase and rehabilitation:

  • Thorough examination - assess severity and rule out fractures/other diagnoses
  • Referral for imaging - chiropractors can refer for X-ray and MRI when needed
  • Soft tissue treatment - after the acute phase (>48-72 hours) to release tension in surrounding muscles
  • Joint mobilization - gentle restoration of hip mobility
  • Rehabilitation guidance - exercises for strength and mobility tailored to the healing process

Important: Manual treatment is contraindicated in the acute phase (first 48-72 hours). Aggressive massage or deep tissue work too early can increase the risk of complications like myositis ossificans.

Self-Care

PRICE protocol (first 48-72 hours):

  • Protection - avoid activities that worsen pain
  • Rest - relative rest, not necessarily complete immobilization
  • Ice - 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours, never directly on skin
  • Compression - elastic bandage if possible
  • Elevation - lie with hip elevated when possible

"NO HARM" rule for the first days: No heat, no alcohol, no running, no massage.

5

Prognosis

The prognosis for hip pointer is generally very good. Surgery is almost never necessary for isolated injuries. Most return to full activity within expected timeframes based on severity:

  • Mild: 1-2 weeks
  • Moderate: 2-4 weeks
  • Severe: 4-8 weeks, with full soft tissue healing over 8-12 weeks
6

Red Flags - When to Seek Medical Attention

Seek emergency care or a doctor immediately if:

  • Abdominal pain, nausea, or vomiting - may indicate internal organ injury (spleen, kidney, intestine)
  • Numbness or burning sensation in the thigh - may indicate nerve compression
  • Extreme swelling that increases - possible compartment syndrome
  • Pain out of proportion - pain much worse than expected
  • Adolescent with point tenderness over the iliac crest - must rule out avulsion fracture with X-ray
  • Symptoms that persist >2 weeks without improvement - may indicate complication or other diagnosis
7

Frequently Asked Questions

How long before I can play again?
Mild injuries: 1-2 weeks. Moderate to severe: 3-6 weeks. It is important to meet return-to-sport criteria (pain-free, full range of motion, strength >90%) before returning to contact training.
Can I exercise with a hip pointer?
Avoid contact activities until pain is gone. Low-impact activities like cycling and swimming can often continue as long as they do not provoke pain.
Do I need an X-ray?
Not necessarily for adults with typical injury mechanism and uncomplicated course. All adolescents should get X-rays to rule out avulsion fractures. Adults should get X-rays if symptoms persist >2 weeks or if fracture is suspected.
Is hip pointer the same as a hip fracture?
No. Hip pointer is a bruise in soft tissue and periosteum, while hip fracture involves the bone itself. Hip fractures are much more serious and often require surgery.
