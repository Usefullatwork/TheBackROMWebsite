Iliopsoas Tendinopathy

Hip flexor pain and snapping hip syndrome

Written by Mads Finstad, Chiropractor

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Tendon problem in the iliopsoas muscle (hip flexor) that attaches to the front of the hip
  • Main symptom: Deep pain in the front of the hip/groin, often with audible "snapping"
  • Who is affected: Runners, dancers, football players - especially with rapid training increases
  • Treatment: Strength training and load management - most recover with conservative treatment
  • Duration: Typically 6-12 weeks with proper treatment
1

What is iliopsoas?

Iliopsoas is the body's most powerful hip flexor. It actually consists of two muscles that merge together:

  • Psoas major - originates from the lumbar spine (L1-L5)
  • Iliacus - originates from the inside of the iliac crest

These two muscles run together and attach to trochanter minor - a bony projection on the inside of the femur. Every time you lift your leg, climb stairs or run - iliopsoas is working.

Iliopsoas tendinopathy occurs when the tendon becomes overloaded and irritated. This causes pain in the front of the hip and can lead to the characteristic "snapping hip". The condition is one of the most common causes of hip pain in athletes.

5-10% of the population have snapping hip
90% of dancers have snapping
12-36% of runners with groin pain
6-12 weeks typical recovery

Snapping hip syndrome

Snapping hip is when you hear or feel a "click" in the hip during movement. Internal snapping (from iliopsoas) occurs when the tendon glides over the hip joint or a bony edge. It is usually harmless but can become painful with overuse. External snapping occurs on the outside of the hip and is related to GTPS/gluteal tendinopathy.

2

Causes and risk factors

Overload is the main cause

Iliopsoas tendinopathy almost always occurs due to too much, too fast:

  • Sudden increase in training volume - more kilometers, harder intervals
  • Repetitive hip flexion - running, cycling, dancing
  • Too little recovery between sessions
  • One-sided training - lack of strength training

Who is affected?

  • Runners - especially middle and long distance
  • Dancers - ballet, modern dance, hip-hop
  • Football players - kicking and high kicks
  • Gymnasts and martial artists
  • Cyclists - especially with low seat position

Biomechanical factors

  • Tight iliopsoas from prolonged sitting
  • Weak hip extensors (gluteus) - can also lead to GTPS
  • Poor pelvic control during activity
  • Altered running technique - too long stride length
3

Symptoms

Typical symptoms

  • Deep pain in the front of the hip - often described as "inside the groin"
  • Audible or palpable snapping during hip flexion
  • Pain when lifting the leg - climbing stairs, running
  • Stiffness after rest - especially in the morning
  • Pain that increases during activity

Pain worsens with:

  • Running or sprinting
  • Climbing stairs - especially going up
  • Kicking a ball
  • Getting up from sitting
  • Lifting the knee toward the chest
  • Prolonged sitting

Snapping - when does it occur?

The characteristic "snapping" is usually heard when you:

  • Extend the hip from a flexed position (standing up, walking)
  • Rotate the hip during movement
  • Make circular movements with the leg

When is snapping a problem?

  • When it causes pain - not just sound
  • When it limits activity
  • When it gets worse over time
  • When it affects athletic performance
4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is primarily made through medical history and clinical examination.

Clinical tests

  • Active hip flexion against resistance - reproduces the pain
  • Thomas test - assesses tightness in iliopsoas
  • Snapping provocation - flexion-extension of the hip
  • Palpation - tenderness at the tendon attachment

Differential diagnoses

Groin pain can have many causes. Important to rule out:

  • FAI/labral tear - often overlapping symptoms
  • Inguinal hernia - especially with coughing/sneezing
  • Adductor problems - pain more medial
  • Stress fracture - with significant weight loss or overtraining

Imaging

Ultrasound can show tendon thickening and fluid around the tendon. MRI provides a more detailed image and can rule out other conditions. Imaging is rarely necessary for diagnosis but useful for persistent problems.

5

Treatment

Phase 1: Pain management (weeks 1-2)

  • Reduce provocative activity - don't stop completely, but adjust
  • Avoid extreme hip flexion - deep squats, high kicks
  • Isometric exercises - holding the position without movement
  • Manual therapy - soft tissue treatment of surrounding muscles

Phase 2: Strength building (weeks 2-8)

  • Progressive strength training for iliopsoas
  • Gluteus strengthening - balances hip musculature
  • Core stability - pelvic and back control
  • Gradual load increase

Phase 3: Return to activity (weeks 6-12)

  • Gradual progression of sport-specific training
  • Running progression - start with intervals
  • Technique focus - avoid overstriding
  • Maintenance program

Chiropractic treatment

  • Soft tissue treatment of iliopsoas and surrounding muscles
  • Mobilization of hip and pelvis
  • Individually tailored exercise program
  • Load management and activity modification

Avoid these mistakes

  • Too aggressive stretching - can irritate the tendon
  • Too rapid return to full activity
  • Only passive treatment - exercise is necessary
  • Ignoring pain signals - "train through the pain"
6

Prognosis

The prognosis for iliopsoas tendinopathy is generally good with proper treatment.

What can you expect?

  • Weeks 1-2: Pain reduction with load management
  • Weeks 2-6: Gradual increase in strength and function
  • Weeks 6-12: Return to full activity
  • Most people: Full recovery within 3 months

Factors affecting prognosis

  • Duration before treatment - early treatment gives faster recovery
  • Adherence to exercise program
  • Willingness to modify activity in the initial phase
  • Underlying factors (FAI, pelvic control)

When is surgery considered?

Surgery is rarely necessary. It is only considered after 6-12 months of conservative treatment without sufficient improvement. The surgery usually involves lengthening or releasing the tendon.

7

Frequently asked questions

Is snapping hip dangerous?
No, snapping hip is usually not dangerous. Up to 10% of the population have it without pain. It only becomes a problem when it is painful or limits activity. Most cases respond well to conservative treatment.
Should I stretch iliopsoas?
Be careful with stretching during active tendinopathy - it can irritate the tendon. Focus on strength training first. When the pain has settled, gentle mobility training can be included.
Can I continue running?
Often yes, but with adjustments. Reduce the volume, avoid hills and intervals initially. Shorter stride length and higher cadence reduces the load on iliopsoas. Listen to your body - pain over 3/10 is too much.
Does massage help?
Soft tissue treatment of surrounding muscles can help, but should be combined with strength training. Direct, hard treatment of the tendon itself can be irritating in the acute phase.
How long does it take to recover?
Most people improve significantly within 6-12 weeks with proper treatment. Full return to sport often takes 3 months. Long-standing cases can take longer.
