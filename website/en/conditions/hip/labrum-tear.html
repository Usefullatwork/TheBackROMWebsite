Hip Labral Tear

Hip Labrum: Causes, symptoms and treatment

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Damage to the cartilage rim (labrum) surrounding the hip socket
  • Main symptom: Deep groin pain, clicking/catching and pain with rotation
  • Cause: Often FAI, sports injuries or overuse
  • Treatment: 50-80% recover with conservative treatment (exercise)
  • Prognosis: Good with proper treatment - surgery is rarely the first choice
1

What is hip labrum?

The labrum is a cartilage ring that sits around the edge of the hip socket (acetabulum). It has several important functions:

  • Deepens the hip socket - increases stability by 22%
  • Seals the hip joint - keeps synovial fluid inside
  • Distributes pressure - protects cartilage surfaces
  • Supports sensory signals - important for balance and control

A labral tear occurs when this cartilage ring is torn, worn or lifted from the bone. This can cause hip pain, clicking and reduced function.

22-55% of hip pain cases
50-80% recover without surgery
20-40 years most common age
3-6 months rehabilitation

Important to know

Many people have labral tears on MRI without having symptoms. Studies show that up to 69% of asymptomatic individuals have labral changes. Findings on MRI do not automatically mean you need treatment.

2

Causes

FAI (Femoroacetabular impingement)

The most common cause of labral tear. With FAI (hip impingement), there is an abnormal shape of the femoral head (cam) or hip socket (pincer) that causes the labrum to be pinched during certain movements.

Other causes

  • Sports injuries - especially rotational sports like football, hockey, dance
  • Acute trauma - falls, collisions
  • Overuse - repetitive movements over time
  • Hypermobility - overly mobile joints that increase wear
  • Hip dysplasia - congenital shallow hip socket
  • Degeneration - natural aging

Risk factors

  • Sports with deep hip flexion - yoga, martial arts, hockey
  • Rotational sports - golf, tennis, football
  • Dancers and gymnasts - extreme mobility
  • FAI (cam/pincer) - structural abnormality
3

Symptoms

Typical symptoms

  • Deep groin pain - often described as "internal" pain
  • Clicking or catching in the hip during movement
  • Pain with rotation - especially internal rotation
  • C-sign - patient places hand like a C around the groin
  • Pain with prolonged sitting - especially in low chairs
  • Stiffness after rest

Pain worsens with:

  • Deep squatting
  • Sitting with crossed legs
  • Getting in and out of a car
  • Climbing stairs
  • Prolonged sitting
  • Pivoting movements

When should you seek help?

  • Persistent groin pain for more than 4-6 weeks
  • Clicking or locking that affects daily activities
  • Pain that wakes you at night
  • Significantly reduced hip movement
4

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of labral tear requires a combination of medical history, clinical tests and often imaging.

Clinical tests

  • FADIR test - flexion, adduction, internal rotation (high sensitivity)
  • FABER test - flexion, abduction, external rotation
  • Scour test - circular movement of the hip under compression
  • Range of motion assessment - especially rotation

Imaging

  • X-ray - shows bone shape (cam/pincer) and rules out osteoarthritis
  • MR arthrography - gold standard for labrum, requires contrast injection
  • Standard MRI - may miss small tears

Imaging is not always necessary

If you respond well to conservative treatment, MRI is often not needed. We treat symptoms and function - not images. MRI is most relevant when conservative treatment does not provide sufficient improvement.

5

Treatment

Conservative treatment (first choice)

Research shows that 50-80% of patients with labral tears recover sufficiently with conservative treatment.

Conservative treatment includes:

  • Activity modification - avoid provocative movements temporarily
  • Strength training - focus on hip musculature and core
  • Mobility training - within pain-free ranges
  • Manual therapy - soft tissue and joint mobilization
  • Gradual return to activity

Chiropractic treatment

As a chiropractor, I can contribute with:

  • Thorough examination - assess extent and causes
  • Manual therapy - mobilization of hip and pelvis
  • Soft tissue treatment - release tight musculature
  • Exercise program - individually tailored rehabilitation
  • Referral when needed - for MRI or orthopedic assessment

Surgery (arthroscopy)

Surgery is considered only when:

  • Conservative treatment over 3-6 months does not provide sufficient improvement
  • Mechanical symptoms (catching) dominate
  • Significant FAI that needs correction

Rehabilitation after surgery

Even after successful surgery, rehabilitation is crucial. Full return to sport typically takes 6-12 months. Without proper rehabilitation, the risk of poor outcome is higher.

6

Prognosis

The prognosis for labral tear is generally good, especially with early and proper treatment.

With conservative treatment:

  • 50-80% achieve satisfactory function
  • Many return to full activity
  • Rehabilitation typically takes 3-6 months

With surgery:

  • 80-90% report improvement
  • Full return to sport: 6-12 months
  • Best results in younger patients without osteoarthritis

Factors affecting prognosis:

  • Age - younger patients have better outcomes
  • Degree of cartilage damage - more intact cartilage means better prognosis
  • Presence of osteoarthritis - reduces success rate
  • Motivation for rehabilitation

When is prognosis poorer?

  • Significant osteoarthritis in the hip
  • Large cartilage damage
  • Long duration of symptoms before treatment
  • Age over 50 years
7

Frequently asked questions

Do labral tears always require surgery?
No, most labral tears can be treated without surgery. Research shows that 50-80% achieve good function with conservative treatment. Surgery is only considered when 3-6 months of exercise and treatment does not provide sufficient improvement.
Can the labrum heal on its own?
The labrum has limited blood supply and therefore heals poorly on its own. However, symptoms can often improve significantly with proper exercise. By strengthening the muscles around the hip, the load on the labrum is reduced, and many become nearly symptom-free.
Can I continue to exercise with a labral tear?
Yes, but you need to modify your training. Avoid exercises that cause pain, especially deep squatting and rotation under load. Many activities like cycling, swimming and strength training can be performed with modifications.
How do I know if I have a labral tear?
Typical signs are deep groin pain, clicking in the hip and pain with rotation. A chiropractor can perform specific tests. MRI with contrast (arthrography) can confirm the diagnosis, but is often not necessary if conservative treatment works.
Is a labral tear the same as hip osteoarthritis?
No, but they can be related. Labral tear affects the cartilage rim around the hip socket, while osteoarthritis is wear of the joint cartilage. Untreated labral tear can over time increase the risk of osteoarthritis, but many with labral tears never develop osteoarthritis.
