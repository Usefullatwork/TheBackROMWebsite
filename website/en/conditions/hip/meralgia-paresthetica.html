Meralgia Paresthetica

Burning pain and numbness on the outer thigh

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Compression of a sensory nerve causing symptoms on the outer thigh
  • Main symptom: Burning pain, numbness or tingling on the outer/front thigh
  • Common causes: Tight pants/belts, obesity, pregnancy, diabetes
  • Treatment: Remove triggering cause - 85% recover without surgery
  • Important: Causes ONLY sensory symptoms - no muscle weakness
1

What is meralgia paresthetica?

Meralgia paresthetica (MP) is a condition where the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve becomes compressed or irritated. This causes burning pain, numbness or tingling on the outer thigh. The condition is one of several causes of hip pain that can be treated conservatively.

The affected nerve:

  • Comes from spinal levels L2-L3
  • Runs through the pelvis and exits under the inguinal ligament
  • Is a purely sensory nerve - only causes sensory symptoms
  • Supplies the skin on the outer and front of the thigh

The nerve is particularly vulnerable to compression where it passes under the inguinal ligament near the hip bone.

4.3/10000 incidence per year
85% recover without surgery
7x increased risk with diabetes
30-40 years most common age

Why is it called meralgia paresthetica?

Meralgia comes from Greek "meros" (thigh) + "algos" (pain). Paresthetica means abnormal sensations. So: "painful sensory disturbances in the thigh".

2

Causes and risk factors

Common causes

  • Tight clothing - pants, belts, corsets, spandex
  • Obesity - fatty tissue puts pressure on the nerve
  • Pregnancy - growing abdomen and hormonal changes (see also pelvic pain in pregnancy)
  • Prolonged standing - increases stretch on the nerve
  • Diabetes - makes nerves more vulnerable
  • Surgery/trauma - in the pelvic or groin area

Mechanical causes

  • Tool belts - police equipment, carpenter belts
  • Car seats - seat belt pressure on long drives
  • Sports equipment - cycling shorts, climbing harnesses
  • Hip surgery - positioning during surgery

Medical causes

  • Diabetes mellitus - 7 times increased risk
  • Ascites - fluid in the abdomen
  • Pelvic tumor - rare, but must be ruled out
  • Scoliosis or pelvic asymmetry - can also cause SI joint problems

Note for diabetes

  • Diabetics have 7 times increased risk for meralgia paresthetica
  • Poor blood sugar control damages nerves in general
  • With diabetes and MP - check blood sugar control
3

Symptoms

Typical symptoms

  • Burning pain on the outer thigh
  • Numbness - reduced skin sensation
  • Tingling or prickling - "ants crawling"
  • Hyperesthesia - hypersensitivity, pain with light touch
  • Symptoms in a defined area - approximately 15x20 cm on the thigh

Important characteristics

  • Only sensory symptoms - never muscle weakness
  • One-sided - usually affects only one thigh (in 80%)
  • Worsens with standing/walking - improves with sitting
  • No back or groin pain

Symptoms worsen with:

  • Prolonged standing
  • Extended walking
  • Hip extension (stretching hip backward)
  • Tight pants or belt
  • Lying on stomach

Symptoms improve with:

  • Sitting - especially with bent hip
  • Loose clothing
  • Bending hip forward

How to distinguish from other conditions?

Meralgia paresthetica causes no weakness in the leg. If you have weakness in thigh or hip, the symptoms are likely from the spine or another nerve. Sciatica causes symptoms on the back of the leg, not the front. Pain on the outside of the hip can also be caused by GTPS/gluteal tendinopathy, while deep buttock pain may indicate piriformis syndrome.

4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is usually made based on medical history and clinical examination. Imaging is rarely necessary.

Clinical findings

  • Reduced sensation in a typical area on the outer thigh
  • Tenderness with pressure 2-3 cm below the anterior hip bone
  • Normal muscle strength in hip and thigh
  • Normal reflexes
  • Pelvic compression test - can relieve symptoms

Tinel's test

Light tapping over the nerve's passage (under the inguinal ligament) can trigger tingling or pain in the affected area. A positive test supports the diagnosis.

When is investigation needed?

  • With muscle weakness - suggests another condition
  • Bilateral - should be investigated for underlying cause
  • Young age without clear cause
  • Rapid worsening

Relevant investigations

  • Nerve conduction test (EMG) - can confirm the diagnosis
  • MRI pelvis - if tumor or other pathology is suspected
  • Blood tests - blood sugar, B12, thyroid if suspected
5

Treatment

Conservative treatment (first choice)

85% recover with simple measures targeting the triggering cause.

First measures:

  • Avoid tight pants and belts
  • Weight loss if overweight
  • Avoid prolonged standing
  • Remove heavy tool belts
  • Adjust car seat and seat belt

For persistent symptoms

  • Nerve mobilization - gentle exercises to "free" the nerve
  • Soft tissue treatment - around the inguinal ligament and hip flexors
  • Hip flexor stretching - reduces tension on the nerve
  • Medications - painkillers or neuropathic agents

During pregnancy

Meralgia paresthetica in pregnancy often resolves after delivery. Measures:

  • Avoid standing too long
  • Use support belt high (not on the hip bone)
  • Rest with slightly bent hips
  • Symptoms usually disappear after delivery

Injection treatment

For persistent symptoms, injection with local anesthetic and cortisone can provide relief. This can be both diagnostic and therapeutic.

Surgery

Considered only when conservative treatment over several months has not helped. Options:

  • Neurolysis - freeing the nerve from surrounding tissue
  • Neurectomy - cutting the nerve (results in permanent numbness)
6

Prognosis

The prognosis for meralgia paresthetica is very good.

What can you expect?

  • 85% recover with conservative treatment
  • Many cases resolve on their own when the cause is removed
  • Pregnancy: Symptoms usually disappear after delivery
  • Recovery can take weeks to months

Recurrence

  • Can happen if triggering cause returns
  • With repeated episodes - consider lifestyle changes
  • Diabetes increases risk of prolonged symptoms

Not dangerous

Meralgia paresthetica is uncomfortable, but not dangerous. It is a benign nerve compression that does not lead to permanent damage. Even without treatment, most will experience improvement over time.

7

Frequently asked questions

Is meralgia paresthetica dangerous?
No, meralgia paresthetica is not dangerous. It is a benign condition that only affects skin sensation - it never causes muscle weakness or permanent damage. Most people recover with simple measures.
Can meralgia paresthetica go away on its own?
Yes, many cases resolve on their own when the triggering cause is removed. For example, symptoms often improve with weight loss, looser clothing or after delivery. It can take weeks to months.
What is the difference between meralgia paresthetica and sciatica?
Meralgia paresthetica causes symptoms on the front/outer thigh and only sensory symptoms. Sciatica causes symptoms on the back of the leg, can go all the way to the foot, and can cause both pain and weakness. They are two completely different nerves.
Can I exercise with meralgia paresthetica?
Yes, most people can exercise. Avoid activities that involve a lot of hip extension (stretching backward) or prolonged standing. Cycling is often well tolerated because the hip is bent. Core and hip strengthening can be helpful.
Do I need surgery?
Rarely. 85% recover without surgery. Surgery is only considered after several months of conservative treatment without effect. Most people never need surgery.
