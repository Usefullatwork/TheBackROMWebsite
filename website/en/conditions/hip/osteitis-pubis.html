Osteitis Pubis - Chiropractor in Oslo

A complete guide to groin pain from the pubic symphysis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Osteitis pubis is a non-infectious inflammatory condition affecting the symphysis pubis - the cartilaginous joint connecting the two pubic bones at the front of the pelvis. Despite the name suggesting bone inflammation, it's not an infection. Rather, the condition represents a stress reaction where repetitive mechanical loading leads to inflammation and pain.

The prevalence varies significantly between different groups. Among athletes, prevalence is reported at 0.5-8%. A study of 189 athletes with groin pain found that osteitis pubis was the main cause in 14%. Among professional football players, 74.8% show radiological findings consistent with the condition, although many are asymptomatic.

Summary

  • What it is: Inflammation in the pubic symphysis, most often caused by overuse in active individuals
  • Most common causes: Sports with kicking/direction changes (football, ice hockey) > pregnancy/childbirth > pelvic surgery
  • Treatment: About 90% respond to conservative treatment
  • Red flags: Fever combined with groin pain, severe illness
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

When should you seek immediate help?

If you have fever combined with groin pain, severe illness or redness/warmth over the pubic bone - contact a doctor or emergency services.

1

What is osteitis pubis?

The symphysis pubis is the anchoring point for two muscle groups that pull in opposite directions: rectus abdominis (abdominal muscles pulling upward) and the adductors (inner thigh muscles pulling downward). When these muscles become imbalanced, abnormal shear forces occur through the joint.

The condition is 2-5 times more common in men than women. Average age at diagnosis is 30-50 years for men and mid-30s for women.

0.5-8% prevalence in athletes
14% of groin pain in athletes
2-5x more common in men
2

Causes and risk factors

From most to least common:

1. Sports-related overuse (most common)

Football, ice hockey, rugby, long-distance running and other sports with kicking, direction changes or repetitive loading. Football players have the highest risk at 10-18% of injuries annually.

2. Pregnancy and childbirth

During pregnancy, pelvic joints loosen due to the hormone relaxin. After prolonged or complicated delivery, inflammation can occur, especially combined with muscular imbalance.

3. Pelvic surgery (least common)

Urological or gynecological procedures can trigger the condition. Incidence after urethropexy surgery is 0.7-2.5%.

Risk factors

  • Weak core and pelvic muscles
  • Limited hip mobility
  • Rapid increase in training load
  • Previous groin injuries
  • Imbalance between adductor and abdominal strength
3

Symptoms

Most experience a gradual onset of symptoms over weeks to months. The pain is typically localized over the pubic symphysis at the front center of the pelvis, but can also radiate to the groin, inner thigh, lower abdomen or perineum.

Typical symptoms

  • Dull, aching pain over the pubic bone that worsens with activity
  • Pain when running, kicking, jumping or changing direction
  • Pain climbing stairs
  • Discomfort lying on side
  • Worsening with coughing, sneezing or straining
  • Morning stiffness that loosens with movement
  • Waddling gait pattern (antalgic gait)
4

Diagnostics

Diagnosis is made primarily through medical history and clinical examination. Imaging is used to confirm suspicion or rule out other conditions.

Clinical examination

Pubic spring test

Direct pressure over the pubic symphysis and lateral pressure on the pubic rami. Reproduction of familiar pain is considered the most specific test.

Squeeze test (adduction test)

The patient lies on their back and squeezes knees together against resistance. The test correlates well with bone marrow edema on MRI.

Imaging

MRI is the gold standard and effectively distinguishes between acute and chronic changes. Acute findings include bone marrow edema and joint fluid, while chronic changes show sclerosis, cysts and osteophytes.

Important to remember

MRI and X-ray findings don't always correlate with symptoms. Many athletes have imaging findings without having any problems.

5

Treatment

Conservative treatment is the first choice and gives good results in most cases. A systematic review found that 97.6% of patients in case series returned to sport with conservative approach. Surgery is rarely necessary.

What can the chiropractor do?

The evidence base for chiropractic treatment of osteitis pubis is limited to case reports. Clinical experience suggests that a combination of several approaches often works well:

  • Mobilization of pelvic and hip joints
  • Treatment of muscular trigger points in adductors, hip flexors and abdominal muscles
  • Assessment and treatment of SI joint (posterior pelvic joint)
  • Individually tailored exercise program focusing on core stability
  • Guidance on load management and gradual return to sport

What can you do yourself?

  • Reduce provocative activity: Avoid kicking, jumping and rapid direction changes in the acute phase
  • Alternative training: Swimming and cycling can often be continued pain-free
  • Core stabilization: Exercises for transversus abdominis and pelvic floor. The Copenhagen exercise has strong Scandinavian evidence
  • Adduction exercises: Gradual strength training of the inner thigh muscles
  • Ice/heat: Can provide temporary symptom relief

Treatment course

With structured rehabilitation, most return to full activity within 9.5 weeks average (4-14 weeks). Rest alone without structured program can take 8 months, often with relapses.

6

Red flags - when to contact a doctor

Seek medical attention if you experience:

  • Fever combined with groin/pelvic pain
  • Severe illness or general weakness
  • Redness, warmth or swelling over the pubic bone
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms without clear cause
  • Pain not relieved by rest or medication
  • Problems with urination or bowel movements

These symptoms may indicate infectious osteomyelitis, which requires prompt medical treatment.

7

Prognosis

Osteitis pubis generally has a good prognosis. The condition is self-limiting, meaning most cases will improve over time - but active treatment and rehabilitation give faster and better results than passive rest.

~90% respond to conservative treatment
3-6 mo to full activity
<25% relapse rate

Important prognostic factors

Early diagnosis and treatment give better outcomes. Patients with bone marrow edema limited to bone alone on MRI have better prognosis than those with edema extending into surrounding musculature.

Relapse risk

With complete rehabilitation, relapse rate is low - under 25% in most studies. Without active rehabilitation, relapse is common, especially in the first year.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I exercise with osteitis pubis?
Yes, but you should modify your training. Avoid activities that provoke pain (kicking, jumping, rapid direction changes). Swimming, cycling and upper body strength training can often be continued. Gradual increase in loading under guidance is key to successful rehabilitation.
How long does it take to recover?
With structured rehabilitation, most return to full activity within 3-6 months. Without active treatment, symptoms can last 8-12 months or longer.
Can a chiropractor help with osteitis pubis?
Yes. Chiropractors can offer manual treatment of pelvic and hip joints, soft tissue treatment and individually tailored exercise programs. Many patients report good results from multimodal chiropractic treatment combined with rehabilitation exercises.
