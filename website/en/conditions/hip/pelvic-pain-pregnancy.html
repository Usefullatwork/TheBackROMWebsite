Pelvic Girdle Pain in Pregnancy

Causes, symptoms and safe treatment during pregnancy

TL;DR - Summary

  • Prevalence: 45-80% of pregnant women experience pelvic or back pain
  • Cause: Hormonal changes (relaxin), weight gain and altered posture
  • Areas: SI joint (back), symphysis (front), or both
  • Treatment: Chiropractic care is safe and effective during pregnancy
  • Prognosis: Most improve significantly 6-12 months after giving birth
1

What is pelvic girdle pain in pregnancy?

Pelvic girdle pain in pregnancy (PGP - Pelvic Girdle Pain) is pain in the pelvic region that occurs during pregnancy. It is one of the most common hip and pelvic problems pregnant women experience.

The pelvis consists of three joints that can be affected:

  • Two SI joints (sacroiliac joints) - at the back, between the sacrum and hip
  • Symphysis - at the front, where the two hip bones meet

During pregnancy, these joints become more mobile to prepare the body for childbirth. This is normal and necessary but can lead to pain and instability.

45-80% of pregnant women get pain
25% have severe problems
7% have persistent problems
6-12 mo recovery after birth

Symphysis Pubis Dysfunction (SPD)

SPD is pain in the symphysis - the joint at the front of the pelvis. Symptoms include pain in the groin area, pain when walking, and difficulty spreading the legs. Treatment is similar to other pelvic pain.

2

Causes

Hormonal changes

During pregnancy, the body produces hormones that make the ligaments more elastic:

  • Relaxin - increases 10-fold, makes ligaments more elastic
  • Estrogen and progesterone - contribute to softer connective tissue

This is necessary for the pelvis to expand during childbirth, but also makes the joints less stable.

Mechanical changes

  • Weight gain - typically 10-15 kg increases the load on the pelvis
  • Changed center of gravity - the belly pulls forward
  • Increased curve in the back (lordosis) - compensation for the belly
  • Changed gait pattern - wider gait

When does the pain occur?

1st trimester

Some experience early pain due to hormonal changes. Less common.

2nd trimester

Pain often begins now - the belly grows and the center of gravity changes.

3rd trimester

Most common period for pelvic pain. Maximum load on the pelvis.

Risk factors

  • Previous pelvic pain - in previous pregnancy or generally
  • Heavy physical work
  • Back problems before pregnancy
  • Previous injuries to pelvis or back
  • Twin pregnancy
  • Existing hip osteoarthritis - can worsen during pregnancy
3

Symptoms

Where is the pain located?

  • SI joint (back) - pain at the junction between lower back and buttock, often one-sided (pain on the outside may also be gluteal tendinopathy)
  • Symphysis (front) - pain in the groin area, middle front (for deep groin pain, consider iliopsoas tendinopathy)
  • Both - many have pain both front and back
  • Radiation - can radiate to buttock, thigh and groin (read about piriformis syndrome for persistent buttock pain, or hamstring tendinopathy for pain higher on the back)

Typical symptoms

  • Pain when walking - especially long distances
  • Pain when turning over in bed
  • Pain when standing on one leg - putting on pants, going up stairs
  • Clicking or crunching in the pelvic joints
  • Feeling of instability in the pelvis
  • Difficulty spreading the legs

Pain worsens with:

  • Going up stairs
  • Turning over in bed
  • Standing on one leg (getting dressed)
  • Prolonged standing or sitting
  • Lifting
  • Walking on uneven surfaces

When should you contact a doctor?

  • Sudden, severe pain
  • Bleeding or discharge
  • Fever together with pain
  • Numbness or weakness in the legs (numbness on the front of the thigh may be meralgia paresthetica)
  • Problems with bladder or bowel
4

Treatment

Is chiropractic treatment safe during pregnancy?

Yes! Chiropractic treatment is safe and effective during pregnancy when performed by an experienced practitioner. Research supports the use of manual treatment for pelvic pain during pregnancy.

Adaptations for pregnant women

  • Gentler techniques - mobilization rather than forceful manipulation
  • Special cushions that make lying on the stomach comfortable
  • Side-lying positions
  • Focus on comfort and safety

What does treatment involve?

  • Manual therapy - gentle mobilization of pelvis and back
  • Soft tissue treatment - release tight muscles
  • Exercises - strength and stability for pelvis and core
  • Ergonomic guidance - sleeping positions, work positions
  • Pelvic belt - can provide support and relief

Self-help and home exercises

  • Pelvic floor exercises - strengthens stability
  • Pillow between knees when lying on side
  • Avoid asymmetric movements - keep legs together
  • Take breaks during prolonged standing/sitting
  • Heat can relieve muscle tension

Practical tips

  • Get dressed sitting down - avoid standing on one leg
  • Take small steps on stairs
  • Turn over in bed with body as a unit - don't twist
  • Sit down when putting on shoes
  • Avoid heavy lifting

Pelvic belt

A pelvic belt can provide good support and pain relief for many pregnant women. It should sit low - under the belly, around the hips. Use especially during activity, not all day.

5

After giving birth

Most experience significant improvement within the first months after giving birth. Hormone levels normalize and the pelvis regains stability.

Expected recovery

  • 0-6 weeks: The body is still affected by pregnancy hormones
  • 6-12 weeks: Hormones normalize, many notice improvement
  • 3-6 months: Significant improvement for most
  • 6-12 months: Most are back to normal

Rehabilitation after birth

  • Pelvic floor first - start gently in the first days
  • Core stability - gradual build-up
  • General activity - start with walks, increase gradually
  • Avoid high intensity too early - wait at least 6-8 weeks

Persistent problems?

  • About 7% have problems that last longer than 12 months
  • Seek treatment if the pain does not improve
  • Early intervention gives better results
  • For persistent groin pain, consider FAI (hip impingement) or labral tear
6

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe to see a chiropractor when I am pregnant?
Yes, chiropractic treatment is safe during pregnancy when performed by an experienced practitioner. Techniques are adapted - gentler mobilization, special cushions, and focus on comfortable positions. Treatment can relieve pelvic pain and improve function.
When during pregnancy can I start treatment?
You can start treatment at any time during pregnancy. Many come in the 2nd or 3rd trimester when the pain is greatest, but early treatment can prevent worsening. There are no restrictions on which trimester is "best".
Will pelvic pain affect giving birth?
Pelvic pain usually does not affect childbirth itself. The pelvis's ability to expand is usually unaffected. Inform your midwife about your problems so that birthing positions can be adapted.
Does a pelvic belt help?
For many, a pelvic belt provides good support and pain relief, especially during activity. It should sit low, under the belly. Don't use it all the time - the muscles also need to work. Combine with exercises for best effect.
Does the pain go away after giving birth?
For most, the pain decreases significantly within 6-12 months after giving birth as hormone levels normalize. About 7% have persistent problems that require follow-up. Early initiation of adapted exercises speeds up recovery.
Can I exercise during pregnancy?
Yes, adapted exercise is important and safe! Focus on exercises that strengthen the pelvic floor, core and gluteal muscles. Avoid exercises that cause pain, high kicks, and extreme positions. Swimming and walking are often well tolerated.
