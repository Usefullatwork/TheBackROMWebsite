Piriformis Syndrome (False Sciatica)

Complete guide - Causes, symptoms, diagnosis and treatment

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Condition where the piriformis muscle in the buttock irritates the sciatic nerve, causing pain similar to disc herniation - but without back problems
  • How common: Accounts for 6-17% of cases with sciatica-like pain. More common in women (3:1 to 6:1)
  • Common causes: Fall/trauma (~50%), overuse from running/cycling, prolonged sitting, anatomical variations (10-16%)
  • Treatment: 79-85% improve with conservative treatment (manual therapy + exercises)
  • Red flags: Bladder dysfunction, numbness in the groin, increasing weakness in both legs - requires urgent medical evaluation
1

What is Piriformis Syndrome?

Piriformis syndrome occurs when the piriformis muscle - a small, pear-shaped muscle deep in the buttock region - irritates or compresses the sciatic nerve. The result is pain radiating down the back of the thigh, often confused with "true" sciatica from a herniated disc in the lower back.

What makes the condition difficult is that it doesn't show up on standard MRI of the back. Many therefore go a long time with symptoms without getting the right diagnosis. Piriformis syndrome is today considered part of the broader concept of "deep gluteal syndrome," which describes various causes of nerve compression in the buttock region.

Prevalence

The condition most commonly affects people between 30-50 years old, with a clear preponderance of women (gender ratio from 3:1 to 6:1). Estimates suggest piriformis syndrome accounts for 6-17% of cases where patients present to specialists with sciatica-like symptoms (Hopayian & Heathcote, 2019).

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Piriformis syndrome is divided into primary (congenital anatomical cause) and secondary (acquired) types. The secondary type accounts for over 85% of cases.

  1. Macrotrauma (~50% of cases) - Fall on the buttock, direct blow to the hip region, car accidents
  2. Overuse/microtrauma - Running, cycling, repetitive hip movements over time
  3. Prolonged sitting - Office workers, truck and taxi drivers
  4. Anatomical variations (10-16%) - The sciatic nerve may have an unusual course through or over the piriformis muscle
  5. Postoperative (rare) - After hip replacement or back surgery

Important about anatomy

Although anatomical variations are relatively common, studies have shown that there is no clear correlation between such variations and the development of piriformis syndrome (Bartret et al., 2018). Anatomy alone does not explain why some people develop symptoms.

3

Symptoms

Piriformis syndrome produces a characteristic symptom pattern that differs from discogenic sciatica. Four cardinal symptoms have been identified (Hopayian & Danielyan, 2018):

  • Deep buttock pain - occurs in 50-95% of cases
  • Worsening with sitting - a key sign (39-97%) that often distinguishes from disc herniation
  • Tenderness to pressure near the hip joint (59-92%)
  • Pain with movements that stretch the piriformis muscle (30-74%)

4

Diagnosis

There is no single test that confirms piriformis syndrome with certainty. The diagnosis is made clinically through medical history, physical examination, and exclusion of other causes.

Clinical Tests

The FAIR test (Flexion, Adduction, Internal Rotation) is the most commonly used. Studies show 88% sensitivity and 83% specificity (Fishman et al., 2002). The test is performed with the patient lying on their side while the examiner flexes, adducts, and internally rotates the hip.

Michel's 12-point scoring system provides the best documented diagnostic accuracy, with 96% sensitivity and 100% specificity at a score of 8 points or higher.

Imaging

  • MRI of the lower back - Often the first examination to rule out disc herniation. In piriformis syndrome, this is typically normal.
  • Ultrasound - Can show increased thickness of the piriformis muscle. A thickness over 9.95 mm gave 95% sensitivity and 88% specificity in one study.
  • MRI of pelvis/hip - Targeted examination can show the piriformis muscle and its relationship to the nerve.
5

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Conservative treatment is the first choice for piriformis syndrome. Studies show that 79-85% of patients achieve good improvement with manual treatment combined with exercises (Benson & Schutzer, 1999; Fishman et al., 2002).

  • Thorough examination - Assess whether symptoms are due to piriformis syndrome or other causes
  • Manual treatment - Mobilization of hip joints and pelvis, soft tissue treatment of piriformis and surrounding muscles
  • Dry needling - Studies show that ultrasound-guided dry needling can provide significant pain reduction
  • Stretching with optimal positioning - Optimized position (hip flexion 115-120°, external rotation 40-50°) can give 30-40% lengthening of the muscle
  • Strengthening exercises - For hip muscles and core stability
  • Referral - When needed for imaging or injection treatment

What can you do yourself?

  • Avoid prolonged sitting - Take breaks, stand up and move every 30-45 minutes
  • Don't sit on your wallet - A classic among taxi drivers - remove objects from the back pocket
  • Stretch the piriformis daily - Hold the stretch for 15-30 seconds, repeat 2-4 times, do it 2-3 times daily
  • Modify activity temporarily - Reduce load from running or cycling in the acute phase, but avoid complete rest
  • Heat or ice - Many benefit from a heating pad on the buttock region

Treatment Course

Phase 1 - Mild cases (2-6 weeks): Conservative treatment with exercises and activity modification

Phase 2 - Moderate cases (4-8 weeks): Regular follow-up with manual treatment

Phase 3 - Chronic/severe cases (12+ weeks): Possibly with supplementary injection treatment

If there is no response after 4-6 weeks, referral for injection treatment or further investigation should be considered.

6

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

URGENT MEDICAL EVALUATION FOR:

  • Urinary problems or loss of bladder control
  • Numbness in the groin/buttock ("saddle numbness")
  • Increasing weakness in both legs
  • Bowel incontinence

These may be signs of cauda equina syndrome requiring emergency treatment.

Other serious signs:

  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Previous cancer history with new pain
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Increasing foot drop (difficulty lifting the forefoot)
7

Prognosis

The vast majority with piriformis syndrome improve significantly with conservative treatment. A study on patients with normal imaging showed that 98% (41 of 42) recovered within 35 days (Vassalou et al., 2018).

  • Mild cases: 2-3 weeks
  • Moderate cases: 4-8 weeks
  • Chronic cases: 3 months or more

About recurrence: Data on long-term prognosis and recurrence rates are limited. A study on steroid injection showed 50% recurrence, with an average effect of only 5.3 weeks. This emphasizes the importance of combining treatment with exercises and lifestyle changes.

Surgery: For the 5-12% who don't respond to conservative treatment, surgical release of the piriformis muscle may be an option. Systematic reviews show 75-82% good/very good results after surgery.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can piriformis syndrome be mistaken for sciatica?

Yes, absolutely. Piriformis syndrome is often called "false sciatica" because the symptoms are very similar. The main difference is that true sciatica from herniation often radiates all the way to the foot and worsens with coughing/sneezing, while piriformis syndrome typically stops above the knee and worsens most with sitting.

Why doesn't piriformis syndrome show up on MRI?

Standard MRI of the lower back focuses on discs and nerve roots in the spine - not the muscles in the buttock. Piriformis syndrome is a soft tissue problem that requires targeted MRI examination of the pelvis/hip or ultrasound to visualize.

Is it dangerous to exercise with piriformis syndrome?

No, but it's wise to modify activity. Avoid exercises that provoke pain (such as deep squats or intense cycling) in the acute phase, but stay active. Complete rest is not recommended.

How long does it take for piriformis syndrome to resolve?

With proper treatment, most improve significantly within 4-8 weeks. Mild cases may improve in 2-3 weeks, while chronic cases may take 3 months or more.

Do injections help?

Corticosteroid injections can provide temporary relief, but the effect is often short-lived (average 5.3 weeks). Botulinum toxin injections have better documentation and can provide longer-lasting effect in selected patients.

Can I see a chiropractor for piriformis syndrome?

Yes. Chiropractors have the expertise to assess whether your symptoms are due to piriformis syndrome or other causes, and can offer manual treatment, exercises, and advice. When needed, we can refer further.
