Proximal Hamstring Tendinopathy

Sit Bone Pain - "High Hamstring Tendinopathy" | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Proximal hamstring tendinopathy (PHT) is a tendon problem where the hamstring muscles attach to the sit bone. It is also called "high hamstring tendinopathy" or "sit bone pain". This is a common cause of hip pain in runners and active people.

The hamstring muscles are three muscles at the back of the thigh: Biceps femoris (outer), Semitendinosus (middle), and Semimembranosus (inner). All three originate from the sit bone (tuber ischiadicum) - the hard bony prominence you sit on. In PHT, this tendon attachment becomes irritated and painful.

Summary

  • What it is: Tendon problem where the hamstrings attach to the sit bone (tuber ischiadicum)
  • Main symptom: Pain where "the buttock meets the thigh" - worst with sitting and running
  • Important: DO NOT stretch hamstrings - it makes things worse!
  • Treatment: Progressive strength training is key - often takes 3-6 months
  • Who is affected: Runners, cyclists, and people who sit a lot

DO NOT stretch hamstrings!

Stretching compresses the tendon against the sit bone and often makes things worse. Many patients have worsened their condition by "stretching more". Strength training is the key treatment - not stretching.

1

Who is affected by proximal hamstring tendinopathy?

PHT is a common running injury that affects 12-15% of all runners. The condition is slightly more common in women (51%) and typically requires 3-6 months of treatment. The good news is that 85% recover with conservative treatment.

Who is typically affected?

  • Long-distance runners - especially with increased volume
  • Middle-aged women - hormonal factors play a role
  • Office workers - prolonged sitting
  • Triathletes - combination of running/cycling
  • Yoga practitioners - lots of hamstring stretching
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Overload is the main cause

PHT typically develops over time due to:

  • Too much, too fast - rapid increase in training load
  • Compression + stretch - the tendon is squeezed against the bone with hip flexion
  • Repetitive loading - running, cycling, rowing
  • Prolonged sitting - constant pressure on the tendon

Typical triggering activities

Activities that increase risk

  • Hill running - especially uphill
  • Interval training - high speed demands
  • Cycling - especially with low saddle height
  • Yoga and Pilates - lots of hamstring stretching
  • Rowing - repetitive hip flexion under load

Biomechanical risk factors

  • Weak gluteal muscles - gluteus must compensate (see also gluteal tendinopathy)
  • Poor pelvic control - too much movement in the pelvis
  • Stiff thoracic spine - compensatory movement in the pelvis
  • Overstriding - too long stride length when running
3

Symptoms

Typical symptoms

  • Localized pain at the sit bone - where "the buttock meets the thigh"
  • Pain with sitting - especially on hard surfaces
  • Pain with running - especially speed and hills
  • Morning stiffness - typical for tendon problems
  • Pain with hamstring stretching

Pain worsens with:

  • Sitting on hard chairs
  • Driving - especially long trips
  • Running - worst with hills and intervals
  • Stretching hamstrings
  • Lunges and deep squats
  • Getting up from sitting

Radiation patterns

Pain can radiate:

  • Down the back of the thigh (can resemble sciatica)
  • Up into the buttock
  • Toward the groin (less common)

Difference from sciatica

PHT causes pain locally at the sit bone and down the back of the thigh. Sciatica from the back often causes pain all the way down to the foot and can cause numbness/tingling. With PHT, sitting on hard surfaces is typically worst - with sciatica, it is often the opposite. Pain on the outside of the hip may indicate GTPS/gluteal tendinopathy instead.

4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is primarily made through history and clinical examination.

Typical history

  • Gradual onset - not acute injury
  • Pain at the sit bone
  • Pain with sitting, especially on hard chairs
  • Increased running load beforehand
  • Attempts at stretching have made things worse

Clinical tests

Common tests

  • Palpation of sit bone - tenderness with pressure
  • Bent-knee stretch - pain with stretching with bent knee
  • Puranen-Orava test - active hamstring contraction
  • Single leg bridge - often reproduces the pain

Imaging

  • MRI - can show tendon thickening and edema
  • Ultrasound - useful for seeing tendon changes
  • X-ray - can show bone deposits (enthesophytes)

Imaging is not always necessary. Many are effectively treated based on clinical findings alone.

Differential diagnoses

  • Sciatica - from the spine
  • Piriformis syndrome - pain higher in the buttock
  • SI joint dysfunction - pelvic joint pain
  • Stress fracture in pelvis - rare
5

Treatment

Progressive strength training is key

Research shows that heavy, progressive strength training is the most effective treatment for PHT.

Treatment principles:

  • Start with isometric exercises (holding position)
  • Gradually build up to heavier loads
  • Focus on strength, NOT stretching
  • Be patient - this takes time

Phase 1: Pain control (weeks 1-2)

  • Reduce compression - avoid prolonged sitting on hard chairs
  • Use a cushion - relieve the sit bone
  • Isometric exercises - hamstring activation without movement
  • Avoid stretching!

Phase 2: Strength building (weeks 2-8)

  • Bridging progressions - from two-leg to single-leg
  • Romanian deadlift - controlled hip flexion
  • Nordic hamstring curl - eccentric strength
  • Hip thrust - gluteal activation

Phase 3: Functional training (weeks 8-12+)

  • High loads - 80-90% of maximum strength
  • Explosive training - jumps, sprints
  • Running-specific training - gradual return

Manual treatment

  • Soft tissue treatment - can relieve muscle tension
  • Pelvic and hip mobilization
  • Nerve mobilization - for nerve irritation

When is injection/surgery considered?

  • Cortisone injection - can provide short-term relief, not first choice
  • PRP injection - uncertain effect
  • Surgery - rare, only for long-term failure of conservative treatment
6

Prognosis

PHT is known to be a long-lasting condition, but most recover with patience and proper treatment.

What can you expect?

  • Weeks 1-4: Pain reduction with load management
  • Months 1-3: Gradual strength building
  • Months 3-6: Return to activity
  • Up to 12 months: For long-standing cases

Factors that affect prognosis

  • Duration before treatment - early diagnosis gives better prognosis
  • Compliance with strength program - necessary for recovery
  • Avoiding stretching - many set themselves back by stretching
  • Gradual return - too rapid progression causes relapse

Relapses are common

Many experience relapses, especially with too rapid progression of training. This is normal and does not mean treatment has failed. Adjust down, build up again - patience is key.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I stretch my hamstrings?
NO - this is a common mistake. Stretching compresses the tendon against the sit bone and often makes things worse. Focus on strength training instead. Gentle dynamic mobility is fine, but avoid prolonged static stretching.
Can I continue running?
Often you need to significantly reduce volume initially. Flat running at low intensity is tolerated better than hills and intervals. Build up gradually as pain subsides. Running should not cause more than 3/10 pain.
How long does it take to recover?
PHT is often long-lasting. Expect 3-6 months of proper treatment for significant improvement. Some need up to 12 months. Early diagnosis and treatment give better prognosis. Patience is key.
Does cortisone help?
Cortisone injection can provide short-term pain relief, but is not first choice. Research shows it does not improve long-term outcomes and can weaken tendon tissue. Strength training is more effective for lasting improvement.
How should I sit?
Avoid hard chairs. Use a seat cushion that relieves the sit bone - either a wedge-shaped cushion or one with a cutout at the back. Get up and move regularly. Consider a standing desk for periods.
