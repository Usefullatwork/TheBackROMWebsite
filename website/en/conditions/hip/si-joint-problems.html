SI Joint Problems and Pelvic Dysfunction

Sacroiliac Joint: Causes, Symptoms and Treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

The SI joint (sacroiliac joint) is the joint that connects the sacrum to the hip bone (ilium) on each side. There are two SI joints - one on each side of the lower back. Problems in the SI joint are a common cause of hip and pelvic pain.

The SI joint is one of the body's most stable joints, surrounded by very strong ligaments. It has only 2-4 degrees of movement, but this small movement is essential for transferring forces between the upper body and legs.

Summary

  • What it is: Dysfunction or irritation in the joint between the sacrum and hip bone (sacroiliac joint)
  • Prevalence: 15-30% of all people with low back pain have SI joint problems
  • Main symptom: One-sided pain in lower back/buttock region, often radiating to the thigh
  • Treatment: Manual treatment combined with specific stabilization exercises
  • Prognosis: Good - most people improve significantly within 4-8 weeks

Pelvic locking - what does it really mean?

"Pelvic locking" is a colloquial expression used when the SI joint becomes stiff, irritated or dysfunctional. The joint cannot physically "go out of place" - it is far too stable for that. But it can develop altered movement and become painful.

1

Who is affected by SI joint problems?

SI joint problems are a common cause of low back pain, and affect women far more often than men.

15-30% of low back pain cases
3:1 women vs men
2-4 degrees of joint movement
4-8 weeks typical recovery time
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Common Causes

  • Asymmetric loading - for example, standing on one leg for long periods
  • Sudden movement - misstep, lifting with twisting
  • Pregnancy - hormones make the ligaments more elastic
  • Fall on the buttocks - direct trauma to the pelvis
  • Prolonged sitting - especially on hard surfaces
  • Leg length difference - creates uneven loading

Risk Factors

  • Female sex - 3 times higher risk
  • Previous pregnancy - especially multiple births
  • Lumbar fusion - surgery that increases load on the SI joint
  • Leg length difference - over 5mm
  • Sports with rotation - golf, tennis, hockey
  • Heavy physical work - especially with lifting and twisting

Why are women affected more often?

Women's SI joints are more mobile due to the hormones estrogen and relaxin. During pregnancy, mobility increases further to prepare the body for childbirth. This increased mobility can lead to irritation and pain.

3

Symptoms

Typical Symptoms

  • One-sided pain in the lower back, right by the "dimples"
  • Buttock pain - can feel deep inside
  • Radiation to the groin or thigh - rarely below the knee
  • Morning stiffness that eases after movement
  • Pain with position changes - getting up, turning in bed

Pain worsens with:

  • Walking up stairs - especially upward
  • Standing on one leg for long periods
  • Turning in bed
  • Getting up from sitting
  • Driving - especially long trips
  • Lifting with twisting

SI joint vs sciatica - what's the difference?

  • SI joint pain rarely radiates further than the knee
  • Sciatica often causes pain all the way down to the foot
  • With sciatica, there is often tingling/numbness - rarely with SI joint
  • SI joint pain is often localized to the "dimples" in the lower back
  • The pain can also be confused with piriformis syndrome or GTPS
4

Diagnosis

SI joint pain is primarily diagnosed clinically. There is no single test that is 100% accurate, so we use a combination of several tests.

Clinical Provocation Tests

  • Distraction test - pressure outward on the hip bones
  • Compression test - pressure inward on the hip bones
  • FABER/Patrick's test - stress on the SI joint via the hip
  • Thigh thrust - pressure through the thigh
  • Gaenslen's test - one leg over the edge of the bed

The 3 of 5 rule

If 3 or more of these 5 tests are positive, there is over 90% probability that the pain originates from the SI joint.

Imaging

X-ray and MRI rarely show anything with functional SI joint problems. Imaging is most useful to rule out other conditions such as:

  • Arthritis in the SI joint
  • Inflammatory disease (Ankylosing spondylitis)
  • Fractures
  • Infection
5

Treatment

Chiropractic Treatment

SI joint problems are one of the most common conditions chiropractors treat, and the results are often good.

  • Joint manipulation - specific adjustment of the SI joint to restore normal function
  • Mobilization - gentler techniques for those who prefer them
  • Soft tissue treatment - of tight muscles around the pelvis
  • Taping - can provide support in the acute phase

Stabilization Exercises

Exercise is important to prevent recurrence. Focus on:

  • Core stability - transversus abdominis, multifidus
  • Hip muscles - gluteus medius and maximus
  • Pelvic floor - often underestimated
  • Balance and coordination

What can you expect?

  • Many notice improvement after 1-3 treatments
  • Most are significantly better within 4-8 weeks
  • Maintenance exercises reduce the risk of recurrence

Self-Help

  • Avoid asymmetric loading - don't stand on one leg for long
  • Pillow between the knees when lying on your side
  • Move regularly - avoid prolonged sitting
  • Heat can relieve stiffness
  • SI belt can provide temporary support
6

SI Joint Pain and Pregnancy

Up to 80% of pregnant women experience pelvic or back pain, and the SI joint is often involved. Learn more about pelvic pain during pregnancy.

Why does the pain occur?

  • Relaxin - hormone that makes the ligaments more elastic
  • Weight change - altered center of gravity
  • Posture changes - increased sway in the lower back
  • Abdominal muscle function - reduced stability

Safe Treatment During Pregnancy

Chiropractic treatment is safe during pregnancy, with adaptations:

  • Gentler techniques
  • Special cushions for lying on the stomach
  • Focus on mobilization rather than manipulation
  • Adapted exercises

After Birth

Most women find that pain subsides 6-12 months after birth as hormone levels normalize. Specific exercises can speed up recovery.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the SI joint really "go out of place"?
No, the SI joint cannot physically go out of place. It is one of the body's most stable joints with very strong ligaments. What happens is that the joint becomes irritated, stiff or develops altered movement - not that it moves. The term "pelvic locking" is colloquially used but is not medically correct.
Why does manipulation help when the joint is not out of place?
Manipulation affects the nervous system and muscles around the joint. It can reduce pain, decrease muscle tension and restore normal movement in a stiff joint. The effect is neurological and biomechanical - not that something is "put back in place".
How long does it take to recover?
Most people with acute SI joint problems improve significantly within 4-8 weeks with proper treatment. Chronic cases may take longer. Maintenance exercises are important to prevent recurrence.
Should I use an SI belt?
An SI belt can provide temporary support in the acute phase, but should not be used for long periods. The goal is to build up the body's own stability through exercise, not to become dependent on external support.
Can I exercise with SI joint pain?
Yes, adapted exercise is important! Avoid exercises that provoke pain, but stay active. Swimming, walking and specific stabilization exercises are often well tolerated. Your chiropractor can give you an individually tailored program.
