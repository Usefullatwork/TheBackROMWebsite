Snapping Hip - When Your Hip Clicks and Pops

Symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Snapping hip syndrome - medically known as coxa saltans - is a condition where you hear or feel a snap, click or "pop" in the hip during movement. The sound occurs when tendons or muscle structures slide over bony prominences in the hip area.

The condition affects approximately 5-10% of the general population, but prevalence varies enormously between groups. Among elite ballet dancers, 91% reported experiencing snapping hip, and 80% had it in both hips.

The important thing to know is that most cases are painless and require no treatment. Many people live with a "clicking hip" without it affecting their daily life.

Summary

  • What it is: Tendons sliding over bone structures creating an audible snap/click
  • Most common types: External (outside) > Internal (groin) > Intraarticular (rare)
  • Prevalence: 5-10% in population, up to 91% in ballet dancers
  • Treatment: Conservative treatment improves about 63%
  • Important: Most cases are painless and require no treatment

Three Types of Snapping Hip

The condition is divided into three main categories based on where in the hip the problem is located:

External Snapping Hip (most common)

The external variant is most prevalent. Here, the iliotibial band (IT band) or the front part of the gluteus maximus tendon slides over the greater trochanter - the large bony prominence on the outside of the thigh.

  • The snap is felt on the outside of the hip
  • Often visible externally - you can see something "jumping"
  • Many can produce the snap voluntarily
  • Typical description: "It feels like the hip is going out of joint"

Internal Snapping Hip

The internal variant involves the iliopsoas tendon (hip flexor) sliding over bone structures at the front of the hip - either the femoral head or the pelvic rim.

  • The snap is felt at the front of the hip, often in the groin
  • Typically occurs at 30-45 degrees of hip flexion
  • Strong female predominance: 62.5-86% of surgical cases are women
  • About 50% have concurrent damage inside the hip joint

Intraarticular Snapping Hip (rarest)

The rarest variant is caused by problems inside the hip joint itself - most often a labral tear (80% of cases) or loose fragments.

  • Often occurs after an acute injury
  • More clicking, catching or locking than a distinct snap
  • Pain is the main symptom, not the sound
  • Usually requires imaging to clarify

Who Gets Snapping Hip?

The condition is most common among:

  • Young adults and adolescents (14-30 years)
  • Dancers and ballet performers
  • Athletes with repetitive hip movements
  • Women (especially for the internal type)
5-10% in general population
91% of elite ballet dancers
80% have it in both hips

A study of 87 elite ballet dancers showed that 91% reported snapping hip, but only 7% had ever taken time off from dancing because of it.

Important to Know

Painless snapping hip can be provoked in up to 40% of asymptomatic individuals during ultrasound examination. This means that the snap itself is not necessarily a problem that needs treatment.

3

Symptoms

Main symptom: An audible or palpable snap/click in the hip during movement - especially when walking, rising from sitting, or during hip flexion.

Painless Cases (the majority)

  • The snap is noticeable but not painful
  • No limitation in activity
  • Many can reproduce the sound voluntarily

Symptomatic Cases

  • Pain develops gradually
  • Discomfort with specific movements
  • Can develop inflammation in the bursa or tendon
  • With internal type: pain at the front of the hip/groin
  • With external type: pain on the outside of the hip
4

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination is Key

In most cases, the diagnosis is clinical - meaning the examination provides the answer without the need for imaging. The most reliable test is reproducing the snap during examination.

When is Imaging Needed?

  • When the snap cannot be reproduced
  • When labral tear is suspected
  • When there is no improvement after 2-3 months
  • With red flags
  • When surgery is being considered

Imaging Modalities

Dynamic ultrasound is the preferred imaging modality for snapping hip outside the joint. MR arthrography is the gold standard when labral tear is suspected, with approximately 90% sensitivity.

5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Conservative treatment is the first choice for symptomatic snapping hip. Most expert sources recommend 6-12 months of conservative treatment before surgery is considered.

One study showed that 63% of patients improved with conservative treatment and did not need surgery.

The chiropractor can contribute with:

  • Examination and correct diagnosis
  • Soft tissue techniques for tight muscles
  • Mobilization of hip and pelvis
  • Individually tailored exercise program
  • Assessment of whether referral is necessary

What Can You Do Yourself?

Stretching

  • For external type: IT band stretching and gluteus maximus stretch
  • For internal type: Thomas stretch for iliopsoas and pigeon stretch

Strength Training

  • Strengthen hip stabilizers - especially gluteus medius
  • Core muscles
  • Gradual progression of load

Surgery - Rarely Necessary

Surgery is considered only after a minimum of 6 months without improvement from conservative treatment.

6

Prognosis

Good news for most: Snapping hip is usually harmless, and the majority of cases are painless.

63% improve with conservative treatment
93% resolution with surgery
<5% recurrence rate
7

When Should You Seek Help?

Red Flags - Seek Medical Attention Promptly

  • Night pain or rest pain - may indicate tumor, infection, avascular necrosis or stress fracture
  • Fever, chills or general feeling of illness - may indicate infection
  • Deep groin pain with loading in female athletes - high suspicion of femoral neck stress fracture
  • Mechanical locking or catching after trauma - suggests loose fragment or labral tear
  • Pain that does not improve with treatment - requires imaging and specialist evaluation
8

Frequently Asked Questions

Is snapping hip dangerous?
No, the condition is not dangerous in itself. Most cases are painless and require no treatment.
Can I continue to exercise with snapping hip?
Yes, as long as it doesn't hurt. If pain develops, adjust your training.
Will the snap go away on its own?
For some, yes - especially in adolescents. But for many, the snap itself persists. The important thing is that the snap often isn't a problem as long as it's painless.
Do I need MRI or X-ray?
Usually not. The diagnosis is typically made clinically.
