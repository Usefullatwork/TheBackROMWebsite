Hip Sports Injuries

Symptoms, treatment and safe return to sport | Chiropractor Mads Finstad

Summary (TL;DR)

  • Groin strain (adductors) accounts for 24.5% of all hip sports injuries
  • Hip flexor injury is second most common at 18.4%
  • Most sports injuries are effectively treated conservatively
  • Gradual return to sport with functional tests is key
  • Returning too early significantly increases risk of re-injury

Overview

The hip region is susceptible to a range of sports injuries, from acute muscle strains to overuse injuries that develop over time. Understanding the different injuries, their mechanisms and treatment is crucial for optimal healing and safe return to sport.

2

The Most Common Sports Injuries

Groin Strain (Adductor Injury)

Groin strain is the most common sports injury in the hip region, especially in sports with rapid direction changes, kicking and acceleration. The injury affects the adductors - the muscles on the inside of the thigh that pull the leg inward.

  • Typical Sports: Football, ice hockey, handball, tennis, ski jumping
  • Injury Mechanism: Overstretching, powerful kick, sudden direction change
  • Symptoms: Acute groin pain, swelling, pain when squeezing legs together
  • Healing Time: Grade 1: 1-3 weeks | Grade 2: 4-8 weeks | Grade 3: 3-4+ months

Hip Flexor Injury (Iliopsoas)

Injury to the hip flexor (iliopsoas) often occurs with powerful kicking, leg lifting or sprinting. Common in sports that require high knee lift and powerful hip flexion.

  • Typical Sports: Football, track and field (sprint, hurdles), martial arts, dance
  • Injury Mechanism: Powerful hip flexion against resistance, overtraining
  • Symptoms: Pain in front of hip/upper thigh, worst when lifting leg, snapping
  • Healing Time: Acute: 2-6 weeks | Chronic: can take months

Proximal Hamstring Injury

Injury to the hamstrings where they attach to the sitting bones. Can range from mild irritation to complete avulsion requiring surgery.

  • Typical Sports: Sprinting, hurdles, football, water skiing, martial arts
  • Injury Mechanism: Eccentric loading (e.g., during sprinting), overstretching
  • Symptoms: Pain under buttock, worst when sitting, running, climbing stairs
  • Healing Time: Moderate: 6-12 weeks | Severe: 3-6+ months

Hip Pointer (Iliac Crest Contusion)

Direct blow to the iliac crest causes bruising of bone and soft tissue. Common in contact sports and sports with fall risk.

  • Typical Sports: American football, rugby, ice hockey, martial arts
  • Injury Mechanism: Direct trauma to the iliac crest
  • Symptoms: Immediate pain, swelling, bruising, pain with movement
  • Healing Time: Mild: 1-2 weeks | Moderate: 2-4 weeks | Severe: 4-6 weeks

FAI - Femoroacetabular Impingement

Structural abnormality in the hip joint where the femoral head or acetabulum has abnormal shape, causing impingement during movement. Very common in athletes.

  • Typical Sports: Football, ice hockey, martial arts, ballet, cycling
  • Symptoms: Groin pain, stiffness, pain with deep hip flexion, clicking
  • Treatment: Conservative first, possibly arthroscopy for persistent problems

Sports Hernia (Athletic Pubalgia)

Weakness in the posterior wall of the inguinal canal without a visible bulge. Causes chronic groin pain that worsens with activity. Often difficult to diagnose.

  • Typical Sports: Football, ice hockey, tennis, running
  • Symptoms: Gradually increasing groin pain, worst with coughing/sneezing, direction changes
  • Treatment: Conservative first 3-6 months, possibly surgery if no improvement

Red Flags - Seek Medical Help Immediately

  • Unable to bear weight on the leg at all
  • Visible deformity or severe swelling
  • Numbness or tingling down the leg
  • Pain that doesn't improve with rest after 48 hours
  • Fever combined with hip pain
4

Acute Treatment - PRICE Principle

For acute injuries, rapid and correct first aid is crucial for optimal healing:

  • P - Protection: Avoid activities that provoke pain
  • R - Rest: Relative rest - not complete immobilization
  • I - Ice: 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours for the first 48-72 hours
  • C - Compression: Elastic bandage if needed
  • E - Elevation: Less relevant for hip than for ankle
5

Return-to-Sport

A gradual, criteria-based return to sport significantly reduces the risk of re-injury. Here are the typical phases:

  1. Acute Phase (0-72 hours): PRICE, pain control, diagnosis
  2. Subacute Phase (3 days - 2 weeks): Pain-free movement, light strength training, maintain fitness
  3. Rehabilitation Phase (2-6 weeks): Progressive strength training, mobility training, functional training
  4. Return-to-Sport Phase: Sport-specific training, gradual increase in intensity, functional tests
  5. Full Activity: Training and competition without restrictions, maintenance program
6

Prevention

  • Adequate warm-up: Minimum 15-20 minutes before intense activity
  • Progressive load increase: Don't increase volume/intensity more than 10% per week
  • Strength training: Focus on hip muscles, especially adductors and gluteus
  • Mobility: Maintain good hip mobility in all planes
  • Recovery: Adequate rest between intense sessions
  • Nutrition: Adequate energy intake and protein for tissue repair

Copenhagen Adductor Protocol

A specific exercise that has been shown to reduce groin injuries by up to 41% in football. The exercise is done in a side-lying position where a partner holds the top leg, and the athlete lifts the body up with the bottom leg. Start with 2x5 repetitions and gradually increase to 3x15.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to return to sport after a groin strain?
Return to sport after groin strain depends on severity: Grade 1 (mild) typically takes 1-3 weeks, grade 2 (moderate) takes 4-8 weeks, and grade 3 (complete tear) can take 3-4 months or more. Gradual progression and functional tests are more important than timeframes.
Can I train with mild hip pain?
Mild pain that disappears during warm-up and doesn't worsen over time can often be tolerated. But pain that increases during activity, continues after training, or disturbs sleep should be examined. The rule is: pain above 3 on a 0-10 scale during activity is a warning sign.
What is the difference between groin strain and sports hernia?
Groin strain is a muscle injury in the adductors with often sudden onset and localized tenderness. Sports hernia (athletic pubalgia) is a weakness in the posterior wall of the inguinal canal without a visible bulge, often with gradual onset and pain with coughing/sneezing. MRI can distinguish between these.
Is FAI common in athletes?
Yes, FAI is very common in athletes, especially in sports with lots of hip movement like football, ice hockey and martial arts. Studies show that up to 55% of elite athletes have radiological signs of FAI, even if not all have symptoms.
