Hip Stress Fracture

Symptoms, diagnosis, treatment and healing | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Stress fractures (also called fatigue fractures) are small cracks in the bone that occur when repetitive loading exceeds the bone's ability to repair itself. Unlike acute fractures, which are caused by a single trauma, stress fractures develop gradually over time.

Stress fractures in the hip region are a serious condition that is often delayed in diagnosis. The average diagnostic delay is 14 weeks.

Summary

  • Women have 2-3 times higher risk than men
  • Average 14-week delay from symptom onset to diagnosis
  • Femoral neck stress fractures are high-risk and may require surgery
  • MRI is the gold standard for early diagnosis
  • Early diagnosis and proper management are crucial for good prognosis
14 weeksaverage diagnostic delay
2-3xhigher risk for women

Critically Important

Femoral neck stress fractures are a medical priority. If not detected and treated properly, they can develop into a complete fracture - with potentially serious consequences including the need for hip replacement.

1

What Is a Stress Fracture?

Stress fractures occur when cumulative loading on the bone exceeds its ability to repair itself. In the hip region, stress fractures can occur in:

Femoral Neck (collum femoris)

High-risk location. Can develop into a complete fracture and require surgical treatment.

Pelvis (os pubis, ramus)

Common in runners. Causes pain in the groin/lower abdomen. Good prognosis with conservative treatment.

Sacrum

Often overlooked. Causes diffuse lower back pain that worsens with sitting and running.

Femoral Shaft

Less common. Causes pain on the outside of the thigh that worsens with loading.

2

Risk Factors

Training-Related Factors

  • Too rapid increase in volume/intensity
  • Insufficient recovery
  • Repetitive loading (running)
  • Hard surfaces
  • Worn or incorrect footwear

Bone-Related Factors

  • Low bone mineral density
  • Vitamin D deficiency
  • Calcium deficiency
  • Previous stress fracture
  • Osteoporosis/osteopenia

Biomechanical Factors

  • Leg length difference
  • Overpronation
  • Muscle weakness (especially hip)
  • Reduced flexibility
  • Poor running technique

Female Athlete Triad / RED-S

Women with stress fractures should be screened for RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport). This includes low energy availability, menstrual disturbances and low bone mineral density.

3

Symptoms of Stress Fracture

Symptom Development Over Time

Early Signs (weeks 1-2)

Vague, diffuse pain that comes on gradually. Typically pain only during activity.

Progression (weeks 2-6)

Pain becomes more localized. Comes earlier in activity and lasts longer afterward.

Established Stress Fracture (week 6+)

Constant pain, even at rest. Night pain common. Limping.

Symptoms by Location

Location Symptoms Risk Level
Femoral Neck Deep groin pain, worst with loading and rotation HIGH RISK
Pelvis Pain in groin/lower abdomen Low risk
Sacrum Diffuse lower back pain Low risk
Femoral Shaft Pain on outside of thigh MODERATE RISK
4

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

Hop Test

Hopping on one leg provokes pain. Simple but sensitive test.

Fulcrum Test

Pain with pressure while bending the thigh. Specific for femoral shaft.

Imaging

MRI (Gold Standard)

High sensitivity, shows bone marrow edema and stress fracture early. Ordered when suspected.

  • X-ray: May be negative for the first 2-6 weeks.
  • CT: Good for characterizing fracture lines.
  • Bone scan: Sensitive, but less specific than MRI.
5

Treatment

Phase 1: Acute Phase - Offloading

Immediate cessation of loading activity. For high-risk stress fractures, crutches may be necessary.

Phase 2: Healing Phase (6-12 weeks)

Gradual increase in loading. Cross-training (swimming, cycling, pool running).

Phase 3: Rehabilitation and Strength

Strength training for hip muscles once pain-free.

Phase 4: Gradual Return to Activity

Structured progression program. 10% increase per week as a general rule.

Surgery for High-Risk Stress Fractures

Stress fractures on the compression side of the femoral neck may require surgical stabilization with screws.

The Chiropractor's Role

Identify risk factors such as muscle weakness and biomechanical deviations. Refer for imaging when suspected.

6

Prevention

Training Principles

  • Don't increase training volume more than 10% per week
  • Introduce new activities gradually
  • Alternate between hard and easy training
  • Include rest days
  • Vary surfaces

Nutrition and Lifestyle

  • Adequate energy intake
  • Adequate calcium (1000-1300 mg/day)
  • Vitamin D (800-2000 IU/day)
  • Good protein intake
  • Good sleep quality
7

Frequently Asked Questions

How do stress fractures in the hip occur?
Stress fractures occur when repetitive loading exceeds the bone's ability to repair itself.
Can stress fractures be seen on regular X-ray?
Not always. MRI is much more sensitive and can detect early stress fractures.
How long does it take for a stress fracture to heal?
Low risk: 6-8 weeks. High risk (femoral neck): 12-16 weeks or more.
Can I exercise while the stress fracture heals?
Activity that doesn't load the injured area is allowed. Swimming and pool running are alternatives.
