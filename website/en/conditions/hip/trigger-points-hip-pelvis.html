Trigger Points in Hip and Pelvis

Relieve myofascial pain and get back to movement

Summary

  • What they are: Hyperirritable spots in taut bands of muscle that cause pain and referred pain
  • Prevalence: 85% of the population has trigger points at some point

What Are Trigger Points?

Trigger points are localized, hyperirritable spots within taut bands of skeletal muscle. They are characterized by local tenderness and can produce referred pain in predictable patterns when compressed.

In the hip and pelvic region, trigger points can cause pain that mimics other conditions such as piriformis syndrome, sciatica, hip osteoarthritis, or GTPS. This makes accurate diagnosis important.

Common Locations

Gluteus Medius

Location: Upper outer buttock

Referred pain: Low back, outer hip, back of thigh

Often mimics GTPS or SI joint pain

Gluteus Minimus

Location: Deep under gluteus medius

Referred pain: Down the side and back of thigh to ankle

Classic "pseudo-sciatica" pattern - can extend all the way to the foot

Piriformis

Location: Deep buttock, from sacrum to greater trochanter

Referred pain: Sacroiliac region, buttock, back of thigh

Related to piriformis syndrome

Quadratus Lumborum

Location: Deep low back, from ribs to pelvis

Referred pain: Low back, hip, buttock, groin

Often overlooked cause of low back pain

Treatment

Chiropractic Treatment

  • Manual pressure release - sustained pressure on trigger points
  • Dry needling - thin needles to deactivate trigger points
  • Instrument-assisted soft tissue mobilization
  • Stretching and strengthening exercises
  • Posture and ergonomic advice

Self-Treatment

  • Foam rolling - sustained pressure on tender spots
  • Tennis ball release - for deeper pressure
  • Stretching - gentle, held stretches
  • Heat - can help relax muscles before self-treatment
  • Regular movement - avoid prolonged static positions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between trigger points and fibromyalgia?
Trigger points are localized, palpable knots with predictable referred pain patterns. Fibromyalgia is a systemic condition with widespread pain, sleep disturbances, and other symptoms. Many fibromyalgia patients also have trigger points.
Can trigger points be confused with sciatica?
Yes, trigger points in gluteus minimus and piriformis can cause pain down the leg resembling sciatica. True sciatica often has neurological findings like reduced strength or reflexes, while trigger point-referred pain does not.
How long does it take to get rid of trigger points?
Acute trigger points can often be resolved in 1-3 treatments. Chronic trigger points may require 6-12 treatments. Maintenance self-exercises help prevent relapse.
