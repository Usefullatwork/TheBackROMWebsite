Jaw Joint Problems (TMD) – Complete Guide

Everything you need to know about causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Painful to yawn. Headache in the temple. Ear pain without infection. The jaw joint sits right in front of the ear and can cause problems that radiate surprisingly far.

TMD is the collective term for problems in the jaw joint and chewing muscles. The joint connects the lower jaw to the skull and is one of the body's most complex – it both rotates and glides. That's why you can open your mouth and move your jaw sideways.

A large meta-analysis with over 170,000 participants found that around 34% experience symptoms during their lifetime (Zieliński, J Clin Med 2024). In Europe, the figure is about 29-34%. But only 5-12% have problems requiring treatment (NIDCR 2018).

Quick Summary

  • 34% experience symptoms in life, 5-12% need treatment
  • Women are affected twice as often as men
  • 75-90% improve with conservative treatment
  • Less than 5% need surgery

Good to know

Pain-free clicking sounds are common – about 33% of the population have them without having TMD. The diagnosis requires pain or movement problems, not just sound.

The Most Common Jaw Conditions

I've written detailed articles about the most common jaw conditions. Here's an overview:

Condition What is it about?
Trigger points in the jaw (37-45%) Most common cause of jaw pain. Tender points in the muscles that send pain to teeth, ear or temple.
Clicking in the jaw (30%) Sounds from the jaw joint when yawning or chewing. Usually completely harmless – 33% of symptom-free people have it.
Bruxism and teeth grinding (22%) Night-time grinding or jaw clenching during the day. Can cause morning stiffness, headache and tooth wear.
Lock jaw (2-8%) Limited mouth opening when the joint disc blocks movement. 95% get better within 12 weeks.
Jaw joint osteoarthritis (8-16%) Wear in the joint causing grinding sounds and stiffness. Most common over 45 years. Often stabilises by itself.
2

Three Main Types – From Most to Least Common

1. Muscle-related problems (myofascial pain) – 45% of cases

Clearly the most common (ScienceDirect 2021). The chewing muscles become tight, overloaded or develop tender points that can send pain to the temple, eye or neck.

2. Joint-related problems

Go to full article on clicking and locking →

Affects the jaw joint itself. Clicking sounds, locking or feeling that the jaw "gets stuck". Can be due to displacement of the joint disc or wear.

3. Combined problems

Many have elements of both. Muscle tension can cause joint problems over time, and vice versa.

3

Symptoms and Causes

How does it feel?

  • Jaw pain: Deep, aching pain in front of the ear or along the jawline. Worse with chewing, yawning or long talking.
  • Headache: Affects over half with TMD. Often as pressure behind the eye or a band around the temple.
  • Ear symptoms: Ear pain, tinnitus or "blocked ear" – without the ear doctor finding anything.
  • Limited opening: Normal is 40-55 mm (about three fingers vertically).
  • Neck pain: Up to 70% of TMD patients have concurrent neck problems.
  • Morning stiffness: Common if you clench your teeth at night.

What causes it?

Stress and muscle tension

Go to full article on stress and jaw pain →

Most common factor. Under stress, many clench their teeth unconsciously – day and night.

Poor posture

Go to full article on text neck and jaw →

Forward-leaning head during screen work pulls the jaw backwards and compresses the joint. 76% of TMD patients have forward head posture (PMC 2021).

Teeth grinding (bruxism)

Go to full article on teeth grinding →

8-10% have night-time bruxism (Zieliński 2024). But the connection with TMD pain is more complicated than previously thought.

Neck problems

Go to full article on neck-jaw connection →

Nerve connections between jaw and upper neck (trigeminocervical nucleus) mean that problems in one place can cause symptoms in the other.

How Can Chiropractic Help?

Most people with TMD improve without surgery. A systematic review from 2024 concluded that conservative treatment should be first choice – surgery is relevant for less than 5% (Dentistry Journal 2024).

Treatment approach

  • Examination: Assessment of jaw, neck, posture and mobility
  • Muscle treatment: Release of tight chewing muscles and trigger points
  • Joint mobilisation: Gentle techniques for better jaw joint mobility
  • Neck treatment: Upper neck vertebrae (C0-C3) are often involved
  • Exercises: Individually tailored home programme
  • Advice: Ergonomics, stress management, sleep hygiene

Typical course: 6-10 treatments over 2-3 months.

Honestly speaking

Relapses are common if triggering factors aren't managed. Stress, poor ergonomics and teeth grinding must be addressed – otherwise the problems often return.

5

Red Flags

Call 999 if:

  • Chest pain radiating to the jaw (may be heart attack)
  • Sudden facial weakness, numbness or speech difficulties
  • Breathing difficulties together with jaw pain

Contact emergency services same day if:

  • Fever + swelling + jaw pain (possible infection)
  • Electric, stabbing pains in the face coming in attacks (possible trigeminal neuralgia)
  • Age over 50 + new pain in temple when chewing (possible temporal arteritis)
  • Suddenly locked jaw that won't release
6

Prevention and Self-Care

What you can do yourself

Resting position for the jaw

Tongue tip against the palate, teeth 2-3 mm apart, lips closed. Reduces unconscious clenching.

Heat or cold

Warm cloth for tight muscles. Ice for acute pain.

Soft diet

Cut food into smaller pieces. Avoid chewing gum when it hurts.

Three simple exercises

Go to full article on jaw exercises →

  • Controlled opening: Open mouth slowly. Hold 2 seconds. Close slowly. 10 times.
  • Chin retraction: Pull chin straight back ("double chin"). Hold 5 seconds. 10 times.
  • Sideways gliding: Move jaw gently to left, centre, right. 5 times each way.

Stop if exercises cause more pain.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Are jaw joint problems serious?

Usually not. TMD is a benign condition. About 40% experience spontaneous improvement without treatment (AAFP 2015). But persistent problems should be examined – early treatment gives faster improvement.

Will the problems come back?

It varies. If you manage stress, improve ergonomics and do exercises regularly, relapses are less likely. If you change nothing, the problems often return.

Do I need a bite splint?

Maybe. The splint protects the teeth, but evidence for pain relief is limited. It doesn't replace active treatment.

Does neck treatment help the jaw?

Yes, often. 70% of TMD patients have concurrent neck problems. Nerve connections mean that treatment of the upper neck can reduce jaw symptoms.

Do I need surgery?

Very rarely. Less than 5% need surgery – and then only after prolonged conservative treatment without results.

Are clicking sounds dangerous?

Pain-free clicking sounds are almost always harmless. About 33% of the population have them. If they're accompanied by pain or locking, you should get it checked.
