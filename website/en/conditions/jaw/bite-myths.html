Bite Problems and Jaw Pain - Myths vs Facts

What the research says about the connection | Chiropractor Majorstua, Oslo

Many patients come to us with a concern: "Could my bite be the cause of my pain?" Perhaps your dentist mentioned an overbite, or you've read that crooked teeth cause headaches. It's understandable to think this way - but what does the research actually say?

In short: The connection between bite problems and jaw pain is considerably weaker than many believe. A systematic review from 2017 in the Journal of Oral Rehabilitation examined 25 studies and found that only 2 of 40 investigated occlusal factors had consistent association with TMJ disorders.

Quick Summary

  • 56% of the population has malocclusion, only 5-12% have TMD
  • Weak connection: Only 2 of 40 occlusal factors show consistent link to TMJ problems
  • Most common causes: Muscle tension, stress/teeth grinding, neck problems
  • 90-95% improve with conservative treatment
  • Red flags: Jaw pain with chest pain (call emergency), sudden intense headache

When should you seek help immediately?

For jaw pain combined with chest pain (call emergency services), sudden intense headache, or jaw fatigue when chewing if you're over 50 with vision disturbances.

1

How common are bite deviations and jaw problems?

Malocclusion (deviation from "ideal" bite) is extremely common. A meta-analysis of 77 studies showed that approximately 56% of the world's population has some form of malocclusion. Among children, around 90% have some degree of malocclusion, but only 10-15% have severe cases requiring treatment.

Temporomandibular dysfunction (TMD) - jaw joint problems - affects fewer people. NIDCR estimates 5-12% in the USA, while global studies show 29-34% depending on how the condition is defined. Women are affected at least twice as often as men.

Important point

Even though 56% have bite deviation and only 5-12% have TMD, this doesn't mean bite deviation causes TMD. Most people with "wrong bite" never have jaw problems. The body is remarkably good at adapting.

2

Causes of jaw pain - from most to least common

Modern research shows that TMD has multifactorial causes. Here are the factors ranked by how big a role they play:

Most common causes (well documented)

Muscle tension and myofascial pain

Tight chewing muscles are the most common cause of jaw pain.

Stress, anxiety, and sleep problems

Psychological factors are strongly associated with TMD. See our article on stress and jaw pain.

Bruxism (teeth grinding/clenching)

Often nocturnal and unconscious, especially during stress. Read more about teeth grinding.

Neck problems (cervicogenic component)

Close connection between neck and jaw.

Less common causes

  • Dental problems: Cavities, abscess, cracked teeth can cause referred pain to the jaw
  • TMJ osteoarthritis: Degenerative changes in the joint itself
  • Disc displacement in the TMJ: Can cause clicking and locking

Rare causes

  • Trigeminal neuralgia: Intense, electric shock-like pain
  • Giant cell arteritis: Inflammation in blood vessels, primarily in elderly
  • Malocclusion as primary cause: Cochrane review and AADR conclude with weak/no evidence
3

The scientific paradigm shift

For decades it was assumed that "wrong bite" was the main cause of jaw problems. This view has been fundamentally challenged by modern research.

What the research says

  • Manfredini et al. (2017): "Clinicians are encouraged to abandon the old gnathological paradigm in TMD practice."
  • Cochrane review (2024): No evidence that occlusion treatment treats or prevents TMD.
  • NIDCR (USA): "For many years it was believed that malocclusion causes TMD; however, there is no evidence supporting this view."

An interesting finding is reversed causality: Several studies show that bite deviations can be consequences of TMD, not causes. For example, open bite can develop from internal TMJ dysfunction.

Norwegian guidelines from the Norwegian Directorate of Health (2016) are clear: "So far, no clear connection has been found between dental occlusion and development of TMD symptoms."

4

What about headache and bite?

The connection between malocclusion and headache is also poorly documented. Van der Meer and colleagues concluded in Cephalalgia (2019) that primary headaches like tension headache and migraine "are not caused by musculoskeletal dysfunction" - even though they may be associated with such symptoms.

The International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD-3) recognizes "headache attributed to temporomandibular dysfunction" as secondary headache. But this relates to TMD itself - not to bite deviation directly.

5

Symptoms of TMJ disorders

TMD can cause a wide range of symptoms:

  • Pain in jaw, face, or in front of the ear
  • Clicking or crunching when opening mouth/chewing
  • Limited jaw opening or jaw locking
  • Fatigue in the chewing muscles
  • Headache, especially in the temples
  • Ear pain or feeling of "blocked" ear (without infection)
  • Neck pain and stiffness

Symptoms vary from mild to significantly disabling. Many experience symptoms that come and go, often in connection with stressful periods.

6

Diagnosis

TMD is primarily diagnosed through clinical examination. The chiropractor will examine the jaw joint, chewing muscles, neck, and head movement. Jaw opening is measured, and we listen for sounds from the joint.

Imaging (MRI/X-ray) is rarely necessary for typical TMD symptoms. Studies show that imaging findings often don't correlate with symptoms - many without problems have "abnormal" findings on MRI, and many with problems have normal images. Clinical examination is therefore more important than images in most cases.

When is imaging relevant?

Imaging may be relevant when structural changes are suspected, after trauma, or if conservative treatment doesn't work over time.

7

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Manual therapy for TMD has moderate to high quality evidence for pain relief and increased jaw opening. A study published in PMC (2015) showed 81% improvement in pain scores with chiropractic treatment.

Treatment may include:

  • Soft tissue treatment of chewing and neck muscles
  • Mobilization of TMJ and cervical vertebrae
  • Trigger point treatment
  • Exercises for jaw and neck
  • Advice on relaxation and ergonomics

The combination of manual treatment and therapeutic exercises gives the best results according to systematic reviews.

What can you do yourself?

Home remedies

  • Heat or cold: Warm cloth on chewing muscles can relieve tension
  • Avoid extreme jaw opening: Be careful with large sandwiches, prolonged dental visits
  • Soft diet: During acute problems, temporarily avoid hard/chewy food
  • Relax the jaw: "Lips together, teeth apart"
  • Stress management: Relaxation exercises and good sleep help significantly
  • Gentle stretching: Calm jaw opening movements and side-to-side movements

Treatment progression

Norwegian guidelines recommend a stepwise approach:

  • Step 1 (0-2 months): Information, self-management, and exercises
  • Step 2 (2-4 months): Bite splint and physiotherapy/chiropractic
  • Step 3: For continuing problems, referral to specialist

Good prognosis

90-95% of TMD cases improve with conservative treatment or without treatment (Norwegian Directorate of Health, 2016). The prognosis is generally good, but relapses are common - especially during stress or changed life situations.

8

Treatments NOT recommended

Based on systematic reviews and international guidelines, the following are discouraged for TMD:

Discouraged treatments

  • Occlusal adjustment (bite grinding): Cochrane review found no difference from placebo
  • Orthodontics to "prevent" TMD: Systematic reviews show "Neutral effect" - neither prevents nor causes
  • Surgical interventions as first choice: BMJ guidelines (2023) strongly recommend against irreversible treatments

INfORM/IADR (2025) summarizes: "Dental and surgical techniques to correct occlusion are not supported" as treatment for TMD.

9

Chiropractor, dentist, or doctor?

Chiropractor can help with:

  • Muscle and joint-related jaw problems
  • TMD with neck component
  • Myofascial pain in chewing muscles
  • Headache associated with jaw/neck problems

Dentist first for:

  • Suspected tooth decay, abscess, or cracked tooth
  • Visible wear patterns on teeth
  • Need for bite splint evaluation

Doctor for:

  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, fatigue)
  • Neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness)
  • Long-term problems (>3 months) without improvement
10

Common myths about bite and jaw pain

Myth 1: "Bad bite causes jaw pain"

Truth: Weak and inconsistent association - no causal connection has been proven. NIDCR states that this belief is not supported by evidence.

Myth 2: "Orthodontics prevents or cures TMJ problems"

Truth: Orthodontic treatment has "Neutral effect" - neither prevents nor causes TMD. Norwegian guidelines clearly state that TMD is not an indication for orthodontics.

Myth 3: "Perfect bite is necessary"

Truth: The body has significant adaptive capacity. Most people with malocclusion never develop TMD. As McNamara wrote already in 1997: "Not achieving ideal occlusion does not result in TMD symptoms."

Myth 4: "TMJ problems require aggressive treatment"

Truth: 90-95% improve with conservative treatment or without treatment. BMJ guidelines strongly recommend against irreversible interventions.

11

Prognosis

The prognosis for TMD is generally good. Most people experience significant improvement with conservative treatment, and many cases resolve on their own. The biopsychosocial model - which considers physical, psychological, and social factors - provides the best understanding and treatment outcomes.

Relapses are common, however, especially with increased stress or lifestyle changes. Long-term results are often similar for different treatment approaches after 6-12 months. The most important thing is therefore to learn self-management strategies that can be used for any relapses.

12

Frequently Asked Questions

Can an overbite cause headaches?
Research shows weak to no connection between overbite and headaches. Headaches associated with the jaw are usually caused by muscle tension, not the bite relationship itself. If you have headaches and suspect the jaw, it's more likely that tension in the chewing and neck muscles is the cause.
Should I get braces to prevent jaw problems?
No, this is not recommended based on research. Orthodontic treatment has shown neither to prevent nor cure TMD. Braces may be relevant for cosmetic or functional reasons, but not to prevent TMJ problems.
Does a bite splint help with jaw pain?
The evidence is conflicting. Cochrane found "insufficient evidence" for effectiveness. Bite splints may help some, especially those with limited jaw opening or nighttime teeth grinding, but should not be the only treatment. Combination with exercises and possibly manual therapy gives better results.
How long does it take for TMJ problems to resolve?
This varies greatly. Acute cases may improve in days to weeks with proper treatment. Subacute problems (6-12 weeks) often require a more systematic approach. Chronic cases (>3 months) may take longer and often require focus on multiple factors, including stress management.
Is jaw clicking dangerous?
Clicking in the jaw joint is common and often harmless. Many have clicking without pain or functional impairment. It usually doesn't require treatment unless accompanied by pain, locking, or gradual worsening.
Can a chiropractor treat jaw problems?
Yes, manual therapy has moderate evidence for treating TMD. Chiropractors who work with the jaw joint can offer soft tissue treatment, mobilization, exercises, and advice. Treatment is especially relevant when there's a connection between neck and jaw, or when muscular factors dominate.
