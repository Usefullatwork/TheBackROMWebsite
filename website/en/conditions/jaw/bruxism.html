Teeth Grinding (Bruxism)

Complete guide to causes and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Bruxism is involuntary grinding or clenching of the teeth. It affects 8-31% of the population, and many don't know they have it until a partner, dentist or jaw pain reveals it.

But here's what most people don't know: There are two completely different types of bruxism, and they require different treatment approaches.

Summary

  • Two types: Sleep bruxism (nighttime, unconscious) and awake bruxism (daytime clenching) - require different approaches
  • Forces: Nighttime grinding can generate 50-80 kg force - far above normal chewing force
  • Sleep apnea connection: Up to 50% of sleep apnea patients have bruxism - must be ruled out before night guard
  • SSRI risk: 20-24% of users develop bruxism, often 3-4 weeks after starting
  • Night guard: Protects teeth but doesn't treat the cause - can worsen sleep apnea
  • Prognosis: Good with proper treatment - often stress-related and manageable

Important: Sleep Apnea and Night Guard

If you have sleep apnea or suspect it (snoring, breathing stops, extreme fatigue), you should be careful with regular night guards. Get evaluated for sleep apnea BEFORE starting with a night guard.

1

The Two Types of Bruxism

Sleep Bruxism

This is the classic "nighttime teeth grinding" most people think of:

  • Rhythmic movements: The jaw moves in repetitive patterns
  • Occurs in light sleep: Especially in transitions between sleep stages
  • Enormous forces: Can generate 50-80 kg pressure - far above normal chewing force of 10-15 kg
  • Sound: Partner often hears loud grinding noise
  • Morning symptoms: Headache, sore jaw, tired chewing muscles

Important: Sleep bruxism is classified as a sleep-related movement disorder and is often linked to other sleep problems.

Awake Bruxism

This type occurs while you're awake and is fundamentally different:

  • Static clenching: You press teeth together without movement
  • Stress trigger: Triggered by concentration, stress, frustration
  • Semi-conscious: You can learn to become aware of it
  • Daytime symptoms: Jaw pain that increases throughout the day
  • More common in women: Studies show higher prevalence

Why the Distinction Matters

Awake bruxism can be treated with awareness training and biofeedback - you can learn to stop. Sleep bruxism happens unconsciously and requires other approaches like sleep hygiene, stress management and protective measures.

2

Symptoms of Bruxism

Many don't know they grind their teeth. Look for these signs:

Direct Symptoms

  • Partner hears grinding at night
  • Wake up with clenched jaws
  • Jaw muscles that feel tired or sore in the morning
  • Tension headaches, especially in the temples

Secondary Conditions

  • Tooth wear: Flat, worn or cracked teeth
  • Enamel damage: Exposed dentin, increased sensitivity
  • Jaw joint problems: Clicking, crepitation, locking
  • Facial pain: Around masseter and temporalis
  • Tinnitus: Ringing in ears linked to jaw strain
  • Headache: Morning headache, migraine triggering
3

Causes of Bruxism

Sleep Bruxism

  • Sleep disorders: Sleep apnea (50% connection), restless legs, insomnia
  • Micro-arousals: Bruxism episodes often follow brief awakenings
  • Dopamine dysfunction: Possible connection to the brain's reward system
  • Genetics: 20-50% have family history
  • Medications: Especially SSRI (see separate section)
  • Substances: Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, drugs

Awake Bruxism

  • Stress and anxiety: Main cause
  • Concentration: Clenching during focused work
  • Habits: Learned behavior that reinforces over time
  • Personality: Perfectionism, high demands on oneself
  • Work environment: High pressure, deadlines
4

SSRI and Bruxism

Do you use antidepressants? Then you should know this:

SSRI-Induced Bruxism

  • 20-24% of SSRI users develop bruxism (vs 2-10% in general population)
  • Symptom onset typically 3-4 weeks after starting medication
  • All SSRIs can cause it, but sertraline and fluoxetine are most commonly reported
  • Mechanism: SSRI affects serotonin which in turn affects dopamine in areas controlling jaw muscles

Important: Never stop SSRI abruptly without talking to your doctor. If you suspect a connection, discuss dose adjustment or alternative medications.

Treatment of SSRI-Induced Bruxism

Alternatives your doctor may consider:

  • Dose reduction (if appropriate)
  • Switch to another type of antidepressant (e.g., bupropion)
  • Addition of buspirone (which can counteract bruxism)
  • Symptomatic treatment in parallel
5

Sleep Apnea and Bruxism

This is perhaps the most important connection many overlook:

  • Up to 50% of sleep apnea patients also have bruxism
  • Grinding may be a protective mechanism - the jaw pushes forward to open airways
  • Treating sleep apnea often reduces bruxism

Symptoms That May Indicate Sleep Apnea

  • Heavy snoring
  • Breathing stops observed by partner
  • Waking up with dry mouth or sore throat
  • Extreme daytime fatigue despite enough sleep
  • Morning headaches
  • Concentration difficulties

Important About Night Guard and Sleep Apnea

  • Traditional night guards can push the lower jaw backward and worsen airway blockage
  • Up to 50% of sleep apnea patients also have bruxism - grinding may be the body's attempt to open airways
  • Get evaluated for sleep apnea before starting with a night guard
  • With confirmed sleep apnea, mandibular advancement devices (MAD) may be a better alternative

Talk to your GP about sleep study if you recognize these symptoms.

6

Diagnosis

Bruxism is often diagnosed through a combination of:

Clinical Examination

  • Dental examination: Wear patterns, enamel damage, cracks
  • Jaw examination: Muscle tenderness, joint clicking
  • Headache evaluation: Type, timing, location

Sleep Study (if sleep bruxism suspected)

  • Polysomnography: Gold standard for sleep bruxism and sleep apnea
  • Home sleep recording: Simpler screening
  • Partner observation: Grinding sounds, breathing stops
7

Treatment of Bruxism

Treatment depends on the type of bruxism and underlying causes:

1. Night Guard (Occlusal Splint)

The most commonly used measure, but important to understand the limitations:

  • Protects the teeth from wear and damage
  • Does not reduce the grinding - only the consequences
  • Should be fitted by a dentist for optimal function
  • Caution with sleep apnea: Can worsen the condition

2. Biofeedback

Especially effective for awake bruxism:

  • EMG biofeedback: Teaches you to recognize muscle tension
  • Awareness training: Place reminders where you often clench
  • Progressive muscle relaxation: Reduces general tension

3. Chiropractic Treatment

I focus on the muscle and joint component:

  • Jaw joint mobilization: Improves mobility and function
  • Neck treatment: Upper neck affects jaw muscles via nerve connection
  • Trigger therapy: Releases tender points in masseter, temporalis
  • Ergonomic guidance: Screen position, posture

4. Treatment of Underlying Causes

  • Sleep apnea: CPAP or mandibular advancement device (MAD)
  • Stress: Cognitive therapy, mindfulness
  • Medication review: Evaluate SSRI connection with doctor
  • Sleep hygiene: Regular bedtime, avoid stimulants

5. Botox (in severe cases)

Injection of botulinum toxin into chewing muscles:

  • Reduces muscle strength and thus grinding intensity
  • Effect lasts 3-6 months
  • Reserved for cases that don't respond to other treatment
8

Self-Help and Prevention

For Awake Bruxism

  • Jaw awareness: Set reminders - "lips together, teeth apart"
  • Rest position: Tongue against palate, jaw relaxed
  • Regular breaks: Especially during screen work
  • Stress management: Identify and reduce triggers

For Sleep Bruxism

  • Sleep hygiene: Fixed bedtime, dark and cool bedroom
  • Avoid stimulants: Caffeine and alcohol, especially evening
  • Relaxation before bed: Warm shower, light stretching, no screens
  • Night guard: Protects teeth while you work on causes

Stretches and Exercises

Do these 2-3 times daily:

  1. Controlled opening: Open mouth slowly, hold 5 sec, close gently (10 reps)
  2. Resistance exercises: Press chin against palm, hold 5 sec (both directions)
  3. Masseter massage: Circular movements on chewing muscle 1-2 min
  4. Neck stretching: Gentle stretch to side and rotation
9

When Should You Seek Help?

  • Dentist notices wear or damage
  • Persistent jaw pain or headache
  • Jaw joint that clicks, locks or is tender
  • Partner reports grinding
  • Symptoms of sleep apnea
  • New bruxism after medication change

Red Flags - See Doctor/Dentist Promptly

  • Acute jaw lock: You can't open or close your mouth
  • Tooth fracture: Cracked or broken tooth from grinding
  • Breathing stops: Observed apnea episodes at night
  • Intense pain: That doesn't respond to usual measures
  • Asymmetry: Sudden crookedness in jaw or face

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between sleep bruxism and awake bruxism?
Sleep bruxism occurs unconsciously at night with rhythmic grinding movements (50-80 kg force), while awake bruxism is daytime clenching often linked to stress and concentration. They require different treatment.
Can SSRI medications cause teeth grinding?
Yes. Studies show 20-24% of SSRI users develop bruxism, vs 2-10% in the general population. Symptoms often start 3-4 weeks after starting. Talk to your doctor if you suspect a connection.
Is there a connection between sleep apnea and teeth grinding?
Yes, research shows that up to 50% of sleep apnea patients also have bruxism. Grinding may be a protective mechanism to open airways. Therefore, sleep apnea should be ruled out before treating with night guard.
Does a night guard help with bruxism?
Night guard protects teeth but doesn't reduce the grinding itself. With sleep apnea, night guard can actually worsen the condition by pushing the jaw backward. The guard should be combined with cause-based treatment.
Can a chiropractor treat bruxism?
Yes, chiropractors can treat the muscle and joint component of bruxism through jaw joint mobilization, neck treatment and trigger therapy in jaw muscles. I often collaborate with dentists.
