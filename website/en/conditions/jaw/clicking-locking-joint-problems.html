Clicking, Locking and Joint Problems in the Jaw

Complete guide to jaw joint problems | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

The jaw joint (temporomandibular joint, TMJ) is one of the body's most complex joints, and problems here fall under jaw pain (TMD). Clicking in the jaw is very common - about 30% of the population has it - and most people live perfectly fine with it without needing treatment.

This article helps you understand when joint sounds are harmless, when they require attention, and what actually works for more serious problems like jaw lock.

Summary

  • Clicking is common: 30% of the population has it - usually completely harmless
  • No pain = no problem: Painless clicking usually doesn't require treatment
  • MRI findings are not disease: 33% of symptom-free people have disc displacement on MRI
  • Jaw lock: 95% of acute cases resolve within 12 weeks without surgery
  • Surgery rarely better: Conservative and surgical treatment give equal results after 5 years
  • Exercises work: Active treatment with exercises has the best documentation

The Most Important First: Clicking Is Rarely Dangerous

If your jaw clicks but you don't have pain and can open your mouth normally, you probably don't need to worry. About 30% of the population has clicking sounds in the jaw joint - most people live perfectly fine with it.

1

Understanding the Jaw Joint

The jaw joint is unique in the body and has several characteristics that make it special:

2 movement types (rotation + gliding)
1000+ daily movements
2 joints that must function synchronously
  • Two-part movement: Rotation + gliding in the same joint
  • Articular disc: A cartilage disc that divides the joint into two chambers
  • Double joint: Right and left jaw joints must function synchronously
  • Extreme movement frequency: Used thousands of times daily (speech, chewing, swallowing)
2

Types of Joint Sounds

There are several types of sounds from the jaw joint, and they mean different things:

1. Clicking (Click)

A clear, distinct sound - like "clicking" your fingers:

  • Often happens when opening, but can also occur when closing
  • Caused by the disc "jumping" back into place
  • Opening click: The disc reduces (returns) when opening
  • Closing click: The disc displaces again when closing
  • Reciprocal clicking: Click both when opening and closing (most common)

What Painless Clicking Means

If you have clicking without pain, it means the disc is probably slightly displaced, but the joint adapts and functions normally. No reason for concern or treatment.

2. Crepitation (Grinding/Scraping)

A continuous, raspy sound - like sand between fingertips:

  • May indicate wear in the joint surface (osteoarthritis)
  • More common in older people
  • Not necessarily painful
  • Can be stable over a long time

3. Popping (Sudden Snap)

  • Loud, sudden sound - like cracking a finger
  • Often caused by cavitation (gas bubble in joint fluid)
  • Usually harmless
3

Disc Displacement - What MRI Images Show (and Don't Show)

Many become worried when they hear that the disc is "displaced". But here's what's important to know:

Surprising MRI Research

  • 33% of completely symptom-free people have disc displacement on MRI
  • Disc displacement is often a normal finding, not a disease
  • MRI findings correlate poorly with symptoms and function
  • MRI rarely changes the treatment plan for typical TMD

Conclusion: Don't be frightened by "disc displacement" on an MRI. What matters most is symptoms and function, not images.

Types of Disc Displacement

  • With reduction: The disc displaces but "jumps" back when opening (causes clicking)
  • Without reduction: The disc remains in front and blocks full opening (causes locking)
4

Jaw Lock (Locked Jaw)

When the jaw locks, the disc sits in front of the joint head and prevents normal opening:

Acute Jaw Lock

  • Suddenly occurring limited opening
  • Can often pinpoint exactly when it happened
  • Typical opening range: 25-30mm (normal >40mm)
  • Previous clicking often disappears (because the disc no longer reduces)

Chronic Jaw Lock

  • Gradually developed limitation
  • Persistent stiffness and limited opening
  • May have episodes of worsening

95% Improve Without Surgery

Research shows that 95% of acute jaw lock cases resolve within 12 weeks with conservative treatment:

  • Joint mobilization and manipulation
  • Gradual opening exercises
  • Anti-inflammatory treatment
  • Soft tissue treatment of jaw muscles
5

Surgery vs. Conservative Treatment

What does research say about surgery for jaw joint problems?

Important Research Findings

  • 5-year follow-up: Patients treated surgically and conservatively have equally good results
  • Surgery carries risk of complications
  • Many who have surgery still need conservative treatment afterward
  • International guidelines always recommend conservative treatment first

When Is Surgery Considered?

Surgery is only relevant in special situations:

  • After 6+ months of failed conservative treatment
  • Severe structural damage (fracture, tumor)
  • Persistent locking that doesn't respond to treatment
  • Significant functional disability

Surgical Options

  • Arthrocentesis: Flushing the joint with saline (least invasive)
  • Arthroscopy: Keyhole surgery to release the disc
  • Open surgery: Rare, only for serious conditions
6

Conservative Treatment of Joint Problems

This is first-line treatment and works for the vast majority:

1. Joint Mobilization

  • Distraction techniques: Gentle stretching of the joint
  • Glide mobilization: Restoring normal gliding
  • Manipulation: For acute locking, attempt to reposition the disc

2. Muscle Treatment

  • Trigger point therapy: Masseter, temporalis, pterygoid
  • Intraoral treatment: Treatment of lateral pterygoid from inside
  • Stretching: Gradual stretch of tight muscles

3. Exercises (Most Important for Long-term Results)

For Clicking:

  • Usually no treatment necessary
  • Avoid "testing" the clicking - it can irritate the joint
  • If painful: gentle movement exercises

For Locking - Gradual Opening Training:

  1. Passive stretch: Use your fingers to gently press down on the lower jaw
  2. Hold 20-30 seconds: At maximum, pain-free opening
  3. Repeat 5-10 times: 3-5 times daily
  4. Progression: Gradually increase over weeks

Resistance Exercises:

  1. Opening against resistance: Press chin down against palm, hold 5 sec
  2. Closing against resistance: Press teeth against finger between teeth, hold 5 sec
  3. Lateral resistance: Press jaw against hand to both sides
  4. Repeat 10 times: 2-3 times daily

4. Bite Splint

  • Can provide pain relief for nighttime clenching
  • Does not permanently change disc position
  • Protects teeth from bruxism
  • Should be fitted by a dentist
7

Self-help for Joint Problems

For Painless Clicking

  • Don't do anything special: Learn to live with the sound
  • Avoid "testing": Stop clicking intentionally
  • Seek help if: Pain develops or opening becomes limited

For Painful Clicking

  • Soft diet: Avoid chewy, hard food for a period
  • Heat: Warm cloth on the jaw 10-15 min
  • Anti-inflammatory: Ibuprofen as needed
  • Avoid extreme opening: Cut up apples, avoid giant burgers
  • Sleep on your back: Avoid pressure on the jaw

For Acute Locking

  • Heat: Warm cloth 10-15 min to loosen muscles
  • Gentle movement: Try careful side movements with the jaw
  • Relaxation: Stress worsens muscle tension
  • Seek treatment: If locking persists >24 hours
8

Clicking That Disappears - Should You Worry?

Many become worried when clicking suddenly stops. Here's what it can mean:

Harmless Causes

  • The joint has adapted and the disc glides better
  • Less stress (stress reduction, better posture)
  • Natural variation

Causes That Require Attention

  • Sudden stop + limited opening = possible transition to locking
  • Sudden stop + increased pain = inflammation in the joint

Rule

If clicking disappears AND you can open normally without pain, it's probably good. If clicking disappears AND you have problems, seek help.

9

Long-term Prognosis

What can you expect over time?

For Clicking

  • Most remain stable without worsening
  • Some experience clicking disappearing on its own
  • Rarely develops into more serious problems without other risk factors

For Locking

  • 95% improve with conservative treatment
  • Most regain normal or near-normal function
  • Opening range increases gradually over weeks to months

Risk Factors for Poorer Prognosis

  • Long-lasting symptoms before starting treatment
  • High stress level
  • Concurrent bruxism
  • Catastrophic thinking about the condition
  • Passivity (avoidance of movement)
10

When Should You Seek Help?

See a Chiropractor/Physiotherapist For:

  • Clicking WITH pain
  • Limited opening (<35mm)
  • Persistent locking >24 hours
  • Clicking that affects function (difficult to eat)
  • Increasing symptoms over time

Red Flags - See a Doctor/Dentist Urgently

  • Sudden facial asymmetry: Swelling, skewness
  • Jaw lock after trauma: Fall, blow to the jaw
  • Fever + jaw joint pain: Possible infection
  • Numbness in face/lips: Nerve involvement
  • Increasing swelling: Especially if warm and red
  • Symptoms after dental treatment: Consider complications
11

Frequently Asked Questions

Is clicking in the jaw dangerous?
No, painless clicking is almost always harmless. About 30% of the population has it. Clicking without pain, locking or functional limitations usually doesn't require treatment.
What happens when the jaw locks?
In jaw lock, the disc sits in front of the condyle and blocks normal opening. You can typically only open 25-30mm compared to normal 40mm+. 95% of acute locks resolve within 12 weeks with conservative treatment.
Do I need an MRI of the jaw joint?
Rarely. 33% of completely symptom-free people have disc displacement on MRI, so findings don't necessarily mean disease. MRI is only recommended for serious symptoms that don't respond to treatment or when other pathology is suspected.
Do I need surgery for jaw joint problems?
Very rarely. Surgery is only considered after 6+ months of failed conservative treatment, and studies show that surgically and conservatively treated patients have equally good results after 5 years.
Does clicking get worse over time?
Usually not. Studies show that most people with symptom-free clicking remain stable over time. Some experience that clicking actually disappears. Clicking rarely develops into locking without other risk factors.
