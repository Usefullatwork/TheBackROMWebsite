Eagle Syndrome

Elongated styloid process - symptoms, diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Eagle syndrome is an underdiagnosed condition that can mimic temporomandibular joint dysfunction (TMD). The condition is caused by an elongated or calcified styloid process - a thin bony extension from the temporal bone just below the ear - which can irritate nerves and blood vessels in the area.

Although it is a rare condition, it is important to know about because the symptoms are often confused with TMD, and because the vascular variant can be serious.

Summary

  • 4% have elongated styloid, but only 4-10% of these have symptoms
  • Normal styloid process is 2.5-3 cm, Eagle is often defined as >3 cm
  • Two types: Classic (throat pain) and stylocarotid (vascular)
  • CT with 3D reconstruction is the gold standard for diagnosis
  • 80-90% improve after surgical treatment

What is Eagle syndrome?

Eagle syndrome was first described by Watt Weems Eagle in 1937. He observed that some patients with throat pain after tonsillectomy had elongated or calcified styloid process.

Anatomy

The styloid process (processus styloideus) is a thin bony extension from the temporal bone (os temporale) just below the ear. It provides attachment for:

Structures attached to the styloid process

  • Stylohyoid ligament: To the hyoid bone
  • Stylomandibular ligament: To the mandible
  • Three muscles: Stylopharyngeus, stylohyoid, styloglossus

Important structures run nearby: carotid artery, jugular vein, and several cranial nerves (IX, X, XI, XII).

4% have elongated styloid
4-10% of these have symptoms
2.5-3 cm normal length
2

Causes and pathophysiology

How elongation occurs

Mechanisms for elongated styloid

  • Congenital variation: Some are born with longer styloid
  • Gradual calcification: The stylohyoid ligament can calcify over time
  • Post-traumatic: Reactive growth after injury
  • Idiopathic ossification: Unknown cause

Mechanisms for symptoms

How elongated styloid causes problems

  • Mechanical irritation: Direct pressure on surrounding structures
  • Nerve involvement: Compression of glossopharyngeal (IX), vagus (X)
  • Vascular: Compression of carotid with TIA-like symptoms
  • Inflammatory: Reactive inflammation in the area
3

Two types of Eagle syndrome

Classic Eagle syndrome

Most often after tonsillectomy. Symptoms:

  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
  • Odynophagia (painful swallowing)
  • Sensation of foreign body in throat
  • Ear pain (otalgia)
  • Throat pain radiating to ear

Stylocarotid syndrome (Vascular type)

Compression of the carotid artery. This is the serious variant:

  • Transient ischemic attacks (TIA)
  • Syncope with head rotation
  • Visual disturbances
  • Headache
  • Dizziness

Important: The stylocarotid variant is serious and can cause stroke. With TIA symptoms or syncope during head movements, the patient should be referred immediately for vascular evaluation.

4

Symptoms that can mimic TMD

Eagle syndrome can present with symptoms easily confused with TMD:

Overlapping symptoms

  • Jaw pain when opening mouth or chewing
  • Pain in front of ear
  • Headache in temple region
  • Ear pain without otological pathology
  • Pain when rotating head
  • Pain on palpation of tonsil region

Differentiating findings

Signs that may point to Eagle rather than TMD:

When to think about Eagle syndrome?

  • Symptoms provoked more by swallowing than chewing
  • Palpable hard mass in tonsil area
  • Pain when rotating head
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
  • Symptoms not responding to TMD treatment
  • Previous tonsillectomy (classic type)
5

Diagnosis

Clinical examination

Examination methods

  • Intraoral palpation: Careful palpation of tonsil area may reveal hard tip
  • Provocation: Pain with head rotation, swallowing
  • Lidocaine test: Injection of local anesthetic in tonsil area can temporarily relieve symptoms

Imaging

Modality Description
CT with 3D reconstruction Gold standard - visualizes length, angle and relationship to surrounding structures
Panoramic X-ray Can show elongated styloid, but often underestimates length
CT angiography When vascular compression is suspected

Diagnostic criteria

Diagnosis is made based on:

  1. Characteristic symptoms
  2. Radiologically demonstrated elongated/calcified styloid or stylohyoid ligament
  3. Exclusion of other causes
  4. Possibly positive response to diagnostic block
6

Differential diagnoses

Eagle syndrome must be distinguished from several other conditions:

Important differential diagnoses

  • Temporomandibular dysfunction (TMD)
  • Glossopharyngeal neuralgia
  • Trigeminal neuralgia
  • Cervical spondylosis
  • Chronic tonsillitis
  • Throat tumors
  • Carotid dissection
  • Migraine
  • Giant cell arteritis (older patients)
7

Treatment

Conservative treatment

Can be tried as first measure:

Conservative options

  • Pain relievers: NSAIDs
  • Anticonvulsants: Carbamazepine or gabapentin for neuralgia component
  • Steroid injection: Locally around styloid process
  • Physiotherapy: Careful myofascial treatment
  • Lifestyle modification: Avoid provoking movements

Surgical treatment

Styloidectomy (removal of styloid process) is considered for:

  • Lack of response to conservative treatment
  • Severe symptoms
  • Vascular compression

Surgical approaches

  • Transoral: Through the mouth - shorter recovery, but limited visibility
  • External (transcervical): Better visibility, recommended for vascular involvement

Prognosis after surgery

  • 80-90% report significant improvement
  • Complications are rare with experienced surgeons
  • Symptom relief is often achieved within weeks after surgery
8

The chiropractor's role

Important for clinicians

  • Know the condition: Have Eagle syndrome in the differential diagnosis for atypical TMD presentation
  • Examine thoroughly: Include intraoral palpation and provocation tests
  • Refer when suspected: To ENT or oral surgeon for further evaluation
  • Avoid treatment when suspected: Neck manipulation is contraindicated with possible Eagle syndrome

Clinical tip

For patients with throat/jaw pain not responding to standard TMD treatment over 6-8 weeks, consider imaging to rule out structural causes like Eagle syndrome.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Eagle syndrome?
Eagle syndrome is a condition where the styloid process (a bony extension under the ear) is elongated or calcified, which can irritate nerves and blood vessels in the area and cause symptoms such as throat and jaw pain.
How common is Eagle syndrome?
Approximately 4% of the population has an elongated styloid process, but only 4-10% of these develop symptoms. This makes symptomatic Eagle syndrome a rare condition.
Can Eagle syndrome be confused with TMD?
Yes, Eagle syndrome can produce similar symptoms to TMD, including jaw pain, ear pain and headache. Important differences are that Eagle syndrome often causes pain with swallowing and head rotation, and a palpable hard mass in the tonsil area.
How is Eagle syndrome diagnosed?
CT with 3D reconstruction is the gold standard for diagnosis. Clinical examination with intraoral palpation and provocation tests can raise suspicion of the condition.
Can Eagle syndrome be treated?
Yes, conservative treatment with pain relievers and anticonvulsants can be tried first. If ineffective, surgical removal of the styloid process (styloidectomy) can be considered, with 80-90% success rate.
Is Eagle syndrome dangerous?
The vascular variant (stylocarotid syndrome) can be serious as it can compress the carotid artery and cause TIA-like symptoms. This type requires prompt evaluation and treatment to prevent stroke.
