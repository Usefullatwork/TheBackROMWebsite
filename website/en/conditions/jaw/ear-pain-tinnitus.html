Ear Symptoms from the Jaw: Tinnitus, Ear Pain and Dizziness in TMD

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

It is a frustrating situation many recognize: Ear pain, ringing in the ears or a feeling of blocked ear that does not go away. You go to the ENT doctor, but the examination shows nothing wrong. The hearing test is normal. The eardrum looks fine. Yet the symptoms persist.

The cause may lie somewhere you perhaps haven't thought of: the jaw.

Studies from ENT clinics show that 35-46% of patients with unexplained ear pain actually have jaw problems as the underlying cause. A recent systematic review from 2024 found that among patients with TMD, 57.5% also have tinnitus.

Key Points

  • 52-58% of TMD patients have tinnitus, 55% have ear pain, 75% have ear fullness
  • 35-46% of unexplained ear pain is caused by jaw problems
  • 92.9% of patients with tinnitus that changes with jaw movement have TMD
  • 41% of TMD patients experience dizziness
  • 83% experience significant improvement in tinnitus after TMD treatment
  • Improvement in ear symptoms often comes 2-3 weeks after jaw pain subsides

The Anatomy that Explains the Connection

The jaw joint lies only 1-2 millimeters from the ear canal. But proximity alone does not explain the symptoms - there are three anatomical connections that cause problems in the jaw to be felt in the ear:

The Discomalleolar Ligament (Pinto's Ligament)

This is a connective tissue strand that connects the jaw joint's disc directly to the hammer (malleus) in the middle ear. Anatomical studies have demonstrated this ligament in all examined individuals. When the jaw moves abnormally or the disc is displaced, this can pull on the ear bones and cause sound disturbances.

Shared Nerve Supply

The trigeminal nerve supplies both the jaw muscles and two important muscles in the middle ear:

  • Tensor tympani: Controls eardrum tension
  • Tensor veli palatini: Opens the Eustachian tube

When the jaw muscles are chronically tense, this can trigger reflex contraction of the middle ear muscles - which gives a feeling of blocked ear, pressure and tinnitus.

The Auriculotemporal Nerve and Referred Pain

The auriculotemporal nerve supplies both the jaw joint and outer ear canal with sensation. Pain from the jaw joint can therefore be perceived as ear pain through so-called "referred pain". You feel the pain in the ear, but the source is in the jaw.

Somatic Tinnitus - When Ringing Comes from the Jaw

A special type of tinnitus is called somatosensory or somatic tinnitus. This is ringing that can be changed - becomes louder, quieter or changes tone - when you move the jaw, bite together or open the mouth wide.

Studies show that 61-69% of tinnitus patients can modulate (change) the tinnitus sound with jaw or neck movements. An Italian study found that a full 97.8% of patients with somatosensory tinnitus had TMD as the underlying cause.

Characteristics of Somatic Tinnitus

  • The tinnitus sound changes when you move the jaw, bite together or open the mouth
  • The tinnitus is often one-sided and on the same side as the jaw problems
  • It can vary in intensity depending on jaw load
  • ENT doctor finds nothing wrong with the ear
  • Symptoms vary day to day, often in step with jaw problems

Dizziness from the Jaw

About 40% of patients with TMD experience dizziness. The connection also goes the other way: Around 65% of patients with unexplained dizziness have concurrent TMD.

Dizziness from TMD usually feels different from "true" vertigo from ear diseases like BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo):

  • Often feels more like being unsteady or "off-balance"
  • A floating feeling or lightheadedness
  • Varies in intensity and duration
  • Not the powerful spinning sensation as in BPPV

How Do You Know if Ear Symptoms Come from the Jaw?

There are several signs that suggest your ear problems may be jaw-related:

  • Normal otoscopy and hearing test (ENT finds nothing wrong)
  • Tinnitus/dizziness changes when you move the jaw
  • Symptoms vary day to day, often in step with jaw problems
  • Ear pain that worsens with chewing, yawning or talking
  • Concurrent jaw symptoms: clicking/grinding in joint, limited opening, pain in chewing muscles
  • Headache, especially in the temple
  • Teeth clenching or teeth grinding (often unconscious, especially at night)

When Should You See an ENT Doctor First?

If uncertain, it makes sense to rule out primary ear pathology with an ENT doctor first. If the examination is normal and you also have jaw symptoms, it points to TMD as the cause - and then a chiropractor is the right next step.

Treatment

What Does the Research Say?

A recent systematic review from 2025 found that manual therapy combined with exercises targeting neck and jaw reduced tinnitus severity and improved quality of life - both immediately and at 3- and 6-month follow-up.

Impressive Treatment Results

  • Wright and Bifano (1997): 52.5% achieved complete recovery from tinnitus, another 30% became significantly better - total 82.5% response rate
  • Wright (2007): In 200 patients, 83% reported significant improvement or complete absence of tinnitus after TMD treatment
  • Systematic review 2024: Multidisciplinary, non-invasive approaches were most effective

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Norwegian guidelines from the Health Directorate specifically mention chiropractors as qualified practitioners for TMD. Treatment focuses on conservative, reversible interventions:

  • Manual treatment of jaw muscles: Soft tissue treatment and trigger point therapy of masseter, temporalis and pterygoid muscles
  • Jaw joint mobilization: Gentle mobilization to improve movement
  • Intraoral treatment: Of pterygoid muscles when needed
  • Neck treatment: Cervico-mandibular manual therapy showed large effects on tinnitus severity in RCT from 2020
  • Exercises: Mobility, strength and relaxation exercises for jaw and neck
  • Patient education: Information about the condition, advice on stress management and habit modification

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Rest position: Lips together, teeth apart, tongue in the palate
  • Awareness: Notice if you clench your teeth when you concentrate or are stressed
  • Heat: Heat packs on jaw muscles can relieve tension
  • Soft food: Rest the jaw during acute periods
  • Avoid extreme opening: Cut food into smaller pieces
  • Stress management: Stress is a main driver of teeth clenching and jaw tension
7

Treatment Course and Prognosis

Typical treatment course is 6-12 treatments over 4-8 weeks.

Important About Timing

Improvement in tinnitus often comes 2-3 weeks after jaw pain begins to subside. Full effect can take 3-6 months. Ear fullness is often the symptom that responds fastest.

For properly selected patients - those with clear connection between jaw and ear symptoms - studies show that 44-83% achieve significant improvement in tinnitus, and about half experience complete or near-complete improvement.

It is Important to be Realistic

  • Not everyone responds equally well - some experience only partial improvement
  • Relapses are common in TMD, especially with persistent stress or teeth clenching
  • Treatment takes time - expect 3-6 months for optimal effect
  • Many need maintenance treatment or periodic follow-up

Red Flags - See a Doctor First

Some ear symptoms require medical evaluation before considering jaw treatment:

  • Sudden hearing loss: Immediate medical help (can be medical emergency)
  • Pulsatile tinnitus: Especially one-sided (can indicate vascular problems)
  • Fever combined with ear pain: May indicate infection
  • One-sided tinnitus with asymmetric hearing loss: Should be investigated
  • Discharge or fluid from ear
  • Neurological symptoms: Numbness, paralysis, coordination problems
  • Dizziness with double vision, swallowing difficulties or slurred speech: Call emergency services
  • Jaw claudication + age over 50: May indicate temporal arteritis

Frequently Asked Questions

Can tinnitus really come from the jaw?
Yes. Studies show that over half of TMD patients have tinnitus. If your tinnitus changes when you move your jaw, bite together or open your mouth, it is likely that the jaw is involved.
Should I see an ENT doctor or chiropractor first?
If uncertain, it makes sense to rule out primary ear pathology with an ENT doctor first. If the ENT examination is normal and you also have jaw symptoms, it points to TMD as the cause - and then a chiropractor is the right next step.
How long does it take before ear symptoms improve?
Most people notice improvement within 3-6 weeks with treatment. Ear fullness often responds fastest. Tinnitus can take longer - improvement often comes 2-3 weeks after jaw pain begins to subside. Full effect can take 3-6 months.
Can neck problems also cause ear symptoms?
Yes, neck and jaw are closely connected. About 90% of TMD patients have concurrent neck problems. Research shows that treating both neck and jaw gives better results than treating only the jaw alone.
Can stress worsen TMD and ear symptoms?
Yes. Stress is a known trigger for teeth clenching and jaw tension, which in turn can worsen both TMD and associated ear symptoms. Stress management is therefore an important part of treatment.
