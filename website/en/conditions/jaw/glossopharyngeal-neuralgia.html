Glossopharyngeal Neuralgia

Throat and ear pain, diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Glossopharyngeal neuralgia is a rare but important condition to know about. The condition causes intense, lightning-like pain in the throat, back of the tongue and ear - symptoms that can easily be confused with TMD or other jaw joint problems.

The glossopharyngeal nerve (cranial nerve IX) innervates the posterior 1/3 of the tongue, pharynx, tonsils and middle ear. The nerve runs close to the vagus nerve (CN X) and shares the nucleus tractus solitarius in the brainstem, which explains the potential cardiac complications.

Key Points

  • Prevalence: 0.2-0.8 per 100,000 - 100x rarer than trigeminal neuralgia
  • Age: Most often debuts after age 40
  • Pain type: Paroxysmal, lightning-like pain (seconds to 2 minutes)
  • Location: Throat, back of tongue, ear, jaw angle
  • Triggers: Swallowing, talking, coughing, yawning

Vagal Complication - Red Flag

In 2-10% of patients, pain attacks can trigger bradycardia, asystole or syncope due to vagal involvement. This requires immediate cardiological evaluation and can be life-threatening.

Classification (ICHD-3)

The International Classification of Headache Disorders defines two types of glossopharyngeal neuralgia:

Classical Glossopharyngeal Neuralgia

  • Idiopathic or caused by vascular compression
  • No demonstrable structural lesion

Symptomatic (Secondary)

  • Caused by underlying pathology
  • Tumor (nasopharynx, tonsil, brainstem)
  • Infection
  • Demyelinating disease
  • Vascular anomalies

Symptoms

Typical Symptoms

Pain Quality and Pattern

  • Pain quality: Lightning-like, stabbing, electric ("tic douloureux")
  • Intensity: Extreme (VAS 9-10/10)
  • Duration: Seconds to 2 minutes
  • Frequency: From few attacks to hundreds daily
  • Periodicity: Periods with attacks alternate with symptom-free intervals

Pain Distribution

Unilateral pain in:

  • Throat/pharynx
  • Tonsil/tonsillar area
  • Back of tongue
  • Ear (otalgia)
  • Angle of jaw

Trigger Factors

What Triggers Pain Attacks?

  • Swallowing (food, drink, saliva)
  • Talking
  • Coughing
  • Yawning
  • Chewing
  • Touching the tonsillar area
3

Differential Diagnoses

Several conditions can cause similar symptoms:

Important Differential Diagnoses

  • Trigeminal neuralgia: Similar pain quality, but different distribution (face)
  • Nervus intermedius neuralgia: Pain in the ear canal
  • TMD: Jaw pain, but usually more constant and muscular
  • Eagle syndrome: Elongated styloid process
  • Tonsillar malignancy: Must be ruled out with imaging
  • Nasopharyngeal cancer: Important to rule out
  • Post-herpetic neuralgia: After herpes zoster

Distinguishing from TMD

Diagnosis

History

Careful mapping of:

  • Pain quality and distribution
  • Trigger factors
  • Attack frequency and duration
  • Pain-free periods
  • Previous syncope or cardiac arrhythmias

Clinical Examination

Examination Methods

  • Neurological status
  • Inspection of oropharynx and tonsils
  • Palpation of tonsillar area (trigger zone)
  • Gag reflex

Diagnostic Test

Topical anesthesia: Application of lidocaine to posterior pharynx/tonsil that temporarily eliminates pain attacks supports the diagnosis.

Imaging

  • MRI brain: Rule out tumor, demyelination, vascular anomaly
  • MR angiography: Vascular compression of CN IX
  • CT neck: If tumor in nasopharynx/tonsil is suspected
5

Treatment

Pharmacological Treatment (First-Line)

Current Medications

  • Carbamazepine: Most commonly used, start 100-200 mg x2, titrate up
  • Oxcarbazepine: Often better tolerated than carbamazepine
  • Gabapentin: Alternative if intolerant
  • Pregabalin: Alternative
  • Baclofen: Can be used as add-on

For Cardiac Involvement

Cardiac Monitoring

  • Continuous ECG monitoring
  • Temporary pacemaker may be necessary
  • Atropine for acute bradycardia episodes

Surgical Treatment

For treatment-resistant neuralgia:

Surgical Options

  • Microvascular decompression (MVD): For proven vascular compression - high success rate (85-95%)
  • Percutaneous rhizotomy: Destruction of nerve root
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery: Gamma knife
6

The Chiropractor's Role

When glossopharyngeal neuralgia is suspected:

Recognition

Be Aware of

  • Paroxysmal throat pain triggered by swallowing
  • Ask specifically about pain in throat, tongue, ear
  • Note attack pattern and duration

Referral

  • Referral to neurologist for confirmation and treatment
  • Acute referral for syncope or cardiac symptoms
  • ENT evaluation to rule out malignancy

What the Chiropractor Should NOT Do

  • Treat the condition as TMD
  • Cervical spine manipulation (can provoke attacks)
  • Intraoral treatment near trigger areas

Clinical Tip

Any patient with paroxysmal throat pain triggered by swallowing, who does not respond to TMD treatment, should be evaluated for glossopharyngeal neuralgia and referred to a neurologist.

7

Prognosis

  • Pharmacological treatment is effective for most
  • Spontaneous remission occurs
  • Surgery for treatment resistance has good prognosis (85-95% success)
  • Important to rule out underlying malignancy

Frequently Asked Questions

What is glossopharyngeal neuralgia?
Glossopharyngeal neuralgia is a rare condition with intense, lightning-like pain in the throat, back of the tongue and ear. The pain is typically triggered by swallowing, talking or coughing.
What is the difference between glossopharyngeal neuralgia and TMD?
Glossopharyngeal neuralgia causes paroxysmal (seconds-long), lightning-like pain triggered by swallowing, while TMD causes more constant, aching pain triggered by chewing. The pain area is also different - throat/tongue/ear vs. jaw joint/masticatory muscles.
Can a chiropractor treat glossopharyngeal neuralgia?
No, glossopharyngeal neuralgia is treated by a neurologist with medications such as carbamazepine or oxcarbazepine. The chiropractor's role is to recognize the condition and refer onwards. Neck manipulation should be avoided as it may provoke attacks.
How common is glossopharyngeal neuralgia?
Glossopharyngeal neuralgia is very rare with a prevalence of 0.2-0.8 per 100,000 - about 100 times rarer than trigeminal neuralgia. The condition most often debuts after age 40.
Can glossopharyngeal neuralgia be dangerous?
In 2-10% of patients, pain attacks can trigger bradycardia, asystole or syncope due to vagal involvement. This requires immediate cardiological evaluation and can be life-threatening.
