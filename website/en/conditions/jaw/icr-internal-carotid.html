Internal Carotid-Related Pain

Carotidynia, dissection, and vascular differential diagnoses | Chiropractor Majorstua, Oslo

Vascular pathology can cause neck and facial pain that mimics musculoskeletal conditions. The internal carotid artery supplies blood to the brain and runs from the neck up through the skull base, near the jaw joint and inner ear.

Carotid dissection and carotidynia are important differential diagnoses to know about - especially because carotid dissection is responsible for 10-25% of strokes in younger adults under 50 years.

Quick Summary

  • Carotid dissection: Can cause stroke - requires immediate evaluation
  • 10-25% of strokes in younger adults are caused by dissection
  • Presentation: Unilateral neck/facial pain with or without neurological symptoms
  • Diagnostics: MR/MRA or CT angiography
  • Red flags: Horner's syndrome, TIA symptoms, pulsatile tinnitus

1

Anatomy and Relevance

The internal carotid artery supplies blood to the brain. It runs from the neck up through the skull base, near the jaw joint and inner ear. Pathology in this artery can cause:

  • Local pain in neck, face, jaw
  • Referred pain (headache)
  • Neurological symptoms with compromised blood flow

Why is this important?

Patients with early carotid dissection may seek treatment thinking they have "ordinary" neck or jaw pain. Early recognition can prevent stroke.

2

Carotid Dissection

Dissection is a tear in the artery wall where blood penetrates between the wall layers. This can cause:

  • Narrowing (stenosis) of the artery
  • Blood clot formation
  • Embolization to the brain
2.5-3 per 100,000 per year
10-25% of strokes in younger (<50 years)
40-45 years average age

Risk Factors

Trauma

Direct blow, whiplash, strangulation - including "minor" trauma that may seem insignificant.

Connective tissue disorders

Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, fibromuscular dysplasia significantly increase risk.

Infections

Recent upper respiratory infection may be associated with increased risk.

Other factors

Migraine, hypertension, oral contraceptives. Neck manipulation has a debated but possible association.

Important for clinicians

Although causality is debated, there is a temporal association between cervical manipulation and carotid dissection. Patients may seek treatment with prodromal symptoms (headache, neck pain) caused by developing dissection. Regardless of cause: Be vigilant for red flags.

3

Symptoms of Carotid Dissection

Pain (70-90% of cases)

  • Unilateral neck or facial pain
  • Headache (often frontal or temporal)
  • Pain behind the eye
  • Pain in jaw or ear
  • Often constant, may be pulsating

Horner's Syndrome (25-40%)

Classic triad on the same side as the dissection:

  • Miosis: Small pupil
  • Ptosis: Drooping eyelid
  • Anhidrosis: Lack of sweating (often subtle)

Neurological symptoms

  • TIA or stroke (hemiparesis, aphasia, visual field defects)
  • Amaurosis fugax (transient vision loss)
  • Cranial nerve palsies (XII, IX, X, XI)
  • Pulsatile tinnitus

Important timeline

Pain often comes days to weeks BEFORE neurological symptoms. This provides a window for diagnosis and treatment.

4

Carotidynia

Carotidynia is a controversial diagnosis referring to pain related to the carotid without detectable structural cause.

ICHD-3 Classification

The International Classification of Headache Disorders describes "Headache attributed to carotid or vertebral artery disorder" (code 6.5), but carotidynia as a standalone diagnosis has been removed.

Possible underlying causes

  • Inflammation in the artery wall
  • Subclinical dissection
  • Atherosclerosis
  • Fibromuscular dysplasia
  • Arteritis

Symptoms

  • Unilateral neck pain over the carotid
  • Tenderness on palpation
  • Pain radiating to face, ear, jaw
  • Can resemble TMD or cervical pathology
5

Red Flags - When to Suspect Vascular Cause

High suspicion

  • New-onset unilateral neck/headache in younger patient
  • Horner's syndrome - must always be investigated
  • Pulsatile tinnitus - not muscular!
  • Neurological symptoms - TIA, vision disturbances, palsies
  • Recent trauma - including "minor" trauma

Moderate suspicion

  • Persistent unilateral pain not responding to treatment
  • Atypical pain pattern
  • Recent upper respiratory infection with subsequent headache/neck pain
  • Known connective tissue disorder
6

Diagnostics

Imaging

Method Advantages
MRI + MRA Preferred - shows dissection flap, wall thickening, stenosis
CT angiography Fast, good availability, good sensitivity
Ultrasound Can be useful, but limited for high dissections
Conventional angiography Gold standard, but invasive

Laboratory tests

When vasculitis or connective tissue disease is suspected:

  • ESR, CRP
  • ANA, ANCA
  • Genetic testing if EDS or Marfan is suspected
7

Treatment of Carotid Dissection

Acute treatment

  • Antithrombotic treatment: Anticoagulation or antiplatelet agents
  • Duration: Usually 3-6 months
  • Follow-up: Control MRA to assess healing

Endovascular/Surgical

Rarely necessary, but considered for:

  • Progressive symptoms despite medical treatment
  • Contraindication to antithrombotic treatment
  • Pseudoaneurysm

Prognosis

  • Most dissections heal spontaneously
  • Stroke risk is highest in the first weeks
  • With early diagnosis and treatment: Good prognosis
  • Restenosis occurs in some
8

The Chiropractor's Role

Screening

For all patients with neck/headache:

  • Ask about neurological symptoms
  • Examine pupils (Horner's?)
  • Be aware of atypical presentations
  • Map risk factors

If suspected

  1. Do not manipulate the neck
  2. Refer for acute neurological evaluation
  3. For acute neurological symptoms: Call emergency services

Contraindications for cervical manipulation

  • New-onset headache of unknown cause
  • Unilateral neck pain with atypical features
  • Any sign of Horner's syndrome
  • Pulsatile tinnitus
  • Neurological symptoms
  • Known connective tissue disorder

Clinical tip

When in doubt, take the safe choice. An unnecessary referral is always better than a missed dissection. Patients understand that caution is professionally responsible.

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Characteristics
Carotid dissection Unilateral, Horner's, TIA, recent trauma
TMD Jaw movement limited, palpable tenderness, clicking
Cervical spondylosis Gradual onset, movement-related, radicular symptoms
Giant cell arteritis Age >50, claudication, chewing difficulties, elevated ESR
Migraine Periodic, aura, nausea, light/sound sensitivity

Frequently Asked Questions

What is carotid dissection?
Carotid dissection is a tear in the artery wall where blood penetrates between the wall layers. This can cause narrowing of the artery, blood clot formation, and embolization to the brain. The condition is responsible for 10-25% of strokes in younger adults under 50 years.
What are the symptoms of carotid dissection?
Common symptoms include unilateral neck or facial pain, headache (often frontal or temporal), pain behind the eye, Horner's syndrome (small pupil, drooping eyelid), pulsatile tinnitus, and neurological symptoms like TIA or vision disturbances.
What is carotidynia?
Carotidynia refers to pain related to the carotid artery without detectable structural cause. Symptoms include unilateral neck pain over the carotid, tenderness on palpation, and pain radiating to the face, ear, or jaw.
When should I seek emergency help?
Seek emergency help for signs of Horner's syndrome, TIA symptoms (transient weakness, vision disturbances, speech difficulties), pulsatile tinnitus, or sudden unilateral neck/headache combined with neurological symptoms. Call emergency services for acute neurological symptoms.
Is neck manipulation dangerous if dissection is suspected?
If carotid dissection is suspected, the neck should NOT be manipulated. Contraindications include new-onset headache of unknown cause, unilateral neck pain with atypical features, signs of Horner's syndrome, pulsatile tinnitus, and neurological symptoms.
