ICR Syndrome

Internal Carotid-Related Pain and Carotidynia | Chiropractor Majorstua, Oslo

You have one-sided neck pain that radiates up toward the jaw, ear, or face. The pain is located over what you think is a large blood vessel, and the area is tender to press. Perhaps the pain came suddenly, or it developed over a few days.

Pain related to the carotid artery (arteria carotis) is rare but important to know about. ICR syndrome (Internal Carotid-Related syndrome) and carotidynia are conditions where the pain originates from the blood vessel itself or structures around it.

Important: Some causes of pain from the carotid artery are serious and require urgent medical evaluation. This page helps you understand when to seek help immediately.

Quick Summary

  • Can be a sign of serious condition
  • Pain over the carotid artery
  • Dissection requires urgent treatment
  • Diagnosis by imaging
  • Referral to neurologist/vascular surgeon

For sudden neck pain with neurological symptoms

Sudden, intense neck pain combined with vision disturbances, weakness, numbness, speech difficulties, or headache can be a sign of carotid dissection. This is an emergency - call emergency services.

1

What is ICR syndrome?

ICR syndrome (Internal Carotid-Related syndrome) is a relatively new term used for pain conditions related to the internal carotid artery. This includes both benign conditions like carotidynia and serious conditions like carotid dissection.

The term was introduced to gather various causes of pain originating from or involving the carotid artery.

Carotid Artery - The Neck Pulse Artery

The carotid arteries are the main blood supply to the brain and face:

  • Common carotid artery: Divides at the level of the larynx (around C4)
  • Internal carotid artery: Supplies the brain - no branches in the neck
  • External carotid artery: Supplies face, tongue, pharynx
  • Carotid sinus: Blood pressure regulating structure at the bifurcation

Historical perspective

The term "carotidynia" was introduced by Fay in 1927 and used for idiopathic pain from the carotid region. ICHD-3 (International Classification of Headache Disorders) removed carotidynia as a separate diagnosis in 2004 because it often turned out to be a symptom of other pathology. Today the term is used more carefully, and thorough investigation is required.

2

Types and Causes

Pain from the carotid region can have several causes, from benign to life-threatening:

Carotid Dissection

SERIOUS

A tear in the artery wall that leads to blood accumulation (hematoma) in the wall. This narrows the artery and can lead to stroke.

  • Can occur spontaneously or after neck trauma/manipulation
  • Often causes one-sided neck/headache with Horner's syndrome
  • High risk of ischemic stroke
  • Requires urgent evaluation and treatment

Transient Perivascular Inflammation of the Carotid artery (TIPIC)

Previously called "idiopathic carotidynia". A transient inflammatory condition in the tissue around the carotid artery.

  • One-sided neck pain over the carotid
  • Tenderness on palpation
  • Imaging shows thickening of tissue around the artery
  • Good prognosis - often resolves on its own

Temporal Arteritis (Giant Cell Arteritis)

SERIOUS

Inflammation in large and medium-sized arteries, most common in people over 50.

  • Headache, often in the temples
  • Jaw claudication (pain when chewing)
  • Vision disturbances - risk of blindness
  • Requires urgent treatment with steroids

Carotid Aneurysm

Bulging of the artery wall that can compress nerves or cause pain.

  • Often asymptomatic until it becomes large
  • Can cause pulsating mass in the neck
  • Risk of rupture or embolization

Atherosclerosis and Carotid Stenosis

Fat deposits in the artery wall leading to narrowing.

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Can cause transient vision loss (amaurosis fugax)
  • Risk of stroke with severe stenosis
3

Symptoms

Symptoms of ICR syndrome vary depending on the underlying cause:

Local symptoms

  • One-sided neck pain: Over the course of the carotid artery
  • Tenderness on palpation: Over the artery
  • Radiating pain: To jaw, ear, face, eye, or head
  • Pulsating pain: In time with the heartbeat

With carotid dissection - warning symptoms

  • Sudden, intense neck/headache: Often one-sided
  • Horner's syndrome: Drooping eyelid, small pupil, decreased sweating (on the same side)
  • Pulsatile tinnitus: Ringing in time with the pulse
  • Vision disturbances: Transient vision loss, double vision
  • TIA symptoms: Transient weakness, numbness, speech difficulties

Warning symptoms for stroke

  • One-sided weakness: In face, arm, or leg
  • Speech difficulties: Slurred speech or difficulty finding words
  • Vision loss: Sudden loss of vision in one eye
  • Confusion: Sudden altered mental status
  • Intense headache: "Worst headache of my life"

These symptoms require immediate medical help - call emergency services.

4

Diagnosis

When ICR syndrome is suspected, imaging is crucial to determine the cause:

Imaging

  • Doppler ultrasound: First-line examination - shows blood flow and wall changes
  • CT angiography (CTA): Gold standard for detecting dissection and stenosis
  • MR angiography (MRA): Can show wall changes and hematoma
  • MRI with fat suppression: Shows intramural hematoma in dissection

Laboratory tests

  • ESR and CRP: Elevated in temporal arteritis
  • Blood tests: Cholesterol, blood sugar (risk factors)

This diagnosis requires specialist evaluation

When ICR syndrome is suspected, the patient should be referred to:

  • Neurologist - for headache and neurological symptoms
  • Vascular surgeon - for confirmed vascular pathology
  • Rheumatologist - for suspected temporal arteritis
  • Emergency department - for acute symptoms
5

Treatment

Treatment depends entirely on the underlying cause:

Carotid Dissection

  • Antithrombotic treatment: Blood thinners (anticoagulation or antiplatelet)
  • Hospitalization: For monitoring and acute treatment
  • Endovascular treatment: Stenting if needed
  • Duration: Antithrombotic treatment for 3-6 months

TIPIC / Idiopathic Carotidynia

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen or naproxen
  • Steroids: If NSAIDs are insufficient
  • Prognosis: Often self-limiting over weeks

Temporal Arteritis

  • High-dose steroids: Started immediately on suspicion
  • Biopsy: Of temporal artery to confirm diagnosis
  • Long-term treatment: Tapering over months to years
6

The Chiropractor's Role

As a chiropractor, my most important role with ICR syndrome is to recognize warning symptoms and refer appropriately. ICR syndrome can resemble jaw pain and other musculoskeletal conditions, but requires different management:

  • History: Identify symptoms that may indicate vascular pathology
  • Red flags: Identify warning symptoms for dissection or temporal arteritis
  • Referral: Send to emergency department or GP for further investigation
  • Caution: Avoid neck manipulation when vascular pathology is suspected

Important warning symptoms to watch for

  • New onset one-sided neck/headache
  • Horner's syndrome (drooping eyelid, small pupil)
  • Pulsatile tinnitus
  • Age over 50 with new headaches + jaw claudication
  • Symptoms after neck trauma
  • Transient neurological symptoms

Contraindications for neck manipulation

  • Suspected carotid or vertebral artery dissection
  • Recent neurological symptoms
  • Known atherosclerosis or aneurysm in neck vessels
  • Ongoing anticoagulation treatment for vascular causes

When in doubt - don't treat. Refer for investigation first.

7

When to Seek Help

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

Sudden neck pain + any of the following:

  • One-sided weakness or numbness
  • Speech difficulties or confusion
  • Sudden vision loss
  • Intense headache ("worst headache")
  • Fainting or decreased consciousness

Contact doctor same day for:

  • New onset one-sided neck pain with tenderness over carotid
  • Horner's syndrome: Drooping eyelid + small pupil
  • Pulsatile tinnitus
  • Age over 50 + new headache + jaw claudication
  • Symptoms that occurred after neck trauma

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ICR syndrome?
ICR syndrome (Internal Carotid-Related syndrome) is a collective term for pain conditions related to the internal carotid artery. It includes carotidynia (pain from the carotid artery) and pain from carotid dissection or other pathology in the artery.
What is carotidynia?
Carotidynia is one-sided neck pain over the carotid artery, often with tenderness on pressure. The pain can radiate to the face, jaw, and ear. The condition is controversial diagnostically, and serious vascular conditions must be ruled out before the diagnosis is made.
Why is ICR syndrome important to know about?
ICR syndrome is important because pain from the carotid artery can be a sign of serious conditions like dissection (tear in the artery wall), which can lead to stroke. Rapid diagnosis and treatment are crucial.
How is ICR syndrome diagnosed?
Diagnosis requires imaging such as CT angiography, MR angiography, or ultrasound of neck vessels to evaluate the artery's condition and rule out dissection, aneurysm, or other pathology.
Can a chiropractor treat ICR syndrome?
No, ICR syndrome is a vascular condition that requires medical evaluation and treatment. The chiropractor's role is to recognize warning symptoms and refer to the appropriate specialist. Neck manipulation is contraindicated when vascular pathology is suspected.

Unsure what's causing your neck pain?

We can help with a thorough assessment to distinguish between musculoskeletal causes and conditions requiring medical investigation. If vascular pathology is suspected, we refer you immediately to the right specialist.
