Jaw Headache: When Headache Comes from the Jaw

Symptoms, self-test and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Do you wake up with headaches in your temples? Does it get worse with chewing? You may have jaw headache - a condition that affects over 60% of patients with jaw pain.

Jaw headache is headache that originates from problems in the jaw joint (temporomandibular joint) or jaw muscles. The condition is often called TMD headache.

Summary

  • What it is: Headache caused by dysfunction in the jaw joint or jaw muscles (TMD)
  • Most common causes: Muscle tension (53%), teeth grinding (68-87%)
  • Strong connection: 61.6% of TMD patients have headaches
  • Prognosis: 70% experience improvement within 2-4 weeks

The Connection Between Jaw and Headache

The connection between jaw and headache is surprisingly strong:

61.6% of TMD patients have headaches
59% of headache patients have TMD
80.5% young adults with TMD have headaches
2

Causes of Jaw Headache

1. Muscle Tension and Trigger Points (53%)

The most common cause. The jaw muscles develop tight bands and tender points that refer pain to the head.

2. Teeth Grinding and Clenching (68-87%)

Found in 68-87% of patients with muscular TMD. Adults who clench their teeth during the day have 5-17 times higher risk of tension headaches.

3. Disc Displacement (43%)

The small cartilage disc in the jaw joint slips out of position and can cause clicking, locking and pain when opening wide.

3

Self-Test: Is the Jaw to Blame?

3Q/TMD Self-Test

  1. Do you have pain in your temples, face or jaw once a week or more?
  2. Do you have pain when you open your mouth or chew once a week or more?
  3. Does your jaw lock or get stuck once a week or more?

If you answer yes to at least one question, you should be examined further.

4

Treatment

Manual treatment of jaw and neck problems has good scientific support.

Chiropractic treatment may include:

  • Manual treatment of jaw muscles
  • Mobilization of jaw joint
  • Neck and upper back - stiff neck contributes to increased load on the jaw (read more about neck-jaw connection)
  • Exercises and guidance
5

What Can You Do Yourself?

Daily Tips

  • Become aware of habits: Notice if you clench your teeth together
  • Relax your jaw: Teeth should have a small gap when mouth is at rest
  • Avoid overloading: Limit chewing gum and hard foods
  • Heat: A warm cloth on the jaw for 10-15 minutes
6

When to See a Doctor

Red Flags - See a Doctor If:

  • Sudden, intense headache
  • Neurological symptoms: Vision disturbances, weakness, numbness
  • Fever combined with jaw pain
  • Swelling or lump in jaw area
