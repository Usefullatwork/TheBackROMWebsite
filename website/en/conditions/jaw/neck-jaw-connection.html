TMD and Neck Pain: Why Jaw and Neck Are Connected

The trigeminocervical complex explained | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

It's not a coincidence that so many with jaw pain also have neck problems. The connection lies in anatomy - and it's stronger than most people think. In the brainstem, there's an area where nerve signals from the jaw and upper neck meet and mix.

This article explains the connection between neck and jaw, why forward head posture is so problematic, and what research says about combined treatment.

Summary

  • 70-90% overlap: Most with TMD also have neck problems - and vice versa
  • Nerve connection: Jaw and neck share pain pathways in the trigeminocervical complex
  • Forward head posture: 60-66% of office workers have this - increases load on jaw and neck
  • Whiplash risk: 5.5x higher risk of TMD after neck injury
  • Combined treatment: Gives better results than treating only jaw or neck alone
  • Posture work: Central for long-term improvement

When Should You Seek Help Immediately?

For sudden severe neck pain with worst headache ever, facial numbness, swallowing difficulties, double vision, or fever with neck stiffness - call emergency services or go to the ER.

1

The Trigeminocervical Complex (TCC)

In the brainstem, there's an area where nerve signals from the jaw (trigeminal nerve) and upper neck (C1-C3) meet and mix. This is called the trigeminocervical complex, and it explains a lot:

  • Pain crossing: Signals from the neck can be "misinterpreted" as jaw pain and vice versa
  • Referred pain: A problem in C2 can cause pain in the jaw joint or temple
  • Shared sensitization: Persistent pain from one area can make the other more sensitive
70-90% TMD patients have neck problems
C1-C3 cervical vertebrae connected to jaw
5.5x increased TMD risk after whiplash

Practical Example

A patient comes in with "jaw pain" that's worst in the morning. On examination, I find tight and tender upper neck muscles (suboccipital) and limited mobility in C1-C2. After treating the neck - without touching the jaw - the jaw pain is significantly reduced. The cause was referred pain via the TCC.

2

Muscular Connections

Several muscles affect both neck and jaw directly:

Sternocleidomastoid (SCM)

Rotates the head but also affects the jaw joint via fascial connections. Often tight in both neck and jaw problems.

Trapezius

Shoulder-neck muscle that is often involved in TMD. Tension here can affect head position and thereby the jaw.

Hyoid Muscles

Connect jaw, neck, and breastbone. Play an important role in swallowing and jaw position.

Suboccipital Muscles

Direct connection to the dura (brain membrane) and trigeminal nucleus. Central in neck-jaw problems.

3

Forward Head Posture (FHP)

One of the most important connections between neck and jaw is posture - specifically what's called forward head posture (FHP). Normally, the ear should be in line with the shoulder when viewed from the side. With FHP, the head is shifted forward.

60-66% of office workers have FHP
60% increased neck load
4.5 kg extra per 2.5 cm forward

Consequences for the Neck

  • For every 2.5 cm the head moves forward, the load on the neck increases by about 4.5 kg
  • With typical FHP, the load can increase by 60% or more
  • Neck muscles must work constantly to hold the head up
  • Upper cervical vertebrae compress backward (extension)

Consequences for the Jaw

  • The lower jaw is pulled back and down
  • Jaw muscles activate compensatorily to keep the mouth closed
  • The jaw joint is loaded asymmetrically
  • Increased risk of teeth grinding (bruxism)

Vicious Cycle

FHP leads to increased neck load, neck muscles tighten, suboccipital muscles compress C1-C2, referred pain to jaw via TCC, jaw muscles tighten in response, increased load on jaw joint, more pain, more muscle tightness, worse posture. The cycle must be broken at multiple points.

4

Whiplash and TMD

If you've experienced a neck injury - for example in a car accident - you have significantly higher risk of jaw joint problems:

  • 5.5 times higher risk of TMD after whiplash (Haggman-Henrikson et al.)
  • Symptoms can appear weeks to months after the injury itself
  • Often overlooked in the acute phase because focus is on the neck

Why Whiplash Causes TMD

Direct Injury

The jaw is thrown back and forth during the accident. Jaw muscles can be directly injured and the jaw joint's capsular ligaments can be stretched.

Central Sensitization

Persistent neck pain makes the jaw more sensitive via the TCC connection.

Reflexive Tightening

Jaw muscles tighten as a protective reaction after trauma.

Important for You with Previous Neck Injury

If you've had whiplash and now experience jaw pain (especially when chewing), clicking in the jaw joint, limited opening ability, or headache radiating from neck to temple/jaw - these symptoms should be seen in connection with your neck injury.

5

Research on Combined Treatment

There is solid research supporting that neck and jaw should be treated together:

Armijo-Olivo et al. (2016)

Treatment of the neck gave significant reduction in jaw pain.

La Touche et al. (2009)

Manipulation of upper cervical vertebrae improved opening range and reduced jaw pain.

von Piekartz & Ludtke (2011)

Combined treatment of neck and jaw gave better results than jaw treatment alone.

Conclusion from Research

Treating only the jaw in TMD is often insufficient. The best approach is to examine and treat the entire system: jaw joint, jaw muscles, upper cervical vertebrae, and neck muscles. Posture guidance and ergonomic adaptation are important additional components.

6

Symptoms That Suggest Neck-Jaw Connection

The following patterns may indicate that the neck is contributing to your jaw problems:

  • Jaw pain that's worse in the morning (neck position during sleep)
  • Headache that starts in the neck and "creeps" forward to temple/jaw
  • Increased symptoms after lots of screen work or reading
  • Stiff neck and tender jaw simultaneously
  • Improvement of jaw symptoms with neck stretching
  • Previous whiplash or neck injury
  • Obvious forward head posture or rounded back
7

Examination of the Neck-Jaw Connection

When neck contribution to jaw joint problems is suspected, the examination includes:

Neck Examination

  • Range of motion test: Especially rotation and flexion/extension in upper neck
  • Palpation: Suboccipital muscles, C1-C2 facet joints
  • Segmental testing: Specifically C0-C1 and C1-C2
  • Neck flexion-rotation test: Specific for C1-C2

Jaw Examination

  • Opening range: Normal >40mm, limited indicates involvement
  • Jaw muscles: Palpation of masseter, temporalis, pterygoid
  • Joint sounds: Clicking, crepitation
  • Tooth contact: Symmetry when biting

Posture Assessment

  • Head position: Degree of FHP
  • Shoulder position: Forward lean, asymmetry
  • Thoracic kyphosis: Rounded upper back
8

Treatment of Neck-Jaw Problems

A holistic approach gives the best results:

Neck Treatment

  • Mobilization/manipulation: Of stiff joints, especially C0-C2
  • Soft tissue treatment: Suboccipital muscles, SCM, trapezius
  • Trigger point therapy: Focus on referred pain patterns

Jaw Treatment

  • Jaw joint mobilization: Distraction and glide techniques
  • Intraoral treatment: Lateral pterygoid, masseter from inside
  • Masseter treatment: External trigger point therapy

Posture Work

  • Ergonomic guidance: Screen height, chair adjustment
  • Chin tucks: Exercise to reduce FHP
  • Shoulder blade exercises: Retraction and depression
  • Thoracic mobility: Stretches and exercises for upper back

Exercises for Home Use

Chin Tuck

  1. Sit or stand with neutral posture
  2. Draw chin straight back (as if making a "double chin")
  3. Hold 5 seconds, release gently
  4. Repeat 10 times, 3-5 times daily

Neck Stretch

  1. Tilt head gently to the side (ear toward shoulder)
  2. Hold 20-30 seconds
  3. Repeat on opposite side
  4. Add gentle rotation for variation

Chest Muscle Stretch

  1. Stand in a doorway with forearms against the frame
  2. Take a step forward until you feel stretch in the chest
  3. Hold 30 seconds
  4. Important for counteracting forward-leaning shoulders
9

Prevention and Long-term Strategies

Workplace Ergonomics

  • Screen height: Top edge at eye level
  • Screen distance: Arm's length away
  • Chair height: Feet flat on floor, knees at 90 degrees
  • Keyboard/mouse: Low, shoulders relaxed

Phone Use

  • Hold phone higher to avoid "text neck"
  • Limit prolonged scrolling
  • Alternate between hands

Sleep Position

  • Avoid stomach sleeping (rotates neck maximally)
  • Side sleeping with supportive pillow that fills the gap between shoulder and head
  • Back sleeping with thin pillow under neck

When Should You Seek Help?

Contact us for assessment if you have:

  • Simultaneous neck and jaw problems over several weeks
  • Headache that starts at the back of the head and radiates forward
  • Persistent neck stiffness with tender jaw
  • Symptoms that worsen with screen work or reading
  • Previous neck injury with new jaw problems

Red Flags - Seek Medical Attention Immediately

  • Sudden severe neck pain with headache (worst headache ever)
  • Neurological symptoms: Facial numbness, swallowing difficulties, speech difficulties
  • Double vision or visual disturbances
  • Dizziness/unsteadiness combined with neck pain
  • Fever and neck stiffness
  • Symptoms after trauma (fall, accident)
11

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do so many with jaw problems also have neck problems?
Nerve fibers from the jaw (trigeminal) and upper neck (C1-C3) meet in the trigeminocervical complex in the brainstem. This means pain signals can cross between areas - neck problems can be perceived as jaw pain and vice versa.
Can forward head posture cause jaw problems?
Yes. When the head pushes forward, the load on neck muscles increases by up to 60%. This postural change activates jaw muscles compensatorily and changes the jaw's resting position. Studies show that 60-66% of office workers have forward head posture.
Can neck injury cause jaw joint problems later?
Yes. Research shows that people with previous neck injury (whiplash) have 5.5 times higher risk of developing TMD. Symptoms can appear weeks to months after the injury itself.
Does neck treatment help jaw pain?
Often yes. Studies show that treatment of upper cervical vertebrae (C0-C2) can reduce jaw pain, and that combined treatment of neck and jaw gives better results than jaw treatment alone.
