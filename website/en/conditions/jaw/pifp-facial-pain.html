PIFP: Persistent Idiopathic Facial Pain

Chronic facial pain without known cause - Diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

PIFP is a challenging diagnosis characterized by persistent facial pain without identifiable cause. The condition was previously known as "atypical facial pain" and can be very frustrating for patients who have often been through many examinations without getting answers.

It is important to understand that PIFP is a real pain condition, not "imaginary". Patients experience real pain, even though we cannot find a structural cause. This recognition is fundamental to good treatment.

Key Points

  • Definition: Persistent facial pain >2 hours/day, >3 months, without identifiable cause
  • Prevalence: Approx. 0.03% - rare, but often underdiagnosed
  • Typical patient: Women, middle-aged, often history of dental treatment
  • Treatment: Multidisciplinary - medications, cognitive therapy, avoid unnecessary procedures
  • Important: Diagnosis of exclusion - other causes must be ruled out first

Important with facial pain

PIFP is a diagnosis of exclusion. Sudden onset facial pain, visual disturbances, or pain together with fever require rapid medical evaluation to rule out more serious causes.

1

Definition and Terminology

Persistent Idiopathic Facial Pain (PIFP) is defined by ICHD-3 (International Classification of Headache Disorders) as:

ICHD-3 Definition

"Persistent facial and/or oral pain with varying presentation, occurring daily for more than 2 hours per day for more than 3 months, without clinical neurological findings."

Historical Terminology

Previous names for the condition

  • Atypical facial pain: Older term, still used
  • Atypical odontalgia: When pain is felt in teeth/gums
  • Phantom tooth pain: Pain after tooth extraction
2

ICHD-3 Diagnostic Criteria

For diagnosis, all of the following criteria must be met:

Mandatory Criteria

  1. Facial and/or oral pain meeting criteria B and C
  2. Recurring daily for >2 hours/day for >3 months
  3. Pain has both of the following:
    • Poorly localized and does not follow distribution of peripheral nerves
    • Dull, aching or prickling quality
  4. Clinical neurological examination is normal
  5. Dental cause is ruled out by thorough examination
  6. Not better explained by another ICHD-3 diagnosis

Important Point

PIFP is a diagnosis of exclusion. This means other possible causes must be ruled out first. Therefore, the workup can take time and involve several specialists.

3

Who is Affected?

PIFP is a relatively rare condition, but is probably underdiagnosed because many patients go through long journeys with various specialists before receiving the correct diagnosis.

0.03% of the population
2-4x more common in women
40-50 years most common debut age

Comorbidity

PIFP often occurs together with other conditions:

  • Anxiety and depression
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Other chronic pain conditions
  • Previous dental treatment history
4

Pathophysiology - Why Does PIFP Occur?

The exact mechanism behind PIFP is unknown, but several theories exist:

Central Sensitization

  • Increased excitability in the central nervous system
  • Altered pain modulation
  • Similar mechanisms as in fibromyalgia

Neuropathic Component

  • Subclinical nerve damage (e.g., after dental treatment)
  • Peripheral sensitization maintained centrally
  • Altered sensory processing in the brainstem

Psychosocial Factors

  • High prevalence of psychiatric comorbidity
  • Stress as an aggravating factor
  • Possible role of early life events

Important Perspective

Even though psychosocial factors may play a role, this does not mean the pain is "imaginary". PIFP is a real biological condition with changes in the nervous system's pain processing.

5

Clinical Presentation

Pain Quality

Typical Pain Characteristics

  • Constant, dull, aching, pressing
  • May be described as burning or prickling
  • Intensity: Often moderate (VAS 4-6)
  • Varies throughout the day

Location

  • Often nasolabial fold, cheek, jaw
  • May be felt in teeth/gums (atypical odontalgia)
  • Poorly defined - "diffuse"
  • May change side or location over time
  • Does NOT follow anatomical nerve distribution

Associated Symptoms

  • Feeling of swelling (without visible swelling)
  • Numbness or altered sensitivity (subjective)
  • Tooth sensitivity
  • Headache
  • Sleep disturbances

Typical Medical History

Many patients have a characteristic background with pain onset after dental procedure (extraction, root canal, implant), often multiple dental treatments in attempts to find relief, repeated consultations without diagnosis, and frustration over not being believed.

6

Differential Diagnoses

PIFP is a diagnosis of exclusion. The following must be ruled out:

Odontogenic Causes

  • Pulpitis/periodontitis
  • Cracked tooth
  • Impacted tooth
  • Complication after treatment

Neuralgias

  • Trigeminal neuralgia
  • Glossopharyngeal neuralgia
  • Post-herpetic neuralgia
  • Painful trigeminal neuropathy

TMD (Temporomandibular Disorders)

  • Myalgia in masticatory muscles
  • Arthralgia in jaw joint
  • Disc disorders

Other Causes

  • Sinusitis
  • Giant cell arteritis
  • Salivary pathology
  • Tumor (nasopharynx, sinuses, brain)
  • Migraine/TAC (trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias)
  • Eagle syndrome
Condition Pain Type Duration
PIFP Dull, constant Persistent, >2 hours/day
Trigeminal neuralgia Sharp, electric Seconds to minutes
TMD Aching, jaw-localized Varies with activity
Odontogenic pain Localized to tooth Provoked by stimuli
7

Workup

History

  • Careful mapping of pain history
  • Previous dental and medical treatment
  • Psychosocial assessment
  • Medication use

Clinical Examination

Examinations to be Performed

  • Full neurological examination (should be normal in PIFP)
  • Cranial nerve function
  • Sensory testing in face
  • TMD assessment
  • Intraoral examination

Imaging

Relevant Investigations

  • Dental X-ray: Rule out odontogenic pathology
  • MRI brain: For atypical features, rule out tumor/MS
  • CT sinuses: If sinus pathology is suspected
8

Treatment

General Principles

Important Treatment Principles

  • Validation: Acknowledge that the pain is real
  • Avoid further procedures: Especially tooth extractions
  • Realistic expectations: The goal is improvement, not necessarily pain-free
  • Multidisciplinary: Often need for multiple professional groups

Warning

Avoid further invasive procedures! Tooth extractions or surgery often make the situation worse, not better. Many PIFP patients have already undergone multiple unnecessary procedures.

Pharmacological Treatment

Current Medications

  • Tricyclic antidepressants: Amitriptyline 10-75 mg, first choice
  • SNRI: Duloxetine, venlafaxine
  • Anticonvulsants: Gabapentin, pregabalin (less evidence)
  • Topical: Capsaicin cream can be tried

Non-Pharmacological Treatment

Adjunctive Treatment

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT): Best evidence
  • Mindfulness-based stress reduction
  • Physiotherapy: For accompanying muscular components
  • Acupuncture: Can be tried, variable effect
9

The Chiropractor's Role

Recognition

  • Be aware of patients with chronic facial pain who have been "everywhere"
  • Ask about previous dental treatment history
  • Know the criteria for PIFP

What Can the Chiropractor Contribute?

  • Rule out TMD as primary cause
  • Treat accompanying neck/jaw components
  • Refer to neurologist/pain clinic if suspected
  • Be part of multidisciplinary team

Limitations

PIFP usually does not respond well to manual treatment alone. Pharmacological treatment is often necessary, and psychological support is important for many patients.

10

Prognosis

PIFP is often a chronic condition, but with proper treatment most can achieve significant improvement.

  • Most achieve improvement, but not necessarily complete freedom from pain
  • Early, correct diagnosis improves outcome
  • Avoidance of unnecessary procedures is important
  • Multidisciplinary treatment gives best results

Positive Factors for Prognosis

  • Early diagnosis and treatment start
  • Good therapeutic alliance
  • Acceptance of the diagnosis
  • Participation in cognitive therapy
11

Frequently Asked Questions

What is PIFP?
PIFP (Persistent Idiopathic Facial Pain) is persistent facial pain lasting more than 2 hours daily for over 3 months, without identifiable cause. It is a diagnosis of exclusion made when other causes have been ruled out.
Can PIFP be cured?
PIFP is often a chronic condition, but most achieve significant improvement with proper treatment. The goal is pain relief and improved quality of life, not necessarily complete freedom from pain.
What is the difference between PIFP and trigeminal neuralgia?
Trigeminal neuralgia causes short, intense pain attacks (seconds to minutes) along the trigeminal nerve pathways. PIFP causes persistent, dull pain that does not follow nerve anatomy and lacks the sharp attacks.
Can a chiropractor help with PIFP?
The chiropractor can help rule out TMD and treat any accompanying neck or jaw components. However, PIFP often does not respond well to manual treatment alone and usually requires a multidisciplinary approach.
Why don't I get a diagnosis after many examinations?
PIFP is a diagnosis of exclusion that requires other causes to be ruled out first. This can take time and many examinations. Many patients have seen several specialists before receiving the correct diagnosis.
Is my pain "imaginary"?
No, PIFP is a real biological pain condition with changes in the nervous system's pain processing. That we cannot find a structural cause does not mean the pain is not real.
Should I have teeth extracted to get rid of the pain?
No! Tooth extractions in PIFP often make the situation worse, not better. Many PIFP patients have already undergone unnecessary procedures that have worsened the condition.
