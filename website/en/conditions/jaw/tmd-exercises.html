Jaw Exercises and Home Treatment for TMD

90-95% improve with exercises | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

TMD (temporomandibular dysfunction) affects about 30% of the population, but only 5-12% have symptoms requiring treatment (AAFP, 2023). Women are affected 2-4 times more often than men, especially during reproductive years.

The good news? Between 90-95% improve with simple measures like exercises, heat and self-massage (PMC, 2021). You rarely need extensive treatment. Norwegian guidelines from the Health Directorate therefore recommend self-treatment as first choice.

Key Points

  • 90-95% improve with exercises and home treatment
  • Rocabado 6x6 is the most documented protocol
  • Coordination exercises show strongest evidence
  • Expect improvement after 6-8 weeks of consistent effort
  • 74% experience relief with heat/cold

Why Do Jaw Exercises Help?

Jaw exercises work by improving blood flow in the masticatory muscles, reducing muscle tension and restoring normal movement in the jaw joint.

A systematic review from 2023 with 812 participants showed moderate pain relief (Idanez-Robles et al., 2023). Shimada et al. (2023) found that coordination exercises show the best effect.

90-95% improve with exercises
6-8 weeks to noticeable improvement
4-6x daily exercise frequency
2

Coordination Exercises - Strongest Evidence

Coordination exercises show the best effect according to systematic review (Shimada et al., 2023). These exercises train the jaw to move controlled and symmetrically.

Controlled Opening and Closing

  • Place the tip of your tongue against the palate, just behind the front teeth
  • Open your mouth slowly while keeping the tongue in place
  • Stop when the tongue begins to release from the palate
  • Close slowly back
  • Repeat 6-10 times, 3-6 times daily
3

The Rocabado 6x6 Program

This is the most documented exercise protocol for TMD. The principle is simple: 6 exercises, 6 repetitions, 6 times daily.

Exercise 1 - Resting Tongue Position

Place the front third of the tongue against the palate with light pressure. Keep the teeth 2-3 mm apart. "Lips together, teeth apart" is the mantra. This position relieves the jaw joint.

Exercise 2 - Controlled Jaw Opening

With tongue on palate, open and close the jaw slowly and controlled. The movement should be straight up and down, without the jaw sliding to the side.

Exercise 3 - Chin Tuck

Pull the chin straight back, as if making a "double chin". Hold for 5-6 seconds. This strengthens the neck muscles and improves head posture.

Exercise 4 - Neck Extension

Combine upper neck flexion (nod down) with lower neck extension (look up). Hold for 5-6 seconds.

Exercise 5 - Shoulder Posture

Pull shoulders back and down. Hold for 5-6 seconds. Good shoulder posture affects the neck and jaw.

Exercise 6 - Isometric Resistance

Place your hand against your chin and press gently while trying to open your mouth. The jaw does not move - the muscle works statically. Repeat with resistance against closing and side movements.

4

Stretching Exercises

Passive Stretch Exercise

Place thumb on lower front teeth and index finger on upper ones. Open mouth gently with finger help, hold for 10-30 seconds. Repeat 3-5 times.

Side Movements

Move lower jaw gently to the right, hold 5 seconds, then to the left. Repeat 5-10 times to each side.

5

Heat and Cold - Proper Use

A survey from the TMJ Association shows that 74% of patients experience relief with thermal therapy.

Use Heat For:

  • Dull, aching pain in masticatory muscles
  • Stiffness in jaw, especially in morning
  • Chronic complaints

Apply a warm cloth or heat pack to jaw for 15-20 minutes. Moist heat penetrates better into the muscles.

Use Cold For:

  • Acute, sharp pain
  • Swelling in jaw area
  • Signs of inflammation

Apply ice pack (with towel between) to jaw for 10-15 minutes. Maximum every two hours.

6

Self-Massage of the Masticatory Muscles

The masseter muscle (the large chewing muscle under the cheek) is often tight and tender in TMD. Simple self-massage can help.

Masseter Massage

  • Find the muscle: Clench your teeth and feel the large muscle that bulges out on the cheek
  • Relax the jaw and press 2-3 fingers into the muscle
  • Use circular movements with moderate pressure
  • Work through the muscle from ear down toward the corner of mouth
  • 2-3 minutes per side, 1-2 times daily
7

Diet and Stress Management

A study from 2021 showed that over 77% of TMD patients change their diet due to symptoms. Soft diet reduces stress on the jaw joint.

Avoid:

  • Chewing gum (main recommendation from all guidelines)
  • Tough meat and hard bread
  • Raw carrots and nuts
  • Large bites requiring wide opening

Prefer:

  • Well-cooked vegetables
  • Soft fish and ground meat
  • Yogurt and smoothies
  • Food cut into small pieces

Stress Management

Anxiety and stress are among the strongest risk factors for TMD (OPPERA study, 2013). Many tighten the jaw unconsciously when stressed.

  • Notice when you clench your teeth - lips closed, teeth apart
  • Avoid holding phone between ear and shoulder
  • Take breaks during screen work
  • Simple breathing techniques can help with relaxation
8

How Long Does It Take for Exercises to Work?

Don't expect miracles overnight. Early response may be noticed after 1-2 weeks, but meaningful improvement typically requires 6-8 weeks of consistent effort (PMC, 2023).

Typical Course

  • Week 1-2: Some notice slight relief, others no change yet
  • Week 4-6: Gradual improvement in pain and mobility
  • Week 8-12: Significant functional improvement in most
  • 3-6 months: Maintenance phase - can reduce exercise frequency

Long-Term Prognosis

The OPPERA study showed that 51% of new TMD cases no longer meet diagnostic criteria after 8 months. Up to 85% show improvement within 3 years.

Important to know: Relapses are common. About 65% of TMD cases are recurring episodes. Therefore, it's wise to continue with maintenance exercises even after symptoms are gone.

9

When Do You Need Professional Help?

Self-treatment is effective for most, but not all. Seek professional evaluation if:

  • Symptoms persist more than 4-6 weeks with consistent self-treatment
  • Pain worsens despite interventions
  • You have problems chewing or speaking normally
  • The jaw locks or clicks forcefully

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

  • Manual treatment: Research shows that the combination of manual therapy and exercises gives better results than exercises alone. A systematic review (PMC, 2020) found that combination treatment gave 6 mm greater improvement in opening ability.
  • Assessment of neck and posture: Neck problems and TMD often go together. Treatment targeting both jaw and neck often gives the best results.
  • Guided exercise program: Individually tailored exercises based on your specific situation.
10

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I do the exercises?
Most protocols recommend 4-6 times daily. The Rocabado program uses 6 repetitions, 6 times daily. Short sessions spread throughout the day are more effective than one long session.
Should it hurt during exercises?
No. Mild discomfort or stretch is fine, but sharp pain means you're pushing too hard. Reduce intensity and talk to your practitioner if pain persists.
Do bite splints help?
Bite splints (stabilization splints) have about the same effect as exercises on pain according to research (Zhang et al., 2021). The advantage of exercises is that they are free and give faster improvement - on average 10 weeks earlier.
Can I exercise with TMD?
Yes, but avoid exercises that stress the jaw. Contact sports, swimming with lots of rotation and activities where you clench your teeth can temporarily worsen symptoms.
How long must I continue with exercises?
After symptoms are under control, maintenance exercises 1-2 times daily long-term are recommended. TMD has a high relapse rate, and prevention is easier than treatment.
Should I get imaging (MRI/X-ray)?
Usually not. Imaging often shows normal findings like wear and degeneration that don't explain the pain. Clinical examination is more important. Imaging is only relevant when serious pathology is suspected.
Can stress alone cause TMD?
Stress is one of the most important risk factors. Many tighten the jaw and clench teeth when stressed - often without noticing. Stress management is therefore an important part of treatment.
