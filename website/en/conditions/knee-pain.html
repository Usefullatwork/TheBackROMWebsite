Knee and Leg Pain - Complete Guide

Everything you need to know about causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Oslo

Knee and leg pain includes all problems from the knee down to the ankle. These are among the most common musculoskeletal conditions - from mild morning stiffness to pain that significantly limits daily life.

This guide covers everything you need to know about knee and leg pain: from knee osteoarthritis and runner's knee to meniscus injuries and shin splints. Each condition has its own detailed article - click on what applies to you.

Summary

  • Knee osteoarthritis affects about 7% of the population and 23% of all people over 40 globally
  • Runner's knee (patellofemoral pain) has 22.7% annual prevalence in the population
  • Degenerative meniscus injuries respond equally well to conservative treatment as to surgery
  • Exercise is the cornerstone of treatment - manual therapy is a useful supplement
  • Most knee and leg problems respond well to conservative treatment

What does it mean that exercise is the cornerstone?

For most knee and leg pain, targeted exercise is the most effective treatment. Strength training of the quadriceps and hip muscles, combined with gradual load increase, gives better long-term results than passive treatment alone.

1

Anatomy

The knee is the body's largest joint and connects the thigh bone to the shin bone. Stability depends on an interaction between four main ligaments (anterior and posterior cruciate ligaments, medial and lateral collateral ligaments), two crescent-shaped cartilage discs (the menisci), and the kneecap which increases muscle force when straightening.

The lower leg consists of the tibia (the weight-bearing bone) and the fibula (thinner, mainly for muscle attachments). The calf muscles - with gastrocnemius and soleus as the most important - are crucial for walking, running and jumping.

2

Symptoms and Causes

Common Symptom Patterns

  • Front knee pain: Pain around the kneecap, worse with stairs, squats or prolonged sitting
  • Inner knee pain: Along the inside of the knee - typical for meniscus problems or wear
  • Outer knee pain: On the outside - often IT band syndrome in runners
  • Leg pain: Along the shin bone (shin splints) or in the calf muscles
  • Additional symptoms: Swelling, stiffness, instability, locking, clicking sounds

Overuse (Most Common Cause)

Overuse injuries occur when tissue is exposed to more repetitive load than it can tolerate. Typical examples: runner's knee, jumper's knee, IT band syndrome and shin splints. Rapid increase in training volume is a common risk factor.

Wear (Degenerative Changes)

With age, many get natural age-related changes in the knee and menisci. Most degenerative meniscus findings on MRI cause no symptoms. Knee osteoarthritis develops gradually over years and causes increasing pain, stiffness and reduced mobility.

Acute Injuries

Cruciate ligament injuries, acute meniscus injuries and muscle strains typically occur with sudden movements, falls or direct impacts. These often require a different approach than overuse injuries.

Biomechanical Factors

The knee doesn't function in isolation. Problems in the hip or ankle affect the load on the knee. Weak hip muscles, overpronation in the foot and poor knee control are common contributing factors that can be addressed with targeted exercise.

3

What Does Research Say?

Research shows that active treatment with exercise is the cornerstone for most knee and leg pain. Manual therapy can be a useful supplement.

Degenerative Meniscus Injuries

A large meta-analysis from 2024 (9 RCT studies, 1200 patients) found no significant difference between surgery and conservative treatment for pain and function (Elsenosy et al., Cureus 2024)

Knee Osteoarthritis

Exercise is recommended as first-line treatment by all international guidelines. Manual therapy combined with exercise provides short-term improvement in pain and function (Zhu et al., 2024)

Patellofemoral Pain (Runner's Knee)

Strength training of quadriceps and hip muscles is documented effective. Consensus from 2018 recommends exercise-based treatment (Collins et al., BJSM 2018)

ACL Injuries: Up to 50% of patients who start structured rehabilitation never need surgery (KANON study)

4

Treatment at the Chiropractor

First Consultation (about 45 minutes)

At the first consultation you get a thorough examination with:

  • Medical history and pain mapping
  • Physical examination of knee, hip and ankle
  • Assessment of movement patterns and muscle strength
  • Possible referral for imaging if necessary

Treatment is tailored to your condition and may include: joint mobilization, soft tissue treatment, stretching, dry needling, shockwave therapy (for tendon problems), and most importantly - an individual exercise program.

Home Exercises - The Key to Improvement

Exercises you do yourself between treatments are crucial for the result. Typical focus areas:

  • Quadriceps strength: Squats, lunges, step-ups
  • Hip muscles: Gluteus exercises, side-lying leg raises, bridging
  • Balance and proprioception: Single-leg standing, balance board
  • Stretching: Quadriceps, hamstrings, calf muscles

Realistic Expectations

Most knee and leg problems respond well to conservative treatment. Typical treatment course varies from 2-4 weeks for simple overuse injuries to 8-12 weeks for more complex conditions. Some conditions, like osteoarthritis, require long-term self-management. Relapses can occur, and maintenance training is often necessary.

5

Red Flags

Seek Emergency Care For:

  • Suspected fracture: Severe pain after fall/trauma, deformity, unable to bear weight
  • Locked knee: Knee cannot bend or straighten at all
  • Signs of blood clot (DVT): Sudden swelling, warmth and pain in the leg
  • Signs of infection: Fever combined with redness, warmth and swelling in knee/leg
  • Compartment syndrome: Intense, increasing pain that worsens with stretching, numbness
  • Circulation problems: Pale, cold foot with pain
6

Prevention and Self-Care

Gradual Progression

The most important prevention strategy is to avoid sudden increases in load. Increase training volume by maximum 10% per week. When starting a new activity - begin carefully and give the body time to adapt.

Strength Training

Strong muscles protect the joints. Focus on: quadriceps (front of thigh), hamstrings (back of thigh), gluteus (buttock muscles) and core muscles. 2-3 strength sessions per week gives good effect.

POLICE Principle for Acute Injuries

For acute injuries in the first 48-72 hours:

  • Protect - Protect against further injury
  • Optimal Loading - Adapted load (not complete rest)
  • Ice - Ice 15-20 minutes, several times daily
  • Compression - Compression bandage
  • Elevation - Elevation above heart level

Footwear and Equipment

Choose shoes suited to the activity and your foot type. Replace running shoes regularly (500-800 km running distance). If needed, individually fitted insoles can be useful.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need an MRI for knee pain?

Not necessarily. MRI often shows findings that don't explain your pain - 61% of meniscus injuries on MRI are asymptomatic. A thorough clinical examination often provides sufficient information to start treatment. MRI may be relevant if serious injury is suspected or if there's no improvement.

How long until I get better?

This varies significantly. Simple overuse injuries: 2-4 weeks. Runner's knee/IT band: 6-12 weeks. Meniscus problems: 8-12 weeks. Osteoarthritis is a chronic condition requiring long-term self-management. Good compliance with the exercise program is crucial for results.

Can I exercise with knee pain?

Often yes - but adapted. Moderate pain (under 4/10) during activity is acceptable as long as pain doesn't increase the day after. Avoid activities that cause severe pain. Alternative training like cycling or swimming can be good options while rehabilitating.

Do I need surgery for my meniscus injury?

Probably not. For degenerative meniscus injuries (gradual wear), research shows that conservative treatment with exercise gives equally good results as surgery. Surgery may be considered for acute, traumatic injuries in younger patients, or for mechanical locking that doesn't improve with conservative treatment.

Does chiropractic help with knee osteoarthritis?

Chiropractic treatment can contribute as part of a comprehensive treatment approach. Research shows that manual therapy combined with exercise provides short-term improvement in pain and function. However, exercise is the cornerstone - manual treatment alone is not sufficient.

Do I need a referral to see a chiropractor?

No. Chiropractors have primary contact status. You can book an appointment directly without a referral from a doctor. The chiropractor can refer you for MRI, X-ray or specialist if needed.
