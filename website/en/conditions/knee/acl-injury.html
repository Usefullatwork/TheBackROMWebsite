ACL Injury (Anterior Cruciate Ligament)

Not everyone needs surgery - what does the research say?

You've felt a "pop" in the knee during pivoting or landing, followed by swelling and instability. ACL injury is one of the most dramatic forms of knee pain, especially common in soccer, handball, and skiing.

The traditional view has been that all ACL injuries require surgery. But modern research nuances this picture: many can function well without surgery, especially with good rehabilitation and if activity level is adjusted.

This article gives you the research-based facts to help make an informed choice about your treatment.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • "Pop" + swelling = classic sign
  • Not everyone needs surgery
  • 9+ months rehab regardless
  • Re-injury risk: 20-25%
  • Return to sport: 65% at 2 years

What is an ACL Injury?

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) is one of four main ligaments in the knee. It runs diagonally inside the knee joint and prevents the tibia from sliding forward relative to the femur. It's also important for rotational stability.

How Does It Happen?

  • Non-contact injury (70%): Sudden direction change, pivoting, awkward landing
  • Contact injury (30%): Direct blow to the knee
  • Sports: Soccer, handball, basketball, skiing
  • Risk factors: Women 2-8x higher risk, fatigue, poor landing technique

Symptoms

Acute Signs

  • "Pop": Audible or felt snap in the knee
  • Rapid swelling: Within 2-12 hours (hemarthrosis)
  • Instability: Knee "gives way" or feels unstable
  • Pain: Often significant initially, can subside
  • Limited mobility: Difficulty bending/extending fully

Seek Medical Help If:

  • Significant swelling after injury
  • Cannot bear weight on the leg
  • Knee feels unstable or gives way
  • Visible deformity of the knee

Surgery vs. Conservative Treatment

The major question after ACL injury is: Do I need surgery? The answer is more nuanced than many think.

What Does the Research Say?

Studies show comparable outcomes for surgery + rehab vs. rehab alone for many patients. The key is high-quality rehabilitation regardless of surgical choice. About 50% of those starting with rehab avoid surgery long-term.

Who Should Consider Surgery?

  • Elite athletes in pivoting sports
  • Persistent instability despite rehab
  • Combined injuries (meniscus, other ligaments)
  • High activity demands
  • Young athletes planning competitive sports

Who Can Try Conservative Treatment First?

  • Lower activity demands
  • No instability in daily activities
  • Willing to modify sport participation
  • Older patients
  • Good muscle strength and control
4

Rehabilitation

Regardless of whether you have surgery, rehabilitation is the most important factor for good outcomes.

Rehabilitation Phases

  • Phase 1 (0-6 weeks): Reduce swelling, restore range of motion, activate quadriceps
  • Phase 2 (6-12 weeks): Strength building, normal walking
  • Phase 3 (3-6 months): Progressive strength training, light jogging
  • Phase 4 (6-9 months): Sport-specific training
  • Phase 5 (9+ months): Return to sport criteria

Return to Sport Criteria

Time is not enough! You should meet objective criteria before returning:

  • Quadriceps strength at least 90% of healthy leg
  • Passed hop tests (4 different tests)
  • Good neuromuscular control
  • Psychological readiness

Risk Factors for Re-injury

  • Return to sport too early (before 9 months)
  • Young age (under 25)
  • Return to high-risk sports
  • Not meeting strength criteria
  • Poor landing mechanics

FAQ

Can the ACL heal on its own?
A completely torn ACL doesn't heal on its own. But many function well without a ligament through good muscle strength and neuromuscular control. This is called being a "coper."
How long until I can play sports again?
Minimum 9 months, regardless of surgery or not. Many experts recommend 12 months. The decision should be based on objective criteria, not just time.
What about long-term osteoarthritis?
Unfortunately, about 50% develop osteoarthritis within 10-20 years, regardless of treatment. Surgery doesn't prevent this. Maintaining muscle strength and normal body weight is important.
