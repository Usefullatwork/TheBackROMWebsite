IT Band Syndrome

Pain on the outside of the knee - strengthen the hip, not just stretch

You're running along when suddenly there's sharp pain on the outside of your knee. It's typically fine at the start, but after a certain distance - always at about the same point - the pain appears and forces you to stop. This is the classic pattern of IT band syndrome.

The iliotibial band (IT band) is a thick fibrous band that runs along the outside of your thigh from the hip to below the knee. When irritated, it causes one of the most frustrating running injuries. It's one of several types of knee pain common in runners.

The good news? Research shows that hip strengthening is more effective than stretching for this condition.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

What is IT Band Syndrome?

IT band syndrome (ITBS) occurs when the iliotibial band becomes irritated where it crosses the lateral femoral condyle (bony prominence on the outside of the knee). This happens repeatedly with every step during running.

Who is Affected?

  • Runners (second most common running injury after runner's knee)
  • Cyclists
  • Hikers, especially with lots of downhill
  • Athletes doing repeated knee bending activities

Causes - The Hip Connection

Research has clearly shown that hip weakness is a major factor in IT band syndrome:

Fredericson's Classic Study (2000)

Runners with IT band syndrome had 23% weaker hip abductors (women) and 29% weaker (men) compared to healthy controls. After 6 weeks of hip strengthening, 92% returned to pain-free running.

Risk Factors

  • Weak hip abductors: Gluteus medius weakness is key
  • Rapid training increase: Too much, too soon
  • Running on camber: Always the same side of the road
  • Downhill running: Increases IT band compression
  • Worn shoes: Over 500-800 km
  • Knee angle: Maximum irritation at 30 degrees flexion
3

Symptoms

Classic Pattern

  • Location: Sharp pain on the outside of the knee
  • Timing: Starts fine, pain comes after consistent distance
  • Character: Sharp, sometimes burning pain
  • Provocation: Running, stairs (especially down), cycling
  • Relief: Rest stops the pain quickly

The "Reproducible Distance"

A hallmark of IT band syndrome is that pain appears at almost exactly the same distance in each run. This "threshold" distance increases as treatment progresses.

4

Treatment

Modern treatment focuses on hip strengthening rather than just stretching:

Hip Strengthening (Most Important!)

  • Side-lying hip abduction: 3 sets x 15 reps
  • Clamshells: With resistance band
  • Single-leg squat: Focus on knee control
  • Side plank: For lateral core stability
  • Monster walks: With band around ankles

Activity Modification

  • Reduce running volume temporarily
  • Avoid downhill and cambered surfaces
  • Cross-train with swimming or cycling (if pain-free)
  • Gradual return based on symptoms

What About Stretching?

The IT Band Can't Really Be Stretched

The IT band is extremely strong fascia - it would take about 2000 lbs of force to stretch it 1%. Traditional IT band stretches likely work on other structures. Foam rolling may help with temporary pain relief but doesn't address the underlying cause.

5

Return to Running

Progression Plan

Weeks 1-2: Rest from running, hip strengthening daily

Weeks 3-4: Short pain-free runs on flat terrain

Weeks 5-6: Gradual increase in distance (10% per week)

Weeks 7+: Return to normal training

When to Run

  • No pain during daily activities
  • No tenderness on the outside of the knee
  • Start with short, easy runs
  • Stop immediately if pain returns
6

FAQ

Should I foam roll my IT band?
Foam rolling can provide temporary relief but doesn't address the underlying cause (hip weakness). It may be painful and isn't necessary for recovery. If you find it helpful, that's fine, but don't neglect hip strengthening.
How is this different from runner's knee?
IT band syndrome causes pain on the OUTSIDE of the knee, while runner's knee (PFPS) causes pain AROUND or BEHIND the kneecap. Both benefit from hip strengthening, but the pain location is different.
Can I cycle instead of running?
Often yes, if it's pain-free. Adjust saddle height to reduce knee flexion angle. But remember that cycling won't fix the underlying problem - you still need hip strengthening.
