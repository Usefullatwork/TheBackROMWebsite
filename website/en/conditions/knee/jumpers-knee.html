Jumper's Knee (Patellar Tendinopathy)

Heavy Slow Resistance is the gold standard - avoid cortisone injections

Conditions/Knee Pain/Jumper's Knee

You notice pain just below the kneecap when you jump, land, or run. It started gradually and has gotten worse over time. Maybe you've asked your doctor about a cortisone injection for quick relief. This type of knee pain is common among athletes in jumping sports.

Stop! Research shows cortisone can make tendon problems worse in the long run. The good news is that we have effective exercise-based treatments that actually work.

Let's look at what jumper's knee is, and how Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) has become the gold standard for treatment.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Heavy Slow Resistance = gold standard
  • AVOID cortisone injections
  • Watch out for fluoroquinolone antibiotics
  • 3-6 months rehabilitation
  • Tendinopathy, NOT tendinitis

What is Jumper's Knee?

Jumper's knee (patellar tendinopathy) is an overuse injury in the patellar tendon - the tendon that connects the kneecap to the shinbone. The condition is also called "jumper's knee." Unlike runner's knee (PFPS) which affects the joint itself, here it's the tendon that's the problem.

Important terminology

Tendinopathy, NOT tendinitis

Modern research shows that tendon problems rarely involve classic inflammation. Therefore, we use the term "tendinopathy" (tendon disorder) rather than "tendinitis" (tendon inflammation). This has consequences for treatment - anti-inflammatory medication helps little.

Who is affected?

  • Most common in volleyball players (45-50%)
  • Basketball, handball, football
  • Track and field (jumping, sprinting)
  • Men are affected somewhat more often than women
  • Most common between ages 15-30
  • Runners can also be affected, but runner's knee and IT band syndrome are more common

Symptoms

  • Pain just below the kneecap in the patellar tendon
  • Worsens with load: Jumping, landing, stairs, knee bending
  • Morning stiffness that improves with movement
  • "Warm-up effect": Pain that decreases during activity, but returns after
  • Possible swelling: Over the tendon

Phases of tendinopathy

Reactive phase

Acute response to overload. The tendon swells and thickens. Often after sudden training increase.

Degenerative phase

Chronic condition with structural changes in the tendon. Requires more time and patience in rehabilitation.

3

Treatment - Heavy Slow Resistance

Exercise-based treatment is the cornerstone for tendon problems. Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) has proven at least as effective as eccentric training.

Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) protocol

Heavy strength exercises with slow tempo:

  • Tempo: 3 seconds up, 3 seconds down
  • Load: 70-85% of maximum strength
  • Repetitions: 6-15 RM
  • Frequency: 3-4 times per week
  • Duration: 12 weeks minimum

Typical exercises

  • Squats: With slow controlled tempo
  • Leg extension: In machine with slow phase
  • Decline squat: On inclined board (25 degrees) for increased tendon load
  • Lunges: With controlled tempo

Pain during training?

Moderate pain (below 5/10) is acceptable during training. Pain should not increase over days/weeks. If pain increases, reduce load temporarily.

4

What You Should AVOID

Cortisone injections

  • Can provide short-term pain relief (weeks)
  • Research shows cortisone can weaken the tendon
  • Increases risk of tendon rupture long-term
  • Often leads to recurrence of symptoms
  • Strongly advised against for tendon problems in guidelines

If your doctor suggests a cortisone injection for tendon problems, ask about alternatives!

Fluoroquinolone antibiotics

Antibiotics like Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, and similar (fluoroquinolones) are associated with increased risk of tendon damage and rupture. If you have tendon problems and need antibiotics, discuss alternatives with your doctor.

Other things to avoid

  • Complete rest: The tendon needs load to adapt
  • Too much stretching: Can irritate the tendon further
  • Too fast progression: Gradual increase is key
5

Prognosis

Tendon problems require patience. Expect 3-6 months of rehabilitation for good effect.

  • 3-6 months: Typical time for significant improvement
  • 12 weeks: Minimum duration of exercise program
  • Gradual return: To sport over 4-8 weeks

Factors affecting prognosis

  • Duration of symptoms (shorter = better)
  • Adherence to exercise program
  • Degree of degenerative changes
  • Ability to adjust training load
6

FAQ

What is Heavy Slow Resistance?
HSR is an exercise program where you perform heavy strength exercises with slow tempo (3 seconds up, 3 seconds down). Research shows this is equally effective or better than eccentric training.
Why should I avoid cortisone?
Cortisone can provide short-term pain relief, but research shows it can weaken the tendon and increase the risk of rupture long-term. Exercise-based treatment is recommended.
What about NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Voltaren)?
NSAIDs can be used for short-term pain relief, but shouldn't be the first choice. They don't affect the underlying tendon condition. Exercise is more important.
What about shockwave therapy?
Shockwave can be a useful supplement to exercise for some patients. It shouldn't replace the exercise program, but can be combined.
Can I continue playing?
Often yes, but with modifications. Load should be kept at a level where pain doesn't worsen over time. Many continue sports parallel with rehabilitation.
