Knee Osteoarthritis

Exercise is the best medicine - evidence-based treatment for osteoarthritis

You notice that your knee is stiff in the morning, and it hurts when you've been sitting for a while. The pain gets better when you move, but can worsen after prolonged activity. Welcome to the reality of knee osteoarthritis - a condition affecting millions of people worldwide.

The good news? Research clearly shows that exercise is the most effective treatment - often as good as or better than medication and injections. And it's never too late to start.

Knee osteoarthritis is one of many types of knee pain that responds well to conservative treatment.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Exercise = best treatment
  • Morning stiffness less than 30 min
  • Images don't correlate with symptoms
  • Avoid surgery as long as possible
  • Chronic condition - can be managed well

What is Knee Osteoarthritis?

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a condition where the cartilage in the joint gradually breaks down. In the knee, this can affect the medial (inside), lateral (outside), or patellofemoral (kneecap) compartment.

Important to Understand

Knee joint anatomy illustration showing cartilage breakdown Image: Knee Osteoarthritis Anatomy

Images don't tell the whole story

There's often poor correlation between what X-rays show and how much pain you have. Many people with significant changes on X-ray have little pain, while others with minimal changes have a lot of pain. The treatment is the same regardless - exercise!

Who is Affected?

  • Increases with age - most common after 50
  • More common in women
  • Overweight is an important risk factor
  • Previous injuries (meniscus, ACL) increase risk
  • Heredity plays a role

Symptoms

Typical Signs

  • Pain: Deep, aching pain in the knee
  • Morning stiffness: Usually less than 30 minutes (longer suggests rheumatoid arthritis)
  • Start-up pain: Pain when starting to move after rest
  • Load-related pain: Worsens with activity, improves with rest
  • Crepitus: Grinding sounds from the knee
  • Occasional swelling: Especially after overexertion

Typical Pain Pattern

Morning: Stiff for 15-30 minutes

During day: Better with movement

Evening: Can worsen after much activity

Night: Usually not pain that wakes you (if so, consider other causes)

Treatment - Exercise is Key

International guidelines are clear: Exercise is the cornerstone of treatment for knee osteoarthritis.

First-Line Treatment (Should always be tried first)

  • Exercise: Both strength and cardiovascular - at least 3x per week
  • Weight loss: If overweight - even 5% can make a big difference
  • Education: Understanding the condition reduces fear

Effective Exercise Types

  • Strength training: Quadriceps, hamstrings, hip muscles
  • Low-impact cardio: Swimming, cycling, walking
  • Balance training: Especially important for fall prevention
  • Stretching: Maintains mobility

Does exercise damage the cartilage?

No! Research shows that moderate exercise is safe and actually helps maintain the cartilage. The cartilage needs loading to stay healthy - it's like a sponge that needs to be squeezed to get nutrients.

Second-Line Treatment

  • Paracetamol: Can be tried, limited effect
  • NSAIDs: Short-term use if needed (ibuprofen, naproxen)
  • Walking aids: Can reduce load
  • Braces: Can help some

What About Injections?

  • Cortisone: Short-term effect (weeks), repeated use can damage cartilage
  • Hyaluronic acid: Mixed evidence, moderate effect at best
  • PRP: Limited evidence, expensive

When is Surgery Needed?

Knee replacement surgery is effective but should be reserved for those who:

  • Have tried exercise and other conservative measures for at least 3-6 months
  • Have significant pain that limits daily function
  • Have severe osteoarthritis on X-ray
  • Are willing to undergo rehabilitation after surgery

Arthroscopy for osteoarthritis?

Research is clear: Arthroscopic "cleaning" of an osteoarthritic knee provides no benefit beyond placebo. This procedure is not recommended for osteoarthritis and will not help with your symptoms.

FAQ

Can I run with knee osteoarthritis?
It depends on symptom level. Many can continue running with modifications. Research shows that running does not accelerate osteoarthritis in the same way as previously believed. Start gradually and listen to your body.
Should I get an X-ray?
Not necessarily. The diagnosis can often be made clinically. X-rays don't change the treatment (exercise!) and the findings often don't correlate with symptoms. Can be relevant if surgery is being considered.
Can osteoarthritis be reversed?
Cartilage damage is largely irreversible, but symptoms can be significantly improved with exercise and lifestyle changes. Many experience significant improvement despite what images show.
What about supplements like glucosamine?
Research shows little to no effect of glucosamine and chondroitin. If it helps you, there's no harm in using it, but don't expect miracles. Exercise has better evidence.
