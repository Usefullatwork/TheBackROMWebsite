Knee Pain from the Hip - The Biomechanical Chain

When the hip deceives you and creates knee problems

Your knee hurts, but the problem is actually in the hip. This may sound strange, but it's actually quite common. The body functions as a chain where joints affect each other - what we call the kinetic chain. Knee pain can therefore have many different causes.

When the hip muscles are weak - especially the gluteus medius (side buttock muscle) and external rotators - you lose control of the femur during loading. The result is that the knee collapses inward, what experts call "dynamic knee valgus." This creates unnecessary stress on structures inside and around the knee.

Research from 2004 showed that 33% of patients with knee pain also had hip problems, and over half of those with hip pain also reported knee symptoms. Among patients with patellofemoral pain syndrome - the most common form of anterior knee pain - 21-36% weaker hip muscles are typically found compared to healthy individuals.

  • Most common: PFPS > ACL > IT band syndrome

How Does the Hip Affect the Knee?

The mechanism is well documented. When you stand on one leg - for example during running, stair climbing, or squats - the hip abductors (especially gluteus medius) should keep the pelvis stable. If these muscles are weak, the pelvis tips down on the opposite side, and the femur rotates inward and adducts (moves toward the midline).

This causes the knee to move medially relative to the foot - i.e., inward. The consequence is increased stress on several structures:

Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)

Valgus loading increases ACL strain by up to 30%. Combined with quadriceps force, ACL forces can double.

Patellofemoral Joint

A 10-degree increase in Q-angle (affected by hip position) increases joint pressure by 45%. Femoral internal rotation of 15 degrees gives over 10% increase in lateral contact pressure.

IT Band

Hip adduction during running is strongly associated with IT band syndrome.

Which Knee Conditions Can Be Caused by the Hip?

Ranked by evidence strength, from strongest to weakest:

1. Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Anterior Knee Pain)

Strong evidence

The most common form of knee pain, with an annual prevalence of 22.7% in the population and 28.9% among youth. Patients consistently show 21-27% weaker hip abduction and up to 36% weaker external rotation compared to healthy individuals. Read more about runner's knee (PFPS).

2. ACL Injuries and Rehabilitation

Moderate to strong

A prospective study of 501 competitive athletes showed that those with hip weakness had more than twice the probability of ACL rupture. Read more about ACL injury.

3. Iliotibial Band Syndrome

Moderate evidence

Fredericson et al. found that female runners with IT band syndrome had 23% weaker hip abductors, while men had 29% weakness. After 6 weeks of hip strength training, 92% (22 of 24) were able to return to pain-free running.

Symptoms - When Should You Think About the Hip?

  • Knee pain that doesn't improve with knee-specific treatment over time
  • Function loss that seems disproportionately large compared to knee findings
  • Limited hip mobility or stiffness on examination
  • Normal MRI images of the knee despite persistent problems
  • Pain that worsens with hip movements or prolonged sitting
  • Visible "knee collapse" inward during single-leg activities

Red Flags - Contact a Doctor If:

  • Trauma or fall with acute pain/swelling
  • Locked knee (cannot bend/extend)
  • Swelling with fever (suspected infection)
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Unexplained weight loss

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Chiropractic Treatment Includes

  • Manual treatment: Mobilization and soft tissue treatment of hip, pelvis, and knee to improve joint mobility and reduce muscle tension.
  • Targeted exercise program: Strength training for hip abductors (gluteus medius) and external rotators, as well as core stability.
  • Movement correction: Training in correct technique for squats, stair climbing, and running to reduce dynamic knee valgus.

Strong Evidence for Combined Approach

A meta-analysis of 14 randomized studies with 673 participants showed that combined hip and knee training gave significantly better results than knee training alone: 1.5 points more pain relief on a 0-10 scale and effect size of 0.7 for function improvement.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Effective Home Exercises for the Hip

  • Side-lying hip abduction: Lie on your side, lift top leg controlled upward. 3 sets x 15 reps.
  • Clamshells: Lie on your side with bent knees, open knees like a shell while feet stay together. 3 sets x 15 reps.
  • Glute bridge: Lying on back with bent knees, lift pelvis. Progress to single-leg variant. 3 sets x 12 reps.
  • Monster walks: Walk sideways with band around ankles or knees. 3 sets x 10 steps each way.

FAQ

How do I know if my knee pain is coming from the hip?
Typical signs include: knee pain that doesn't improve with standard knee treatment, normal MRI images of the knee, visible "knee collapse" inward during loading, and possibly stiffness or reduced mobility in the hip.
Do I need to stop exercising?
No, quite the opposite. Active treatment with strength training for the hip is the most effective approach. However, modify activities that provoke pain and focus on exercises that strengthen the hip abductors and external rotators.
How long does it take to get better?
With hip-focused training, you can expect significant improvement after just 4 weeks. Full recovery for moderate to severe cases can take 6-12 months.
Is this relevant for me as a runner?
Absolutely. IT band syndrome and patellofemoral pain syndrome are among the most common running injuries, and both are strongly associated with hip weakness and reduced dynamic knee control. Hip strengthening should be part of any running program.
