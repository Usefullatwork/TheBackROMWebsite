Meniscus Injury

Not everyone needs surgery - what the research shows

You've felt a "click" or pain in the knee, maybe after a twist or squat. Your doctor has taken an MRI and found a meniscus tear. The next step is surgery, right? Not necessarily.

Modern research has revolutionized our understanding of meniscus injuries. Many tears are normal and don't cause symptoms. And for degenerative tears (which most are), exercise therapy often works just as well as surgery. This is one of many types of knee pain.

This article gives you the research-based facts about when surgery is needed - and when it's not.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 60% of 50-year-olds have meniscus tears on MRI
  • Exercise = surgery for degenerative tears
  • Avoid arthroscopy for osteoarthritis
  • Acute traumatic tears may need surgery
  • 3-6 months conservative treatment first

What is the Meniscus?

The knee has two menisci - C-shaped cartilage discs that act as shock absorbers between the thighbone and shinbone. The medial (inner) meniscus is most frequently injured.

Types of Tears

  • Degenerative tears: Gradual wear, common with age, often horizontal
  • Traumatic tears: Acute injury, often in younger people, typically vertical or bucket-handle

Important Finding

Studies show that 60% of people over 50 have meniscus tears on MRI - without any symptoms! This means an MRI finding doesn't necessarily explain your pain.

Symptoms

Typical Signs

  • Pain: Along the joint line, medial or lateral
  • Swelling: Gradual (degenerative) or rapid (traumatic)
  • Locking: Knee "locks" and can't fully extend (bucket-handle tear)
  • Clicking/snapping: Clicking sounds during movement
  • Instability: Feeling of knee giving way

When Surgery May Be Needed

  • True locking: Knee mechanically stuck (can't extend fully)
  • Acute traumatic tear in young patient
  • Bucket-handle tear with displacement
  • Combined ligament injury (ACL + meniscus)
3

Treatment - Surgery vs. Exercise

Research has fundamentally changed practice for meniscus injuries:

For Degenerative Tears (most common)

  • First-line: Exercise therapy for 3-6 months
  • Why: Studies show no difference in outcome compared to surgery
  • Additional: Weight loss if relevant, pain management

For Acute Traumatic Tears

  • Repair: May be possible if the tear is in the vascular zone
  • Partial removal: Last resort if repair fails
  • Young patient: Higher threshold for surgery

Warning About Arthroscopy

Arthroscopic "cleaning" for degenerative tears provides no better result than exercise, but carries surgical risks and accelerates cartilage loss. International guidelines recommend avoiding this for degenerative tears.

Exercise Therapy

An effective program includes:

  • Quadriceps strengthening (most important)
  • Hamstring and hip strengthening
  • Balance and proprioception training
  • Gradual return to activity
4

Prognosis

With Conservative Treatment

  • About 75% are satisfied without surgery
  • Most see improvement within 6-12 weeks
  • Some may need surgery later (about 25-30%)

Long-term

  • Meniscus surgery increases risk of osteoarthritis
  • The more meniscus removed, the higher the risk
  • Exercise therapy may be more protective long-term
5

FAQ

Can a meniscus tear heal on its own?
Some tears in the outer vascular zone can heal. But many degenerative tears stabilize and become asymptomatic even without healing - the tear remains but doesn't cause problems.
My MRI shows a tear - shouldn't I have surgery?
Not necessarily! MRI findings don't always correlate with symptoms. Many people have tears without knowing it. Treatment should be based on symptoms and function, not just MRI.
Can I continue exercising?
Yes, often! Avoid deep squats and twisting initially. Low-impact activities like cycling and swimming are safe. Strengthen the muscles around the knee.
