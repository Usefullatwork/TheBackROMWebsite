PCL Injury (Posterior Cruciate Ligament)

The often overlooked knee ligament

The posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) is the strongest ligament in the knee, yet it's much less talked about than the ACL. PCL injuries are less common but can occur with direct trauma to the shin or a fall on a bent knee.

The good news? Most isolated PCL injuries can be treated without surgery. The knee often adapts well, and many return to high activity levels with rehabilitation. PCL injury is one of the less common causes of knee pain.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Common in car accidents and sports
  • Most treated conservatively
  • Quadriceps strength = key
  • Combined injuries may need surgery
  • 6-12 months rehabilitation

What is a PCL Injury?

The PCL prevents the tibia from sliding backward relative to the femur. It's about 30-40% stronger than the ACL, which is why it takes more force to injure.

How Does It Happen?

  • Dashboard injury: Classic mechanism - knee hits dashboard in car accident
  • Fall on bent knee: With the foot pointed down
  • Sports contact: Direct blow to the front of the shin
  • Hyperflexion: Extreme bending of the knee

Classification

  • Grade I: Partial tear, less than 5mm posterior translation
  • Grade II: Complete isolated tear, 5-10mm translation
  • Grade III: More than 10mm translation, often combined injuries
2

Symptoms

  • Pain: Deep in the knee, behind the kneecap
  • Swelling: Often less dramatic than ACL injuries
  • Instability: Feeling of knee "sagging" backward
  • Stairs: Discomfort descending stairs
  • Kneeling: Pain when kneeling on the affected knee

Often Overlooked

PCL injuries can be subtle and are sometimes missed in the acute phase. If you've had trauma to the shin and have persistent knee problems, a PCL injury should be considered.

3

Treatment

Treatment depends on the severity and whether other structures are involved:

Conservative Treatment (Grade I-II isolated)

  • Acute phase: RICE, possible brace in extension
  • Early rehab: Quadriceps activation is critical
  • Progressive strength: Focus on quadriceps and hamstrings
  • Return to activity: Based on strength and function criteria

Surgical Indications

  • Combined injuries (posterolateral corner, multiple ligaments)
  • Avulsion fracture with displaced bone fragment
  • High-level athletes with persistent instability
  • Grade III injuries with functional limitations
4

Prognosis

  • Isolated Grade I-II: Excellent prognosis with conservative treatment
  • Return to sport: Most return to previous activity level
  • Long-term: Some develop patellofemoral symptoms or osteoarthritis over time
  • Quadriceps strength: The most important factor for good function
5

FAQ

Do I need surgery for a PCL injury?
Most isolated PCL injuries don't require surgery. Grade I and II injuries typically heal well with rehabilitation. Surgery is considered for combined injuries, avulsion fractures, or persistent instability despite rehab.
Can I return to sports?
Yes, most can return to their previous activity level. The key is regaining quadriceps strength and neuromuscular control. Timeline is typically 6-12 months depending on severity.
Why is quadriceps strength so important?
The quadriceps pulls the tibia forward, counteracting the backward sag that occurs with a PCL-deficient knee. Strong quadriceps can functionally compensate for a PCL injury.
