Pes Anserine Bursitis

Pain on the inside of the knee - the often overlooked diagnosis

Do you have pain on the inside of your knee, just below the joint line? The pain may worsen with stairs, getting up from a chair, or when lying on your side at night. This could be pes anserine bursitis - one of several causes of knee pain.

Pes anserine (Latin for "goose foot") is the name for the area where three tendons meet and attach to the shinbone. A bursa (fluid-filled sac) lies between the tendons and the bone, and when this becomes irritated, you get bursitis.

The condition responds usually well to conservative treatment with stretching, strengthening, and sometimes ice and anti-inflammatory measures.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Pain on the inside of the knee
  • Common with osteoarthritis and diabetes
  • Runners and swimmers at risk
  • Stretching + strength = main treatment
  • Usually improves within 4-6 weeks

What is Pes Anserine Bursitis?

The pes anserine bursa lies about 5 cm below the knee joint on the inside of the tibia. It acts as a cushion between the three tendons (sartorius, gracilis, semitendinosus) and the bone.

Who is Affected?

  • People with knee osteoarthritis (up to 75%)
  • Overweight individuals
  • Those with diabetes
  • Runners, especially with increased training
  • Swimmers (breaststroke kick)
  • Women more often than men

Symptoms

Typical Signs

  • Location: Tender on the inside of the knee, 5 cm below the joint line
  • Character: Aching, sometimes burning pain
  • Provocation: Stairs (especially up), getting out of a chair, kneeling
  • Night pain: Can wake up when lying on the side (knees touching)
  • Swelling: Sometimes visible/palpable swelling

How does it differ from medial meniscus injury?

The pain from pes anserine bursitis is below the joint line, while meniscus pain is typically at the joint line. Bursitis also doesn't give locking or clicking like a meniscus injury can.

Treatment

Treatment is almost always conservative with good results:

First-Line Treatment

  • Ice: 15-20 minutes several times daily in acute phase
  • Relative rest: Reduce provocative activities
  • Stretching: Hamstrings, hip adductors, hip flexors
  • Strengthening: Quadriceps and hip muscles

Additional Measures

  • NSAIDs: Short-term use can help with acute inflammation
  • Night pillow: Place a pillow between the knees
  • Weight loss: If relevant
  • Cortisone injection: Can be considered if conservative treatment fails

Manual Treatment

Chiropractic can include:

  • Soft tissue treatment of the involved muscles
  • Mobilization of knee, hip, and ankle
  • Dry needling of trigger points
  • Guidance on exercise and stretching

FAQ

How long does recovery take?
Most experience significant improvement within 4-6 weeks with consistent treatment. Chronic cases can take longer. Addressing underlying factors (osteoarthritis, overweight) is important for lasting results.
Should I get a cortisone injection?
Cortisone can provide temporary relief, but shouldn't be first-line treatment. Studies show that physical therapy provides equally good results with lasting effect. Injection can be considered if conservative treatment fails.
Can I continue exercising?
Yes, but modify activities. Reduce provocative movements (deep knee bends, breaststroke) and focus on low-impact alternatives like cycling or swimming (front crawl). Continue strengthening exercises.
