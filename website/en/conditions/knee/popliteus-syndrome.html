Popliteus Syndrome

Pain behind the knee - the runner's hidden problem

Do you have pain behind and on the outside of your knee that worsens when running downhill? It could be popliteus syndrome - a condition often overlooked or confused with other knee problems.

The popliteus is a small but important muscle located deep behind the knee. It plays a key role in "unlocking" the knee from an extended position and controlling rotation - especially important when walking or running downhill.

The condition usually responds well to conservative treatment with strength training, stretching, and gradual return to activity.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Pain behind/lateral in the knee
  • Worsens with downhill running
  • Common in runners and soccer players
  • Strength + stretching = main treatment
  • Improvement typically within 4-8 weeks

What is Popliteus Syndrome?

Popliteus syndrome is irritation, overuse, or inflammation of the popliteus muscle or its tendon. The condition is also called popliteus tendinopathy or popliteal tendinitis.

Anatomy

The popliteus is a triangular muscle located deep in the popliteal fossa (back of the knee). It has a complex course:

  • Origin: From the lateral femoral condyle and lateral meniscus
  • Insertion: On the posterior-medial tibia
  • Tendon: Runs inside the knee joint, outside the joint capsule

Functions of the Popliteus

  • Unlocks the knee: Initiates knee flexion from full extension by rotating the tibia inward
  • Controls rotation: Prevents excessive external rotation of the tibia on the femur
  • Stabilizes the knee: Important for posterolateral stability
  • Pulls the meniscus: Draws the lateral meniscus backward during knee flexion
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Popliteus syndrome usually develops gradually as a result of overuse:

Downhill Running

Most common cause

During downhill running, the popliteus must work hard to control knee flexion and prevent hyperextension. Trail running and hiking are typical triggers.

Overload

Too rapid increase in training volume, intensity, or change in surface can overload the popliteus before it has time to adapt.

Biomechanical Factors

  • Overpronation
  • Weak hip muscles
  • Tight hamstrings or calves
  • Poor knee control
3

Symptoms

Main Symptoms

  • Localized pain: Behind and on the outside of the knee, in the popliteal fossa
  • Worsens downhill: Pain increases significantly with downhill and stairs
  • Pain on startup: Stiffness and pain when you start moving
  • Improves with warm-up: May get better after a few minutes, but worsens again

When Should You See a Doctor?

  • Acute swelling: Rapid swelling after injury
  • Instability: Knee gives way or feels unstable
  • Locked knee: Cannot extend or bend the knee
  • Calf swelling: May indicate blood clot (DVT)
4

Treatment

Popliteus syndrome is almost always treated conservatively with good results:

Acute Phase (weeks 0-2)

Relative Rest

  • Reduce or avoid activities that provoke pain
  • Especially important to avoid downhill and stairs
  • Replace running with cycling or swimming

Rehabilitation Phase (weeks 2-8)

Stretching

  • Hamstrings: Hold 30-60 seconds, 3-4 times daily
  • Calf muscles: Both gastrocnemius and soleus
  • IT band: Lateral stretching

Strength Training

  • Hip abductors: Gluteus medius strength improves knee control
  • Eccentric knee flexion: Controlled hamstring work
  • Single-leg balance: Proprioception and control

Specific Measures

  • Gradual progression: Don't increase volume too quickly, especially downhill
  • Strengthen hip muscles: Gluteus medius is key for knee control
  • Vary terrain: Don't always run on the same sloped surface
  • Proper shoes: Replace running shoes regularly

FAQ

What is popliteus syndrome?
Popliteus syndrome is irritation or inflammation of the popliteus muscle or its tendon. The popliteus is a small muscle behind the knee that plays an important role in knee rotation and stability, especially during downhill running.
How does popliteus syndrome differ from IT band syndrome?
Both cause pain on the outside of the knee, but popliteus pain is typically further back and down, while IT band pain is more directly over the lateral femoral condyle. Popliteus worsens especially with downhill, while IT band often worsens with all types of running.
How long does popliteus syndrome take to heal?
With proper treatment, most see significant improvement within 4-6 weeks. Full return to running can take 6-12 weeks depending on severity. Gradual progression and strength training are key for lasting improvement.
