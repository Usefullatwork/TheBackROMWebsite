Prepatellar Bursitis

Housemaid's knee - swelling at the front of the knee

You notice a soft, egg-shaped swelling directly over the kneecap. It may be tender to touch and worse when kneeling. This is prepatellar bursitis - also known as "housemaid's knee" or "carpet layer's knee."

The condition occurs when the bursa (fluid-filled sac) in front of the kneecap becomes irritated, usually from repeated kneeling or direct trauma. It's one of several types of knee pain.

Most cases resolve with conservative treatment - avoiding kneeling, ice, and sometimes compression. Surgery is rarely needed.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Swelling over the kneecap
  • Caused by kneeling or trauma
  • Avoid kneeling = most important
  • Use knee pads for prevention
  • Usually resolves in 2-4 weeks

What is Prepatellar Bursitis?

The prepatellar bursa is a small fluid-filled sac that lies between the kneecap and the skin. Its job is to reduce friction when you bend and straighten your knee.

Causes

  • Repeated kneeling: Carpenters, cleaners, gardeners, plumbers
  • Direct trauma: Fall onto the knee, blow to the kneecap
  • Friction: Repeated pressure on the kneecap
  • Infection: Can occur if bacteria enter through a wound (septic bursitis)

At-Risk Groups

  • Tradespeople who kneel frequently
  • Athletes (wrestling, volleyball, football)
  • Gardeners and cleaners
  • Those with gout or rheumatoid arthritis
2

Symptoms

Typical Signs

  • Swelling: Soft, fluctuant swelling directly over the kneecap
  • Pain: Tender to touch, worse when kneeling
  • Warmth: May feel warm (especially if infected)
  • Limited movement: Discomfort with full knee bending

Signs of Infection (Seek Medical Care)

  • Significant redness spreading from the knee
  • Fever or feeling unwell
  • Rapid increase in swelling
  • Break in the skin over the swelling
  • Severe pain out of proportion to the swelling
3

Treatment

Conservative Treatment (Most Cases)

  • Avoid kneeling: The most important measure
  • Ice: 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Compression: Elastic bandage can help reduce swelling
  • NSAIDs: For pain and inflammation if needed
  • Aspiration: Draining the fluid can provide relief (done by doctor)

Prevention

  • Knee pads: Use when kneeling is unavoidable
  • Cushioned surface: Kneel on a pad or cushion
  • Frequent breaks: Change position regularly
  • Alternative positions: Squat instead of kneel when possible

When Surgery May Be Needed

  • Chronic bursitis not responding to conservative treatment
  • Recurrent episodes
  • Septic bursitis requiring drainage
4

FAQ

How long does it take to heal?
Most cases resolve within 2-4 weeks with proper treatment (avoiding kneeling). Chronic cases may take longer. If infected, antibiotic treatment is needed and recovery depends on severity.
Should I have the fluid drained?
Aspiration (draining) can provide relief and is sometimes done to rule out infection or gout. However, it's not always necessary - many cases resolve with conservative measures alone.
Can I continue working?
If your work requires kneeling, you may need to modify your activities or use protective knee pads. Complete avoidance of kneeling speeds recovery.
