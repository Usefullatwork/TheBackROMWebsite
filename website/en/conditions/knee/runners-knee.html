Runner's Knee (PFPS) - How to Return to Full Distance

The hip paradox and cadence are the keys to recovery

You feel it after 3 kilometers. An aching pain at the front of your knee, around or behind the kneecap. It started gradually, but now it comes earlier and earlier in each running session. Stairs have become a nuisance, and prolonged sitting gives you stiff, sore knees.

This is runner's knee - the most common running injury among all types of knee pain. 22.7% of all runners are affected at some point. The good news? With the right approach, most can return to full running activity.

Let's look at what the research says about causes, and why the solution often lies in the hip - not the knee.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 22.7% of runners affected
  • Hip weakness is often the cause
  • Increase cadence by 5-10%
  • Improvement typically within 6-12 weeks
  • 40% have some symptoms after 1 year

What is Runner's Knee (PFPS)?

Runner's knee, or patellofemoral pain syndrome (PFPS), describes pain around or behind the kneecap (patella). It's an overuse injury where the joint between the kneecap and thighbone becomes irritated. Unlike jumper's knee which affects the patellar tendon, runner's knee pain is in the joint itself.

Who is affected?

  • The most common knee injury in runners (22.7%)
  • Women are affected somewhat more often than men
  • Most common in the 18-35 age group
  • Often with rapid training increases or changed terrain
22.7% of runners affected

The Hip Paradox

Here's what surprises many: The cause of knee pain often lies in the hip, not the knee itself.

Dynamic knee valgus

When the hip abductors (gluteus medius and minimus) are weak, they can't control the thighbone during running. The result is that the knee "collapses inward" with each step - called dynamic knee valgus. This overloads the patellofemoral joint and causes pain.

The biomechanical chain

The body functions as a chain. Weakness or tightness in one place affects the joints above and below:

  • Weak hip abduction: Knee collapses inward
  • Overpronation in the foot: Tibial rotation pulls the kneecap
  • Tight IT-band: Pulls the kneecap laterally
  • Weak quadriceps: Reduced knee control

Why hip training helps

Key insight

By strengthening the hip abductors, control over the thighbone improves. The knee tracks better, load is distributed more evenly, and pain decreases - even without ever touching the knee itself.

3

Symptoms

Typical characteristics

  • Location: Pain around, behind, or under the kneecap
  • Character: Aching, dull pain (rarely sharp)
  • Provocation: Stairs (especially down), knee bending, prolonged sitting
  • Onset: Gradual increase, not an acute event
  • Crepitus: Grinding sounds from the knee when bending

"Movie theater syndrome"

A classic sign of PFPS is pain after sitting for a long time with bent knees - e.g., at the movies or on a plane. The pain improves when you extend your leg or stand up.

When should you see a doctor?

  • Sudden swelling after trauma (may indicate meniscus injury or ACL injury)
  • Knee "gives way" or feels unstable
  • Locking - knee cannot be bent/extended (possible meniscus issue)
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Visible misalignment of the kneecap
4

Treatment

Treatment of runner's knee focuses on addressing the underlying causes, not just the symptoms:

1. Hip strengthening

Key exercises

  • Side-lying hip abduction: Focus on gluteus medius
  • Clamshells: With resistance band for increased load
  • Single-leg bridging: Functional hip activation
  • Step-downs: Eccentric control

2. Quadriceps strengthening

Strengthening the quadriceps is important, but start in pain-free ranges and progress gradually to more knee flexion.

3. Running form adjustments

Increase cadence by 5-10%

One of the most effective single interventions! Higher cadence gives:

  • Shorter stride length
  • Less vertical force per step
  • Reduced load on the patellofemoral joint

Practical tip: Use a metronome app or run to music with the right BPM. Measure your current cadence first, then increase gradually.

4. Load management

Pain scale 0-10

During running: Keep pain below 3/10

After running: Pain should return to baseline within 24 hours

If not - train a bit less next time

5. Manual treatment

Chiropractic care may include

  • Mobilization of patella and knee joint
  • Soft tissue techniques for tight muscles
  • Treatment of hip and ankle
  • Kinesio taping to support the kneecap

Running with Runner's Knee

Many wonder if they have to stop running. The answer is usually no - but you need to adjust:

Can I continue running?

Yes, often - but with modifications. The key principle is to find the "dose" of running the knee can tolerate, and build from there.

Practical tips

  • Reduce distance: Start at 50-70% of what causes pain
  • Flat terrain: Avoid hills temporarily
  • Softer surfaces: Gravel paths can be kinder than asphalt
  • Intervals: Run-walk-run can be useful initially
  • No two hard days in a row: Give the knee time to recover

Progression plan

  • Weeks 1-2: Find pain-free distance
  • Weeks 3-4: Increase distance by 10% per week
  • Weeks 5-6: Introduce light hill training
  • Weeks 7-8: Gradual increase toward normal training
6

Prognosis - An Honest Picture

Most people with runner's knee get better, but it takes time:

6-12 weeks to significant improvement
40% have some symptoms after 1 year
80%+ return to running

Important to be realistic

Research shows that 40% still have some symptoms after 1 year. This doesn't mean they can't run - but that complete symptom resolution can take time. Patience and consistent training are key.

Factors for better prognosis

  • Shorter duration of symptoms before treatment starts
  • Good adherence to exercise program
  • Addressing hip weakness
  • Gradual training progression
7

Prevention

If you've had runner's knee once, the risk of recurrence is higher. Prevention is therefore important:

The 10% rule

Never increase weekly training volume by more than 10% at a time. This applies to both distance and intensity.

Maintenance training

Weekly strength program

  • 2-3 times per week with hip exercises
  • Maintain hip strength throughout the running season
  • Include single-leg exercises
  • Don't drop strength training when things are going well!

Variety

  • Vary surfaces (asphalt, gravel, forest)
  • Include easy days in your program
  • Cross-training (cycling, swimming) is beneficial

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the hip paradox?
The hip paradox describes how weak hip muscles can cause knee pain. When the hip abductors are weak, the knee collapses inward with each step, overloading the patellofemoral joint. The solution is to strengthen the hip.
Can I continue running?
Often yes, but with adjustments. Reduce distance and intensity to a level where pain stays below 3/10. Many benefit from increasing cadence by 5-10%.
How long is the rehabilitation time?
Most see improvement within 6-12 weeks with proper treatment. 40% have some symptoms after 1 year, but this doesn't mean they can't run - just that complete symptom resolution can take time.
Do orthotics help?
Research is mixed. Some benefit, especially with overpronation, but they're not first-line treatment. Hip strengthening and running form adjustments have better documented effectiveness.
Should I change shoes?
Shoes should be comfortable, but there's no "best" shoe for runner's knee. If shoes are worn out (over 600-800 km), it's wise to replace them. Avoid drastic changes in cushioning/drop.
What about knee braces/patella tape?
Can provide short-term symptom relief for some, but doesn't address the underlying causes. Use as a supplement if helpful, but not as a replacement for exercise.
