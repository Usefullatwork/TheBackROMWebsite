Shin Splints (MTSS)

Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome - the runner's classic enemy

You've started running more, and now there's a nagging pain along the inside of your shin. It's worst at the start of a run, may improve during, but returns afterward. This is shin splints - or more correctly, medial tibial stress syndrome (MTSS).

Shin splints is one of the most common running injuries, affecting up to 35% of runners at some point. While often associated with knee pain, the problem is actually in the lower leg.

The good news? Most cases resolve with relative rest and gradual progression. But it's important to distinguish from stress fracture, which requires different management.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

What is Shin Splints?

Shin splints (MTSS) is a bone stress injury affecting the inside edge of the tibia (shinbone). It represents the bone's reaction to repetitive loading - not quite a stress fracture, but on the same spectrum.

Who is Affected?

  • Runners (especially new runners or rapid training increase)
  • Military recruits
  • Dancers
  • Athletes in high-impact sports

Symptoms

Typical Pattern

  • Location: Diffuse tenderness along the inside of the shin (lower 1/3)
  • Character: Aching, dull pain that may be sharp with activity
  • Timing: Worse at start of run, may improve, returns after
  • Tenderness: Over a length of bone (typically 5+ cm)

When to Suspect Stress Fracture

  • Point tenderness: Pain at ONE specific spot (not diffuse)
  • Night pain: Pain that wakes you
  • Pain at rest: Hurts even when not active
  • Swelling: Visible swelling over a focal area
  • Progressive worsening: Despite rest

If you have these signs, seek medical evaluation before continuing activity.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

  • Training errors: Too much, too soon (most common)
  • Hard surfaces: Concrete, asphalt
  • Worn shoes: Over 500-800 km
  • Flat feet: Overpronation may contribute
  • Weak calf muscles: Reduced shock absorption
  • Female sex: Higher risk, especially with low energy availability
  • Previous shin splints: History is a risk factor
4

Treatment

Acute Phase

  • Relative rest: Reduce running, don't stop completely
  • Cross-train: Swimming, cycling, elliptical (if pain-free)
  • Ice: After activity if helpful
  • NSAIDs: Short-term if needed for pain

Rehabilitation

  • Calf strengthening: Heel raises, single-leg variations
  • Hip strengthening: Reduces tibial stress through better mechanics
  • Running form: Increase cadence by 5-10%
  • Gradual return: 10% increase per week maximum

Do Orthotics Help?

Evidence is Mixed

Shock-absorbing insoles may help some people with shin splints, but they're not a cure-all. If you have significant overpronation, custom orthotics could be considered. But strengthening and training modification are more important.

5

Prevention

  • 10% rule: Don't increase weekly distance by more than 10%
  • Vary surfaces: Mix grass, track, trail with road
  • New shoes: Replace every 500-800 km
  • Calf strength: Regular heel raise exercises
  • Cadence: Higher step rate reduces impact forces
  • Listen to your body: Early warning signs mean back off
6

FAQ

How long does it take to heal?
Most cases improve within 2-6 weeks with appropriate activity modification. Severe cases may take longer. Complete rest is usually not necessary - modify rather than stop.
Can I run through shin splints?
Mild cases - possibly, with reduced volume and intensity. But if pain is getting worse, you risk progressing to stress fracture. It's better to back off temporarily than be forced to stop completely later.
Do I need an X-ray or MRI?
Not usually. Shin splints is a clinical diagnosis. Imaging is mainly to rule out stress fracture if there are concerning features (focal tenderness, night pain, failure to improve with rest).
