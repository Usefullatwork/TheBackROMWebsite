Lower Back Pain – Complete Guide

Everything you need to know about causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua Oslo

Lower back pain – also called lumbago – is pain in the lower part of the back, between the ribs and the buttock muscles. This is one of the most common reasons for doctor visits and sick leave worldwide. According to the Global Burden of Disease Study (Lancet 2023), lower back pain is the #1 cause of disability globally, and approximately 4 in 10 people experience lower back pain in any given month.

This guide covers everything you need to know about lower back pain: from acute lumbago and sciatica to chronic complaints, disc herniation, pelvic pain and spinal stenosis. Each condition has its own detailed article – click through to what applies to you.

Key Facts

  • 4 in 10 people experience lower back pain each month
  • 85–95% are "non-specific" – no serious underlying cause (WHO, 2023)
  • 23% of adults have chronic lower back pain
  • Recurrence is common (69% within 12 months) – prevention is key

What does "non-specific back pain" mean?

Non-specific lower back pain accounts for 85–95% of all cases (WHO, 2023). This means serious causes have been ruled out – the pain comes from muscles, joints or other structures that respond well to conservative treatment.

1

Acute Lumbago ("Throwing Out Your Back")

Go to full article on acute lumbago →

Acute lumbago is a sudden, intense pain in the lower back that makes it difficult to straighten up. With acute lumbago, there is usually an acute muscle spasm or joint locking.

Common Triggers

  • Lifting with twisting – especially heavy objects
  • Sudden movements or stumbling
  • Prolonged sitting followed by quick movement
  • No clear cause (up to 50% of cases)

Symptoms of Acute Lumbago

  • Sudden, intense pain – often described as "locking" or "stabbing"
  • Pronounced stiffness – difficult to straighten up, bend or rotate
  • Muscle spasms you can feel as hard "knots"
  • Pain relief at rest – often in fetal position

Acute Lumbago vs. Sciatica

With acute lumbago, the pain stays in the back – it doesn't radiate down the legs. Radiating pain below the knee, tingling or numbness may indicate sciatica.

Prognosis: Most people experience significant improvement within 1–2 weeks. About 70% are substantially better within one week. With proper treatment, many experience pain relief after the first session.

2

Sciatica – Nerve Pain Down the Leg

Go to full article on sciatica →

Sciatica – also called lumbar radiculopathy – is pain that radiates from the lower back down into the leg, caused by irritation of a nerve root. Only 3–5% of the population has lumbar radiculopathy (Medscape, 2024) – it's much less common than people think.

Most Common Causes of Sciatica

  • Disc herniation – most common cause (about 90%). The disc material presses on the nerve root.
  • Spinal stenosis – narrowing of the spinal canal, more common in older adults
  • Spondylolisthesis – vertebral slippage that can pinch the nerve
  • Piriformis syndrome – muscle in the buttock that irritates the sciatic nerve

Symptoms of Sciatica

  • Radiation below the knee – this distinguishes sciatica from regular back pain
  • Burning, stabbing or electric pain down the back of the thigh
  • Tingling and numbness in the leg or foot
  • Weakness – difficulty lifting the foot
  • Worse when sitting – the nerve is stretched in sitting position

L4-L5 (L5 nerve root)

  • Pain on the outer thigh and calf, numbness on top of the foot
  • Weakness when lifting the big toe, difficulty walking on heels

L5-S1 (S1 nerve root)

  • Pain in the back of the thigh and calf, down to the little toe
  • Weakness when walking on toes, reduced Achilles reflex

Prognosis: 50% experience significant improvement within 6 weeks. 75% are better within 12 weeks. 90% improve within 3–6 months without surgery (PMC, 2019).

3

Disc Herniation – When the Disc Bulges Out

Go to full article on disc herniation →

A disc herniation occurs when the soft core of the intervertebral disc pushes through the outer ring and can press on nerve roots. Only 5–15% of patients with lower back pain have lumbar disc herniation (OAMJMS, 2022).

Important to Know About Disc Herniation

Many people have disc herniations on MRI without symptoms – the finding alone doesn't mean you need treatment. 29% of 20-year-olds and 43% of 80-year-olds without pain have protrusion on MRI (Brinjikji, 2015).

Can a Disc Herniation Heal On Its Own?

Yes! The body has a remarkable ability to reabsorb herniated disc material: 66–77% of herniations are spontaneously reabsorbed over time (Zhong et al., 2017). The larger the herniation, the greater the chance of resorption.

Treatment of Disc Herniation

Conservative Treatment (First Choice)

  • Manual therapy adapted to the condition
  • McKenzie approach for centralizing symptoms
  • Gradual increase in activity and exercises

Surgery is Considered For:

  • Cauda equina syndrome (emergency)
  • Progressive neurological deficit
  • Persistent symptoms after 6–12 weeks of conservative treatment

SI Joint and Pelvic Pain

Go to full article on SI joint →

The SI joint is the connection between the sacrum and the pelvic bone. The SI joint is the pain generator in 15–30% of patients with chronic lower back pain (Cohen et al., 2013).

Who Gets SI Joint Problems?

  • After pregnancy and childbirth (very common)
  • With asymmetric loading (one leg shorter)
  • After trauma to the pelvis
  • With hypermobility

Symptoms

  • One-sided pain in the lower back, over one buttock
  • Radiation to the groin, hip or back of the thigh – but rarely below the knee
  • Worse with asymmetric loading – standing on one leg, climbing stairs
  • Turning in bed often provokes the pain

"Pelvic Dysfunction" – What Is It?

This term usually describes reduced mobility in the SI joint, muscle spasms in the pelvic musculature and asymmetry. SI joint problems often respond very well to manual treatment.

5

Spinal Stenosis

Go to full article on spinal stenosis →

Spinal stenosis means narrowing of the spinal canal. Most common in people over 50–60 years. Radiological stenosis is found in up to 47% of people over 60, but most are asymptomatic (Jensen et al., 2020).

The Classic Symptom: Neurogenic Claudication

  • Pain and heaviness in the legs when walking that comes gradually
  • Symptoms ease when you sit down or bend forward
  • You can walk longer with a shopping cart – because you bend slightly forward
  • Cycling often works better than walking – the back is in flexion

Treatment

  • Flexion-based exercises (bending forward – opens the canal)
  • Manual therapy to optimize function
  • Core muscle strengthening

Important: Conservative treatment and surgery yield similar long-term results for many patients.

Pregnancy and Lower Back Pain

Go to full article on pregnancy and back pain →

Approximately 50% of pregnant women experience lower back pain (Wu et al., European Spine Journal 2004). For some, the problems start early; for others, not until the last trimester.

Why Do Pregnant Women Get Back Pain?

Hormonal changes:

  • Relaxin loosens ligaments and joints – provides less stability
  • Muscles have to work harder to compensate

Mechanical changes:

  • Center of gravity shifts forward
  • Increased curve in the lower back (hyperlordosis)
  • Weight gain of 10–15 kg

Types of Pain During Pregnancy

  • Lumbar pain: Central in the lower back, similar to regular lower back pain
  • Pelvic pain: Four times more common. Over the SI joints and/or pubic symphysis.
  • Symphysis dysfunction: Pronounced pain over the pubic symphysis, difficulty walking

Treatment During Pregnancy

Chiropractic treatment is safe during pregnancy:

  • Adapted joint treatment
  • Soft tissue therapy
  • Pelvic belts for pelvic pain
  • Exercises and ergonomic advice

What Does an MRI Really Show?

MRI findings often correlate poorly with pain. Brinjikji et al. (2015) analyzed 3100+ pain-free individuals:

Finding Age 20 Age 40 Age 60 Age 80
Disc degeneration 37% 68% 88% 96%
Disc bulging 30% 50% 69% 84%

What Does This Mean?

MRI findings are often a natural part of the aging process and don't necessarily explain the pain. A thorough clinical examination is more important than the image alone.

8

Red Flags

Call Emergency Services or Go to the ER Immediately If You Experience:

  • Numbness in the groin or around the anus ("saddle anesthesia")
  • Problems urinating – especially if you can't feel that your bladder is full
  • Loss of bowel control
  • Increasing weakness in both legs

With treatment within 48 hours, the chances of full recovery are significantly better.

9

Prognosis and Recurrence

For acute lower back pain, 39% are pain-free after 6 weeks, 73% after 1 year (Henschke, BMJ 2008). For chronic pain, 67.2% had the same pain level after 4 years (Peng, 2012).

Recurrence is Common

69% experienced recurrence within 12 months (da Silva, 2019). Lower back pain is therefore an episodic, long-term condition – prevention is important.

What Does the Research Say About Treatment?

  • JAMA 2017: Manipulation provides ~10 points improvement on 100-point scale – equivalent to NSAIDs
  • BMJ 2019: High-quality evidence for effect in chronic pain
  • CCGPP (2016) recommends 6–12 treatments over 2–4 weeks as a starting point
