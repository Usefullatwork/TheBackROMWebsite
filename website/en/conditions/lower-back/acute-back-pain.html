Acute Back Pain (Lumbago) - Causes, Symptoms and Treatment

Complete guide to acute lower back pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Acute back pain, or lumbago, is a sudden and often intense pain in the lower back that can make even the simplest movements painful. The condition is very common and affects up to 80% of the population at least once in their lifetime.

Although the pain can be overwhelming, acute back pain is usually a benign condition with a good prognosis. Most people improve significantly within a few weeks, and early treatment and proper guidance can accelerate healing.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 80% affected at least once in their lifetime
  • Sudden pain after lifting, twisting, or without warning
  • 90% improve within 6-8 weeks
  • Keep moving - bed rest is not recommended
  • Red flags: urinary problems, numbness in groin, fever

What is Acute Back Pain?

Acute back pain is a common term for sudden lower back pain (lumbar region). Medically, it is often called acute lumbago. The pain typically occurs suddenly, often in connection with a movement such as lifting, twisting, or bending, but can also occur without a clear trigger.

The condition is characterized by:

  • Sudden onset pain in the lower back
  • Stiffness and reduced mobility
  • Muscle tension that can feel like the back is "locked"
  • Pain often worsens with movement, coughing, or sneezing

Why is it called "lumbago"?

The term comes from the Latin word "lumbus" meaning loin. Lumbago simply refers to pain in the lumbar (lower back) region. Today, we know the causes are quite mundane, but the medical term persists.

2

Causes of Acute Back Pain

In most cases (about 90%), acute back pain is what is called non-specific lower back pain. This means the pain cannot be attributed to a specific pathological cause that can be detected on imaging.

Common causes and risk factors:

Muscle and Ligament Strain (most common)

Sudden movements, heavy lifting, or unusual activities can overload muscles and ligaments in the lower back, triggering acute pain and muscle tension.

Facet Joint Irritation

Facet joints are small joints between the vertebrae. Irritation or "locking" of these can cause sudden pain, especially with certain movements.

Discogenic Pain

The discs between the vertebrae can be a source of pain, even without a herniation. Pressure on the outer layer of the disc (annulus) can cause local lower back pain.

Risk factors:

  • Sedentary lifestyle or physically demanding work
  • Previous episodes of lower back pain
  • Poor core muscle strength
  • Stress and psychosocial factors
  • Smoking increases the risk of back pain
  • Overweight
3

Symptoms of Acute Back Pain

Symptoms of acute back pain are typically localized to the lower back and can vary in intensity:

Characteristic symptoms:

  • Acute pain: Sudden onset, often described as "stabbing" or "paralyzing"
  • Stiffness: Significantly reduced mobility in the back
  • Muscle tension: Often palpable tension in the back muscles
  • Pain intensification: Movement, coughing, sneezing, and straining worsen the pain
  • Relief at rest: Pain often decreases in stationary positions

Important distinction: Uncomplicated vs. Complicated

In uncomplicated acute back pain, the pain is limited to the lower back without radiating symptoms to the legs. If you experience radiating pain, numbness, or weakness in the legs, this may indicate nerve involvement (sciatica) and should be evaluated by a healthcare professional.

4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of acute back pain is made primarily through medical history and clinical examination. Imaging is usually not necessary for uncomplicated cases.

What happens during the examination?

  • Medical history: How and when the pain started, character and location of pain
  • Functional examination: Assessment of mobility, posture, and pain-provoking movements
  • Neurological screening: Rule out nerve involvement with radiating symptoms
  • Red flag assessment: Systematic review to rule out serious underlying disease

When is imaging appropriate?

MRI or X-ray is rarely necessary at the first consultation. It is appropriate when serious underlying disease is suspected (red flags), with persistent symptoms beyond 6-8 weeks without improvement, or with progressive neurological deficits.

5

Treatment of Acute Back Pain

Treatment of acute back pain focuses on pain relief, restoration of function, and prevention of recurrence. Research supports an active approach rather than passive rest.

Recommended measures:

Keep Moving (most important)

Bed rest is not recommended. Gentle activity and normal daily tasks promote healing. Take short walks and do gentle movement exercises as soon as pain allows.

Manual Treatment

Chiropractic treatment including joint mobilization, soft tissue massage, and muscle release techniques can provide pain relief and improve mobility. Treatment is individually adapted based on findings and symptoms.

Exercise Guidance

Specific exercises to restore movement and strength are important. You will receive guidance on exercises adapted to your phase of healing.

Heat/Cold

Heat can relieve muscle tension, while ice can help with acute inflammation in the first 48-72 hours. Use what provides you with the best relief.

Medication

If needed, over-the-counter pain relievers such as paracetamol or ibuprofen can be used short-term. For severe pain, a doctor may consider stronger pain relievers or muscle relaxants.

"Early active treatment provides better results than waiting and seeing. Research shows that patients who receive treatment within the first weeks have better long-term outcomes."
6

Red Flags - When You Must See a Doctor

Although acute back pain is usually a benign condition, there are warning signs that require prompt medical evaluation.

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY FOR:

  • Cauda equina symptoms: Problems with urination or bowel function combined with numbness in the genital/buttock region
  • Progressive weakness: Increasing weakness in the legs
  • Fever and back pain: The combination may indicate infection
  • Unexplained weight loss: Combined with back pain
  • Night pain: Pain that wakes you at night unrelated to movement
  • Previous cancer history: Back pain in a person with known cancer
  • Recent trauma: Especially with osteoporosis risk

These symptoms are rare but require immediate evaluation to rule out serious underlying causes.

7

Prognosis and Course

The good news is that acute back pain has a very good prognosis for most people:

90% improve significantly within 6-8 weeks
50% experience significant improvement in first 2 weeks
2-10% develop long-term problems

Risk of Recurrence

About 30-40% of those who have had acute back pain will experience a new episode within a year. Preventive measures such as regular activity and strength training significantly reduce this risk.

Factors affecting prognosis:

  • Early active treatment provides better outcomes
  • Positive attitude and good coping strategies
  • Maintaining normal activity as much as possible
  • Avoiding prolonged sick leave
8

Prevention

While acute episodes cannot always be prevented, there is much you can do to reduce the risk:

Practical preventive measures:

  • Regular physical activity: Both aerobic exercise and strength training strengthen the back
  • Core muscle training: Strengthening abdominal and back muscles provides better stability
  • Proper lifting technique: Lift with legs, keep the load close to your body
  • Vary work positions: Avoid prolonged sitting or standing without breaks
  • Maintain normal weight: Overweight increases the load on the back
  • Stress management: Chronic stress can contribute to muscle tension

Important to remember

The back is strong and can handle a lot. An episode of acute back pain does not mean the back is damaged or weak. With the right approach, most people return to full function.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does acute back pain last?
Most experience significant improvement within 2-4 weeks. About 90% are symptom-free within 6-8 weeks. With proper treatment and guidance, many can experience relief within the first few days.
Should I rest with acute back pain?
Bed rest is not recommended. Research shows that early, gentle movement provides faster recovery than strict rest. Normal daily activities (as much as pain allows) promote healing.
Do I need X-ray or MRI for acute back pain?
For uncomplicated acute back pain, imaging is rarely necessary. Studies show that findings on MRI often do not correlate with symptoms. Imaging is appropriate for red flags or persistent symptoms beyond 6-8 weeks without improvement.
Can I work with acute back pain?
Many can work in modified capacity during recovery. Research supports that staying active and working (as much as possible) provides better long-term outcomes than complete sick leave.
When should I see a doctor or emergency room?
Contact a doctor immediately for: problems with urination/bowel function combined with numbness in the genital area (cauda equina), back pain with fever and weight loss, recent severe trauma, or progressive weakness in the legs.
Can acute back pain be prevented?
While acute episodes cannot always be prevented, regular physical activity, core muscle training, and good lifting techniques significantly reduce the risk. Those who stay active have lower risk of repeated episodes.
