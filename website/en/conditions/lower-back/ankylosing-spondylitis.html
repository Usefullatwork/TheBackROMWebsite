Ankylosing Spondylitis

Inflammatory spinal disease: symptoms, diagnosis and treatment

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

You have had back pain for a long time, perhaps for years. The pain is worst in the morning, and you need to "walk it off" before it eases. You wake up in the middle of the night with lower back pain. This is not like ordinary back pain.

Ankylosing spondylitis is a chronic inflammatory disease that affects the spine. It typically begins before age 45 and has a characteristic pattern that distinguishes it from mechanical back pain.

Early diagnosis is crucial. With modern treatment, most people can live active lives and prevent the structural changes that previously characterized the disease.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Typically starts before age 45
  • Morning stiffness >30 min that improves with activity
  • HLA-B27 positive in 90-95%
  • Exercise is cornerstone of treatment
  • Biologic medications when needed

What is Ankylosing Spondylitis?

Ankylosing spondylitis is a chronic inflammatory disease that primarily affects the spine and sacroiliac (SI) joints. The name comes from the Greek words "ankylos" (bent) and "spondylos" (vertebra).

The disease belongs to a group of conditions called spondyloarthropathies, which also includes psoriatic arthritis, reactive arthritis, and arthritis associated with inflammatory bowel disease.

What happens in the body?

In ankylosing spondylitis, the immune system attacks the body's own tissue, particularly where tendons and ligaments attach to bone (entheses). This causes inflammation that over time can lead to:

  • Erosion and damage to joint cartilage
  • New bone formation (syndesmophytes)
  • Gradual fusion of vertebrae
  • Reduced mobility in the spine

How common is ankylosing spondylitis?

0.1-0.5% of the population
20-30 years typical age of onset
2-3:1 men:women ratio

Axial Spondyloarthritis

Axial spondyloarthritis is an umbrella term that includes both ankylosing spondylitis (with X-ray changes) and non-radiographic axial spondyloarthritis (without visible X-ray changes yet). Both have the same symptoms and are treated similarly.

Symptoms of Ankylosing Spondylitis

The symptoms of ankylosing spondylitis differ significantly from common back pain. The classic pattern is called "inflammatory back pain."

Key features of inflammatory back pain

The five characteristic signs

  • Onset before age 40 (95% start before 45)
  • Insidious onset - gradual development over weeks/months
  • Improvement with activity - opposite of mechanical back pain
  • No improvement with rest - may actually worsen
  • Night pain - waking from pain, especially in the second half of the night

Morning stiffness

One of the most characteristic symptoms is pronounced morning stiffness lasting more than 30 minutes - often 1-2 hours or more. The stiffness gradually eases with movement and activity.

Other symptoms

Peripheral joints

Inflammation in other joints, especially hips, shoulders, and knees. Asymmetric joint inflammation in larger joints.

Enthesitis

Inflammation where tendons attach to bone. Common sites include the Achilles tendon, plantar fascia (heel), and pelvic area.

Uveitis (eye inflammation)

25-40% experience this

Acute inflammation in the eye with pain, redness, and light sensitivity. Requires prompt treatment by an ophthalmologist.

Fatigue

Pronounced fatigue is common and can be as debilitating as the pain.

Comparison: Inflammatory vs. mechanical back pain

Feature Inflammatory (AS) Mechanical (common)
Age of onset Before 40 years Any age
Onset Insidious, gradual Often sudden
Morning stiffness >30 minutes <30 minutes

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made based on a combination of medical history, clinical examination, blood tests, and imaging. Unfortunately, it often takes 8-10 years from symptom onset to diagnosis, which underscores the need for increased awareness.

Clinical examination

  • Assessment of spinal mobility (Schober's test)
  • Examination of SI joints
  • Measurement of chest wall expansion
  • Assessment of posture and gait pattern

Blood tests

HLA-B27

90-95% positive

A gene found in most people with ankylosing spondylitis. However: 8% of the general population also has this gene without developing the disease. A positive test is supportive but not diagnostic alone.

Inflammatory markers

CRP and ESR (erythrocyte sedimentation rate) may be elevated but are normal in up to 40% of patients. Normal CRP does not rule out ankylosing spondylitis.

Imaging

Pelvic X-ray

Shows sacroiliitis (changes in the SI joints). Required for classic AS diagnosis according to New York criteria. May be normal early in the disease course.

MRI

Important for early diagnosis

Can show active inflammation (bone marrow edema) in SI joints and spine before X-ray changes occur. Crucial for diagnosing non-radiographic axial spondyloarthritis.

When should you consider ankylosing spondylitis?

Back pain that started before age 40, has lasted more than 3 months, is worst in the morning with stiffness over 30 minutes, improves with activity, and disturbs sleep - should lead to investigation for inflammatory spinal disease.

Treatment

The treatment of ankylosing spondylitis has undergone a revolution in the last 20 years with the introduction of biologic medications. The goal is to control inflammation, relieve symptoms, maintain function, and prevent structural damage.

Exercise - the cornerstone

Why exercise is so important

  • Maintains mobility in spine and chest
  • Strengthens muscles that support the back
  • Improves posture and prevents kyphosis
  • Reduces pain and stiffness
  • Improves sleep quality and reduces fatigue

Recommended activities

  • Swimming: Particularly beneficial - supports joints and provides all-round training
  • Water aerobics: Combines movement and relaxation
  • Yoga and pilates: Mobility and core strength
  • Walking and cycling: Light aerobic exercise
  • Specific exercises: Daily stretching and mobility exercises

Medication

NSAIDs (anti-inflammatory)

First choice

Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac. Often provide good effect on pain and stiffness. In inflammatory spinal disease, continuous use may be considered to slow disease progression.

Biologic medications

When NSAIDs insufficient

TNF inhibitors (adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab) and IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab). Highly effective for controlling inflammation and preventing damage.

JAK inhibitors

Newer class of medication (tofacitinib, upadacitinib) taken as tablets. Alternative for those who don't respond to or tolerate biologic medications.

What can the chiropractor contribute?

The chiropractor's role

  • Early detection: Identify inflammatory signs and refer for investigation
  • Soft tissue treatment: Relieve muscle tension and enthesitis-related pain
  • Mobilization: Gentle mobilization of stiff joints (except fused ones)
  • Exercise guidance: Customized exercise program for mobility and strength
  • Posture advice: Prevent and correct unfavorable posture patterns

Important about manipulation

In known ankylosing spondylitis with vertebral fusion (ankylosis), traditional manipulation is contraindicated due to increased fracture risk. Gentler techniques and soft tissue treatment are safe alternatives.

Prognosis and Living with AS

The prognosis for ankylosing spondylitis has improved dramatically with modern treatment. Most people can live active, productive lives with good disease control.

The positive:

  • Biologic medications provide good disease control for most
  • Early treatment can prevent structural damage
  • Many maintain full work capacity
  • Regular exercise significantly improves quality of life
  • The disease "burns out" in some after years

The realistic:

  • Chronic disease requiring lifelong follow-up
  • Flare-ups may occur
  • Fatigue can be challenging to manage
  • Some experience gradual reduced mobility despite treatment
  • Increased risk of osteoporosis and fractures

Lifestyle adaptations

  • Stop smoking: Smoking significantly worsens the disease and reduces treatment effectiveness
  • Regular exercise: Daily exercises for mobility
  • Good sleep hygiene: Firm mattress, correct pillow height
  • Ergonomics: Adaptations at work and home
  • Regular follow-up: With rheumatologist and possibly other specialists

When to See a Doctor?

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY FOR:

  • Sudden worsening of back pain after a fall or trauma (fracture risk)
  • Acute eye pain with redness and light sensitivity (uveitis)
  • New onset numbness or weakness in legs
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Fever combined with worsening back pain

People with ankylosing spondylitis have increased risk of spinal fractures, even from minor trauma. Always take pain worsening after falls/impacts seriously.

You should contact a doctor if:

  • You have had back pain for more than 3 months that started before age 40
  • The pain is worst in the morning and improves with activity
  • You have morning stiffness lasting more than 30 minutes
  • You have family members with ankylosing spondylitis or other spondyloarthritis
  • You have had eye inflammation (uveitis) before

Frequently Asked Questions

What is ankylosing spondylitis?
Ankylosing spondylitis is a chronic inflammatory disease that primarily affects the spine and SI joints. Over time, the inflammation can lead to stiffening of the spine.
How does ankylosing spondylitis differ from common back pain?
With ankylosing spondylitis, pain is worst at night and morning, improves with activity, and is often accompanied by morning stiffness over 30 minutes. Common back pain typically worsens with activity and improves with rest.
Can ankylosing spondylitis be cured?
There is no cure, but modern treatment can control inflammation, relieve symptoms and slow disease progression. Early diagnosis and treatment provide the best prognosis.
Is ankylosing spondylitis hereditary?
There is a genetic component. The HLA-B27 gene is found in 90-95% of patients with AS, but most people with this gene never develop the disease. Risk is elevated if first-degree relatives have the condition.
Can I exercise with ankylosing spondylitis?
Yes, exercise is a cornerstone of treatment. Regular movement and specific exercises help maintain mobility, strength, and posture. Swimming and water aerobics are particularly beneficial.
What is the difference between AS and axial spondyloarthritis?
Axial spondyloarthritis is an umbrella term. Ankylosing spondylitis is the form with visible changes on X-ray, while non-radiographic axial spondyloarthritis has inflammation without X-ray changes yet.
