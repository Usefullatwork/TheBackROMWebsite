Disc Herniation in the Lower Back

Symptoms, spontaneous resorption, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

The intervertebral discs function as shock absorbers between the vertebrae. Each disc has a hard outer ring (annulus fibrosus) and a soft, gel-like core (nucleus pulposus). A herniation occurs when this core is pushed through cracks in the outer ring and can irritate or compress nerve roots.

Disc problems are nevertheless a rare cause of back pain. The WFNS Spine Committee (2024) states that degenerative disc disease accounts for less than 5% of all lower back pain cases. Most back pain is due to muscular and non-specific causes (80-90%).

Quick Summary

  • 1-3% lifetime risk, 95% affect L4-L5 or L5-S1
  • 66-77% resolve without surgery
  • Sequestrations have 87-96% regression
  • 85-90% improve within 6-12 weeks conservatively
  • Red flags: Groin numbness, bladder/bowel control

For numbness in the groin, problems with urination/bowel control, or increasing weakness in both legs - call emergency services or go to the ER.

Who is Affected by Disc Herniation?

Disc herniation typically affects adults between 30 and 50 years old, with approximately twice as many men as women (StatPearls, 2025). The lifetime risk for symptomatic herniation is 1-3% (WFNS, 2024).

95% of all lumbar herniations occur at the two lowest levels: L4-L5 and L5-S1. This is because these segments bear the most weight and have the greatest mobility.

1-3% lifetime risk
95% affect L4-L5 or L5-S1
30-50 yrs most common age group

Causes and Risk Factors

From most to least common:

Degenerative Changes (Most Common)

With age, the disc loses fluid and elasticity. The nucleus pulposus changes composition with reduced water retention, while the annulus fibrosus gradually weakens.

Genetic Predisposition

Twin studies show up to 74% heritability for disc degeneration (Seminars in Spine Surgery, 2016). If you have close family with herniation, your risk is higher.

Mechanical Load

Heavy work with repeated lifting and twisting increases the risk. Prolonged sitting also significantly increases disc pressure.

Lifestyle Factors

  • Smoking: 15-65% increased risk
  • Obesity: 10-90% increased risk depending on degree
  • Diabetes: Approximately 50% increased risk
3

Symptoms

The classic symptom is radicular pain - a sharp, burning or electric pain that radiates down the leg. The pain follows the nerve's distribution area and often extends below the knee.

L4-L5 Herniation (Most Common, 40-45%)

  • Pain from lower back to buttocks, lateral thigh, down to the top of the foot and big toe
  • Reduced sensation between big toe and second toe
  • Possible weakness in lifting the foot (foot drop)

L5-S1 Herniation (35-40%)

  • Pain in buttocks, back of thigh, calf and out to the outside of the foot
  • Reduced or absent Achilles reflex
  • Possible weakness in toe-walking
4

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

A thorough clinical examination is the foundation for diagnosis.

Straight Leg Raise (Lasegue)

Sensitivity 92%, specificity 28% (Cochrane, 2010). High sensitivity means the test rarely misses a herniation. Positive when pain radiates below the knee with leg elevation between 30-70 degrees.

Crossed SLR

Sensitivity 28%, specificity 90% (Cochrane, 2010). When this is positive, there is very high probability of herniation.

Slump Test

Sensitivity 84%. Performed sitting and combines neck flexion with knee extension.

Imaging

MRI is the gold standard for visualizing disc herniations. But an important point: Studies show that 30-60% of pain-free people have disc changes visible on MRI. Therefore, it is the symptom picture - not the MRI finding alone - that determines the diagnosis.

When is MRI Appropriate?

MRI is usually considered after 4-6 weeks if symptoms don't improve with conservative treatment, or earlier with worsening neurological deficits.

5

Spontaneous Resorption - The Herniation Can Resolve on Its Own

This is perhaps the most important information many patients don't receive: The body has a remarkable ability to resorb herniated disc material. Multiple meta-analyses confirm this.

Resorption Rates by Type

A recent meta-analysis of 31 studies with 2233 patients (Zou et al., Clinical Spine Surgery 2023) shows clear patterns:

Herniation Type Resorption Rate
Sequestration (detached fragment) 87-96%
Extrusion (breach through outer ring) 67%
Protrusion (bulging with intact ring) 38%
Bulging (general bulging) 13%

Why Do the "Worst" Herniations Resolve Best?

It seems counterintuitive, but the explanation is biological: When disc material breaks completely through and lies free in the spinal canal (sequestration), it is exposed to the body's immune system. This triggers an inflammatory response with macrophages that "eat up" the foreign tissue. Neovascularization (new blood vessel formation) supports this process.

With smaller herniations that still have contact with the disc, this immune response is weaker, and resorption proceeds more slowly.

Timeline for Resorption

  • Main period: Most resorption occurs within 3-6 months
  • Early resorption: 24.7% show signs of resorption within 3 months
  • At 12 months: 67-81% have documented resorption on MRI
  • Average time: 8.7 +/- 3.2 months to complete resorption

Symptoms Improve BEFORE MRI Images Change

An important point that often confuses patients: Clinical improvement typically comes 4-6 weeks before MRI shows changes. You can feel much better even if the herniation still shows on images.

6

Treatment

Conservative Treatment - First Line

WFNS guidelines from 2024 recommend conservative treatment as first choice for most herniations. Surgery is considered only with failure of conservative treatment, severe motor deficit, or progressive neurological dysfunction.

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

  • Manual treatment: Mobilization and manipulation can contribute to pain relief and improved function
  • McKenzie method: Systematic approach focusing on centralization of symptoms
  • Nerve mobilization: Gentle techniques that improve nerve gliding through tissues
  • Exercise therapy: Significant improvements in pain and function without reported side effects

Chiropractic Treatment and Reduced Need for Surgery

A 2022 study with over 100 million health records found that adults who received chiropractic treatment for newly diagnosed herniation had reduced chance of needing surgery over 1-2 years of follow-up (BMJ Open, 2022).

What Can You Do Yourself?

In the Acute Phase (0-2 weeks)

  • Avoid complete bed rest - movement is important
  • Walking is often the best exercise
  • Short, frequent breaks when sitting
  • Ice or heat according to what provides relief

After the Acute Phase

  • McKenzie extension exercises if they centralize pain
  • Gradual increase in activity level
  • Avoid heavy lifting and twisting

When is Surgery Appropriate?

Surgery is considered for:

  • Significant neurological deficits that don't improve
  • Pronounced symptoms after 6-12 weeks of conservative treatment
  • Cauda equina syndrome (emergency surgery)

Important About Surgery vs. Conservative Treatment

Most studies show that conservative treatment and surgery give equally good long-term results after 1-2 years, but surgery may provide faster improvement for some.

7

Prognosis - What to Expect?

85-90% of patients experience significant improvement within 6-12 weeks with conservative treatment.

85-90% improve within 6-12 weeks
66-77% spontaneous resorption
5-15% recurrence risk within 2 years

Recurrence Risk

Previous herniation increases the risk of new episodes. Studies show 5-15% risk of new herniation within 2 years. Preventive measures like regular exercise, good lifting technique, and weight control can reduce recurrence.

8

When Should You Seek Help?

Emergency (call emergency services)

  • Symptoms of cauda equina syndrome as described under red flags

Within 24-48 hours

  • Significant or increasing muscle weakness in foot or leg

Planned Evaluation

  • Persistent leg pain affecting function
  • Numbness or tingling that doesn't improve
  • Pain affecting sleep and daily activities

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a herniation disappear completely?
Yes. Studies show that 43% of sequestrations (loose fragments) achieve complete resorption. Partial resorption is even more common, with 66-77% showing significant regression.
Can chiropractic cure a herniation?
No, chiropractic does not cure the herniation directly. The body must resorb the prolapsed material over time. Treatment can contribute to symptom relief and functional improvement, and support the body's natural healing process.
Is chiropractic treatment safe with a herniation?
For most people with uncomplicated herniation, chiropractic treatment is safe when performed by a licensed chiropractor. Treatment is adapted to the condition and is not appropriate for cauda equina syndrome, progressive neurological deficit, or sequestered herniation with severe symptoms.
How long until I notice improvement?
Treatment response varies. Some experience relief after the first treatment, while others need several weeks for significant improvement. 85-90% experience improvement within 6-12 weeks. Symptom improvement often comes 4-6 weeks before MRI shows changes.
Do I need an MRI to get treatment?
No, MRI is usually not necessary for conservative treatment. Clinical examination is often sufficient. MRI is recommended for red flags, progressive neurological deficit, or lack of improvement after 4-6 weeks.
Do I need a new MRI to check if the herniation is gone?
Usually not. Clinical improvement is more important than MRI findings. You can be nearly pain-free while the herniation still shows on images. A new MRI is most appropriate with lack of improvement or worsening.
Can I exercise with a herniation?
Yes, gradual activity and adapted exercises are an important part of treatment. Walking is often the best exercise in the acute phase. Only avoid activities that significantly worsen symptoms.
Can a herniation come back?
Yes, previous herniation increases the risk of new episodes (5-15% within 2 years). Preventive measures include regular exercise, core muscle strengthening, good lifting technique and weight control.
What's the difference between herniation, disc herniation and sciatica?
Herniation and disc herniation are the same thing - bulging of disc material. Sciatica is a symptom (radiating pain down the leg) that can be caused by herniation, but also other conditions like spinal stenosis or piriformis syndrome.
