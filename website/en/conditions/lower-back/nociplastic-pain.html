Nociplastic Pain: When the Nervous System Becomes Oversensitive

Central Sensitization in Chronic Back Pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You've had back pain for years. Your MRI shows no serious findings, or perhaps just "normal age-related changes." You've tried countless treatments without lasting effect. The pain spreads to new areas. You react to touch that didn't bother you before. You feel like no one believes you.

This pattern may indicate nociplastic pain - a condition where the nervous system has become oversensitive and amplifies pain signals without detectable tissue damage.

The good news? This type of pain can be treated - but it requires a different approach than traditional pain management. And most importantly: your pain is just as real as any other pain.

Key Takeaways

  • The nervous system has become oversensitive
  • The pain is 100% real
  • Mismatch between findings and symptoms
  • Treated with graded activity + pain education
  • Requires a multidisciplinary approach

What is Nociplastic Pain?

In 2017, the International Association for the Study of Pain (IASP) introduced the term nociplastic pain as a third pain category alongside the two traditional ones:

The Three Types of Pain

Type Cause Examples
Nociceptive Tissue damage activates pain receptors Sprain, fracture, osteoarthritis
Neuropathic Damage to the nervous system itself Sciatica, carpal tunnel, diabetic neuropathy
Nociplastic Altered pain processing in the nervous system Fibromyalgia, chronic low back pain, IBS

In nociplastic pain, the nervous system has become "turned up" - it sends and amplifies pain signals even without corresponding input from the tissues. This is also called central sensitization.

An Analogy: The Volume Knob

Think of the pain system as an amplifier. Normally, the volume is set at an appropriate level - you feel pain when something is wrong, and the pain subsides when the injury heals. With central sensitization, someone has turned the volume up to 10 and broken off the knob. Now even weak signals are amplified into intense pain.

How Common is This?

20-30% of chronic pain patients
Women affected more often than men
Often overlapping conditions

Why Does Nociplastic Pain Develop?

Central sensitization rarely develops suddenly. It's usually a gradual process that can be triggered by several factors.

Biological Factors

Persistent Pain Input

Prolonged pain from an original injury can "train" the nervous system to become more sensitive. Even after the original cause is gone, the pain system can remain on "high alert."

Neurochemical Changes

Increased release of excitatory neurotransmitters and reduced function of inhibitory systems in the spinal cord and brain.

Genetic Vulnerability

Some people have a genetic predisposition for altered pain processing and increased risk of chronic pain.

Psychological Factors

Stress and Anxiety

Chronic stress activates the body's alarm system and can amplify the pain experience. Fear of pain can lead to avoidance behavior that worsens the condition.

Catastrophizing

The tendency to focus on the worst possible outcome. "This pain means something is seriously wrong." This amplifies pain signals.

Sleep Disturbances

Poor sleep weakens the body's natural pain inhibition and increases sensitization. A vicious cycle often develops.

Social Factors

Not Being Believed

When healthcare providers, employers, or family don't take the pain seriously, this can increase stress and worsening.

Work Stress and Financial Strain

Stressful life situations increase the body's stress levels and can contribute to sensitization.

Important to Understand

That psychological factors play a role does not mean the pain is "imaginary" or "all in your head." The brain is precisely the organ that produces all pain experiences - both in acute injury and in sensitization. The pain is biological and real regardless.

Symptoms and Signs of Nociplastic Pain

Nociplastic pain has some characteristic features that distinguish it from nociceptive and neuropathic pain.

Typical Characteristics

Clinical Signs of Central Sensitization

  • Allodynia: Pain from stimuli that are normally not painful (light touch, clothing against skin)
  • Hyperalgesia: Enhanced pain response to painful stimuli
  • Widespread pain: Pain spreads to multiple areas over time
  • Temporal summation: Repeated light stimuli feel increasingly painful
  • After-pain: Pain persists long after the stimulus is gone

Other Common Symptoms

  • Fatigue: Pronounced tiredness that doesn't improve with rest
  • Sleep problems: Difficulty falling asleep and/or frequent awakenings
  • Cognitive difficulties: "Brain fog," concentration problems
  • Headache: Often tension headache or migraine
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms: Irritable bowel-like symptoms
  • Mood changes: Anxiety, low mood

Mismatch Between Findings and Symptoms

An Important Characteristic

In nociplastic pain, there is often a mismatch between objective findings and symptom intensity. MRI and other tests show no or only minimal changes that don't explain the degree of pain and functional impairment.

This is not a sign that the pain is imaginary. It's a sign that the pain arises in the nervous system's processing, not in the tissue being scanned.

4

Diagnosis

There is no simple test for nociplastic pain. The diagnosis is made clinically based on medical history, symptom patterns, and exclusion of other causes.

Clinical Assessment

  • Thorough medical history focusing on symptom development over time
  • Mapping of pain location and spread
  • Assessment of sensory phenomena (allodynia, hyperalgesia)
  • Screening for anxiety, depression, and sleep disturbances
  • Neurological examination to rule out nerve damage

Standardized Questionnaires

Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI)

A validated questionnaire with 25 questions that maps symptoms associated with central sensitization. A score above 40 out of 100 indicates probable central sensitization.

Pain Catastrophizing Scale

Maps the degree of catastrophizing about pain, which is associated with central sensitization.

Imaging

Important to Know

MRI and X-rays cannot detect nociplastic pain. Imaging is primarily used to rule out other causes (red flags, nerve compression, etc.). Findings such as disc degeneration and osteoarthritis are common in pain-free individuals as well and do not explain nociplastic pain.

5

Treatment

Treatment of nociplastic pain requires a fundamentally different approach than traditional pain management. The goal is not to "fix" something in the body, but to help the nervous system reduce its oversensitivity.

Pain Education (Pain Neuroscience Education)

Why Understanding is Important

  • Understanding why pain occurs reduces fear and catastrophizing
  • Knowledge that pain doesn't mean damage provides confidence to move
  • Changes how the brain interprets signals from the body
  • Studies show that pain education alone can reduce pain and improve function

Graded Activity and Exposure

Graded Exercise Training

Central to treatment

Controlled increase in physical activity and exposure to feared movements. The goal is to show the nervous system that movement is safe, so the "alarm" gradually decreases.

  • Start at a level that doesn't provoke significant pain flare-ups
  • Increase gradually in small steps
  • Focus on function, not pain level
  • Accept that some discomfort is normal and not dangerous

Psychological Approaches

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Helps identify and change unhelpful thought patterns and behaviors related to pain. Reduces catastrophizing and avoidance behavior.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)

Focuses on accepting pain without fighting it, while living in accordance with personal values. Reduces the amplifying effect of the struggle against pain.

Mindfulness

Attention training can reduce the nervous system's reactivity and decrease the stress response. Can help "observe" pain without amplifying it.

What Can the Chiropractor Contribute?

The Chiropractor's Role

  • Pain education: Explain what's happening in the nervous system and reduce fear
  • Safe exposure: Guided return to movement and activity
  • Soft tissue treatment: Gentle techniques can provide temporary relief and help "normalize" touch
  • Exercise guidance: Tailored program for graded activity progression
  • Multidisciplinary collaboration: Refer to psychologist, physiotherapist, or doctor as needed

Medication Treatment

Medications That May Help

  • Antidepressants: Amitriptyline, duloxetine - work on pain-inhibiting pathways
  • Anticonvulsants: Pregabalin, gabapentin - reduce nerve signals
  • SNRIs: Venlafaxine - for concurrent anxiety/depression symptoms

Common painkillers (paracetamol, NSAIDs, opioids) often have limited effect in nociplastic pain.

6

Important to Understand

THIS IS IMPORTANT

  • Your pain is real. Nociplastic pain is not imaginary, psychosomatic, or "just in your head."
  • It's not your fault. You haven't "chosen" to have this type of pain.
  • It can get better. The nervous system has the ability to change back (neuroplasticity).
  • Treatment takes time. This is a gradual process, not a quick fix.

Common Misconceptions

"The doctors can't find anything, so it must be psychological"

Wrong. Nociplastic pain is a neurological condition. That the MRI is "normal" only means the cause of the pain isn't visible on the images - not that the pain is imaginary.

"I just need to find the right practitioner who can fix me"

With nociplastic pain, there's nothing to "fix" in the traditional sense. Treatment is about helping the nervous system function differently, and this requires active participation from you.

"I need to rest until the pain goes away"

Wrong approach. Prolonged rest and avoidance often reinforce sensitization. Graded, safe movement is necessary for improvement.

7

Prognosis

The prognosis for nociplastic pain varies, but many experience significant improvement with the right approach.

Positive Factors:

  • The nervous system is plastic and can change back
  • Multidisciplinary treatment yields good results for many
  • Understanding the condition reduces fear and catastrophizing
  • Graded activity helps the nervous system "calibrate down"

Realistic Expectations:

  • Improvement often takes months to years, not weeks
  • The goal is often better function and quality of life, not necessarily zero pain
  • Setbacks can occur, but often become milder over time
  • Active self-effort is crucial for the outcome

Frequently Asked Questions

What is nociplastic pain?
Nociplastic pain is pain that arises from altered pain processing in the nervous system, without clear tissue damage or nerve involvement. The nervous system has become oversensitive and amplifies pain signals.
Is nociplastic pain real pain?
Yes, absolutely. The pain is just as real as any other pain. The difference is that the cause lies in the nervous system's processing of signals, not in detectable tissue damage. Modern brain imaging shows that the pain activates the same brain regions as other types of pain.
Can nociplastic pain be treated?
Yes, but it requires a different approach than traditional pain treatment. Treatment focuses on "reprogramming" the nervous system through graded activity, pain education, stress management, and cognitive therapy.
How do I know if I have nociplastic pain?
Typical signs include pain that doesn't match examination findings, widespread pain across multiple areas, hypersensitivity to touch and other stimuli, and pain that doesn't respond to conventional treatment. A thorough clinical evaluation is necessary.
Is central sensitization the same as fibromyalgia?
Fibromyalgia is considered a form of nociplastic pain with central sensitization. However, central sensitization can occur in many conditions, including chronic back pain, migraine, and irritable bowel syndrome.
Can a chiropractor help with nociplastic pain?
Yes, a chiropractor can contribute as part of a multidisciplinary team. Treatment focuses on safe exposure to movement, graded activity, pain education, and soft tissue techniques that can help "normalize" the nervous system's response.
