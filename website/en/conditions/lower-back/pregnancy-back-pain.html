Pregnant with Back Pain? How a Chiropractor Can Help

Chiropractor Mads Finstad | Klinikk for alle, Majorstua, Oslo

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • What it is: Back and pelvic pain affects about half of all pregnant women, especially in the second and third trimesters
  • Common causes: Hormonal changes (relaxin), altered posture, increased load on pelvis and lower back
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence for pain relief - safe throughout pregnancy with adapted techniques
  • Red flags: Vaginal bleeding, fever, numbness/weakness in legs, problems with bladder control

When should you seek immediate help?

If you experience numbness in the groin area, problems with urination/bowel function, or increasing weakness in both legs - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

What is Pregnancy-Related Back Pain?

Back and pelvic pain during pregnancy is so common that it's almost considered a normal part of pregnancy. A large meta-analysis from 2023 found that approximately 40% of pregnant women experience lower back pain. When pelvic pain is included, the figure rises to around 63%.

You may have heard that "9 out of 10 pregnant women get back pain." This is exaggerated - research shows that about half experience pain bothersome enough to affect daily life.

The pain typically starts between weeks 20 and 28, and often becomes more intense as pregnancy progresses.

2

Pelvic Girdle Pain vs. SI Joint Dysfunction

Many use "pelvic girdle pain" as a catch-all term, but there are actually two different conditions that require different approaches.

Pelvic Girdle Pain (symphysis pubis dysfunction)

This is the most common variant during pregnancy and is caused by the hormone relaxin making the ligaments in the pelvis more elastic. The body prepares for delivery, but the increased mobility can cause pain.

  • Diffuse, often bilateral pain in the front of the pelvis (over the symphysis)
  • "Waddling gait"
  • About 20% of pregnant women experience troublesome pelvic pain

SI Joint Dysfunction

Here the problem is the opposite - one or more joints in the pelvis have reduced mobility.

  • Often one-sided, sharp pain in the back of the pelvis
  • Typically localized to one sacroiliac joint
  • Pain may radiate to the buttock, groin, or thigh

Why is this important?

Treatment differs. For pelvic girdle pain, I focus on stabilizing exercises, while SI joint dysfunction often responds well to joint mobilization.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

Pregnancy-related back pain has several intertwined causes:

Hormonal Changes

The hormone relaxin loosens ligaments and connective tissue to prepare the body for delivery. This makes the pelvic joints more unstable and can cause pain.

Changed Posture

As the belly grows, the center of gravity shifts forward. Many compensate by arching the lower back more, which increases the load on facet joints and muscles.

Increased Weight and Load

Weight gain of 10-15 kg during pregnancy means significantly increased load on the lower back and pelvis.

Who is at highest risk?

  • Previous back pain: 85% chance of experiencing it again in a new pregnancy
  • Back problems before pregnancy: Twice the risk
  • Overweight: BMI over 25 increases risk
  • Heavy physical work: Norwegian studies show a connection
  • Multiple pregnancies: Risk increases with each pregnancy

Sciatica during pregnancy is rarer than many think - only about 1% of pregnant women have confirmed sciatica. Much of what is called "sciatica" is actually referred pain from the pelvis or muscles.

4

Symptoms - What Characterizes the Complaints?

For Pelvic Girdle Pain

  • Pain in front of the pelvis, over the symphysis
  • Pain is often diffuse and can be felt on both sides
  • Worse with walking, stairs, turning in bed
  • Feeling that the pelvis is "unstable"

For Lower Back Pain

  • Aching pain in the lower back
  • Often localized to the sacroiliac joints
  • Can radiate to buttock and back of thigh
  • Rarely below the knee (which distinguishes it from true sciatica)

With Combination

Many experience a combination of both, which can be particularly challenging. Combined pain often causes the greatest functional impairment.

5

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made primarily through medical history and clinical examination. European guidelines for pelvic pain recommend specific tests:

Important Clinical Tests

Two tests are particularly valuable for assessing SI joint problems during pregnancy:

  • P4 test (Posterior Pelvic Pain Provocation): A test with high diagnostic value for detecting sacroiliac joint problems
  • ASLR test (Active Straight Leg Raise): Assesses functional stability in the pelvis

Important: When both P4 and ASLR are positive, the risk of long-term complaints is more than 7 times higher.

Imaging (X-ray, MRI): Rarely necessary and avoided during pregnancy due to radiation. Clinical examination usually provides sufficient information to start treatment.

6

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Chiropractic treatment during pregnancy is safe when performed with adapted techniques. A systematic review from Cochrane found moderate evidence that manual treatment can reduce pain and improve function in pregnant women.

A study by Peterson et al. (2014) found that 52% of pregnant women reported improvement after just one week of chiropractic treatment.

Typical Treatment Methods

  • Gentle joint mobilization of pelvis and lower back
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight muscles
  • Guidance in exercises and posture
  • Pregnancy pillows or side-lying positions to avoid pressure on the abdomen

What can you do yourself?

  • Stabilizing exercises: Norwegian research shows that 68% had minimal or no pain after exercise treatment
  • Stay active: Moderate activity like swimming and walking is often better than rest
  • Pillow between knees: Relieves the pelvis when you sleep on your side
  • Avoid asymmetric loads: Sit down when putting on pants
  • Pelvic belt: Can provide support for pelvic girdle pain

Treatment Course

Research shows that many experience improvement relatively quickly. One study reported that patients needed an average of 1.8 treatments before noticing clear improvement.

Many pregnant women benefit from treatment every 2-4 weeks throughout pregnancy, with more frequent visits as needed.

7

Red Flags - When You Should Contact a Doctor

Contact doctor or emergency room for:

  • Vaginal bleeding or unusual discharge
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Numbness, tingling, or weakness in the legs
  • Problems controlling bladder or bowels
  • Severe abdominal pain, especially on one side
  • Pain that is not relieved by rest or position change

These symptoms may be signs of serious conditions that require prompt medical evaluation.

8

Prognosis - What to Expect?

Most improve significantly after giving birth. A large Danish study found that 67-75% were symptom-free three months after delivery, and over 90% after two years.

Important to Know

Prognosis depends on several factors:

  • Women with pain in all three pelvic joints have a poorer prognosis - about 20% still have complaints after two years
  • Norwegian guidelines recommend specialized physiotherapy if complaints are not significantly better six weeks after delivery
  • The risk of getting pelvic pain in the next pregnancy is high - about 85% experience the complaints again

Early treatment and exercises can still significantly reduce severity.

9

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe to see a chiropractor when pregnant?

Yes, chiropractic treatment is considered safe throughout pregnancy when performed by an experienced practitioner who adapts the techniques. A literature review found very few reported side effects.

When should I start treatment?

You don't need to wait. Early treatment often leads to faster improvement, and preventive measures can reduce the risk of symptoms becoming chronic.

Can the chiropractor treat me in all trimesters?

Yes, but techniques are adapted. In the third trimester, I use side-lying positions and special equipment to avoid pressure on the abdomen, and I take extra care due to increased ligament laxity.

What is the difference between pelvic girdle pain and regular back pain?

Pelvic girdle pain typically causes pain in front of the pelvis and worsens with walking and stairs. Regular back pain is located higher up and is often related to muscles and posture.

Will the pain go away after giving birth?

For most, yes. About 75% are symptom-free three months after delivery. Some have long-term complaints, especially those with pain in all three pelvic joints.

Can exercises replace treatment?

Exercises are an important part of treatment and can often provide good results on their own. The combination of manual treatment and exercises often gives the best results.

Does insurance cover chiropractic care during pregnancy?

Most private health insurance policies cover chiropractic treatment. Check with your insurance company for details about coverage.
