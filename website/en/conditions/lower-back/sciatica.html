Sciatica: Everything You Need to Know

True vs. false sciatica, duration, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You wake up with a burning, electric pain radiating from your lower back, through your buttock and down your leg. Perhaps all the way to your foot. The pain is so intense you can barely sit, and every time you cough or sneeze it feels like an electric shock through your leg.

This is classic sciatica. Or is it?

Sciatica is one of the most misunderstood concepts in back problems. Many believe that all pain radiating down the leg is sciatica, but the reality is more nuanced. Let's look at what actually happens, and why the distinction between "true" and "false" sciatica is crucial for your treatment.

Key Takeaways

  • Only 3-5% of all back problems are true sciatica
  • Causes: Disc herniation (~90%), spinal stenosis, foraminal stenosis
  • 50% improve within 4 weeks, median 10-12 weeks
  • Manual therapy + exercises recommended
  • Red flags: Bladder/bowel dysfunction, saddle numbness

What is Sciatica?

Sciatica (also called lumbar radiculopathy) describes pain that occurs when the sciatic nerve or the nerve roots that form it become irritated, inflamed or compressed. The sciatic nerve is the body's longest and thickest nerve with a diameter of up to 2 cm. It starts in the lower back and runs through the buttock all the way down to the foot (StatPearls, 2024).

The important thing to understand is that "sciatica" is a symptom, not a diagnosis in itself. It describes where the pain is felt, but doesn't necessarily say what's causing it.

How common is sciatica?

Despite many believing sciatica is common, true sciatica actually only accounts for 3-5% of all back pain cases (StatPearls, 2024). The vast majority with back pain have non-specific or muscular causes (80-90%).

True Sciatica vs. False Sciatica

This distinction is crucial for correct treatment.

True sciatica (lumbar radiculopathy)

In true sciatica, there is actual irritation or compression of the nerve root in the spine. The most common causes, ranked from most to least frequent:

1. Disc herniation (~90% of cases)

The gel material from the disc bulges out and presses on the nerve. About 95% of all lumbar herniations occur at L4-L5 or L5-S1 level (StatPearls, 2023).

2. Spinal stenosis

Narrowing of the spinal canal, more common in older people. Can cause bilateral symptoms and worsens with walking. Read more about spinal stenosis.

3. Foraminal stenosis

Narrowing at the nerve exit from the vertebrae. Often age-related degeneration.

4. Spondylolisthesis

Slippage of one vertebra in relation to another. Particularly common at L5-S1.

False sciatica (pseudosciatica)

In false sciatica, the nerve itself is not being compressed. Pain is referred from other structures that mimic sciatica:

1. Trigger points and muscular causes (most common)

Tender knots in gluteal muscles, quadratus lumborum or hamstrings can cause referred pain down the leg.

2. SI joint dysfunction

Referred pain from the back of the pelvis. Often causes pain to the buttock and back of the thigh.

3. Facet joint syndrome

Referred pain from the joints between the vertebrae. Typically worsened by extension and rotation.

4. Piriformis syndrome (0.3-6% of cases)

The piriformis muscle in the buttock compresses the sciatic nerve. The diagnosis is controversial and often considered overdiagnosed (Cleveland Clinic, 2022).

How to distinguish true from false sciatica?

Characteristic True Sciatica False Sciatica
Pain distribution Below the knee, often to foot/toes Usually stops above the knee
Pain quality Sharp, electric, burning Dull, aching, diffuse
Neurology May have numbness, tingling, weakness Normal neurological examination
Straight leg raise Positive (pain with leg raise) Often negative

Symptoms of True Sciatica

Classic symptoms include pain from the lower back/buttock, down the back of the thigh, below the knee, potentially to the foot and toes. The pain pattern depends on which nerve root is affected:

L4 root

  • Pain to the front of the thigh and inner calf
  • Weakened knee extension

L5 root (L4-L5 herniation)

  • Pain to the outer calf and top of the foot
  • Weakened toe dorsiflexion

S1 root (L5-S1 herniation)

  • Pain to the back of the calf and outer foot
  • Weakened toe plantar flexion, reduced Achilles reflex

Pain quality and provocation

  • Pain quality: Sharp, burning, electric. Often described as "current" through the leg.
  • Worsening: Coughing, sneezing, straining, sitting, forward bending typically increases pain.
  • Relief: Lying on your back with bent knees, gentle movement.

How Long Does Sciatica Really Last?

There's a widespread belief that "90% recover within 6 weeks." Newer research shows this is too optimistic. Here's what studies actually show:

At 4 weeks

About 50% experience significant improvement in symptoms (Koes et al., BMJ 2007). Half still have symptoms.

At 6 weeks

Around 50% show clinically meaningful improvement in both pain and function (StatPearls, 2024). Far from "90% recovered."

At 12 weeks (3 months)

The ATLAS cohort study from the UK found that 55% had achieved at least 30% reduction in disability. Median time to improvement is 10-12 weeks.

At 6-12 months

A Norwegian multicenter study found success rates of 49-58% at 6 months. At 12 months, 55-70% have recovered.

What affects recovery time?

Strongest predictor: The patient's own belief that symptoms will become chronic. Those who believe they will recover have nearly four times higher chance of actually doing so.

  • Long symptom duration before treatment
  • High pain intensity at onset
  • Psychosomatic comorbidities

Recurrence is common

Even after successful treatment, many experience recurrence:

  • 24% have recurrence within 4 years after non-operative treatment
  • 25% of those who were recovered at 2 years had recurrence by 4 years
  • Recurring back pain (43%) is more common than recurring leg radiation

This underscores the importance of preventive exercises and good ergonomics - even after recovery.

5

Diagnosis

Clinical examination

Diagnosis is primarily based on medical history and clinical examination. A thorough neurological examination can often locate which level in the spine is affected.

Straight leg raise test is the most predictive test for identifying candidates for surgery. The test is positive when it reproduces the patient's pain with a leg raise under 45 degrees with radiation below the knee (StatPearls, 2023).

Imaging

MRI or CT is not routinely recommended. Danish national guidelines discourage routine imaging for new-onset sciatica (European Spine Journal, 2018).

Important to understand

Imaging often shows normal findings like wear and degenerative changes that don't explain the pain. Studies show that up to 20-80% of symptom-free people have MRI findings like disc bulging, degeneration or protrusion (StatPearls, 2023).

Imaging is indicated for:

  • Suspicion of serious pathology (red flags)
  • Persistent symptoms after 6-8 weeks of conservative treatment
  • When considering surgery
6

Treatment

Conservative treatment (first choice)

Danish national guidelines recommend manual therapy (including spinal manipulation) combined with guided exercise for lumbar radiculopathy (Stochkendahl et al., European Spine Journal, 2018).

What can the chiropractor do?

  • Manual therapy: Spinal manipulation and mobilization can relieve pain and improve mobility
  • Soft tissue treatment: Muscle work on piriformis, gluteal muscles and hamstrings for false sciatica
  • Neural mobilization: Nerve-glide exercises to improve nerve mobility
  • Exercise guidance: Core muscle training and graded activity tailored to your situation

What can you do yourself?

  • Stay active - avoid prolonged bed rest. Gentle movement is important.
  • Nerve-glide exercises - gentle stretching that mobilizes the nerve without irritating it.
  • Ergonomic adjustments - avoid prolonged sitting, take breaks.
  • Pain relief as needed in the acute phase.
  • Be patient - improvement often takes 10-12 weeks.

Surgery

Surgery is only considered when:

  • Serious conservative treatment for 6-12 weeks doesn't provide sufficient improvement
  • There are progressive neurological deficits (increasing muscle weakness)
  • For cauda equina syndrome (acute surgical condition)

Important about surgery

Surgery provides faster improvement in the short term, but after 1-2 years results are equally good with conservative treatment. A Dutch study showed no significant difference in function or pain after 1 year between early surgery and extended conservative treatment (Peul et al., NEJM, 2007).

Red Flags - When You Must Seek Medical Attention Immediately

CAUDA EQUINA SYNDROME - CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES

  • Numbness in the saddle area (around rectum/genitals)
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Bilateral (both legs) rapidly increasing weakness

This is an emergency surgical situation. Delay can cause permanent damage. Cauda equina syndrome is rare (0.3-7 cases per 100,000 annually), but the consequences of delayed treatment are serious.

Other serious signs requiring medical evaluation:

  • Progressive muscle weakness (increasing day by day)
  • Foot drop (difficulty lifting the foot)
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Known cancer with new back pain

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

Here's the good news: the prognosis for sciatica is generally good, but it often takes longer than many think.

The positive:

  • The majority improve without surgery
  • 50% experience significant improvement within 4 weeks
  • 55-70% have recovered within a year
  • Disc herniations can shrink and be reabsorbed over time

The realistic:

  • Median time to improvement is 10-12 weeks - not 6 weeks
  • 20-30% have persistent symptoms after 1-2 years
  • Recurrence affects about 1 in 4 within a few years

Frequently Asked Questions

Can sciatica resolve on its own?
Yes, many cases improve spontaneously over time. However, early treatment can lead to faster recovery and reduce the risk of chronic symptoms.
Do I need an MRI?
Not routinely. MRI is indicated when serious pathology is suspected, when there's no improvement after 6-8 weeks, or when surgery is being considered. Many normal age-related changes appear on MRI without explaining the pain.
Should I rest?
No. Prolonged bed rest is discouraged. Gentle movement and activity as tolerated is important for recovery.
What is the difference between sciatica and back pain?
Sciatica is pain that radiates down the leg from nerve root irritation. Common back pain (which accounts for 80-90% of cases) mainly causes pain in the back itself, without nerve involvement.
How many treatments are needed?
This varies. Median time to improvement is 10-12 weeks. I evaluate progress throughout and adjust the plan accordingly.
Can a disc herniation shrink?
Yes. The body has a remarkable ability for self-healing. Disc herniations can shrink and be reabsorbed over time, and the inflammation irritating the nerve gradually subsides.
