SI Joint Pain and Pelvic Dysfunction

Causes, symptoms, diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

The sacroiliac joint - called the SI joint - is the connection between the sacrum and the hip bone (ilium) on each side of the pelvis. You have two SI joints, one on each side. These joints are among the body's largest and have a very special task: They transfer all load from the upper body down to the legs.

SI joint pain accounts for 15-30% of all chronic lower back pain (Pain Medicine, 2025). Women are affected more often than men - they make up 60-65% of patients. This is due to women's wider pelvic angle, greater joint mobility, and hormonal factors during pregnancy.

Quick Summary

  • 15-30% of chronic lower back pain is caused by the SI joint
  • Biomechanical overload is the cause in 88% of cases
  • 72% success rate with manual treatment
  • 95% respond well to conservative treatment
  • Red flags: Fever with pelvic pain, morning stiffness >30-60 min

What is the SI Joint?

The SI joint is a unique joint - part synovial joint (movable), part fibrous joint (stable). Normal movement is very limited: only 2-4 mm rotation and 1-2 mm gliding. Nevertheless, these minimal movements are essential for normal walking function and for absorbing shock.

Why does the SI joint cause pain in the back, buttocks and thigh?

The SI joint receives sensory innervation from both anterior (L2-S2) and posterior (L4-S3) nerve roots. This complex innervation explains why SI joint pain can produce varying referral patterns - from lower back to buttocks, groin, thigh and sometimes down toward the foot.

How common is SI joint pain?

International consensus guidelines from 2025, developed in collaboration between 27 organizations, establish that the SI joint is the cause of 15-30% of chronic lower back pain without radiation down the leg (Pain Medicine, 2025). A systematic review by Simopoulos et al. found point prevalence of approximately 25%.

15-30% of chronic lower back pain
60-65% of patients are women
2-4 mm normal joint mobility

Pelvic Locking vs. Pelvic Loosening

The terms "pelvic locking" and "pelvic loosening" describe two completely different conditions that require different treatment approaches:

Pelvic Locking (Hypomobility)

Occurs when the SI joint becomes too stiff or "locks up". The joint doesn't move as it should, causing sharp, localized pain with certain movements and a feeling that the pelvis "gets stuck".

Pelvic Loosening (Hypermobility)

Occurs when the joints become too loose and unstable. Typical during pregnancy due to the hormone relaxin. Causes diffuse, aching pain that is difficult to localize, and a feeling of instability in the pelvis.

Causes and Risk Factors

Pain from the SI joint can have both intra-articular (inside the joint) and extra-articular (around the joint) causes. Here are the most common, ranked from most to least frequent:

1. Biomechanical Overload and Microtrauma (88%)

By far the most common cause (StatPearls, 2024). This includes repetitive stress from work or sports, asymmetric standing or sitting over time, heavy lifting with twisting, ligament injury and myofascial trigger points.

Athletes have increased risk

  • Rowing gives 54% prevalence in elite athletes
  • Golf and tennis create risk through rotation with axial loading
  • Football/rugby through contact injuries

2. Pregnancy and Childbirth

45-50% of pregnant women experience pelvic pain. Filipec et al. (2018) found that 78.7% of first-time pregnant women develop SI joint dysfunction. Most improve within 3-4 months after delivery, but 8-10% have persistent problems 1-2 years later.

3. Previous Spinal Surgery (15-33%)

When vertebrae are surgically fused, the load on adjacent segments increases. 15.8% develop SI joint pain after lumbar fusion, and as many as 32.9% when the fusion includes the sacrum.

4. Trauma and Falls

44% of patients with confirmed SI joint pain have identifiable traumatic events. In car accident-related chronic lower back pain, the SI joint is the pain source in 26% of cases.

5. Leg Length Difference

Leg length difference over 10 mm dramatically increases the load:

  • 1 cm = nearly 3x increase in load
  • 2 cm = 5x increase
  • 3 cm = 7x increase

Symptoms - What SI Joint Pain Feels Like

Pain Location

The pain is usually located right over the SI joint - slightly to the side from the midline, just below the hip crest. Most experience one-sided pain localized to the area around or just below the posterior superior iliac spine (PSIS). In 94% of cases, the patient can point to the pain within 10 cm below and 3 cm lateral to the PSIS.

Fortin's Finger Test - A Simple Self-Test

Point with one finger to where it hurts most. Patients with SI joint pain typically point to an area within 1 cm of the posterior iliac spine. This test has shown 77% sensitivity and 100% specificity in studies.

Referred Pain

The pain can radiate to:

  • Buttocks (most common)
  • Groin (14%)
  • Back and outside of thigh (50%)
  • Below the knee (28%) - but rarely further down

Important: With pure SI joint pain, the pain usually doesn't go below the knee - unlike true sciatica/radiculopathy.

Aggravating Factors

  • Getting up from a seated position
  • Walking stairs (especially up)
  • Standing on one leg
  • Turning over in bed
  • Driving (asymmetric pedal use)
  • Prolonged sitting or standing

Diagnosis - How is the Diagnosis Made?

Clinical Examination is the Gold Standard

SI joint problems cannot be seen on X-ray or MRI. The diagnosis is made primarily clinically through pain location, provocation tests, and palpation. Individual tests have limited value - therefore a cluster of provocation tests is used. Three or more positive tests give 94% sensitivity and 78% specificity.

The Five Most Important Provocation Tests

Distraction test, compression test, thigh thrust, Gaenslen's test, and sacral thrust are used in combination to make the diagnosis.

Differential Diagnosis

Herniation/Sciatica Radiation below knee, sensory loss, weakness Neurological deficits, positive straight leg raise
Hip Osteoarthritis Groin pain, stiffness on rotation Pain in front, not back
Ank. Spondylitis Bilateral, morning stiffness >30-60 min Onset before 40, improves with activity
Piriformis Syndrome Deep buttock pain Worsens with sitting, positive FAIR test

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Conservative treatment is the first choice. A recent systematic review by Trager et al. (JMMT, 2024) with 16 RCTs and 776 patients found significant improvement in function. Visser et al. (2013) compared three approaches:

72% success with manual treatment
50% success with cortisone injection
20% success with exercise alone

Treatment Methods

  • Joint Manipulation (HVLA): Quick, controlled movement that often produces an audible "crack"
  • Mobilization: Slower, repeated movements to improve joint mobility
  • Muscle Energy Technique (MET): The patient actively contracts against resistance
  • Soft Tissue Treatment: Of gluteus, piriformis and quadratus lumborum

What Can You Do Yourself?

Stabilization exercises are crucial for lasting improvement. Norwegian research from Britt Stuge at OUS showed that specific stabilization exercises gave 68% success rate versus only 23% for general physiotherapy - with effects that held at 2-year follow-up.

Pelvic Bridge

  • Lie on your back with bent knees
  • Draw your navel in toward your spine
  • Lift your buttocks calmly toward the ceiling, hold 5-10 seconds
  • 3 sets x 10 repetitions

Bird Dog

  • Start on all fours, tighten your stomach lightly
  • Lift opposite arm and leg, keep your body stable
  • Hold 10 seconds, 3 sets x 8 repetitions each side

Pelvic Belt

A tight belt can provide immediate pain relief by stabilizing the SI joint. Studies show that 82% experience improvement with a belt after 4 weeks. Particularly effective in the acute phase and for pregnancy-related problems. Place just below the hip crest, not around the waist. Does not replace exercises.

Treatment Course

Acute phase (weeks 1-4): 2-3 treatments per week. Focus on pain relief and restoration of function.

Follow-up phase (weeks 4-12): 1-2 treatments per week. Progression of exercise program.

Typically 4-8 treatments over 2-6 weeks are needed. Many notice improvement after 2-3 treatments.

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

EMERGENCY - Seek immediate care for:

  • Sudden bladder or bowel control problems
  • Numbness in groin/buttocks (saddle anesthesia)
  • Rapidly increasing weakness in both legs
  • Fever combined with severe pelvic pain

URGENT - Book a doctor's appointment soon for:

  • Pain that started before age 40 and lasts >3 months
  • Pronounced morning stiffness lasting >30-60 minutes
  • Bilateral (two-sided) pain in young patient
  • Pain improves with activity, worsens with rest
  • Known psoriasis, IBD, or uveitis
  • Unexplained weight loss or previous cancer

Prognosis - What to Expect?

Generally Good Prognosis

Up to 95% of patients respond well to conservative treatment with significant pain reduction within 1-3 months. 80% improve significantly within 2 weeks.

Recurrence is common: The recurrence rate is over 30% in chronic cases. The risk increases if you resume strenuous activities too quickly or stop doing stabilization exercises. Maintenance training 2 times per week can prevent recurrence.

Pregnancy-Related SI Joint Pain

  • Most improve within 3-4 months after delivery
  • 25% still have pain after 3 months, only 10% after 8 months
  • 8-10% have persistent problems 1-2 years postpartum

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the SI joint "go out of place"?
No, not in the usual sense. The SI joint is one of the body's most stable joints, reinforced by strong ligaments. It cannot "pop out" like a shoulder. What happens is that the joint's movement can become asymmetric or blocked. "Pelvic locking" describes this functional blockage.
How do I know if the pain comes from the SI joint and not the back?
SI joint pain is typically localized just below the lower back, over the pelvic ring itself. You can often point to the pain with one finger. The pain worsens with stair climbing, getting up from a chair, and turning in bed. Unlike sciatica, pure SI joint pain rarely causes symptoms below the knee.
Do I need an MRI or X-ray?
Usually not. Imaging has limited value for mechanical SI joint pain and is primarily used to rule out other conditions. Clinical examination with provocation tests is often more informative.
What's the difference between SI joint pain and sciatica?
SI joint pain typically radiates to the buttocks and thigh, but rarely below the knee. No neurological deficits. Sciatica is caused by nerve root involvement and often causes pain all the way to the foot, with numbness, tingling or weakness.
Can I exercise with SI joint pain?
Yes, but adapted. Avoid activities that worsen pain in the acute phase. Swimming, cycling and walking are often well tolerated. Strength training for glutes and core muscles is important for long-term improvement.
Is an SI belt useful?
Yes, especially in the acute phase and for pregnancy-related problems. 82% experience improvement after 4 weeks. The belt does not replace exercises, but is a useful supplement.
