Spinal Stenosis - Narrowing in the Spine

Causes, symptoms, diagnosis and treatment at the chiropractor

Spinal stenosis means that the spinal canal or nerve exits in the spine have become narrower than normal. The word "stenosis" comes from Greek and simply means "narrowing." The condition most often affects the lower back (lumbar stenosis), but can also occur in the neck.

Many are worried when they receive this diagnosis, but here's the good news: Most people who have stenosis on MRI images have no symptoms at all. And even among those with symptoms, most manage well without surgery.

In this article, you'll learn what spinal stenosis really is, what causes it, how it's diagnosed, and most importantly - what can be done to help you.

TL;DR - Key Takeaways

  • 47% over 60 have stenosis on MRI
  • Most common cause: Age-related changes
  • Classic sign: Easier to cycle than walk
  • Conservative treatment works for most
  • Red flags: Bladder/bowel problems, saddle numbness

What is Spinal Stenosis?

Spinal stenosis is a condition where the spinal canal (where the spinal cord and nerves run) or nerve exits (foramen) become narrower. This can lead to pressure on nerves and cause symptoms like pain, numbness or weakness in the legs.

The most important thing to understand is that stenosis on MRI images doesn't necessarily mean you have problems. Many people have narrowing in their spine without noticing anything at all.

11% of the general population
47% of people aged 60-69
78% have stenosis on MRI without symptoms

Important Point

The Wakayama Spine Study found that 77.9% of participants had moderate central stenosis on imaging, but only 17.5% of those with severe stenosis actually had symptoms. This means MRI images should never guide treatment alone.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Spinal stenosis is almost always caused by age-related changes in the spine. Over 95% of cases are degenerative stenosis.

Facet Joint Osteoarthritis

Wear in the small joints at the back of the spine leading to thickening and bone spurs.

Thickening of Ligamentum Flavum

The yellow ligament located behind the spinal canal can become thicker with age and take up space from the nerves.

Disc Degeneration

The discs between the vertebrae lose height and can bulge into the canal. This can also lead to herniation.

Osteophytes

Bone spurs that grow into the spinal canal or nerve exits.

Risk Factors with Documented Association

  • Age over 60: 3-5 times higher prevalence
  • Obesity: BMI over 30 gives more than double the risk
  • Diabetes: Found in 29% of stenosis patients vs 10% in the general population
  • Spondylolisthesis: Vertebral slippage can contribute to stenosis
3

Symptoms

The classic symptom of lumbar spinal stenosis is called neurogenic claudication - a term describing the typical pattern many experience:

  • Pain, heaviness or numbness in one or both legs
  • Symptoms come gradually when walking or standing upright
  • Relief when sitting down, bending forward or leaning on a shopping cart
  • Often easier to cycle than walk (because you sit slightly forward-leaning)

These symptoms can resemble sciatica, but with stenosis the typical pattern is that symptoms worsen with walking and improve with rest in a forward-bent position.

The Shopping Cart Sign

This is so characteristic that it's used diagnostically worldwide. Many patients report they can walk much further when leaning on a shopping cart. This is because leaning forward expands the spinal canal and gives more room for the nerves.

4

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

As a chiropractor, I perform a thorough examination including assessment of gait pattern and posture, testing of reflexes, strength and sensation in the legs, and provocation of symptoms in different positions.

Imaging - An Important Caveat

MRI is the first-choice examination when imaging is indicated. But here's a very important point: There is often poor correlation between MRI findings and symptoms.

MRI Findings in Symptom-Free People

Studies show that 21% of symptom-free people over 60 have stenosis on MRI, and that 65% of healthy volunteers received a stenosis diagnosis from radiologists. This means MRI images should never guide treatment alone.

Imaging is most relevant for:

  • Suspicion of serious pathology (red flags)
  • Persistent symptoms after 6-8 weeks of conservative treatment
  • When considering surgery
5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

There is moderate evidence that conservative treatment with manual therapy and targeted exercises helps with lumbar spinal stenosis.

A large randomized study (Whitman, Spine 2006) showed that 79% achieved improvement after 6 weeks of treatment with manual therapy and exercises, compared to 41% in the group that only received flexion exercises.

Treatment May Include

  • Manual therapy to improve joint mobility
  • Flexion-distraction that increases space in the spinal canal
  • Myofascial techniques
  • Individually tailored exercise program

What Can You Do Yourself?

Flexion-based exercises have moderate evidence and utilize the simple mechanism that leaning forward gives more room for the nerves:

Knee-to-Chest Stretch

  • Lying on your back, pull one or both knees toward your chest
  • Hold for 20-30 seconds
  • Repeat 3-5 times

Core Stabilization

  • Exercises for abdominal and back muscles
  • Bridge, plank, bird-dog
  • Focus on controlled movement

Treatment Course

Based on available literature, a trial period of 8-12 treatments over 6-12 weeks is usually recommended. Many experience first signs of improvement after 2-4 weeks and significant progress in function after 6-12 weeks.

6

Red Flags - When You Must Contact a Doctor Immediately

EMERGENCY - CALL FOR HELP WITH THESE SYMPTOMS:

  • Sudden loss of bladder control - cannot hold urine or empty the bladder
  • Numbness in the saddle area - the area you sit on
  • Loss of bowel control
  • Rapidly increasing weakness in both legs

This may be a sign of cauda equina syndrome - a rare but serious complication requiring prompt surgical evaluation. Occurs in about 0.3% of stenosis patients. Read more about red flags.

7

Prognosis - What Happens Long Term?

Here I have good news: The natural course of spinal stenosis is often more favorable than many think.

Without Treatment (JAMA Review 2022)

About 33% report spontaneous improvement, 50% remain stable, and 10-20% experience worsening over 3 years.

Surgery vs Conservative Treatment

The large SPORT study showed that surgery had somewhat better results in the short term, but after 6-8 years there was no significant difference between surgical and conservative treatment.

Recurrence: It's important to be realistic - this is a condition related to aging of the spine, and recurrence can occur. Regular maintenance exercise is important to maintain function.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get better without surgery?
Yes, most people with spinal stenosis manage well without surgery. Long-term studies show that conservative treatment gives equally good results as surgery after 6-8 years for most patients.
Why do symptoms improve when I sit down?
When you bend your back forward, the spinal canal expands and gives more room for the nerves. When you extend backward, the canal becomes narrower. This is why many find it easier to cycle than to walk.
Do I need an MRI for the diagnosis?
Not necessarily. Clinical examination is often sufficient to make the diagnosis and start treatment. Remember that MRI findings often don't correlate with symptoms.
How long does a treatment series last?
Usually 8-12 treatments over 6-12 weeks are recommended as an initial trial period. Then the need for maintenance treatment or self-exercise is evaluated.
Can I continue exercising with spinal stenosis?
Absolutely! Activity is important. Cycling, swimming and water aerobics are often well tolerated because the body is in a forward-leaning position that opens up the spinal canal.
