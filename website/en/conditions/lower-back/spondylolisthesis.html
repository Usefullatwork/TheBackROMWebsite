Spondylolisthesis: Vertebral Slippage

Causes, grades, symptoms and treatment options

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

You may have been told that you have spondylolisthesis - that a vertebra in your spine has slipped forward. The word sounds dramatic, and it's natural to be worried. But what does it actually mean for you?

Spondylolisthesis is actually quite common, and most cases are mild and can be managed well without surgery. Let's look at what this condition involves, how it's graded, and what you can do to live well with it.

The most important thing to understand is that a structural change on an image doesn't necessarily mean you have to have pain. Many people with spondylolisthesis live completely symptom-free.

TL;DR - Key Takeaways

  • Most common at L4-L5 and L5-S1 level
  • Grade I-II (up to 50% slippage) treated conservatively
  • Core muscle training is key
  • Chiropractic + exercises recommended
  • Surgery only for severe cases

What is Spondylolisthesis?

Spondylolisthesis (from Greek: spondylos = vertebra, listhesis = slippage) is a condition where a vertebra in the spine slips forward relative to the vertebra below. This most often occurs in the lower part of the lower back, particularly at L4-L5 or L5-S1 level.

The slippage can be congenital, occur after an injury, or develop gradually over time due to wear. Regardless of the cause, it's important to understand that the degree of slippage doesn't always correlate with the degree of pain.

How common is spondylolisthesis?

Spondylolisthesis is more common than many think. Studies show that up to 5-7% of the population has some form of spondylolisthesis, but many never know because they have no symptoms.

5-7% of the population
L5-S1 most common level
85% are grade I or II

Types and Grades of Spondylolisthesis

Five main types

1. Isthmic spondylolisthesis

Most common

Caused by a defect or fracture in the pars interarticularis (spondylolysis). Most common in young athletes, especially in sports with lots of extension and rotation like gymnastics, wrestling and weightlifting.

2. Degenerative spondylolisthesis

Most common in elderly

Occurs due to age-related wear in facet joints and intervertebral discs. Most common in people over 50, especially women, and typically occurs at the L4-L5 level.

3. Dysplastic (congenital) spondylolisthesis

Caused by a congenital malformation in the vertebra that causes instability. Often discovered in childhood or adolescence.

4. Traumatic spondylolisthesis

Occurs after acute injury or fracture of the vertebra. Can be caused by accidents, falls or sports injuries.

5. Pathological spondylolisthesis

Caused by disease that weakens bone tissue, for example osteoporosis, infection or tumor.

Meyerding Classification: Grades of Slippage

The slippage is measured as a percentage of the vertebra's width:

Grade Slippage Significance
Grade I 0-25% Mild - often asymptomatic
Grade II 25-50% Moderate - conservative treatment
Grade III 50-75% Severe - may require surgery
Grade IV 75-100% Very severe
Grade V >100% Spondyloptosis (complete slippage)

Important to know

The vast majority of cases (about 85%) are grade I or II, which usually respond well to conservative treatment. High-grade slippages are rare.

Symptoms of Spondylolisthesis

Many people with spondylolisthesis have no symptoms at all. When symptoms occur, they depend on the degree of slippage and any nerve involvement.

Common symptoms

  • Lower back pain: Often worse with activity and better at rest
  • Stiffness: Especially in the morning or after sitting still
  • Hamstring tightness: Characteristic finding, can cause altered gait pattern
  • Pain with backward bending: Extension often provokes symptoms
  • Muscle spasms: In lower back and gluteal muscles

With nerve involvement

Radiculopathy (nerve root symptoms)

  • Pain radiating down the leg (sciatica-like)
  • Numbness or tingling in the leg or foot
  • Weakness in the leg or foot
  • Worse with walking, better with rest or forward bending

Neurogenic claudication

With degenerative spondylolisthesis, narrowing of the spinal canal (stenosis) can develop. This often causes pain and fatigue in the legs when walking, which is relieved by sitting or bending forward (for example over a shopping cart).

4

Diagnosis

Clinical examination

The examination includes assessment of posture, mobility, gait pattern and neurological status. A classic finding is a visible or palpable "step deformity" in the lower back where the vertebra has slipped forward.

Other clinical findings may include:

  • Reduced lumbar mobility, especially in extension
  • Tight hamstrings
  • Characteristic waddling gait in severe cases
  • Neurological findings if nerve root is affected

Imaging

X-ray

First choice

Standing X-ray images show the slippage and grade. Oblique projections can show the pars defect ("Scottie dog" sign).

MRI

Shows soft tissues, discs, nerves and any compression. Important for neurological symptoms or surgical planning.

CT

Provides detailed image of bone structures and pars defects. Useful for preoperative planning.

Treatment

Conservative treatment (first choice)

Most people with spondylolisthesis grade I-II respond well to conservative treatment. The goal is to strengthen stabilizing muscles, improve mobility and reduce pain.

What can the chiropractor do?

  • Manual therapy: Careful mobilization and soft tissue treatment to improve mobility and reduce muscle tension
  • Stabilizing exercises: Core muscle training tailored to your condition
  • Posture guidance: Learn correct technique for daily activities
  • Ergonomic advice: Adaptations at work and home

Important exercises

  • Pelvic tilts: Strengthens deep abdominal muscles and stabilizes the lower back
  • Dead bug: Core muscle training without loading the back
  • Bird dog: Balance and core muscle control
  • Plank: Static core muscle strength
  • Bridge: Strengthens gluteus and hamstrings

Avoid exercises with extreme backward bending (extension) of the spine.

What can you do yourself?

  • Stay active: Moderate activity is more important than rest
  • Regular exercise: Focus on core muscle strength and flexibility
  • Weight control: Excess weight increases load on the spine
  • Good ergonomics: Correct sitting and lifting technique
  • Avoid provoking activities: Reduce activities involving lots of backward bending

Surgery

Surgery is only considered when:

  • Conservative treatment for 3-6 months doesn't provide sufficient relief
  • There are progressive neurological symptoms
  • High-grade slippages (grade III or higher)
  • Significantly reduced quality of life

Types of surgery

Decompression: Removes tissue pressing on nerves. Fusion: Fuses the vertebrae together to prevent further slippage. Often these are combined.

6

Red Flags - When You Must Seek Medical Help

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY FOR:

  • Sudden loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Numbness in the saddle area (around rectum/genitals)
  • Rapidly increasing weakness in both legs
  • Severe, sudden worsening of symptoms

These symptoms may indicate cauda equina syndrome, which requires urgent medical evaluation.

Other symptoms requiring medical evaluation:

  • Progressive muscle weakness in leg or foot
  • Symptoms that don't improve after 6-8 weeks of treatment
  • Significant functional loss in daily life
  • New-onset back pain in children or adolescents (to rule out high-grade slippages)

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

The prognosis for spondylolisthesis is generally good, especially for low-grade slippages.

The positive:

  • Most with grade I-II manage well with conservative treatment
  • Many are completely symptom-free even with documented slippage
  • Slippage usually stabilizes in adults
  • Regular exercise can prevent worsening

The realistic:

  • The condition often requires lifelong attention to exercise and ergonomics
  • Some activities may need to be modified
  • Flare-ups can occur
  • Regular monitoring can be useful

In children and adolescents

Young athletes with spondylolysis or low-grade spondylolisthesis have a good prognosis. Most can return to their sport after a period of rest and rehabilitation. Regular monitoring during growth periods is important to detect any progression.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is spondylolisthesis?
Spondylolisthesis is a condition where a vertebra in the spine slips forward relative to the vertebra below. This most often occurs in the lower back, particularly at L4-L5 or L5-S1 level.
Is spondylolisthesis dangerous?
Most cases are mild (grade I-II) and can be managed well with conservative treatment. More severe grades may require surgical evaluation, but this is rare.
Can you exercise with spondylolisthesis?
Yes, exercise is important. Focus should be on core muscle training and stabilizing exercises. Avoid exercises with extreme extension (backward bending) of the spine.
Do I need surgery for spondylolisthesis?
Most people with spondylolisthesis don't need surgery. Surgery is only considered for severe grades, progressive neurological symptoms, or when conservative treatment doesn't provide sufficient relief.
What is the difference between spondylolisthesis and spondylolysis?
Spondylolysis is a fracture or defect in the pars interarticularis (part of the vertebra), while spondylolisthesis is the actual slippage of the vertebra. Spondylolysis can lead to spondylolisthesis over time.
Can spondylolisthesis worsen over time?
In most adults, the condition is stable. In children and adolescents, slippage can increase during growth periods. Regular monitoring and stabilizing training can prevent worsening.
