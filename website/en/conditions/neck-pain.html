Neck Pain - Complete Guide to Causes, Treatment and Red Flags

Everything you need to know about causes, symptoms and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua Oslo

Neck pain is one of the most common musculoskeletal complaints in the world. Norway actually has the world's highest prevalence of neck pain, with 6,151 per 100,000 inhabitants (Safiri et al., BMJ 2020). About one in three experience neck problems during a year, and two in three will have neck pain at some point in their lives.

Most cases are what's called non-specific neck pain - meaning complaints that aren't caused by a specific injury or disease, but rather strain, stiff muscles or joint irritation.

Key Facts

  • Norway has the world's highest prevalence with 6,151 per 100,000
  • Most common causes: Muscle/fascia problems (80-90%), facet joints, trigger points
  • Rare causes: Nerve root involvement (approx. 1 per 1000 annually)
  • Early treatment gives twice the chance of improvement
  • Red flags: Clumsy hands + gait problems, thunderclap headache

What Causes Neck Pain?

Non-specific neck pain (80-90% of cases)

Between 80 and 90 percent of all neck pain has no specific cause detectable on X-ray or MRI. This doesn't mean the pain is imaginary - it can be very troublesome.

Typical characteristics: Stiffness, soreness and heaviness in neck and shoulders. Pain often comes gradually and may be related to sedentary work, stress, poor sleep or one-sided strain.

Myofascial pain syndrome (88.9% have trigger points)

Tight muscles with trigger points - tender knots - are very common with neck pain. A study found that 88.9% of patients with chronic non-specific neck pain had at least one trigger point.

Facet joint pain (54-60% of chronic cases)

The facet joints are the small joints on the back of neck vertebrae. In chronic neck pain, studies show facet joints are the pain source in 54-60% of patients.

Text neck and forward head posture

Modern smartphone use puts significant strain on the neck. At 60 degrees forward (typical phone position), the neck bears 27 kg of load instead of the normal 4.5-5 kg head weight.

2

Cervical Radiculopathy - Nerve Root Pain

When a nerve in the neck gets pinched, it can cause radiation down the arm with tingling, numbness or weakness. This affects approximately 0.8-1.8 per 1000 people annually. The good news: 75-90% improve with conservative treatment.

Which nerve is affected?

  • C6 nerve: Numbness in thumb and index finger, weak biceps
  • C7 nerve: Most common (60-70%). Numbness in middle finger, weak triceps
  • C8 nerve: Numbness in ring/little finger, weak grip

Bakody's sign

If symptoms improve when you place your hand on your head (shoulder in abduction), it strongly suggests the problem comes from the neck. This test has 80-100% specificity for cervical radiculopathy.

3

Conditions That Often Accompany Neck Pain

Cervicogenic dizziness (43% of patients)

43% of patients with prolonged neck pain also report dizziness. The neck has a high density of position sensors - when these malfunction due to muscle tightness or joint dysfunction, a sensory conflict occurs creating dizziness.

Cervicogenic headache (15-20% of all headaches)

Headache originating from the neck affects approximately 2.2% of the population. Typically starts in the neck and radiates toward forehead or eye - often one-sided.

Red Flags - When You Must Act Quickly

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY

  • Neck pain after trauma with numbness or weakness
  • Fever combined with stiff neck and headache (possible infection)
  • Sudden, intense headache reaching max in under 1 minute ("thunderclap")
  • Increasing weakness in arms or legs
  • Problems with urination or bowel control
  • Clumsy hands + gait difficulties
5

MRI and Degeneration - "Gray Hair for the Spine"

Many become worried when MRI shows "wear and tear" or "degeneration". But such findings are normal in adults and rarely explain your pain.

  • 88.5% of healthy volunteers have cervical degenerative changes
  • 52% of asymptomatic adults have disc bulging
  • 86% over 60 have disc signal loss

When is imaging necessary?

For ordinary neck pain, imaging is usually not necessary. Only relevant when there's suspicion of serious underlying disease, after significant trauma, or if you don't respond to treatment over time.

6

Treatment of Neck Pain

The combination of manual treatment and exercises has documented effect for neck pain. A 2023 meta-analysis found that manipulative therapy gave better results on pain and function compared to control groups.

Why early treatment pays off

A 2016 study showed early treatment gave twice the chance of clinically meaningful improvement in both pain and function. Early intervention was also more cost-effective.

Treatment course expectations

  • Acute neck pain (under 6 weeks): 2-3 treatments/week for 2-4 weeks, total 6-12 treatments
  • Chronic problems (over 12 weeks): 1-2 treatments/week over longer period with home exercises

Prognosis

  • 50-85% experience recurrence within 1-5 years
  • About 30% of acute cases develop chronic problems
  • For cervical radiculopathy: 43% become symptom-free, 29% mild problems, 27% persistent

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do typical neck pains last?

Most acute episodes resolve within 2-6 weeks. Improvement happens fastest in the first 6-12 weeks.

Should I wait and see if it goes away?

No, early treatment often gives better results. Treatment within 4 weeks doubles the chance of significant improvement.

Can neck pain cause dizziness?

Yes, 43% of patients with prolonged neck pain report dizziness. Treatment of neck problems often improves dizziness.

Do I need an MRI of the neck?

For ordinary neck pain, MRI is rarely necessary. 88.5% of healthy people have degenerative changes without symptoms.
