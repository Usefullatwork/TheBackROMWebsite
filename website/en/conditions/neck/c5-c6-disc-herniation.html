C5-C6 Disc Herniation: Thumb and Index Finger Numbness

C6 Radiculopathy: Symptoms, Prognosis, and Treatment - Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

You've woken up with a stiff neck for several days. Gradually, you've developed a tingling, numb sensation in your thumb and index finger. Perhaps you notice it's harder to bend your elbow when lifting something, or that pain radiates down your arm when you turn your head.

These are classic signs of C5-C6 disc herniation, where the herniation presses on the C6 nerve root. It's the second most common form of cervical radiculopathy and affects approximately 25% of all people with neck radiculopathy.

The good news? The vast majority recover without surgery. Let's look at what happens, how it differs from other conditions, and what you can do.

Key Points

  • C5-C6 = 25% of cervical radiculopathy
  • Symptom: Numb thumb/index finger, weak biceps
  • 85% recover within 8-12 weeks
  • Manual treatment + exercises recommended
  • Don't confuse with carpal tunnel syndrome
1

What is C5-C6 Disc Herniation?

C5-C6 refers to the level in the neck where the herniation occurs, between the fifth and sixth cervical vertebrae. When the disc between these vertebrae bulges out or ruptures, it can press on the nerve root that exits at this level: the C6 nerve.

Cervical radiculopathy (nerve root involvement in the neck) affects approximately 83 per 100,000 people annually. The C5-C6 level is involved in about 25% of cases, making it the second most common level after C6-C7.

Anatomy: The C6 Nerve Root

The C6 nerve supplies specific areas in the arm and hand:

  • Sensory: The thumb, index finger, and outer forearm
  • Motor: Biceps (bends the elbow) and wrist extensor muscles
  • Reflex: The brachioradialis reflex (tested on the outer forearm)

Why Does Herniation Occur at the C5-C6 Level?

The C5-C6 segment is one of the most mobile areas in the neck. This high mobility makes the disc more susceptible to wear and injury over time. Risk factors include:

  • Prolonged sedentary work with forward head posture
  • Previous neck injuries (e.g., whiplash)
  • Genetic predisposition to disc degeneration
  • Age (most common between 40-50 years)
  • Smoking (reduces blood supply to the discs)

Symptoms of C5-C6 Disc Herniation

The symptoms of C5-C6 disc herniation are quite specific and follow the distribution of the C6 nerve. They can vary in intensity from mild numbness to significant pain and weakness.

Typical C6 Symptoms

  • Pain: Radiates from the neck, out to the shoulder, down the outer upper arm to the thumb and index finger
  • Numbness/tingling: Thumb and index finger, sometimes the middle finger
  • Weakness: Difficulty bending the elbow (biceps) or extending the wrist
  • Reflex change: Reduced brachioradialis reflex

Pain Provocation

Symptoms typically worsen with:

  • Turning the head toward the affected side
  • Bending the neck backward (extension)
  • Coughing or sneezing
  • Pressing down on the head (Spurling's test)

Bakody's Sign - Relief from Raising the Arm

A characteristic finding in cervical radiculopathy is that pain is relieved when you place your hand on your head. This reduces tension on the nerve root and is a useful diagnostic sign.

Pain Pattern Throughout the Day

With cervical radiculopathy, pain is often worst at night and early morning. This is because the neck remains in a static position for a long time, and any swelling around the nerve has time to accumulate.

3

Is It C5-C6 Disc Herniation or Carpal Tunnel Syndrome?

This is one of the most important questions to clarify, because the treatment is very different. Both conditions cause numbness in the fingers, but the causes and patterns are different.

Characteristic C5-C6 Herniation (C6 Radiculopathy) Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
Numbness Thumb and index finger Thumb, index finger, middle finger, half of ring finger
Pain location Neck, shoulder, arm Wrist, palm
Worsened by Neck movements, Spurling's test Repetitive hand work, bent wrist at night
Relieved by Arm over head (Bakody's sign) Shaking the hand (Flick sign)
Weakness Biceps, wrist extension Thumb grip (thenar atrophy in severe cases)
Worst when Morning, after static neck position At night, early morning

Double Crush Syndrome

In some cases, you can have both conditions simultaneously. The nerve can be irritated both in the neck and at the wrist. This is called "double crush syndrome" and requires treatment of both locations for optimal recovery.

Other Conditions to Rule Out

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS)

The nerve is compressed between neck muscles and ribs. Symptoms can be similar, but often worsen when raising the arm overhead (opposite of Bakody's sign).

Shoulder Pathology

Rotator cuff injuries or impingement can cause radiation to the arm. Shoulder mobility is usually normal with pure neck problems.

4

Prognosis: How Long Does It Take?

The prognosis for C5-C6 disc herniation is generally good. Research shows that the vast majority recover with conservative treatment, without the need for surgery.

85% significant improvement without surgery
2-4 weeks to first noticeable improvement
8-12 weeks median time to significant improvement

What Affects Prognosis?

Good Prognostic Factors

  • Early treatment initiation
  • Younger age (under 50)
  • Predominantly radicular pain (arm pain dominates)
  • No serious neurological deficits
  • Good daily function despite pain

Factors That May Prolong Recovery

  • Delayed treatment initiation
  • Concurrent depression or anxiety
  • Catastrophic thinking about the pain
  • Passive coping strategies (avoidance, bed rest)
  • Prolonged neck stiffness before symptom onset

Can the Herniation Shrink?

Yes. Disc herniations can actually be reabsorbed over time. The body has a remarkable ability to break down and remove the protruding disc material. Inflammation around the nerve gradually decreases, and symptoms often improve before the herniation itself is completely gone.

Studies show that larger herniations (sequestered) actually have better reabsorption rates than smaller bulges, because the immune system more easily "discovers" and breaks down the loose disc material.

5

Treatment for C5-C6 Disc Herniation

Treatment of C5-C6 disc herniation is in the vast majority of cases conservative (non-surgical). The goal is to reduce pain, preserve function, and support the body's natural healing process.

Acute Phase (Week 1-2)

Pain Management and Modified Activity

  • Avoid provoking positions (backward bending, rotation toward painful side)
  • Short-term use of pain medication as needed
  • Ice or heat according to preference
  • Maintain as normal activity as possible without provoking pain

Subacute Phase (Week 2-8)

Manual Treatment

Chiropractic treatment may include:

  • Joint mobilization of the cervical spine
  • Myofascial release of neck and shoulder muscles
  • Nerve mobilization (gentle nerve gliding)
  • Thoracic manipulation (often well tolerated and effective)

Exercises

  • Isometric neck exercises: Strengthening without movement
  • Neck retractions: The "chin tuck" exercise
  • Scapular stabilization: Important for relieving the neck
  • Nerve gliding exercises: Gentle mobilization of the C6 nerve

When Is Surgery Considered?

Surgery is rarely necessary and is only considered for:

Surgical Indications

  • Progressive motor weakness despite conservative treatment
  • Myelopathy (spinal cord involvement with gait and balance problems)
  • Persistent disabling pain after 6-12 weeks of adequate treatment
  • Significant impact on daily function

Most patients (85-90%) never need surgery.

6

Red Flags: When Should You Contact a Doctor?

Most cases of C5-C6 disc herniation are harmless and resolve on their own. But some symptoms require prompt medical evaluation. See our article on red flags for more information.

Seek Medical Attention Immediately For:

  • Myelopathy signs: Problems with balance and gait, "clumsy" hands, difficulty buttoning buttons
  • Progressive weakness: Increasing muscle weakness in arm or hand over days
  • Bladder/bowel disturbances: Difficulty urinating or controlling urine/stool
  • Bilateral symptoms: Numbness or weakness in both arms
  • Fever combined with neck stiffness: May indicate infection

These symptoms are rare but require prompt evaluation.

Cervical Myelopathy

Myelopathy occurs when the herniation or degenerative changes press on the spinal cord itself, not just the nerve root. This is more serious than pure radiculopathy and requires faster evaluation.

Signs of Myelopathy

  • Unsteady gait, especially in the dark
  • Difficulty with fine motor skills (writing, buttoning buttons)
  • Lhermitte's sign: Electric shock sensation down the spine with neck flexion
  • Hyperactive reflexes in the legs

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between C5-C6 disc herniation and carpal tunnel syndrome?
With carpal tunnel syndrome, symptoms are often worst at night and improve when you shake your hand. Numbness affects the thumb, index finger, middle finger, and half of the ring finger. With C5-C6 disc herniation, symptoms worsen with neck movements, and only the thumb and index finger are affected. The biceps may also be weak.
Do I need an MRI for C5-C6 disc herniation?
Not routinely. MRI is only indicated for serious neurological deficits, suspected serious underlying pathology, or if you haven't improved after 6-8 weeks of conservative treatment. Many normal age-related changes appear on MRI without explaining the symptoms.
How long does it take for C5-C6 disc herniation to heal?
About 85% experience significant improvement within 8-12 weeks with conservative treatment. Most notice progress within 2-4 weeks. Full recovery can take up to 6 months, and the herniation can shrink and be reabsorbed over time.
Can I exercise with C5-C6 disc herniation?
Yes, adapted activity is important. Avoid extreme neck extension and heavy overhead lifting during the acute phase. Light neck mobility exercises, isometric exercises, and general low-intensity training are recommended. A chiropractor can guide you with specific exercises.
When should I contact a doctor immediately?
Seek medical attention immediately for: Increasing weakness in arm or leg, balance problems when walking, bladder or bowel disturbances, numbness in the groin area, or severe worsening of symptoms despite rest.
