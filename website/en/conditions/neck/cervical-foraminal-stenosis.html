Cervical Foraminal Stenosis - Narrowed Nerve Canal in Neck

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Key Points

  • What it is: Narrowing of the nerve openings (foramen) in the cervical vertebrae that compresses nerves
  • Most common symptoms: Radiation to arm/hand, numbness, tingling, weakness - often on one side
  • Conservative treatment: 75-90% get good relief without surgery
  • Chiropractic approach: Mobilization, traction exercises, posture correction and strength training
  • Red flags: Progressive weakness, muscle wasting, gait disturbances, bladder/bowel problems
1

What is Cervical Foraminal Stenosis?

Cervical foraminal stenosis means that the nerve openings on the sides of the cervical vertebrae have become narrower. These openings are called "foramen" (plural: foramina) and are where the spinal nerves leave the spine to supply the arms and hands with sensation and movement.

When the foramen becomes narrower - either due to wear, disc bulging, or bone spurs - the nerve passing through can become irritated or compressed. This causes symptoms that radiate from the neck down to the shoulder, arm, or hand.

Understanding the Anatomy

Think of the foramen as a tunnel through which the nerve travels. The tunnel is formed by:

  • The vertebral arch above and below - forming the roof and floor
  • The facet joint - forming the back wall
  • The intervertebral disc - forming the front wall

Changes in any of these structures can make the tunnel narrower and irritate the nerve.

Most Common Levels

Foraminal stenosis is most common at the following levels in the neck:

  • C5-C6: Affects the C6 nerve - symptoms to thumb and index finger
  • C6-C7: Affects the C7 nerve - symptoms to middle and ring finger
  • C4-C5: Affects the C5 nerve - symptoms to shoulder and upper arm
2

Causes of Foraminal Stenosis

Spondylosis (Wear and Tear)

The most common cause. Over time, the vertebrae can develop bone spurs (osteophytes) that grow into the foramen and make the opening narrower.

Disc Changes

Disc bulging or herniation can press on the nerve root. A "hard" herniation with bone spurs is more common in stenosis than "soft" disc herniation.

Facet Joint Osteoarthritis

Wear in the facet joints leads to thickening of the joint capsule and bone spurs that reduce space in the foramen.

Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases significantly after 50
  • Previous neck injury: Whiplash or other trauma
  • Neck-straining work: Prolonged work with head forward or in rotation
  • Genetics: Some naturally have smaller foramen
  • Smoking: Accelerates degenerative changes in the spine
3

Symptoms

Typical Symptoms

  • Radicular pain: Sharp, burning, or electric pain that radiates from neck to arm
  • Numbness and tingling: Often in specific fingers depending on which nerve is affected
  • Weakness: Reduced strength in specific arm or hand muscles
  • Neck stiffness: Especially with movements that close the foramen (extension + rotation toward painful side)

Spurling's Test

A classic test for foraminal stenosis: The head is bent backward and rotated toward the painful side, then light pressure is applied downward. A positive test reproduces radiation to the arm. This "closes" the foramen and provokes symptoms.

4

Treatment

Most people with cervical foraminal stenosis respond well to conservative treatment. Studies show that 75-90% achieve good symptom control without surgery.

Chiropractic treatment may include:

  • Manual mobilization: Gentle mobilization of neck joints to improve mobility
  • Manual traction: "Stretching" of the neck that opens the foramen and provides nerve relief
  • Soft tissue treatment: Treatment of tight muscles around neck and shoulder
  • Nerve mobilization: Gentle techniques to improve nerve gliding
  • Posture correction: Advice on ergonomics and positional changes

Exercises That Often Help

Cervical Retractions (Chin Tuck)

Pull your chin straight back as if making a "double chin." Hold for 5 seconds. This exercise centralizes neck discs and can open the foramen.

Side Bend Away from Painful Side

Gently bend your head toward the shoulder on the pain-free side. This opens the foramen on the painful side and can provide immediate relief.

Prognosis

With proper treatment and exercises, most achieve significant improvement within 6-12 weeks. Some experience periods of worsening, but the long-term prognosis is generally good with conservative treatment.

5

Red Flags - When Should You See a Doctor?

CONTACT A DOCTOR IMMEDIATELY IF:

  • Progressive weakness in arm or hand that is worsening
  • Visible muscle wasting (atrophy)
  • Gait disturbances or unsteadiness
  • Problems with urination or bowel control
  • Numbness in both hands or feet
  • Symptoms in both arms simultaneously
  • Fever combined with neck pain

These symptoms may indicate cervical myelopathy (spinal cord involvement) or other serious conditions requiring prompt evaluation.

When Is Surgery Considered?

Surgery is usually only considered when:

  • Conservative treatment for 6-12 weeks provides no improvement
  • There is progressive neurological deterioration
  • There are signs of myelopathy (spinal cord involvement)
  • Pain is unbearable and significantly affects quality of life

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between foraminal stenosis and central stenosis?

Foraminal stenosis affects the nerve openings on the sides of the spine where nerves exit, causing symptoms in the arm/hand. Central stenosis affects the spinal canal itself and can cause symptoms in both arms and legs, as well as gait disturbances.

Can cervical foraminal stenosis be treated without surgery?

Yes, most people with cervical foraminal stenosis respond well to conservative treatment. Studies show that 75-90% get good relief without surgery through manual treatment, traction exercises, and strength training.

What exercises help with foraminal stenosis in the neck?

Cervical retraction exercises (chin tucks), neck traction exercises, and scapular stabilization are often effective. Exercises that open the foramen (gentle side bending away from the painful side) can provide immediate relief.

Is cervical foraminal stenosis dangerous?

Foraminal stenosis itself is not dangerous, but untreated it can lead to progressive nerve damage. Seek medical attention for increasing weakness, muscle wasting, or problems with fine motor skills.
