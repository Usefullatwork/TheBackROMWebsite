Neck Degeneration - What MRI Findings Really Mean

Degenerative changes are normal - like gray hair in the spine | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You just received your MRI results, and words like "degenerative changes," "spondylosis," and "degeneration" fill the report. It sounds serious - as if your neck is about to fall apart.

But here's the good news: Degenerative changes are a normal part of aging, much like gray hair or wrinkles. Most people over 50 have such findings on MRI - and many of them have no pain whatsoever.

Let's look at what these MRI findings actually mean, why they often don't explain your pain, and what you can do to feel better.

Key Points

  • Normal aging: Degenerative changes are normal - like gray hair in the spine
  • Poor correlation: MRI findings often correlate poorly with symptoms
  • Asymptomatic findings: Over 80% of 50-year-olds have degeneration on MRI without pain
  • Treatment works: Symptoms can be treated effectively even with degeneration
  • Important: Don't let MRI findings scare you - focus on function, not images

What are degenerative changes?

Degenerative changes - also called spondylosis, degeneration, or osteoarthritis - describe normal age-related changes in the spine. This includes changes in the intervertebral discs, facet joints, and ligaments that occur gradually throughout life.

Think of it as "gray hair" in the spine. Just as hair turns gray and skin gets wrinkles, the spine will show age-related changes over time. This is a natural process and does not automatically mean you have pain or will develop problems.

The Gray Hair Analogy

Neck degeneration is like gray hair: Almost everyone gets it with age, it's completely normal, and it doesn't mean something is wrong. You wouldn't go to the doctor because you got gray hair - and degeneration on MRI is also nothing to worry about in itself.

Common MRI Descriptions

Disc Degeneration

The intervertebral discs lose fluid and height over time. This is normal and starts already in your 20s.

Osteophytes (Bone Spurs)

Small bone spurs that form along the edges of the vertebrae. The body's way of stabilizing.

Facet Joint Osteoarthritis

Wear in the small joints on the back of the vertebrae. Very common with increasing age.

Disc Protrusion/Bulging

The disc bulges out slightly. Found in most adults and is often completely asymptomatic.

2

What Research Shows

Studies that have taken MRI scans of healthy people without symptoms show surprising findings:

98% of 50-year-olds have disc degeneration
84% of 50-year-olds have disc bulging
36% of 20-year-olds have disc degeneration
0 of these had pain

Important Research

A systematic review in Radiology (Nakashima et al., 2015) of MRI in asymptomatic individuals showed that degenerative changes are so common they must be considered normal age-related changes, not pathology.

Correlation Between MRI and Pain

Several large studies have shown that there is very poor correlation between MRI findings and symptoms:

  • Many with significant degeneration on MRI have no pain
  • Many with pain have minimal findings on MRI
  • MRI findings cannot predict who will develop pain
  • MRI findings don't disappear even when the pain goes away
3

The Problem with MRI Descriptions

The way MRI findings are described can create unnecessary fear and worry. Words like "degeneration," "wear," and "osteoarthritis" sound serious but actually describe normal age-related changes.

The Nocebo Effect

Scary MRI descriptions can actually make pain worse. Research shows that patients who receive negative descriptions of their MRI findings often experience more pain, more fear of movement, and worse prognosis than those who receive neutral information.

Common Misconceptions

"My back is ruined"

Fact: The spine is robust and adaptable. Degenerative changes are normal and don't mean the spine is "ruined."

"I must avoid movement"

Fact: Movement is medicine for the spine. Inactivity often worsens symptoms, while adapted activity improves them.

"It's only going to get worse"

Fact: Symptoms can be treated and improved even with degeneration. Many live well with degenerative changes for many decades.

4

Treatment and What You Can Do

Although the degeneration itself cannot be reversed, symptoms can often be treated very effectively. Focus should be on function and symptoms - not on the MRI images.

Chiropractic treatment can help with:

  • Better mobility: Mobilization of stiff joints
  • Pain relief: Treatment of muscular components
  • Posture correction: Reduce strain on the neck
  • Exercise instruction: Strength and stretching exercises
  • Proper information: Reduce unnecessary fear

Exercises and Lifestyle

  • Stay active: Regular movement is important for the neck
  • Strength training: Strong neck and shoulder support muscles protect
  • Good posture: Avoid prolonged forward head position
  • Screen breaks: Vary positions throughout the day

Prognosis

Most people with neck pain and degenerative changes experience good improvement with treatment. The degeneration doesn't disappear, but symptoms can be effectively controlled. Many live symptom-free for long periods.

5

Frequently Asked Questions

Is neck degeneration dangerous?
No, degenerative changes are a normal part of aging and are usually not dangerous. Most people over 50 have some degree of degeneration on MRI, and many have no symptoms.
Can degeneration be treated?
The degeneration itself cannot be reversed, but symptoms can often be treated effectively. Chiropractic treatment, exercises, and lifestyle changes can provide significant symptom relief and improved function.
Does degeneration on MRI mean I have pain?
Not necessarily. Studies show poor correlation between MRI findings and symptoms. Many with significant degeneration on MRI have no pain, while others with minimal findings may have significant problems.
Do I need surgery for degeneration?
Very rarely. Surgery is considered only in specific situations such as progressive nerve damage or myelopathy. The vast majority are treated effectively without surgery.
Should I avoid movement and exercise?
No, quite the opposite! Movement is important for the neck. Adapted strength training and activity protects and can reduce symptoms. Inactivity often worsens problems.
