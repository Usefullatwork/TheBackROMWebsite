Finger Numbness - Neck or Hand?

Differential Diagnosis: Carpal Tunnel vs. Cervical Radiculopathy | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Numbness, tingling or "sleeping" fingers is a common symptom that can have many causes. The most important clinical task is to find out where the nerve is affected - in the neck, along the arm, or in the hand.

The nerves that supply the hand and fingers start in the neck and run through the shoulder, arm and forearm before reaching the fingers. A nerve can be compressed or irritated at any point along this path.

Key Points

  • Two main causes: Numbness can originate from the neck (radiculopathy) or from the hand/arm (carpal tunnel, ulnar nerve)
  • Finger distribution matters: Thumb/index/middle finger = often carpal tunnel. Little/ring finger = often neck or elbow
  • Neck causes: Often accompanied by neck pain and arm radiation
  • Hand causes: Often worse at night, relieved by shaking the hand
  • Seek help: For persistent numbness, weakness, or reduced grip strength
1

Three Main Nerves to the Hand

Nerve Anatomy

Median nerve: Supplies thumb, index finger, middle finger and half of ring finger (palm side)
Ulnar nerve: Supplies little finger and half of ring finger
Radial nerve: Supplies the back of the hand and extends the fingers

2

Neck Causes (Cervical Radiculopathy)

When a nerve root in the neck becomes compressed or irritated, it's called cervical radiculopathy. This can be caused by disc herniation, foraminal stenosis (narrow nerve canal), or degenerative changes.

Characteristics of Neck Cause

  • Neck pain that often worsens with movement
  • Radiation from neck, down into shoulder and arm
  • Symptoms follow a dermatome pattern (see below)
  • Can worsen when tilting head backward or toward the affected side
  • Often better at night compared to carpal tunnel

Dermatome Distribution - Which Nerve Root is Affected?

  • C5: Numbness over shoulder and upper arm (outer side)
  • C6: Numbness in thumb and index finger, outer forearm
  • C7: Numbness in middle finger, often with weakness when straightening elbow
  • C8: Numbness in ring and little finger (similar to ulnar nerve)
  • T1: Numbness on inner forearm

Typical Presentation of C6 Radiculopathy

Pain from neck radiating into shoulder, upper arm and forearm to thumb and index finger. Numbness in these fingers. May have weakness in biceps and wrist extension. Biceps reflex may be diminished.

Typical Presentation of C7 Radiculopathy

The most common cervical radiculopathy. Pain and numbness in middle finger. May have weakness when straightening elbow (triceps). Triceps reflex may be diminished.

3

Hand Causes

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

The most common cause of finger numbness. The median nerve becomes compressed in the carpal tunnel - a narrow passage in the wrist.

Characteristics of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

  • Fingers affected: Thumb, index finger, middle finger, and half of ring finger
  • Worse at night: Many wake up from numbness and need to shake their hand
  • Relieved by shaking: Shaking the hand often provides temporary relief
  • No neck pain: Symptoms are localized to hand and forearm
  • Risk factors: Pregnancy, diabetes, hypothyroidism, repetitive work
  • Weakness: In severe cases, thumb muscles may become weaker

Ulnar Nerve Involvement (Cubital Tunnel Syndrome)

The ulnar nerve can become compressed at the elbow (cubital tunnel) or at the wrist (Guyon's canal).

Characteristics of Ulnar Nerve Involvement

  • Fingers affected: Little finger and half of ring finger (ulnar side)
  • Elbow position: Often worse with elbow bent or when resting on elbow
  • Inside of hand: Can cause weakness in the small hand muscles
  • "Claw fingers": In severe cases, fingers may develop claw-like position

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS)

The nerves and blood vessels to the arm can become compressed in the area between the base of the neck and the armpit.

Characteristics of TOS

  • Numbness and tingling in the entire hand or multiple fingers
  • Often worsened by holding arms overhead
  • Can cause cold sensation and color changes in the hand
  • Often associated with poor posture or extra cervical rib
4

Comparison: Neck vs. Hand

Characteristic Neck Cause (Radiculopathy) Hand Cause (Carpal Tunnel)
Neck pain Yes, often present No
Arm radiation Yes, typical No (only hand/forearm)
Worse at night Rarely Yes, typical
Relieved by shaking No Yes
Worsened by neck movement Yes No
Finger distribution Follows dermatome Thumb, index, middle (median)
Reflex changes May be present No

Important to Remember

Some patients can have both neck problems and carpal tunnel at the same time - this is called "double crush syndrome." When a nerve is already compromised at one location, it becomes more vulnerable to compression elsewhere.

5

How is the Diagnosis Made?

Clinical Examination

A thorough clinical examination can often distinguish between neck and hand causes:

The examination includes:

  • Medical history: Where are the symptoms? What worsens/relieves?
  • Neck examination: Mobility, pain provocation, Spurling's test
  • Neurological examination: Sensation, strength, reflexes
  • Specific tests: Tinel's sign, Phalen's test (for carpal tunnel)
  • Dermatome mapping: Which fingers and areas are affected?

Imaging and Nerve Studies

  • MRI of neck: Can show disc herniation, stenosis or other structural causes
  • Nerve conduction study (EMG/NCV): Can confirm carpal tunnel or show where nerve is damaged
  • Ultrasound: Can visualize the median nerve in the carpal tunnel
6

Treatment

Treatment depends on the cause of the numbness.

For Neck Cause (Cervical Radiculopathy)

Treatment options:

  • Chiropractic treatment: Manual therapy, mobilization, nerve mobilization
  • Exercises: Neck exercises, posture correction, nerve gliding
  • Ergonomics: Workstation and sleeping position adjustment
  • Medication: Anti-inflammatory if needed (via doctor)
  • Surgery: For severe cases with persistent weakness

For Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Treatment options:

  • Night splint: Keeps wrist in neutral position at night
  • Chiropractic treatment: Mobilization of wrist and forearm
  • Ergonomic adjustment: Avoid prolonged wrist bending
  • Cortisone injection: Can provide temporary relief
  • Surgery: Carpal tunnel release for severe or prolonged cases

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

A chiropractor can examine and treat both neck and hand-related causes of finger numbness. Treatment is adapted to the underlying cause:

  • Manual treatment of neck, shoulder and arm
  • Nerve mobilization techniques
  • Soft tissue treatment of tense muscles
  • Ergonomic advice and exercises
  • Referral for further investigation if needed
7

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor?

Seek medical attention quickly for:

  • Sudden onset numbness without clear cause
  • Numbness in both hands/feet simultaneously
  • Progressive weakness in arm or hand
  • Bladder or bowel problems
  • Numbness after injury (fall, car accident)
  • Combination with headache, vision disturbances or speech difficulties
  • Rapid worsening over hours to days

These symptoms may indicate more serious conditions requiring faster evaluation, such as spinal cord compression, stroke, or multiple sclerosis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the numbness come from the neck or hand?

It depends on which fingers are affected and accompanying symptoms. Thumb, index and middle finger are typical for carpal tunnel, while little and ring finger often point to neck or elbow. Neck pain and arm radiation indicate neck cause.

What is the difference between carpal tunnel syndrome and cervical radiculopathy?

Carpal tunnel syndrome is caused by compression of the median nerve in the wrist and causes numbness in thumb, index and middle finger. Cervical radiculopathy is caused by nerve root involvement in the neck and often causes radiation from neck down into arm, along with numbness in a dermatome pattern.

Why do my fingers become numb at night?

In carpal tunnel, numbness at night is typical because we often sleep with bent wrists, which increases pressure in the carpal tunnel. Using a night splint that keeps the wrist straight can help significantly.

Can a chiropractor treat finger numbness?

Yes, chiropractors can examine and treat both neck and hand-related causes of numbness. Treatment targets the underlying cause, whether it's a neck problem, carpal tunnel, or other nerve involvement.

When do I need surgery for finger numbness?

Surgery is usually considered only after conservative treatment hasn't helped over several months, or in severe cases with significant weakness or muscle wasting. A nerve conduction study (EMG) can help assess severity.

Can finger numbness go away on its own?

Mild numbness, for example after sitting with a bent arm, usually passes quickly. Persistent numbness lasting weeks should be examined, as early treatment often yields better results.
