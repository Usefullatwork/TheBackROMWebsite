Nausea from Neck Pain - Why It Happens

Written by Mads Finstad, Chiropractor

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Nausea that occurs due to neck dysfunction, often together with dizziness
  • Most common causes: Muscle tension (most common), joint problems in upper neck, whiplash injury
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence - manual therapy can provide good results
  • Red flags: Sudden severe headache, vision disturbances, speech difficulties, facial numbness
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

What is Neck-Related Nausea?

Many are surprised that neck problems can cause nausea. But the connection is well documented: The neck continuously sends information to the brain about head position, and when this information is disrupted, it can trigger dizziness and nausea.

A Turkish multicenter study with 2361 patients found that 40% of people with neck pain report dizziness (Vural et al., 2021). Norwegian research shows similar numbers - 43% of patients with chronic neck pain experience dizziness (Knapstad et al., 2022). Nausea often follows as an additional reaction.

After thorough examination to rule out other causes, about 8% of neck pain patients have confirmed cervicogenic dizziness - meaning dizziness that actually comes from the neck (Kolmez et al., 2024).

Why Do Neck Problems Cause Nausea?

The Neck is an Advanced Sensory System

The upper neck contains extremely many proprioceptors (position sensors). The small muscles just below the skull have 242 muscle spindles per gram - compared to only 2.2 per gram in the trapezius muscle (Kulkarni et al., 2001). These sensors tell the brain where the head is in relation to the body.

The Connection to the Balance System

Signals from the neck go directly to the vestibular nuclei in the brainstem, where they are integrated with information from the balance organ in the inner ear. Research has shown that nerve fibers from the C2-C3 level in the neck connect directly to these balance nuclei (Matsushita et al., 1995; Rubin et al., 1975).

When something is wrong in the neck - whether due to tight muscles, locked joints or injury - incorrect signals are sent. A sensory conflict then occurs: What the neck reports does not match what the balance system registers.

The body reacts to this conflict with protective responses - including nausea. It is actually the same mechanism that causes motion sickness.

The Trigeminocervical Complex

Here, nerve fibers from the face/head (trigeminal nerve) and upper neck (C1-C3) meet. This fusion explains why neck problems often cause headache, dizziness AND nausea simultaneously (Bartsch & Goadsby, 2002).

Causes - From Most to Least Common

Muscular Causes (Most Common)

Myofascial tension in the neck muscles is the most common cause, often with trigger points. About 35% of patients with myofascial pain syndrome in the neck experience dizziness (Sahin et al., 2008). Prolonged sedentary work, stress and poor posture are typical triggers.

Symptoms often include:

  • Stiffness and tension in the neck
  • Headache radiating from the neck
  • Dizziness that worsens with certain movements
  • Nausea, especially with rapid head movements

Joint Problems in Upper Neck

Dysfunction in the joints between the uppermost neck vertebrae (C0-C3) can significantly disrupt proprioceptive signals. This is often seen after prolonged strain, minor trauma (which can cause torticollis) or with degenerative changes.

Whiplash Injury

In people with chronic whiplash, the prevalence is dramatically higher: 74.5% report dizziness, and 25-50% experience nausea (Treleaven et al., 2003). The combination of soft tissue injury, joint problems and altered sensory input makes these patients particularly vulnerable.

Rare Causes

Vertebrobasilar insufficiency - reduced blood supply through the arteries in the neck - is rare but important to rule out. This typically causes the "5 D's": Dizziness, diplopia (double vision), dysarthria (slurred speech), dysphagia (swallowing difficulties) and drop attacks (sudden falls).

Symptoms - How to Recognize It

Typical Presentation

Cervicogenic dizziness and nausea differ from other conditions:

Dizziness is often described as:

  • Unsteadiness or "nautical" feeling - like being on a boat
  • Lightheadedness
  • NOT intense rotational vertigo (as with BPPV)

Duration: Minutes to hours, not seconds (as with BPPV).

Triggering factors:

  • Neck movements
  • Certain head positions
  • Prolonged static posture (desk work)

Nausea:

  • Occurs with or right after dizziness
  • Often improves when neck pain eases
  • Rarely vomiting, more a general feeling of unwellness

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination is Key

Cervicogenic dizziness is an exclusion diagnosis - meaning other causes must be ruled out first. A thorough examination includes neck examination (mobility, palpation, upper neck assessment) and balance/dizziness examination (Dix-Hallpike test, eye movement tests, position tests).

Cervical torsion test has the highest diagnostic value: The body is rotated while the head is held still. If this causes dizziness, it points to the neck as the cause (sensitivity 72%, specificity 92%).

Imaging

MRI and X-rays of the neck often show degenerative changes that don't explain the symptoms. Such "normal findings" are common in people without any symptoms. Imaging is primarily relevant when serious underlying disease is suspected, with neurological deficits, or significant trauma.

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Systematic reviews support manual treatment for cervicogenic dizziness with moderate evidence (Lystad et al., 2011; Yaseen et al., 2018). This means research shows effect, but more studies are needed.

Treatment may include:

  • Joint mobilization of upper neck
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight muscles
  • Specific mobilization techniques (such as SNAGs/Mulligan technique)
  • Exercises for neck and balance

Expected Response

Studies typically show improvement within 2-6 treatments over 6 weeks. About 75% of patients improve with conservative treatment (Vestibular Disorders Association). The effect often lasts - one study showed maintained improvement at 12-month follow-up (Reid et al., 2014).

What Can You Do Yourself?

Exercises that can help:

  • Neck mobilization: Gentle movements in all directions, 10-15 repetitions, 2-3 times daily
  • Gaze stabilization: Focus on a point while slowly moving your head back and forth
  • Posture correction: Adjust your workstation, take regular breaks

A 2023 study showed that self-exercises significantly improve both dizziness and neck symptoms when combined with treatment (Piromchai et al., 2023).

Treatment Course

Most experience improvement within 4-8 treatments, depending on how long symptoms have lasted and severity. Acute cases (<6 weeks) often respond faster than chronic ones.

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Contact a doctor or emergency room immediately if:

  • Sudden, severe headache - different from previous headaches
  • The 5 D's: Dizziness combined with double vision, slurred speech, swallowing difficulties or sudden falls
  • Numbness in the face or around the mouth
  • Weakness or numbness in arms/legs
  • Horner's syndrome: Drooping eyelid + small pupil on one side
  • Symptoms after neck injury or manipulation

These symptoms may indicate serious conditions such as stroke, blood clot or vascular damage in the neck.

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

Most people get better. With proper treatment, about 75% see significant improvement. Early treatment often gives better results than "wait and see."

Relapses can occur, especially with:

  • Persistent strain factors (sedentary work)
  • Previous whiplash injury
  • Long-lasting symptoms before starting treatment

Self-exercises and attention to work posture reduce the risk.

Long-term perspective: Studies show that treatment effects are often maintained at 12-month follow-up, but some need periodic "maintenance treatment" (Reid et al., 2014).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can nausea really come from the neck?

Yes. The neck continuously sends position information to the brain. When these signals are disrupted, it can trigger nausea through the same mechanisms as motion sickness. Research shows that up to 40% of neck pain patients experience dizziness, and nausea often follows.

How does this differ from BPPV?

With BPPV you get intense rotational vertigo lasting seconds, triggered by head movements relative to gravity. Neck-related dizziness is more of an unsteadiness that lasts longer and is provoked by neck movements.

Do I need an MRI of my neck?

Not necessarily. Clinical examination is often more informative. MRI often shows degenerative changes that are common in symptom-free people as well. Imaging is reserved for cases where serious underlying disease is suspected.

How long does it take to get better?

Most notice improvement within 2-6 treatments. Acute cases often respond fastest. For long-lasting symptoms it may take longer, but 75% significantly improve with conservative treatment.

Should I wait and see if it goes away?

No, early treatment often gives better results. Chiropractors can help acutely - you don't need a referral from a doctor.
