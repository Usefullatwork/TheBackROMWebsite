Cervical Disc Herniation and Pinched Nerve in the Neck

Complete guide: Cervical radiculopathy, symptoms by nerve root, and treatment

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Nerve root irritation or compression in the neck causing radiating pain to shoulder, arm and hand
  • Annual incidence: 83 per 100,000, most common in age group 50-54
  • Most common causes: Spondylosis/degeneration (68-75%), disc herniation (20-25%), trauma (15%)
  • Most common level: C7 nerve via C6-C7 (60-70%), then C6 nerve via C5-C6 (20-25%)
  • Prognosis: 83-90% recover without surgery. Recurrence in ~32% over 5 years
  • Treatment: Manual therapy + exercises is first choice (Danish guidelines, 2017)
  • Red flags: Walking difficulties, clumsiness in hands, bladder/bowel dysfunction, symptoms in both arms
1

What is disc herniation and cervical radiculopathy?

Cervical radiculopathy means a nerve root in the neck is irritated or compressed. This causes symptoms radiating to the shoulder, arm or hand - often called "nerve root involvement," "brachialgia" or colloquially: "pinched nerve in the neck."

Cervical disc herniation (cervical disc prolapse) occurs when the soft core of an intervertebral disc bulges out or ruptures through the outer ring. When this material presses on a nerve root, you get radiculopathy.

Important distinction

You can have a herniation on MRI without symptoms, and you can have radiculopathy without a clear herniation. A Japanese study of 1211 healthy people found disc bulging in 87.6% (Nakashima, Spine 2015). Imaging must always be interpreted together with clinical findings.

Causes and risk factors

Interestingly, only 14.8% had physical exertion or trauma just before symptom onset in the Rochester study. Most cases develop gradually without a specific triggering event.

Causes ranked from most to least common

Spondylosis and foraminal stenosis (68-75%)

Age-related changes gradually lead to narrowing of the opening where the nerve passes. Dominant cause in people over 50.

Disc herniation (20-25%)

Acute bulging of the intervertebral disc pressing on the nerve root. Most common in younger patients in their 20s-40s.

Injury/trauma (~15%)

Whiplash-type injuries or other neck trauma.

Myofascial trigger points

Active trigger points in the upper trapezius occur in 30-40% of patients with chronic neck pain. Causes radiation that can mimic radiculopathy.

Risk factors

  • Age over 50
  • Heavy physical work with lifting over 25 kg
  • Work with vibrating equipment
  • Prolonged driving
  • Smoking (affects blood supply to discs)
  • Sedentary work with poor ergonomics
  • Previous back pain (41% have had lumbar radiculopathy)
3

Which nerves are affected - and what characterizes each?

The neck has seven cervical vertebrae, and the nerves exiting between them are named after the vertebra above. Note: Only 62.6% of patients have standard dermatome patterns, and 67.3% show typical myotome patterns (Neurospine 2022). About one-third have atypical symptoms.

C7 radiculopathy (most common - 60-70% of cases)

  • Disc level: C6-C7
  • Sensory: Tingling, numbness in the middle finger (finger 3). May also be felt on the back of the forearm.
  • Muscle weakness: Triceps (elbow extension), wrist flexors and finger extensors.
  • Reflex change: Reduced triceps reflex.

C6 radiculopathy (second most common - 20-25%)

  • Disc level: C5-C6
  • Sensory: Tingling or numbness in thumb and index finger (fingers 1 and 2).
  • Muscle weakness: Biceps and wrist extensors.
  • Reflex change: Reduced biceps reflex and/or brachioradialis reflex.

NOTE: C6 radiculopathy shows the most variation - only 47.6% present with typical pattern.

C5 radiculopathy (~5% of cases)

  • Disc level: C4-C5
  • Sensory: Pain and tingling in the shoulder area and outer upper arm.
  • Muscle weakness: Deltoid (shoulder abduction) and biceps.
  • Reflex change: Reduced biceps reflex.

C8 radiculopathy (~5% of cases)

  • Disc level: C7-T1
  • Sensory: Numbness in ring and little finger (fingers 4 and 5) plus inner forearm.
  • Muscle weakness: Small muscles of the hand and finger flexors. Causes problems with fine motor skills.
  • Reflex change: No reliable reflex for C8.

Carpal tunnel or neck? How to tell the difference

Numbness in the hand can come from the neck (cervical radiculopathy) or from the wrist (carpal tunnel syndrome). Symptoms can be similar, but there are important differences. One study showed that 62.2% of patients with cervical radiculopathy also have carpal tunnel syndrome - so-called "double crush syndrome."

Characteristic Carpal tunnel syndrome Cervical radiculopathy
Which fingers? Thumb, index, middle finger Depends on nerve root
When worst? At night, wake up numb With neck movement
What gives relief? Shaking the hand Arm over head (Bakody's sign)
Pain location Wrist and hand Neck -> shoulder -> arm -> hand
One or both sides? Often bilateral Usually unilateral

Bakody's sign

If symptoms are relieved when you place your hand on your head (shoulder in abduction), it strongly suggests the problem comes from the neck. The test has 80-100% specificity for cervical radiculopathy.

5

Diagnosis

Clinical tests

A thorough clinical examination is often more important than imaging. The chiropractor assesses neck mobility, muscle strength, sensation and reflexes.

Spurling's test

The neck is extended, side-flexed and rotated toward the symptomatic side, then pressure is applied. High specificity of 92%, but sensitivity of only 53%. A positive test strongly increases suspicion, but a negative test does not rule out the condition.

Upper Limb Tension Test (ULTT)

97% sensitivity - a negative test makes the diagnosis unlikely. Many false positives (22% specificity).

Arm Squeeze Test

96.7% sensitivity for distinguishing neck problems from shoulder problems. Squeezing the middle of the upper arm causes pain in neck pathology, but not in pure shoulder pathology.

Imaging

MRI is not routine and should be reserved for: persistent symptoms after 4-6 weeks of conservative treatment, progressive neurological deficit, suspicion of serious underlying disease, or before surgical evaluation.

MRI findings in symptom-free people (Brinjikji, AJNR 2015)

At age 30: 52% have disc degeneration, 40% disc bulging
At age 50: 80% have disc degeneration, 60% disc bulging
At age 70: 93% have disc degeneration, 77% disc bulging

Treatment

This is the key message: 75-90% of patients with cervical disc herniation and radiculopathy improve significantly without surgery. Danish national guidelines from 2017 recommend manual treatment combined with exercises as first choice, before medication is considered.

What can the chiropractor do?

A systematic review from 2021 found that manual treatment showed effect in all included studies, regardless of technique. The consensus is that the combination of manual therapy and exercises gives better results than each treatment alone.

Treatment may include:

  • Joint mobilization of cervical vertebrae and upper back
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight muscles in neck and shoulder
  • Neural mobilization (gentle movement of the nerve along its path)
  • Exercise instruction - strength and stability, especially deep neck flexors
  • Ergonomic guidance and workstation adjustment

What can you do yourself?

  • Stay active - avoid prolonged bed rest
  • Exercises - do the exercises your practitioner gives you regularly
  • Ergonomics - adjust screen, chair and work position
  • Pillow - a supportive pillow that keeps the neck in neutral position
  • Heat or cold - experiment with what gives the best relief

Treatment course

  • Acute phase (0-4 weeks): 2-3 treatments per week, focus on pain relief and mobility
  • Rehabilitation phase (4-12 weeks): 1-2 treatments per week, increasing focus on self-exercises and function
  • Maintenance: As needed. Guidelines recommend conservative treatment for at least 8-12 weeks before surgery is considered.

Surgery vs. conservative treatment

A Norwegian randomized study from Oslo University Hospital (Grotle, NEJM Evidence 2025) with 180 patients showed that for disc herniation, surgery gave statistically significantly better results after 12 months - but this was below the threshold for clinically meaningful change. For spondylosis, there was no difference between surgery and conservative treatment.

Surgery is considered for:

  • Progressive neurological deficit (increasing weakness)
  • Signs of myelopathy (spinal cord involvement)
  • Persistent radiculopathy after 8-12 weeks of adequate conservative treatment
  • Intractable pain
7

Red flags - when you should see a doctor

SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION IMMEDIATELY FOR:

  • Walking difficulties or unsteadiness (signs of myelopathy)
  • Symptoms in both arms or legs simultaneously
  • Clumsiness in hands - difficulty with fine motor skills (buttoning)
  • Bladder or bowel dysfunction
  • Rapidly increasing weakness in arm or hand
  • Fever combined with neck pain
  • Unexplained weight loss or known cancer
  • Nighttime pain that is not relieved by position changes

These signs may indicate cervical myelopathy (spinal cord involvement) or other serious conditions requiring prompt evaluation.

8

Prognosis

The prognosis for cervical radiculopathy is generally good. Most improve significantly with conservative treatment, and only a minority need surgery.

Phase Timeframe Expected outcome
First improvement 2-4 weeks 75% notice clear pain relief
Acute phase 6-12 weeks 85-90% significantly improved
Maximum improvement 4-6 months Peak symptom reduction
Full recovery 24-36 months 83% fully recovered
Long-term follow-up ~5 years 90% symptom-free or mild symptoms

Recurrence

About 32% experience recurrence during the follow-up period (Radhakrishnan, 1994). Risk factors include prolonged symptoms before treatment, marked numbness, significant neck pain, anxiety/depression and smoking.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a chiropractor help with a pinched nerve in the neck?

Yes. Systematic reviews show moderate evidence that manual treatment combined with exercises can reduce pain and improve function. The vast majority recover without surgery.

How long does it take for cervical radiculopathy to resolve?

Most experience significant improvement within 4-8 weeks with treatment. Complete recovery can take 3-6 months, and in some cases up to 2-3 years. Early treatment appears to lead to faster recovery.

Is an MRI necessary for neck and arm pain?

Not routinely. Clinical examination is often sufficient for diagnosis and treatment. MRI is considered only with lack of improvement after 4-6 weeks, signs of serious disease, or before surgical evaluation. Remember that 87.6% of asymptomatic people have disc bulging on MRI.

What is the difference between disc herniation and degeneration in the neck?

Disc herniation is an acute bulging of the intervertebral disc that presses on the nerve. Degeneration (spondylosis) is gradual age-related narrowing of the nerve canal. Degeneration is much more common (68-75% vs. 20-25%) and is mainly seen in people over 50.

How do I know if it's the neck or carpal tunnel?

Neck problems often cause neck pain, affect the back of the hand (not just the palm), can cause reflex changes, and less commonly have nighttime symptoms. Carpal tunnel syndrome typically causes wake-up-at-night symptoms that improve by shaking the hands.

Can I exercise with a pinched nerve?

Yes, but adapt your activity. Avoid exercises that worsen arm pain. General cardiovascular training and specific neck exercises are recommended - preferably in consultation with a chiropractor or physiotherapist.

When should I consider surgery?

Surgery is relevant for progressive neurological deficit, persistent significant symptoms after 8-12 weeks of conservative treatment, or signs of spinal cord involvement (myelopathy). For most people, conservative treatment is sufficient.

Can the condition come back?

Yes, recurrence occurs in about 32% over 5 years. Good neck ergonomics, maintenance exercises and early treatment for new episodes can reduce the risk of prolonged symptoms.
