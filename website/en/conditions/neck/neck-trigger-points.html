Trigger Points in the Neck - Muscle Knots and Referred Pain

Symptoms, treatment and self-help | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Myofascial pain syndrome (MPS) is a condition where painful knots - called trigger points - develop in muscles and fascia. These points are tender to pressure and can cause pain that radiates to other areas of the body. A trigger point in the upper trapezius, for example, can cause headache up toward the temple.

The condition is very common. Among patients with chronic neck pain, 88-100% have at least one trigger point (Cerezo-Tellez 2016; Ezzati 2021). In pain clinics, up to 85% of patients have a myofascial component.

Key Points

  • What it is: Tender, tight points in muscle tissue that can cause local pain and radiation to other areas
  • Prevalence: 88-100% of patients with chronic neck pain have trigger points
  • Most common muscles: Upper trapezius (93%), levator scapulae, sternocleidomastoid, scalenes
  • Treatment: Manual treatment and dry needling often provide short-term relief. Combined stretching and strength training is most important for lasting improvement
  • Red flags: Unexplained weight loss, fever, neurological symptoms, gait disturbances - see a doctor
  • Consultation: 45 min first time, 30 min follow-up

Causes and Risk Factors

Trigger points develop near muscle fibers that become overloaded or damaged. The most common causes, ranked by frequency:

Static Loading and Poor Ergonomics (Most Common)

Prolonged desk work, screen use with forward head position, and repetitive work are by far the most common causes. Office workers and manual laborers have similar trigger point distribution, with an average of 6 active and 10-11 latent trigger points (Fernandez-De-Las-Penas 2012).

Muscular Overload

Repetitive movements, heavy lifting, or sudden increased training load.

Stress and Tension

Psychological stress often leads to chronic muscle tension, especially in the neck and shoulders.

Other Factors

  • Sleep problems and fatigue: Poor sleep reduces the muscles' ability to recover
  • Vitamin D deficiency: About one-third of MPS patients have low vitamin D levels (StatPearls 2025)
  • Trauma: Whiplash and other injuries. Among whiplash patients, 55-65% have trigger points in levator scapulae

Who is affected?

MPS occurs most often in the 27-50 age group. Women are affected more often than men - 65% versus 37% among middle-aged adults (Steen, Muscle & Nerve 2025).

2

Symptoms

Trigger points in the neck produce a characteristic symptom pattern:

Local Tenderness

A distinct point that is very tender to pressure.

Referred Pain

Pain radiates in a predictable pattern. Upper trapezius often causes pain toward the back of the head and temple. Sternocleidomastoid can cause pain around the eye and forehead.

Stiffness and Reduced Mobility

Difficulty turning the head or looking up.

Headache

80% of patients with chronic tension headache have active trigger points in the upper trapezius (Alonso-Blanco 2011).

"Stiff Neck" Sensation

Typical with trigger points in levator scapulae, with pain at the angle between neck and shoulder.

Upper Trapezius (93% of chronic neck pain patients)

Causes pain along the neck, up toward the back of the head, temple and behind the eye. The classic "tension headache."

Levator Scapulae (55-65%)

Causes the typical "stiff neck" sensation with pain at the neck-shoulder junction and along the inner border of the shoulder blade.

Sternocleidomastoid (SCM)

Can cause headache in the forehead and around the eye, as well as dizziness and balance disturbances.

Scalene Muscles

Can cause radiation to the chest, shoulder, arm and thumb - sometimes mistaken for heart symptoms or nerve root involvement.

4

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made through clinical examination. An international Delphi study from 2018 with 60 experts from 12 countries concluded that the most important diagnostic criteria are: a taut band in the muscle, a hypersensitive point, and reproduction of referred pain with pressure (Fernandez-de-las-Penas & Dommerholt 2018).

Important to know

There are no internationally validated diagnostic criteria for MPS. Inter-examiner reliability is only moderate. This means that two practitioners examining the same patient may find trigger points in slightly different locations. We therefore use the clinical examination as a starting point, but also emphasize your experience and response to treatment.

Imaging

MRI and X-ray are rarely necessary for trigger points. Images often show age-related changes (wear, degeneration) that don't explain your pain. Ultrasound can detect trigger points as darker areas with increased stiffness, but this is currently mostly used in research.

5

Differential Diagnoses

It's important to distinguish trigger points from other conditions that can cause similar symptoms:

Cervical Radiculopathy (Nerve Root Involvement)

Follows dermatomal patterns (specific skin areas), often with tingling/numbness and weakness. Trigger points follow muscle-specific radiation patterns.

Fibromyalgia

Widespread pain for more than 3 months, with tenderness in many areas of the body, often with fatigue and sleep problems.

Cervical Facet Joint Arthrosis

Pain from the joints between vertebrae, often with morning stiffness that improves throughout the day.

6

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

A network meta-analysis from 2024 including 40 randomized studies and 2227 participants shows that manual treatment provides short-term improvement in pain, pressure sensitivity and function (Liu et al. 2024). Evidence quality is classified as low according to the GRADE system, but the effects are clinically relevant for many patients.

Treatment I offer includes:

  • Ischemic compression: Sustained pressure on the trigger point. A 2022 meta-analysis shows significant improvement in pressure pain threshold (Lu et al. 2022)
  • Dry needling: Thin needles are inserted directly into the trigger point. Equally effective as manual trigger point treatment in short and medium term (Lew et al. 2021)
  • Myofascial techniques and mobilization: Soft tissue treatment of muscles and fascia
  • Joint mobilization/manipulation: For concurrent joint dysfunction in the neck or thoracic spine
  • Exercise-based rehabilitation: The most important for lasting improvement

Honest expectations

Short-term treatment effects are well documented. Long-term effects (6-12 months) are less well studied, and at this time horizon there is little difference between different treatment methods. What gives the best lasting effect is combining treatment with self-exercise and addressing underlying factors like stress, ergonomics and sleep.

What can you do yourself?

A 2017 systematic review shows that combined stretching and strength training provides the best effect on myofascial pain (Mata Diz et al. 2017):

Stretching

Hold for 30 seconds - this is the optimal duration according to research (Mansoori et al. 2021). Stretch trapezius, levator scapulae and scalenes daily.

Strength Training

Exercises for deep neck muscles and shoulder blade stabilizers. I'll show you specific exercises tailored to your trigger points.

Ergonomics

Screen at eye level, forearms supported, regular breaks. Avoid forward head position.

Self-massage

Use a tennis ball or lacrosse ball against the wall for gentle pressure on tender points. 30-60 seconds per point.

Heat and Stress Management

Heat can provide temporary relief and increase blood flow. Chronic stress maintains muscle tension - consider breathing techniques, mindfulness or other relaxation methods.

7

Treatment Course

There are no standardized guidelines for treatment frequency specifically for MPS. In practice, we often see:

Acute Problems (<6 weeks)

Often 3-6 treatments over 2-4 weeks, with gradual transition to self-exercise.

Chronic Problems (>12 weeks)

May require longer treatment period. Focus on identifying and addressing maintaining factors.

You don't need to wait to seek help. Early treatment often leads to faster improvement than "waiting it out" for several weeks.

8

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Contact a doctor for the following symptoms:

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fever
  • Neurological symptoms
  • Gait disturbances

These symptoms may indicate more serious conditions such as infection, tumor or neurological disease and require medical evaluation.

9

Prognosis

Most people with trigger points in the neck experience good improvement with treatment and self-exercise. Short-term treatment effects are well documented in research.

Recurrence is common

Without addressing underlying factors like ergonomics, stress, inactivity and potential nutritional deficiencies, trigger points often return. This is why we place great emphasis on self-exercise and lifestyle factors.

At 6-12 months, long-term results are often similar regardless of which specific treatment method was used. What appears most important for lasting improvement is the combination of treatment, exercises and adjustment of activity and ergonomics.

10

Frequently Asked Questions

Do trigger points actually exist?
There is ongoing professional debate about the exact nature and pathophysiology of trigger points. What we know is that many patients have tender points in their muscles that produce recognizable radiation patterns, and that treatment targeting these points often provides symptom relief. Norwegian researchers have noted that the diagnostics have limited reliability, but this doesn't mean your pain isn't real.
What's the difference between trigger points and fibromyalgia?
Trigger points cause localized pain with specific radiation from defined points in muscles. Fibromyalgia is a condition with widespread pain across large parts of the body for more than 3 months, often combined with fatigue, sleep problems and cognitive fog. The two conditions can also occur together.
Does dry needling hurt?
The needles used are very thin (about like acupuncture needles). Most people experience a small prick at insertion, and a characteristic "twitch" when the trigger point is hit. This discomfort is brief. Some experience soreness in the area for 1-2 days afterward, similar to after hard exercise.
How many treatments do I need?
It varies. For acute problems, we often see improvement after 3-6 treatments. For long-standing problems, it may take longer, and the focus is then more on finding and addressing the factors maintaining the problem. We always evaluate along the way and adjust the treatment plan based on your response.
Can trigger points cause dizziness?
Yes, trigger points in the sternocleidomastoid (SCM) can cause dizziness, balance disturbances and a feeling that surroundings are moving. This is likely due to effects on proprioceptors (position sense) in the neck muscles. We always investigate other causes of dizziness before concluding it's due to trigger points.
Is massage or chiropractic better for trigger points?
Research shows that different manual techniques - including massage, trigger point therapy, dry needling and mobilization - have similar effects in the short term. What's most important is that treatment is combined with exercises and that underlying factors are addressed. As a chiropractor, I can offer a wide range of techniques and tailor treatment to your needs.
