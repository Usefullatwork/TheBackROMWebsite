Red Flags for Neck Pain - When to See a Doctor

Warning signs for neck pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

The vast majority who visit a chiropractor with neck pain have non-specific muscle and joint problems. These usually respond well to treatment, and early intervention often leads to faster improvement. But in rare cases, neck pain can be a sign of something serious.

"Red flags" are warning signs indicating that symptoms may be caused by an underlying condition requiring acute medical evaluation - not chiropractic treatment. The two most important "red flag" conditions with neck pain are:

Summary

  • Cervical artery dissection (CAD): A tear in the blood vessel wall that can lead to stroke
  • Cervical myelopathy: Pressure on the spinal cord that can cause permanent neurological damage
  • 82% of strokes with CAD occur within 7 days of symptom onset
  • Acute symptoms: Sudden intense headache, walking difficulties, groin numbness

When should you seek help immediately?

For sudden intense headache/neck pain, walking difficulties, speech problems, one-sided weakness, or groin numbness - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

Cervical Artery Dissection (CAD)

Cervical artery dissection occurs when a tear forms in the inner wall of one of the blood vessels supplying the brain - either the carotid artery (arteria carotis interna) or the vertebral artery (arteria vertebralis). Blood can enter the vessel wall and form a blood collection that narrows or blocks blood flow to the brain.

CAD is rare in the general population - historically estimated at 2.6-3.0 per 100,000 per year (Lee et al., Neurology 2006). But the condition is one of the most important causes of stroke in younger adults: Data from the Helsinki Young Stroke Registry shows that CAD accounts for approximately 15% of all ischemic strokes in patients under 50 years (Putaala et al., Stroke 2009).

Risk Factors for CAD

Over half of cases are classified as "spontaneous" - without clear triggering trauma. Documented risk factors include:

Connective Tissue Disorders

Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan syndrome, and fibromuscular dysplasia (1-5% of cases)

Recent Infection

Increases risk nearly 3 times in the first week (Grau et al., Arch Neurol 1999)

Migraine

Moderate association demonstrated (Jacobs et al., 2025)

High Blood Pressure

Odds ratio 1.4-2.2

Minor Traumas

Including sports, hairdresser visits with head tilted back, roller coaster rides

2

Symptoms of CAD

The most common symptom is headache and/or neck pain (65-95% of patients). But CAD-related pain has characteristics that distinguish it from common muscle problems:

Warning Signs of CAD

  • Sudden onset: Pain comes abruptly, often over minutes
  • One-sided: 74% have pain on the same side as the dissection (Thomas et al., 2023)
  • "Different from anything else": 75% describe the pain as unlike any previous headache (Mayer-Suess et al., 2022)
  • Does not respond to treatment: Pain does not ease with movement, position, or painkillers
  • Cannot be reproduced: Examination of the neck does not provoke the pain

Neurological Signs of CAD

  • Horner's syndrome (25-44%): Constricted pupil and drooping eyelid on one side
  • Cranial nerve involvement (12%): Tongue deviation, hoarseness, swallowing difficulties
  • Pulsatile tinnitus (8-10%): Rhythmic sound in ear synchronous with pulse

The Critical Time Window

Data from Biousse et al. (Stroke 1995) showed that 82% of strokes occur within 7 days after symptoms started. This means early recognition can prevent stroke.

3

Cervical Myelopathy

Cervical myelopathy occurs when the spinal cord in the neck becomes compressed or pressed on. This differs from ordinary neck pain or nerve root irritation in that the spinal cord itself - not just the nerves exiting from it - is affected. The condition is considered the most common cause of spinal cord dysfunction in adults (Lancet Neurology, 2019).

The prevalence increases with age. Approximately 1-5% of adults over 40 years have symptomatic cervical myelopathy, and average age at diagnosis is 56-64 years (AOSpine, 2015). Men are affected 2-3 times more often than women.

Causes of Myelopathy

Degenerative Causes (80-90%)

  • Cervical spondylosis: Wear changes with disc bulging and bone spurs. C5-C6 level most common (StatPearls, 2025)
  • Thickened ligaments: Ligamentum flavum hypertrophy
  • Congenitally narrow spinal canal: Most important anatomical risk factor

Less Common Causes

Disc herniation with central compression, tumors, infections, trauma

4

Symptoms of Myelopathy

Cervical myelopathy has a characteristic symptom triad (AAFP, 2020):

The Three Main Symptoms

  1. Walking difficulties: The most important symptom. Unsteadiness, balance problems, difficulties on stairs, unexplained falls
  2. Clumsiness in hands ("myelopathy hand"): Difficulty buttoning, problems writing, dropping things
  3. Bilateral numbness: Symptoms on both sides, often in both hands and feet

Important

Neck pain is surprisingly rarely the dominant symptom in myelopathy. Many patients have little or no pain, which can delay diagnosis. About 50% are initially misdiagnosed as carpal tunnel syndrome (Kim et al., 2013).

Clinical Findings

On examination, we look for upper motor neuron signs:

  • Hoffman's sign: Sensitivity 48-59%, specificity 67-78%
  • Babinski's sign: Low sensitivity (13%), but nearly 100% specificity
  • Hyperreflexia: Increased reflexes in arms and legs
  • Clonus: Sustained rhythmic muscle contractions

Important clinical point: Normal findings on examination do not rule out myelopathy. Tests are specific but not sensitive - positive findings give strong suspicion, but negative findings do not provide reassurance.

5

Prognosis Without Treatment

Natural course without treatment is concerning:

  • Approximately 75% experience episodic worsening
  • 20% show slow steady progression
  • Complete normalization never occurs without treatment
  • Without surgery, 20-62% of patients worsen within 6 months (StatPearls, AAFP)
6

The Chiropractor's Role with Red Flags

When arterial dissection or myelopathy is suspected, the chiropractor's most important task is early recognition and rapid referral. I do not treat patients where there is suspicion of these conditions.

At all new patient encounters, I conduct a thorough medical history and neurological screening. If you have symptoms as described above, I will assess whether you need acute medical evaluation - either via emergency room, emergency services, or direct referral to imaging and specialist.

Measures that may be safe while awaiting specialist evaluation

  • Soft tissue mobilization of thoracic region (avoiding deep pressure on neck)
  • Patient education on posture and activity modification
  • Gentle isometric neck exercises

Contraindicated Measures

  • HVLA neck manipulation
  • Neck mobilization with end-range movements
  • Mechanical neck traction
  • Aggressive mobility exercises for the neck
7

Frequently Asked Questions

How common are these conditions really?
Both are rare. Arterial dissection affects 2.6-9 per 100,000 per year. Myelopathy occurs in 1-5% of adults over 40 years. The vast majority of neck pain (80-90%) is caused by harmless muscle and joint problems.
Can chiropractic treatment cause arterial dissection?
The American Heart Association (2014) concluded that a causal relationship has not been established. The Cassidy study (Spine 2008) found that the risk of stroke was equally elevated after visiting a general practitioner as after visiting a chiropractor - suggesting that patients with ongoing dissection seek help for their symptoms regardless of practitioner type.
How do I know if my neck pain is dangerous?
Most neck pain is harmless. Seek emergency help if the pain is sudden and intense, one-sided, "different from anything you've had before," and especially if you simultaneously have neurological symptoms such as walking difficulties, speech problems, or one-sided weakness.
What is the difference between myelopathy and disc herniation?
With disc herniation, usually a nerve root is compressed - this gives symptoms on one side and in one specific area. With myelopathy, it is the spinal cord itself that is affected, which gives symptoms on both sides and often in both arms and legs simultaneously.
Can myelopathy be treated?
Yes, but it requires surgery for moderate to severe cases. The AOSpine study (2015) showed that 85% of patients had improvement one year after surgery. Early diagnosis gives better prognosis - symptom duration over 18 months gives poorer outcomes.
How quickly should I seek help?
If arterial dissection is suspected: Call emergency services immediately - 82% of strokes occur within the first week. If myelopathy is suspected: Contact a doctor or chiropractor the same day for evaluation and referral.
