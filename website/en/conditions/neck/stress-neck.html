Stress Neck and Trigger Points

Symptoms, treatment and prevention | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

"Stress neck" is not a medical diagnosis, but a descriptive term for neck pain and muscle tension strongly linked to psychological stress. It is one of the most common causes of neck pain among office workers and others with sedentary occupations.

When we experience stress, the body activates the "fight-or-flight" response. Part of this response is increased muscle tension - especially in the neck, shoulders and jaw. Over time, persistent tension can lead to the development of trigger points, pain and reduced mobility.

Key Points

  • What it is: Muscle tension and trigger points in neck/shoulders caused by stress and poor posture
  • Common symptoms: Tight neck, tension headache, tender knots, pain between shoulder blades
  • Treatment: Trigger point therapy, mobilization, exercises and stress management
  • Prognosis: Good with treatment - but often requires lifestyle changes for lasting improvement
  • Important: Chronic stress can maintain pain even after treatment
1

What is stress neck?

Stress neck describes a condition where psychological stress manifests as physical tension in the neck and shoulder muscles. When we experience stress, the body activates the "fight-or-flight" response which increases muscle tension - especially in the neck, shoulders and jaw.

The Stress-Tension-Pain Cycle

Many get caught in a vicious cycle:

  • Stress - increased muscle tension
  • Tension - trigger points and pain
  • Pain - more stress and worry
  • More stress - even more tension

Treatment often needs to address multiple links in this cycle for lasting improvement.

What are trigger points?

Trigger points are hypersensitive areas in muscle tissue that feel like hard knots when palpated. They have two important characteristics:

  • Local tenderness: The point is painful when pressed
  • Referred pain: Pressing the point can trigger pain in other areas - e.g., trigger points in the trapezius can cause headache
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Psychosocial stress

Work pressure, conflicts, financial worries and other stressors activate the body's stress response and increase muscle tension.

Poor work posture

Forward head position in front of screens, raised shoulders at keyboard work, and lack of movement throughout the day.

Sleep deprivation

Poor sleep reduces muscles' ability to recover and increases sensitivity to pain.

Inactivity

Lack of physical activity weakens muscles and makes them more vulnerable to overload.

Common trigger point locations

  • Upper trapezius: Between neck and shoulder - causes referred pain to temple and eye
  • Levator scapulae: From neck to shoulder blade - causes stiff neck and pain with rotation
  • Suboccipital muscles: At the base of skull - causes headache behind the eye
  • Sternocleidomastoid: Front of neck - causes dizziness, blurred vision and headache

Trigger points and headache

Trigger points in the neck muscles are an underdiagnosed cause of tension headache and migraine-like symptoms. Treatment of these can often provide significant headache relief.

3

Symptoms

Typical symptoms of stress neck

  • Tight, stiff neck: Feeling that the neck is "tense" or "locked"
  • Tender muscles: Painful when touching neck and shoulders
  • Tension headache: Often band-like pressure around the head
  • Pain between shoulder blades: Burning or aching sensation
  • Raised shoulders: Involuntary raising of shoulders toward ears
  • Fatigue: Feeling of exhaustion in neck and shoulders

Aggravating factors

  • Prolonged computer work
  • Stress and worry
  • Poor sleep
  • Cold and drafts
  • Static positions over time

Symptoms suggesting trigger points

  • Pain radiating from a specific point
  • Palpable knots in the muscles
  • Headache starting in the neck
  • Improvement with pressure or massage on the tender point
4

Treatment

Chiropractic treatment may include:

  • Trigger point therapy: Targeted pressure to release active trigger points
  • Manual mobilization: Restore normal joint mobility in neck
  • Soft tissue treatment: Massage and myofascial techniques
  • Dry needling: Needle treatment of trigger points
  • Exercise instruction: Home exercises for strength and relaxation

Exercises you can do yourself

Neck stretches

Gently tilt your head toward one shoulder. Hold for 30 seconds. Repeat to the opposite side. Do 3 times each side, several times daily.

Shoulder rolls

Roll your shoulders slowly backward in large circles. Do 10 repetitions. This loosens tension in the upper trapezius.

Chin tucks

Pull your chin straight back as if making a "double chin." Hold for 5 seconds. Repeat 10 times. Corrects forward head posture.

Breathing exercises

Deep diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces muscle tension. Breathe in for 4 seconds, hold for 4 seconds, breathe out for 6 seconds. Repeat for 5 minutes.

Stress management

For lasting improvement, underlying stress often needs to be addressed:

  • Regular physical activity
  • Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
  • Relaxation techniques (meditation, yoga)
  • Social support and work-life balance
  • If needed: Professional help for stress management

Prognosis

Most people experience good improvement with treatment. The challenge often lies in preventing relapse, which requires ongoing focus on ergonomics, exercises and stress management.

5

Prevention and Ergonomics

Workplace ergonomics

  • Screen height: Top of screen at eye level
  • Distance: Arm's length to screen
  • Chair: Support for lower back, feet flat on floor
  • Keyboard and mouse: Elbows at 90 degrees, relaxed shoulders

Break routines

  • Micro-break every 20-30 minutes (look away from screen, move neck)
  • Longer break every hour (stand up, walk around)
  • Movement breaks 2-3 times during the workday

The 20-20-20 rule

Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 meters away for 20 seconds. This reduces strain on eyes and neck during screen work.

6

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the connection between stress and neck pain?

Stress activates the body's fight-or-flight response which increases muscle tension, especially in the neck and shoulders. Chronic stress leads to persistent muscle tension that creates trigger points and pain.

What are trigger points?

Trigger points are hypersensitive areas in muscles that feel like hard knots. They can cause local pain and referred pain to other areas - for example, trigger points in the neck can cause headache.

How is stress neck treated?

Treatment combines manual therapy (trigger point treatment, mobilization, massage), exercises for strength and mobility, ergonomic advice, and stress management techniques.

Can I release trigger points myself?

Yes, self-massage with a tennis ball or foam roller can help. Place the ball between your shoulder blade and wall, and roll gently over tender points. Hold pressure on tender points for 30-60 seconds.

How long does it take to get rid of stress neck?

Acute symptoms can be relieved in 1-3 treatments. Lasting improvement often requires 4-8 weeks of treatment combined with exercises and lifestyle changes.
