Text Neck: From Mobile Use to Neck Pain

Symptoms, exercises and treatment for "text neck"

You're sitting on the bus, hunched over your phone. After an hour you feel it: an aching pain in your neck, stiffness between your shoulder blades, maybe a dull headache building behind your forehead.

Welcome to text neck - our time's most widespread posture-related complaint. With an average of 4-7 hours of daily mobile use, our necks are under constant pressure and neck pain is becoming increasingly common.

The good news? Text neck can be effectively prevented and treated. Let's look at what's happening in your neck and what you can do to fix the problem.

Key Points

  • 60 degrees forward lean puts 27 kg load on the neck
  • Symptoms: Neck/headache, stiffness, rounded back
  • Improvement: 2-4 weeks with right measures
  • Treatment: Exercises + posture change is key
  • Important: Prolonged strain can lead to degeneration
1

What is text neck?

Text neck is a collective term for neck pain and problems that arise from prolonged use of mobile devices with the head bent forward. The term was first used by American chiropractor Dean Fishman in 2008.

The problem is not the mobile phone itself, but the position you assume when using it. When the head falls forward, the load on the neck's muscles, joints and discs increases dramatically.

Load on the neck at different angles

Dr. Kenneth Hansraj published a study in 2014 showing how the load on the neck increases exponentially with the degree of forward lean:

Neck Angle Load on Neck Typical Activity
0 (neutral) 4.5-5.5 kg Head directly over shoulders
15 12 kg Slight forward lean
30 18 kg Reading on screen
45 22 kg Intense mobile use
60 27 kg Typical text neck position

27 kg - like carrying an 8-year-old on your neck

At 60 degrees forward lean, the load on the neck equals the weight of an average 8-year-old child. This load is applied to the neck for hours every day.

700-1400 hours yearly with bent neck
5x increased load with text neck

Symptoms of text neck

Text neck produces a characteristic symptom pattern that develops gradually over time:

Neck pain and stiffness

Most common

Aching, dull pain in the lower neck and the transition to shoulders. Stiffness that worsens throughout the day, especially after longer periods of mobile use.

Tension headache

Headache that starts in the neck and radiates up to the back of the head, temples and forehead. Often described as "tightness" around the head.

Pain between shoulder blades

Burning or aching discomfort in upper back, between and under the shoulder blades. Caused by muscles here working overtime to hold the head up.

Forward head posture

Over time, a permanent forward head posture develops with "goose neck" and rounded shoulders. This becomes visible even when not using your phone.

Numbness and tingling

In more severe cases, you may feel numbness or tingling radiating into shoulder and arm. This suggests nerve structures are becoming irritated.

When symptoms typically occur

  • After longer periods of mobile use (30+ minutes)
  • Toward end of workday after lots of screen work
  • In the morning after sleeping with stiff neck
  • During stress periods (stress worsens muscle tension)
3

What happens in the body?

Text neck is not just a muscular complaint - it affects the entire neck and back structure:

Muscular overload

  • Posterior neck muscles: Work constantly to keep head from falling forward
  • Upper trapezius: Overactive and tense, causes shoulder and neck pain
  • Levator scapulae: Tight and tender, often with trigger points
  • Suboccipital muscles: Tight, contribute to headache

Front structures shorten

  • Pectoralis minor: Chest muscle becomes short and pulls shoulders forward
  • Sternocleidomastoid: The large neck muscle in front becomes shortened
  • Scalene muscles: Can compress nerve structures

Long-term consequences

If text neck persists over time

  • Earlier degenerative changes in cervical spine
  • Reduced neck curvature (loss of lordosis)
  • Increased risk of disc problems
  • Chronic tension headache
  • Permanent postural changes

Exercises for text neck

These exercises counteract forward head posture and strengthen the muscles that hold the head in neutral position. Do them daily for best effect.

1. Chin Tucks ("Double Chin")

  1. Sit or stand with straight back
  2. Pull chin straight back (as if making a double chin)
  3. Hold position for 5 seconds
  4. Release slowly
  5. Repeat 10-15 times, 3-4 times daily

This is the most important exercise - it strengthens the deep neck stabilizers and counteracts forward head position.

2. Doorway Chest Stretch

  1. Stand in a doorway with forearms against the door frame
  2. Elbows should be at shoulder height, 90 degree angle
  3. Take a step forward and lean gently into the stretch
  4. Feel stretch across chest and front of shoulders
  5. Hold for 30-60 seconds, repeat 2-3 times

3. Shoulder Blade Squeeze

  1. Sit or stand with arms at sides
  2. Pull shoulder blades together and down (as if putting them in back pockets)
  3. Hold for 5-10 seconds
  4. Release slowly
  5. Repeat 10-15 times, several times daily

4. Wall Angels

  1. Stand with back against wall, heels 5-10 cm from wall
  2. Lower back, upper back and back of head should touch wall
  3. Raise arms to sides with elbows bent 90 degrees ("stick em up")
  4. Push forearms and back of hands against wall
  5. Slowly slide arms up and down along wall
  6. 10-15 repetitions, 2-3 sets

This exercise strengthens back muscles and improves shoulder mobility simultaneously.

Exercise frequency

For best results:

  • Chin tucks: Every hour during screen work
  • Shoulder blade squeeze: Together with chin tucks
  • Chest stretches: 2-3 times daily
  • Wall angels: 1-2 times daily
5

Prevention: How to use your phone correctly

The best treatment is prevention. Here are concrete measures to reduce the load:

Posture during mobile use

  • Raise the phone: Hold it at eye level instead of bending head down
  • Support elbows: Use a table or pillow to support arms
  • Maintain neck curve: Keep head in neutral position over shoulders

Take breaks

  • 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 meters away for 20 seconds
  • Micro-breaks: Every 30 minutes, stand up and stretch
  • Variation breaks: Switch between sitting and standing work

Ergonomics at PC/laptop

  • Top of screen at eye level
  • Screen an arm's length away
  • Keyboard and mouse at elbow height
  • Use external monitor for heavy laptop use

Reduce screen time

Consider how much of your mobile use is necessary. Many use phone out of habit rather than need. Cutting 1-2 hours daily gives neck a noticeable break.

6

Professional treatment

If self-care isn't enough, or you've had problems for a long time, chiropractic treatment can help:

Chiropractic treatment for text neck

  • Joint mobilization/manipulation: Restores movement in stiff neck segments
  • Myofascial treatment: Loosens tight muscles and trigger points
  • Thoracic treatment: Important to address rounded upper back
  • Posture guidance: Individual review of ergonomics and exercises
  • Dry needling: Can be effective for stubborn trigger points

What can you expect?

Most notice improvement after just 2-4 treatments. But text neck is a lifestyle-related condition - lasting improvement requires changing habits. Treatment alone without behavioral change only gives temporary relief.

3-6 treatments typical

Frequently Asked Questions

What is text neck?

Text neck is a collective term for neck pain and problems that arise from prolonged mobile phone use with head bent forward. When head is 60 degrees forward, load on neck increases from about 5 kg to 27 kg.

Can text neck cause headache?

Yes, text neck is a common cause of cervicogenic headache. The constant load on neck muscles and joints can cause tension headache that starts in neck and radiates to forehead and temples.

How long does it take to get rid of text neck?

With the right measures, most see improvement within 2-4 weeks. Key is to change mobile habits, do regular exercises, and possibly get treatment. Long-standing cases may require longer rehabilitation.

Which exercises help with text neck?

The most important exercises are chin tucks (double chin), chest stretches, and shoulder blade squeezes. These counteract forward head posture and strengthen muscles that hold head in neutral position.

Is text neck dangerous?

Text neck is not dangerous in itself, but prolonged strain can contribute to earlier degenerative changes in neck. It's important to address the problem early to avoid it becoming chronic.
