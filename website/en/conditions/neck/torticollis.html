Acute Torticollis (Wry Neck)

Wry neck? 90% experience this during their lifetime. Chiropractor at Majorstua can speed up your recovery.

TL;DR - Summary

  • What it is: Sudden, painful stiff neck where the head becomes "locked" to one side - very common and harmless in most cases.
  • Most common causes: Facet joint dysfunction ("locking"), awkward sleeping position, minor muscle strain. Rarely serious.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence that manual therapy combined with exercises can provide faster improvement (Cochrane, 2025).
  • Red flags: Fever + sore throat + headache, neurological symptoms, or trauma - then you should see a doctor.
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up.

What is acute torticollis?

Acute torticollis - often called "wry neck" or "stiff neck" - is a sudden condition where the neck muscles spasm and the head becomes "locked" in a crooked position. You typically wake up in the morning with pain on one side of the neck and cannot move your head normally.

The condition is very common. According to StatPearls (2023), approximately 90% of the population will experience at least one episode during their lifetime, and about 70% get neck pain at some point. A Swedish study from Northern Sweden (Guez, 2002) found that 43% of the population reported neck pain - 48% of women and 38% of men.

Acute torticollis most often affects younger people between 12 and 30 years old (Physio Network; Counsell, 2016), and women are affected about twice as often as men. The positive news is that the condition almost always resolves on its own within 1-2 weeks.

Causes and Risk Factors

In most cases, no clear cause is found - the condition appears "suddenly" without warning. However, there are several known triggering factors:

Most common (majority of cases)

  • Idiopathic/unknown cause - most have no identifiable trigger (Patient.info, 2023)
  • Facet joint dysfunction - "locking" in the neck joints, the most accepted mechanism (Physio Network)
  • Sleeping position - awkward position or poor pillow support (Patient.info, 2023)
  • Minor muscle strain - slight overload of neck muscles
  • Rapid neck movements - sudden twisting or movement

Less common

  • Discogenic irritation - cervical disc problem causing gradual onset stiff neck
  • Post-viral/Upper respiratory infection - infection triggering swelling and muscle spasms
  • Trauma - accounts for 10-20% of all torticollis cases, including whiplash (Medscape, 2024)
  • Drug-induced - acute dystonia from certain medications (phenothiazines, metoclopramide, haloperidol)

Rare (requires urgent evaluation)

Atlantoaxial rotatory subluxation, retropharyngeal abscess, tumors in neck or posterior fossa. These conditions require medical evaluation and are very rare.

Symptoms

Typically you wake up in the morning with symptoms without being able to point to any clear trigger. Some experience it after a long car trip, flight, or after sleeping in an unfamiliar bed.

  • One-sided neck pain - which can radiate up toward the head or down toward the shoulder, and sometimes cause dizziness
  • Limited movement - the head is "locked" tilted toward the painful side, with the chin rotated the opposite way
  • Pain-free at rest - you can often find a position where it doesn't hurt
  • Muscle tightness - neck muscles (especially trapezius and sternocleidomastoid) feel tight and tender

Facet joint vs. discogenic torticollis

With facet joint problems, symptom onset is sudden and you can find a comfortable position. With discogenic cause, pain comes on more gradually, and it's difficult to find rest.

Diagnosis

For typical acute torticollis without warning signs, the diagnosis is clinical - meaning the chiropractor makes the diagnosis based on medical history and physical examination. Imaging is usually not necessary.

When is imaging appropriate?

According to guidelines from AAFP (2020) and Royal Children's Hospital (2020), X-ray or MRI may be appropriate if:

  • Symptoms don't improve within 1-2 weeks
  • You have neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, walking problems)
  • There has been trauma
  • You have fever or other systemic symptoms

Important to know

MRI and X-ray often show "findings" like wear and degenerative changes - this is normal in most adults and rarely explains your pain. Clinical examination often provides more useful information than images.

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Most cases of acute torticollis resolve on their own within 1-2 weeks. A systematic Cochrane review from 2025 (Chacko et al.) shows that manual therapy combined with exercises can provide better function and some pain relief compared to no treatment - with an actual benefit of about 14% for function and 24% for pain.

In clinical practice, the chiropractor often uses a combination of:

  • Gentle mobilization - movement in pain-free directions to regain mobility
  • Soft tissue treatment - massage and trigger point treatment to loosen tight muscles
  • Manipulation - if indicated, joint adjustment can be helpful
  • Exercises and advice - home exercises and ergonomic tips to prevent recurrence

Treatment starts in pain-free directions of movement - this is safer and more comfortable than pushing against the pain (Clinical Edge, 2024).

What can you do yourself?

  • Keep moving - move your neck gently as far as possible without too much pain. Avoid keeping your neck completely still.
  • Heat or cold - ice for the first 48-72 hours can reduce acute inflammation, then heat may feel soothing. Use 15-20 minutes at a time.
  • Pain relievers - paracetamol at recommended dose is often sufficient. Ibuprofen can be used if needed in the acute phase.
  • Avoid neck collar - immobilization weakens muscles and delays healing (Joint Academy).

Treatment Course

For typical acute facet joint torticollis, many can experience significant improvement after 1-3 treatments over a few days to a week (MSK Doctors). With discogenic cause, healing takes longer - often 4-8 weeks (Cetilar).

Honest expectation

A control study showed that 79% of patients who didn't receive treatment also reported improvement after one year (Pain Medicine, 2021). Treatment can speed up improvement, but most people get better regardless.

Red Flags

Seek medical attention or emergency room if you have:

  • Fever combined with sore throat and headache
  • Increased drooling or difficulty swallowing
  • Weakness or numbness in arms or legs
  • Walking problems or balance issues
  • Vision disturbances or double vision
  • Trauma or injury to the neck
  • Tearing or stabbing pain in the neck (vascular cause)

A study (Starc et al., 2018) showed that the combination of fever, sore throat, and headache gives 32-58% risk of serious underlying disease.

Prognosis

The prognosis for acute torticollis is very good. Most people recover completely within 1-2 weeks, regardless of treatment (Medscape, 2024). The most common form (facet joint torticollis) can often resolve within 24-48 hours.

Recurrence is common - approximately 50% experience a new episode at some point (Range Physiotherapy - clinical estimate). Therefore, it's wise to work on prevention: good ergonomics, suitable pillow, regular movement, and strengthening exercises for neck and shoulders.

A small minority may develop persistent problems over months. In such cases, thorough investigation is important to rule out other causes.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does wry neck last?

Most people experience significant improvement within 2-7 days, and full recovery within 1-2 weeks. With discogenic cause, it may take 4-8 weeks.

Should I keep my neck still?

No. Gentle movement is better than stillness. Keeping the neck completely still weakens muscles and can prolong healing. Move within your pain tolerance.

Is acute torticollis dangerous?

In the vast majority of cases, it is completely harmless. Rare cases with fever, neurological symptoms, or trauma should be examined by a doctor to rule out more serious conditions.

Do I need X-ray or MRI?

Usually not. The diagnosis is made clinically. Imaging is only appropriate for red flags, persistent symptoms beyond 2 weeks, or neurological deficits.

Does a chiropractor help with wry neck?

The Cochrane review from 2025 shows moderate evidence that manual therapy combined with exercises can provide somewhat faster improvement in function and pain. Most people recover regardless, but treatment can speed up the process.

Can I prevent recurrence?

Yes, partially. Good sleeping position with the right pillow, regular movement, ergonomic workplace, and strengthening exercises for neck/shoulders can reduce the risk of new episodes.

When should I seek help?

You don't need to wait to seek help - a chiropractor can evaluate you promptly. Early treatment can provide faster pain relief. For red flags (fever, neurological symptoms, trauma), you should see a doctor the same day.
