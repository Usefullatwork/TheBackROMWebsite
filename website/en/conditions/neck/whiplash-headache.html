Headache After Whiplash - Post-Traumatic Headache

Symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Key Points

  • What it is: Headache that occurs after whiplash, often combined with neck pain, dizziness and concentration difficulties
  • Common headache types: Migraine-like (31-61%), tension-type (21-40%), cervicogenic headache (from neck)
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence for manual treatment - early mobilization is clearly better than rest and collar (Cochrane 2007)
  • Red flags: Double vision, speech difficulties, facial numbness, weakness in arms/legs, bladder/bowel problems
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up
1

What is post-traumatic headache after whiplash?

Post-traumatic headache (PTH) is headache that develops after an injury to the head or neck. In whiplash, this typically occurs after an acceleration-deceleration mechanism - such as rear-end collision, falls, or sports injuries.

Between 30-90% of all who experience whiplash develop headache in the acute phase (Al-Khazali et al., 2020). A meta-analysis from 2023 showed that 47% still have headache one year after the injury (Herrero Babiloni et al., Pain 2023).

In Northern Sweden, approximately 235 whiplash injuries per 100,000 inhabitants are reported annually (Styrke et al., Spine 2012).

The International Headache Classification (ICHD-3, 2018) distinguishes between:

Acute PTH

Headache that starts within 7 days of injury and lasts less than 3 months.

Persistent (chronic) PTH

Headache that continues beyond 3 months after the injury.

The Quebec Task Force classifies whiplash (WAD) into grades from 0-IV, where grades I-II make up approximately 90% of all cases. Grade II - with neck pain and reduced mobility - has the highest risk of developing chronic symptoms.

2

Why do you get headache after whiplash?

Most common causes - from most to least common

1. Facet joint injuries (approx. 60% of chronic cases)

Studies with diagnostic blocks show that the facet joints in the neck are the main source of pain in the majority with chronic whiplash (Lord & Barnsley, Spine 1996). The most frequently affected levels are C2-C3 (36%) and C5-C6 (35%).

2. Muscle and ligament injuries

The deep neck muscles and ligaments - especially around the upper cervical joints (C1-C2) - often sustain microtrauma. MRI studies have shown fatty infiltration in these muscles after whiplash.

3. Trigeminocervical convergence

Pain nerves from the neck (C1-C3) meet the trigeminal nerve in the brainstem. This explains why neck injury causes headache - pain signals from the neck are "misinterpreted" as headache.

4. Central sensitization

In chronic whiplash, the nervous system can become hypersensitive. Systematic reviews confirm that chronic WAD involves central sensitization with lower pain thresholds and enhanced pain perception (Van Oosterwijck et al., 2013).

3

Symptoms

Post-traumatic headache often has a mix of several headache types:

Most common headache types (Ashina et al., 2020)

Migraine-like headache (31-61%)

Pulsating pain, often unilateral, with nausea, light and sound sensitivity. Many experience this for the first time after the injury.

Tension-type headache (21-40%)

Pressing, squeezing pain like a band around the head.

Cervicogenic headache

Pain that starts in the neck and radiates up toward the back of the head and temples.

74% experience light sensitivity
72% experience sound sensitivity
55% experience nausea
25-50% experience dizziness

Many also report "brain fog", concentration difficulties and increased fatigue.

4

Diagnosis

Clinical examination is most important

For whiplash, we always start with a thorough clinical examination. We examine neck mobility, joint function, muscles and neurological status. The Canadian C-Spine Rule is used to assess whether imaging is necessary.

When is imaging needed?

X-ray or CT is only necessary when fracture or dislocation is suspected. Indications include: age over 65, dangerous injury mechanism, tingling/numbness in arms or legs, or inability to actively move the neck.

MRI - often misunderstood

MRI in whiplash often shows normal findings or age-related wear that doesn't explain the pain. Studies show that MRI findings in people with whiplash often don't differ from healthy individuals. Clinical examination usually provides better information than imaging.

5

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

A Cochrane review from 2007 reviewed 23 studies with over 2,300 participants. The main conclusion was clear: Early active mobilization is clearly better than rest and neck collar.

We use gentle techniques adapted to the injury phase:

  • Mobilization: Soft techniques to restore movement in locked joints. In acute whiplash, we avoid forceful manipulation.
  • Soft tissue treatment: Treatment of tight muscles that try to "protect" the neck.
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: Many become dizzy after whiplash. We provide exercises to train balance and coordination between eyes, ears and neck.

A meta-analysis from 2022 (Bini et al., Chiropractic & Manual Therapies) showed moderate to large short-term effects on headache intensity in cervicogenic headache treated with manual therapy.

What can you do yourself?

Norwegian health authorities recommend that exercise should start within 4 days after the injury. Neck collar is unnecessary in most cases and can actually prolong symptoms if used for more than 48 hours.

Early movement

Repeated, gentle neck and head movements provide more relief than rest.

Deep neck muscles

Exercises for the deep neck flexors are important. These muscles often "shut off" after whiplash, and the larger superficial muscles take over - which can cause more headache.

Avoid inactivity

Those who rest and use collar have twice the risk of persistent symptoms as those who move early (McKinney et al., 1989).

Treatment course

There are no clear guidelines for exact number of treatments for whiplash. A systematic review (American Journal of PM&R, 2024) found best results with at least 2 sessions per week for minimum 6 weeks.

  • Acute phase (0-6 weeks): Focus on pain relief, restoring movement and reassurance
  • Subacute phase (6-12 weeks): Gradual increase in activity and stabilizing exercises
  • Chronic phase (>12 weeks): Multimodal approach with focus on psychosocial factors

Most with uncomplicated whiplash improve significantly within 3 months. But some need longer follow-up, especially with grade II-III.

6

Red flags - When to see a doctor immediately

Call emergency services or go to emergency room for the following symptoms:

Neurological symptoms:

  • Acute weakness or paralysis in arms/legs
  • Numbness or tingling, especially on both sides
  • Bladder or bowel problems
  • Walking difficulties or coordination problems

Vascular warning signs ("5D3N"):

  • Double vision, speech difficulties, swallowing difficulties
  • Sudden dizziness, facial numbness, nystagmus

Signs of arterial dissection:

  • Severe one-sided neck pain with sudden onset
  • Thunderclap headache (worst headache ever)
  • Horner's syndrome (drooping eyelid + small pupil)

Prognosis - What happens?

The first 3 months are crucial

Research shows that improvement mainly occurs during the first 3 months after the injury. After this, the improvement curve flattens dramatically. A study found that 86% of those who were still symptomatic at 3 months remained symptomatic after 2 years.

Three typical courses (Sterling et al., JOSPT 2016)

~50% Mild course: Full recovery
~30% Moderate course: Persistent moderate symptoms
~20% Severe course: Chronic disability

Risk factors for poor outcome

High initial pain intensity, high functional disability from the start, cold sensitivity, pain catastrophizing and post-traumatic stress symptoms increase the risk of chronicity. Low expectations of improvement is a strong negative prognostic factor.

Relapse is common

Even among those who recover, many experience relapse. Long-term studies show that only about 35% are fully recovered after 5 years. This underscores the importance of learning self-management strategies.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does headache after whiplash last?

In most people (50-80%), the headache resolves within 3 months. Around 20-50% develop chronic symptoms that last longer. Early active treatment reduces the risk of chronicity.

Should I use a neck collar?

No, in most cases. Norwegian health authorities recommend that collar not be used, or maximum 48 hours for grade II-III. Research shows that collar can prolong symptoms.

Can a chiropractor help with headache after whiplash?

Yes, manual treatment has moderate evidence for improving headache and neck pain in the short term, especially combined with exercises. Early treatment gives better results than waiting.

When should I get an MRI of my neck?

MRI is rarely necessary for uncomplicated whiplash. Imaging is only indicated with neurological deficits or suspicion of serious injury. Clinical examination is often more informative.

Can I exercise after whiplash?

Yes, and you should start early. Norwegian health authorities recommend starting within 4 days. Begin with gentle movements and increase gradually. Avoid contact sports and heavy lifting in the acute phase.

What's the difference between migraine and headache from the neck?

Cervicogenic headache starts in the neck and worsens with neck strain. Migraine is often pulsating, unilateral and accompanied by nausea/light sensitivity. In post-traumatic headache, we often see a mix. Read more about occipital neuralgia for information about other types of headache that can occur after neck injury.

How many treatments do I need?

This varies. Acute cases can improve after 4-6 treatments. Chronic symptoms may require longer follow-up. We evaluate continuously and adapt treatment.
