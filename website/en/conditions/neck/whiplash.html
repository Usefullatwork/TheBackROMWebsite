Whiplash

Symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Key Points

  • What it is: Whiplash-associated disorders (WAD) are symptoms after acceleration/deceleration injury to the neck
  • Common symptoms: Neck pain (62-100%), headache (60-88%), stiffness (72%), dizziness (25-50%)
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence for multimodal treatment (manual therapy + exercises)
  • Important: Early mobilization gives better results than rest - neck collar is not recommended
1

What is whiplash?

Whiplash describes an injury mechanism where the head is suddenly thrown forward and backward. The term "whiplash-associated disorders" (WAD) is used for symptoms that occur after such an injury. It is therefore not a diagnosis in itself, but a description of how the injury occurred.

Rear-end collision in a car is the most common cause, but whiplash can also occur during sports accidents, falls, or other situations with rapid movement of the neck. 83% of people involved in car accidents experience some degree of whiplash injury (PMC, 2009).

Cervical sprain, cervical strain and whiplash - the same condition

The terms "cervical sprain" and "cervical strain" are often used interchangeably. The Quebec Task Force acknowledged as early as 1995 that these conditions cannot be distinguished through clinical examination or standard imaging. Theoretically, "sprain" refers to ligament damage, while "strain" describes muscle-tendon injury. In practice, this distinction has no significance for treatment.

Prevalence

Norway has the world's highest prevalence of neck pain, with 6,151 cases per 100,000 inhabitants according to the Global Burden of Disease study. In the Nordic countries, approximately 235-383 people per 100,000 are affected by whiplash annually. Women have approximately twice the risk of men for developing symptoms after rear-end collision.

WAD Classification (Quebec Task Force)

Grade Description
WAD 0 No neck complaints or clinical findings
WAD I Neck complaints (pain, stiffness, tenderness) without clinical findings
WAD II Neck complaints with musculoskeletal findings (reduced mobility, point tenderness) - most common (~82%)
WAD III Neck complaints with neurological findings (reduced reflexes, weakness, sensory deficits)
WAD IV Fracture or dislocation - requires immediate medical evaluation

The vast majority have WAD grade II. Headache, dizziness, tinnitus, and jaw problems can occur at all grades.

3

Causes and risk factors

Most common causes

Car accidents (whiplash)

The classic cause. During rear-end collision, the neck is exposed to rapid acceleration-deceleration that can damage soft tissues.

Sports injuries

Contact sports such as football, rugby, and martial arts increase risk through direct impacts or falls.

Falls and accidents

Falls from height or sudden movements can cause soft tissue injuries.

Risk factors for prolonged symptoms

Research has identified several factors that increase the risk of chronic symptoms (Walton et al., 2013):

  • High pain intensity from the start (above 5.5/10)
  • High functional disability from the start
  • Catastrophizing (excessive worry about symptoms)
  • Fear of movement
  • Previous neck problems
  • Post-traumatic stress symptoms

Interesting finding

Research shows that the collision itself (direction, speed, headrest type) does not significantly affect prognosis. Imaging findings also do not predict prognosis - psychosocial factors matter far more.

4

Symptoms

Main symptoms

  • Neck pain and stiffness - 62-100% (the most common symptom)
  • Headache - 60-88%, often originating from the neck
  • Shoulder and upper back stiffness - approx. 72%
  • Fatigue - 70-80%
  • Dizziness - 25-50% (up to 85% in some studies)
  • Concentration difficulties - approx. 41%

Delayed symptom onset is common

Symptoms don't need to appear immediately. Many experience delayed onset from 12 hours to several days after the accident. This delay of 12-72 hours is characteristic and is due in part to adrenaline and endorphins masking pain in the acute phase.

5

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Systematic reviews show that multimodal treatment - the combination of manual therapy and exercises - gives better results than single interventions alone (Hidalgo et al., 2017).

Treatment may include:

  • Manual treatment: Gentle mobilization of neck joints and muscles
  • Soft tissue treatment: Trigger point therapy, muscle stretching and fascial treatment
  • Active rehabilitation: Specific exercises for strength, stability and mobility
  • Advice and guidance: Information about the condition and what you can do yourself

Early mobilization - not rest

One of the most important findings from whiplash research is that early activity gives better results than rest and neck collar. A study by McKinney (1989) showed that 46% of those who were told to rest with a neck collar still had symptoms after two years, compared to only 23% of those who received advice about early mobilization.

Neck collar is not recommended

Neck collar and prolonged rest are not recommended in modern guidelines. International guidelines give Grade A recommendation against the use of neck collar.

What can you do yourself?

  • Stay active: Avoid prolonged rest and bed rest - gradual return to normal activities promotes healing
  • Move neck gently: Calm movements in all directions several times daily, within comfort zone
  • Avoid neck collar: Unless a doctor has specifically prescribed it
  • Home exercises: The chiropractor will give you customized exercises as part of treatment

Treatment course

For acute whiplash (WAD I-II), the OPTIMa guidelines recommend:

  • Acute phase: 2-3 treatments per week for 2-4 weeks (6-12 treatments total)
  • Evaluation: Assessment of response after 6-12 treatments
  • Persistent symptoms: 1-2 treatments per week with gradual tapering, increasing focus on self-training
6

Red flags - When to see a doctor

CONTACT DOCTOR OR EMERGENCY ROOM IMMEDIATELY FOR:

  • Severe headache that doesn't subside, especially in the back of head
  • Sudden intense headache ("thunderclap")
  • Double vision, vision loss or other visual disturbances
  • Weakness, numbness or tingling in arms or legs that increases
  • Balance or walking problems
  • Speech or swallowing difficulties
  • Bladder or bowel problems
  • Fever combined with neck stiffness

These symptoms may indicate fracture, arterial injury, spinal cord involvement, or other serious conditions requiring immediate evaluation.

Prognosis - What can you expect?

Three typical courses (Sterling et al., Pain, 2010)

Mild course (~45-50%)

Full recovery within 2-3 months with minimal residual symptoms.

Moderate course (~30-40%)

Partial recovery with persistent mild to moderate symptoms.

Severe course (~15-20%)

Poor recovery with chronic moderate to severe symptoms.

The first three months are crucial

An important finding is that the first three months are decisive for long-term prognosis. A study showed that 93% of those who were symptom-free at three months remained symptom-free after two years. Conversely, 86% of those with symptoms at three months still had symptoms after two years.

~50% recover within 3 months
53% reduced relapse risk with exercise
93% symptom-free at 3 months stay well

Preventing relapse

Exercise reduces the risk of new episodes by 53% (RR 0.47) according to a meta-analysis from 2023 (Campos, JOSPT, 2023). This is the most important preventive factor. Regular exercise - especially neck and shoulder stabilizing exercises - is highly recommended.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does whiplash take to heal?

Most people experience significant improvement within 2-3 months. About half recover fully, while approximately 50% have some persistent symptoms after one year.

Should I use a neck collar?

No, neck collar is not recommended for normal whiplash (Grade A evidence against). Research shows that early movement and activity give better results than immobilization.

What's the difference between cervical sprain, strain and whiplash?

In practice, none. Theoretically, "sprain" involves ligaments and "strain" muscles/tendons, but they cannot be distinguished through examination or imaging. The Quebec Task Force acknowledged this as early as 1995. Treatment is identical.

Can symptoms appear several days after the accident?

Yes, this is common. Symptoms can appear from 12 hours to several days (typically 12-72 hours) after the injury.

How can I prevent new episodes?

Regular exercise - especially neck and shoulder stabilizing exercises - reduces the risk of relapse by over 50%.
