AC Joint Osteoarthritis: Everything You Need to Know

Causes, symptoms, treatment and weightlifter's shoulder

Quick Summary

  • 75% over 40 have MRI findings
  • Only 10% have symptoms
  • Weightlifters: 93% improve with modification
  • Activity adjustment is key
  • Surgery rarely necessary

You feel an aching pain on top of your shoulder, exactly where the collarbone meets the shoulder blade. The pain worsens when you lift your arm overhead or when you reach across to the opposite shoulder. Perhaps you've also heard grinding or crepitus from the joint. This is one of many types of shoulder pain that can affect you.

These are typical symptoms of AC joint osteoarthritis - wear in the small joint between the collarbone (clavicle) and the shoulder blade's projection (acromion).

But here's what's important: 75% of people over 40 have degenerative changes in the AC joint on X-ray or MRI. Yet only 10% of these have symptoms. This means that an image showing wear doesn't necessarily explain your pain.

1

What is the AC Joint and AC Joint Osteoarthritis?

Anatomy

The AC joint (acromioclavicular joint) is a small joint at the top of the shoulder where:

  • The collarbone (clavicle) meets
  • The acromion - a projection from the shoulder blade

The joint has a small cartilage disc (discus) and is stabilized by several ligaments. It allows movement when you lift your arm and is involved in most shoulder movements.

AC joint osteoarthritis is wear (degeneration) of the cartilage and structures in this joint. The changes include:

  • Cartilage that becomes thinner and more uneven
  • Bone spurs (osteophytes) that form
  • Joint space that becomes narrower
  • Subchondral sclerosis (thickening of the bone under the cartilage)
75% over 40 have MRI findings
10% actually have symptoms
40-50 yrs typical onset age

Important Perspective

Degenerative changes in the AC joint are normal age-related changes. Most people who have such findings on X-ray or MRI have no symptoms. Focus on your symptoms, not the image.

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Primary Causes

Age-Related Wear

Most Common

Normal degeneration over time. The AC joint is one of the first joints in the body to show age-related changes, often from the 20-30 age range.

Overload and Repetitive Activity

Occupations or sports with lots of overhead work or heavy lifting put extra stress on the joint. Common in:

  • Powerlifters and crossfitters
  • Carpenters and painters
  • Overhead athletes (volleyball, handball, tennis)

Previous Injury

An AC joint separation or collarbone fracture increases the risk of later osteoarthritis in the joint.

Weightlifter's Shoulder (Distal Clavicular Osteolysis)

Special Condition in Strength Athletes

Weightlifter's shoulder is a condition where the end of the collarbone breaks down due to repetitive microtrauma. Common in those who train heavy bench press, shoulder press and dips.

  • Pain on top of the shoulder
  • Worsens with heavy lifting
  • X-ray shows dissolution of the outer end of the collarbone
  • 93% improve with activity modification

Risk Factors

  • Age over 40 years
  • Heavy physical work or strength training
  • Previous AC joint injury
  • Overhead sports
  • Previous collarbone or shoulder fracture
3

Symptoms

Symptoms of AC joint osteoarthritis are often characteristic and localized:

Typical Symptoms

  • Localized pain: On top of the shoulder, often pinpointable
  • Provocation by movements: Reaching across to opposite shoulder (cross-body)
  • Pain with overhead: Especially at end range of movement
  • Crepitus: Grinding or clicking from the joint
  • Night pain: When lying on affected side

Activities That Typically Provoke

  • Bench press and shoulder press
  • Dips and push-ups
  • Putting on a jacket
  • Reaching up to cabinets
  • Sleeping on affected side

Distinguishing from Other Conditions

AC joint pain can resemble other shoulder conditions but has some distinctive features:

Signs That Point to the AC Joint

  • Pain localized directly on top of the shoulder
  • Positive cross-body adduction test
  • Tenderness on direct palpation over the AC joint
  • Pain at end range of overhead movement
  • Pain relief with injection into the AC joint
4

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made based on medical history, clinical examination and possibly imaging.

Clinical Tests

  • Cross-body adduction: Moving the arm across toward the opposite shoulder. Positive if pain in the AC joint.
  • Palpation: Direct tenderness over the AC joint
  • O'Brien's test: Can be positive, but also with SLAP
  • AC joint injection test: Pain relief after anesthetic injection confirms the AC joint as the pain source

Imaging

X-ray

First choice. Shows:

  • Narrowed joint space
  • Bone spurs (osteophytes)
  • Subchondral sclerosis
  • In weightlifter's shoulder: Dissolution of outer clavicle

MRI

Rarely necessary for AC joint osteoarthritis alone. Most useful for ruling out concurrent pathology (rotator cuff, etc.).

Important to Remember

75% of people over 40 have arthritic changes in the AC joint on imaging. An imaging finding does not necessarily mean this is the pain source. The diagnosis must correlate with clinical findings.

5

Red Flags and Differential Diagnoses

Seek Medical Attention If:

  • Trauma followed by visible deformity (possible AC joint dislocation)
  • Swelling, redness and warmth (could be infection)
  • General symptoms like fever, weight loss
  • Previous cancer with new pain
  • Sudden loss of arm strength

Differential Diagnoses

Rotator Cuff Tendinopathy

Causes pain more on the outside of the shoulder and with specific movements. Can coexist with AC joint osteoarthritis.

Subacromial Impingement

Pain with abduction 60-120 degrees. Overlapping symptoms possible.

Cervical Radiculopathy

Pain from the neck radiating to the shoulder. Neck movements often provoke the pain.

AC Joint Separation

Acute injury with visible deformity. Distinguished from gradual onset osteoarthritis.

6

Treatment

The vast majority with AC joint osteoarthritis improve with conservative treatment. The key is activity modification and strength training.

Conservative Treatment (First Choice)

Activity Modification

This is the most important intervention, especially for weightlifter's shoulder:

  • Temporarily avoid exercises that provoke pain
  • Modify grip (narrower grip on bench press)
  • Reduce depth on dips
  • Use less weight with more repetitions
  • Avoid overhead press until pain subsides

Training Modifications for Strength Athletes

  • Bench press: Narrower grip, stop before bar reaches chest, floor press
  • Shoulder press: Replace with lateral raises, face pulls
  • Dips: Reduce depth or replace with triceps pushdowns
  • Push-ups: Hands closer to body, limited depth

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

  • Manual treatment: Mobilization of AC joint, thoracic spine and neck
  • Soft tissue treatment: Work on musculature around the shoulder
  • Exercise guidance: Help modifying training program
  • Taping: Can provide temporary relief

Injection Treatment

Cortisone Injection

Can provide good pain relief during acute flare-ups. The effect is often temporary (weeks to months), but can be combined with activity modification for better long-term results.

Surgery

Surgery is considered only when conservative treatment over 3-6 months doesn't provide satisfactory improvement:

Distal Clavicle Resection (Mumford Procedure)

The outer end of the collarbone is removed to eliminate painful bone-on-bone contact. Can be done arthroscopically or open. Good results in those with indication.

7

Prognosis

The prognosis for AC joint osteoarthritis is generally good with conservative treatment.

The Positive:

  • Most achieve good symptom control without surgery
  • 93% with weightlifter's shoulder improve with activity modification
  • Many can continue training with an adjusted program
  • Surgery has good results in those who need it

The Realistic:

  • The wear itself doesn't disappear
  • Symptoms can vary over time
  • Some must permanently modify training/activity
  • Flare-ups can occur

Factors That Affect Prognosis

  • Willingness to modify activity: Crucial for improvement
  • Degree of wear: Significance uncertain - many have wear without symptoms
  • Concurrent pathology: Rotator cuff problems can complicate the picture
  • Early intervention: Faster improvement with early activity adjustment
8

What You Can Do Yourself

For Acute Symptoms

  • Ice on the AC joint 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Avoid activities that provoke pain
  • Anti-inflammatory medications as needed
  • Sleep on opposite side or on your back

Training Modifications

  • Reduce weight and increase repetitions
  • Avoid deep movements that stretch the AC joint
  • Use narrower grip on bench press
  • Replace overhead press with lateral raises
  • Focus on rotator cuff strengthening

Exercises That Often Help

  • External rotation with band: Strengthens rotator cuff without loading AC joint
  • Rows: Pulling exercises are often well tolerated
  • Face pulls: Strengthens posterior shoulder and scapula
  • Scapula exercises: Improves shoulder blade control

Exercises to Be Careful With

  • Heavy bench press with wide grip
  • Deep dips
  • Overhead press with heavy weight
  • Flyes with deep movements
9

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I continue to exercise with AC joint osteoarthritis?
Yes, but with modifications. Avoid exercises that provoke pain, especially heavy bench press with wide grip, deep dips and overhead press. Many can continue to train by adjusting grip, angle and load.
Does wear on X-ray mean I need surgery?
No. 75% of people over 40 have degenerative changes on MRI/X-ray, but only 10% have symptoms. Wear is normal and doesn't necessarily mean you need treatment. Treatment is based on symptoms, not images.
Does cortisone help with AC joint osteoarthritis?
Cortisone injection in the AC joint can provide good pain relief, especially during acute flare-ups. The effect is often temporary (weeks to months), but can be combined with activity modification for better long-term results.
What is weightlifter's shoulder?
Weightlifter's shoulder (distal clavicular osteolysis) is a condition where the end of the collarbone breaks down due to repetitive microtrauma from heavy lifting. 93% improve with activity modification.
How long does it take for AC joint pain to improve?
With proper treatment and activity adjustment, many experience significant improvement within 6-12 weeks. For weightlifter's shoulder, it may take 3-6 months. Most achieve good symptom control without surgery.
Will the wear get worse over time?
Degenerative changes develop gradually over time, but this doesn't necessarily mean symptoms worsen. Many with significant wear on imaging have few or no symptoms. Focus on function, not images.
