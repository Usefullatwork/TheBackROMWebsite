Biceps Tendinopathy: Everything You Need to Know

Causes, diagnosis, treatment and prognosis

You notice an aching pain at the front of your shoulder, right where the arm meets the body. The pain worsens when you lift your arm, carry shopping bags, or do bicep curls at the gym. Perhaps you have also heard a click or felt something "jumping" at the front of your shoulder.

This may be a sign of biceps tendinopathy - a condition that affects the long head of the biceps tendon where it passes through the shoulder joint. It is a common form of shoulder pain.

The good news? Most people recover with conservative treatment. 75% of patients avoid surgery, and the right exercises and treatment can provide significant improvement.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • 95% have concurrent shoulder pathology
  • 75% avoid surgery
  • Most common: 40-60 years, overhead sports
  • Eccentric training is key
  • Rarely isolated - look for other causes

What is biceps tendinopathy?

Biceps tendinopathy refers to pain and dysfunction in the long head of the biceps tendon (caput longum). This tendon runs from the shoulder joint, through a groove in the upper arm bone (bicipital groove), and down to the forearm.

Anatomy

The biceps muscle has two attachment points (heads) at the top:

  • Long head (caput longum): Attaches to the top of the labrum inside the shoulder joint. This is most susceptible to injury.
  • Short head (caput breve): Attaches to the coracoid process on the shoulder blade. Rarely affected.

The long head is unique because it passes inside the shoulder joint before it emerges and runs down along the upper arm. This makes it vulnerable to irritation from multiple sources.

Terminology

Several terms are used:

  • Tendinitis: Inflammation of the tendon (acute phase)
  • Tendinosis: Degenerative changes without active inflammation (chronic phase)
  • Tendinopathy: Umbrella term covering both conditions

Important perspective

Biceps tendinopathy rarely occurs in isolation. Up to 95% of cases have concurrent shoulder pathology, such as rotator cuff injury, impingement or SLAP lesion. Therefore, thorough examination is important to find all causes of the pain.

2

Causes and risk factors

Biceps tendinopathy can develop in several ways:

Primary causes

Overuse

Most common

Repetitive movements over time, especially overhead activities. Common in:

  • Throwing sports (handball, baseball, volleyball) - see thrower's shoulder
  • Swimmers (especially crawl and butterfly)
  • Weightlifters (military press, bench press)
  • Work with arms overhead (painters, carpenters) - see ergonomics and shoulder

Secondary to other shoulder pathology

The biceps tendon often becomes irritated as a result of:

  • Rotator cuff tendinopathy or ruptures
  • Subacromial impingement
  • SLAP lesion (injury to the biceps attachment)
  • Shoulder instability

Degenerative changes

Age-related wear of the tendon. More common after age 40. The tendon becomes less elastic and more susceptible to injury.

Risk factors

40-60 years typical age group
95% have concurrent pathology
Overhead sports and work
  • Age over 40 years
  • Overhead sports or occupations
  • Previous shoulder problems
  • Poor warm-up before training
  • Rapid increase in training load
  • Poor shoulder mechanics and weak rotator cuff
3

Symptoms

The symptoms of biceps tendinopathy are often characteristic:

Main symptoms

  • Localized pain: At the front of the shoulder, in the bicipital groove (sulcus). Often pinpointable.
  • Provocation: Pain with overhead lifting, bicep curls, heavy lifting in front of the body
  • Clicking or snapping: Feeling of something "jumping" at the front of the shoulder
  • Night pain: May be present, especially when lying on the affected side - see sleep and shoulder pain

Activities that typically provoke pain

  • Lifting heavy objects with straight arms
  • Bicep curls and hammer curls
  • Dips and pushups
  • Overhead press
  • Throwing movements
  • Reaching with arms above shoulder height

Differentiation from other conditions

Biceps tendinopathy can resemble and occur together with other shoulder problems:

Signs pointing to biceps

  • Point tenderness directly over the bicipital groove (sulcus)
  • Pain worsens with resisted supination
  • Positive Speed's test and Upper cut test
  • Clicking or snapping with rotation
4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is usually made clinically based on history and physical examination.

Clinical tests

Upper cut test combined with palpation

This combination has proven particularly useful. Studies show that the Upper cut test combined with tenderness on palpation over the bicipital groove has a sensitivity of 88.3%. This means the test catches most cases of biceps tendinopathy.

Other useful tests:

  • Speed's test: Pain with forward elevation of straight arm with supinated hand
  • Yergason's test: Pain with resisted supination with bent elbow
  • Palpation: Tenderness over the bicipital groove (sulcus bicipitalis)

Imaging

Ultrasound

First choice for imaging. Can show:

  • Thickening or thinning of the tendon
  • Fluid around the tendon
  • Partial tear
  • Subluxation (tendon jumping out of the groove)

MRI

Most useful for evaluating:

  • Concurrent rotator cuff pathology
  • SLAP lesion (injury to labrum/biceps attachment)
  • Intra-articular portion of the tendon

When is imaging necessary?

  • Lack of improvement after 6-8 weeks of conservative treatment
  • Suspicion of concurrent rotator cuff injury
  • Suspicion of SLAP lesion
  • Clinical suspicion of rupture
  • Preparation for possible surgery
5

Red flags and differential diagnoses

It is important to rule out other conditions that can cause similar symptoms.

Seek medical attention if:

  • Sudden "pop" followed by severe pain and visible lump on upper arm (may be rupture)
  • Significant loss of strength in the arm
  • Swelling, redness and warmth (may be infection)
  • Pain that does not subside with rest over several weeks
  • General symptoms such as fever, weight loss

If you suspect complete rupture of the biceps tendon (seen as "Popeye arm"), you should seek medical attention promptly, although the condition itself is rarely dangerous.

Conditions that can resemble or occur simultaneously

SLAP lesion

Injury to the labrum where the biceps tendon attaches. Often difficult to distinguish from biceps tendinopathy. MR arthrography is the gold standard for diagnosis.

Rotator cuff tendinopathy

Often present simultaneously. Supraspinatus tendinopathy causes pain more on the outside of the shoulder but can overlap.

AC joint osteoarthritis

Pain localized more to the top of the shoulder. Worsens with cross-body movements (cross-body adduction).

Subscapularis injury

Can cause pain at the front of the shoulder and affect the biceps tendon secondarily (subscapularis stabilizes the biceps in the groove).

6

Treatment

Conservative treatment is the first choice, and 75% of patients recover without surgery.

Conservative treatment

What can the chiropractor/physiotherapist do?

  • Manual therapy: Soft tissue work, mobilization of shoulder and thoracic spine
  • Eccentric training: Graded loading to stimulate tendon healing
  • Rotator cuff strengthening: Address any weaknesses contributing to the problem
  • Scapula stabilization: Improve shoulder blade control
  • Exercise guidance: Modification of exercises that provoke symptoms

Exercise modifications

Exercises to avoid or modify temporarily

  • Deep dips
  • Heavy bicep curls with straight arms
  • Barbell bench press with wide grip
  • Pull-ups with wide grip
  • Overhead press with heavy weight

Exercises that are often well tolerated

  • Hammer curls (neutral grip)
  • Rows with controlled movement
  • Face pulls
  • Eccentric bicep curls with light weight
  • Rotator cuff exercises with resistance band

Other treatment options

Cortisone injection

Can provide short-term pain relief but is used cautiously. Repeated injections can weaken the tendon and increase the risk of rupture. Not first choice. Read more about cortisone for shoulder pain.

PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma)

Injection of platelets from the patient's own blood. Evidence is still limited, but some studies show promising results for tendon problems.

Surgery

Surgery is only considered when conservative treatment over 3-6 months does not provide satisfactory improvement. Options include:

  • Tenotomy: The tendon is cut and allowed to retract (simpler, faster rehabilitation)
  • Tenodesis: The tendon is reattached to the humerus (preserves strength and contour)

Prognosis after surgery

Both tenotomy and tenodesis give good results in most patients. The choice depends on age, activity level and cosmetic preferences. Tenodesis is often preferred in younger, active patients.

7

Prognosis

The prognosis for biceps tendinopathy is generally good with proper treatment.

The positives:

  • 75% recover with conservative treatment
  • Significant improvement often within 6-12 weeks
  • Eccentric training has good documentation
  • Surgery gives good results for those who need it

The realistic expectations:

  • Full recovery can take 3-6 months
  • Concurrent pathology (rotator cuff, SLAP) can prolong the course
  • Recurrence can occur with premature return to strenuous activity
  • Persistent symptoms may require further investigation

Factors affecting prognosis

  • Duration: Shorter symptom duration gives better prognosis
  • Concurrent pathology: Isolated biceps tendinopathy has better prognosis
  • Compliance: Those who follow the exercise program have better results
  • Age: Younger patients often have faster healing
  • Activity modification: Willingness to adjust training/work
8

What you can do yourself

In acute phase (first 1-2 weeks)

  • Avoid activities that provoke pain
  • Ice on the shoulder 15-20 minutes several times daily
  • Anti-inflammatory medication if needed
  • Maintain gentle mobility

In rehabilitation phase

  • Start with pendulum exercises and gentle mobility
  • Gradual eccentric biceps training
  • Strengthen the rotator cuff
  • Work on scapula stability
  • Gradual return to normal activity

Exercises

Here are some exercises that are often recommended (start carefully and increase load gradually):

  • Eccentric bicep curl: Use light weight, focus on controlled lowering
  • External rotation with band: Elbow at side, rotate forearm outward
  • Scapula retraction: Squeeze shoulder blades together, hold 5 sec
  • Wall climbing: Gentle mobility exercise
  • Sleeper stretch: To maintain internal rotation

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between biceps tendinopathy and SLAP lesion?
Biceps tendinopathy affects the tendon itself, while a SLAP lesion is an injury to the attachment where the tendon connects to the labrum in the shoulder. They can cause similar symptoms and occur simultaneously, but require different treatment.
Can I continue to exercise with biceps tendinopathy?
Yes, but with modifications. Avoid exercises that provoke pain, especially heavy overhead movements, deep dips and heavy bicep curls. Graded loading and eccentric training can actually promote healing.
Do I need an MRI or ultrasound?
Not always. The diagnosis is often made clinically. Imaging is useful when there is suspicion of concurrent rotator cuff injury, SLAP lesion, or when conservative treatment does not provide improvement.
How long does it take for biceps tendinopathy to heal?
With proper treatment, many experience significant improvement within 6-12 weeks. Full recovery can take 3-6 months, depending on severity and compliance with the treatment program.
Does cortisone injection help?
Cortisone injection can provide short-term pain relief, but recent research shows limited long-term effect and possible risk of tendon weakening. Injections are therefore used cautiously and rarely as first choice.
Can the biceps tendon rupture?
Yes, but it is relatively rare. Complete ruptures often cause an audible "pop," sudden pain and a visible lump on the upper arm ("Popeye arm"). This requires medical evaluation but is rarely acutely dangerous.
