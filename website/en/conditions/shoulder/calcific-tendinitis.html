Calcific Tendinitis: Everything You Need to Know

Causes, the three phases, treatment and prognosis

Quick Summary

  • Prevalence: 2.7-7.8% of population
  • Women affected 2x more often, typically 40-60 years
  • 3 phases: Formation, resting, resorption
  • 80-99% recover without surgery
  • ESWT has Level I evidence

You wake up in the middle of the night with intense, throbbing pain in your shoulder. The pain is so severe that you can barely lift your arm. Perhaps you had mild symptoms for weeks or months before this, but suddenly it has become dramatically worse. If you experience general shoulder pain, there may be several possible causes.

This is a classic presentation of calcific tendinitis in the resorptive phase - when the body actively breaks down the calcium deposit. Paradoxically, this intense pain is often a sign that the body is in the process of resolving the problem itself.

Calcific tendinitis is a self-limiting condition where calcium deposits form in the tendons around the shoulder, most commonly the supraspinatus tendon. Although the symptoms can be very intense, 80-99% of patients recover without surgery.

1

What is Calcific Tendinitis?

Calcific tendinitis is a condition where calcium crystals deposit in the rotator cuff tendons, most commonly in the supraspinatus tendon. The deposits vary in size from small dots to lumps several centimeters large.

The condition goes through several phases and is self-limiting - meaning the calcium will eventually be reabsorbed by the body. The problem is that this can take anywhere from a few months to several years, and in the meantime, the pain can be significant.

How common is calcific tendinitis?

Calcific tendinitis is one of the most common causes of shoulder pain:

2.7-7.8% prevalence in population
2x women affected more often
40-60 years typical age group
10-20% have it in both shoulders

Who gets calcific tendinitis?

  • Women are affected twice as often as men
  • Most common between 40 and 60 years
  • Often the non-dominant shoulder (left for right-handed people)
  • Increased prevalence with metabolic disorders (diabetes, thyroid)
  • Sedentary work may be a risk factor
2

The Three Phases

Calcific tendinitis follows a characteristic course in three phases. Understanding which phase you are in helps both predict the course and choose the right treatment.

Phase 1: Pre-calcification and Formation (Formative phase)

In this phase, normal tendon tissue transforms into fibrocartilage, and calcium begins to deposit. The process can take weeks to months.

  • Symptoms: Mild to moderate pain, often vague and diffuse
  • X-ray: The calcium looks "fluffy" or unclear
  • Duration: Varies from weeks to months

Phase 2: Resting Phase (Resting/Chronic phase)

The calcium is fully formed and lies relatively stable in the tendon. This is often the longest phase and can last for years.

  • Symptoms: Variable - can be almost symptom-free or cause persistent problems
  • X-ray: The calcium looks dense and well-defined
  • Duration: Months to years

Phase 3: Resorption (Resorptive phase)

The body begins actively breaking down the calcium. Paradoxically, this is the most painful phase, but signals that the condition is resolving.

  • Symptoms: Intense, acute pain - often at night. Dramatically reduced mobility
  • X-ray: The calcium looks "fluffy" with unclear borders
  • Duration: Days to weeks

Important to understand

Many patients become worried when the pain suddenly becomes much worse. But this worsening is often a good sign - it means the body is actively breaking down the calcium. The resorptive phase is usually short-lived (days to weeks), and afterwards the pain decreases significantly.

3

Symptoms

Symptoms of calcific tendinitis vary significantly depending on which phase the condition is in:

Typical symptoms

  • Pain: Localized to the outside of the shoulder/upper arm. May radiate down to the elbow.
  • Night pain: Common, especially when lying on the affected side. Read more about sleep and shoulder pain
  • Restricted movement: Difficulty lifting the arm to the side and overhead
  • Sudden worsening: Acute, intense pain during transition to the resorptive phase

Acute presentation (resorptive phase)

Sudden onset of intense pain, often at night. The shoulder may be very tender to touch, and the patient often holds the arm completely still to avoid pain. The condition can resemble septic arthritis or gout.

Chronic presentation (resting/formation phase)

Gradually increasing pain over time. The pain worsens with activity, especially movements above shoulder height. May resemble impingement syndrome.

Clinical tests

On examination, we often find:

  • Painful arc during abduction (60-120 degrees)
  • Positive Neer's and/or Hawkins test
  • Point tenderness over the supraspinatus attachment
  • In acute phase: Heat, swelling, marked tenderness
4

Diagnosis

Calcific tendinitis is usually easily diagnosed with X-ray, which clearly shows the calcium deposits.

X-ray

X-ray is the first choice and shows calcium deposits well. The appearance of the calcium can provide information about which phase the condition is in:

  • Formative phase: Unclear, "fluffy" calcium
  • Resting phase: Dense, well-defined calcium with sharp borders
  • Resorptive phase: The calcium becomes "fluffy" with unclear borders again

Ultrasound

Ultrasound can also detect calcium deposits and assess the condition of the tendon. The advantage is that you can assess the entire rotator cuff without radiation.

MRI

MRI is rarely necessary, but may be useful for:

  • Uncertain diagnosis
  • Suspected concurrent rotator cuff tear
  • Preparation for possible surgery

Incidental findings

Calcium deposits are found incidentally in many people without symptoms. Studies show that up to 20% of asymptomatic individuals have calcium in the shoulder on X-ray. Not all calcium causes problems.

5

Red Flags and Differential Diagnoses

Although calcific tendinitis is usually a benign condition, it is important to rule out other causes of shoulder pain.

Seek medical attention if you experience:

  • Fever combined with intense shoulder pain and swelling (may be infection)
  • Significant unexplained weight loss
  • Previous cancer history with new pain
  • Trauma with sudden loss of strength in the arm
  • General feeling of illness

Acute calcific tendinitis in the resorptive phase can mimic septic arthritis. When in doubt, seek medical attention to rule out infection.

Conditions that may resemble calcific tendinitis

Rotator cuff tendinopathy/tear

Similar symptom picture, but X-ray will not show calcium. Ultrasound or MRI can distinguish between the conditions.

Impingement syndrome

Overlapping symptoms with painful arc. Calcific tendinitis can cause secondary impingement.

Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis)

Also causes significant restriction of movement, but with a different pattern (stiffness in all directions).

AC joint arthritis

Pain localized more to the top of the shoulder. X-ray shows changes in the AC joint, not calcium in the tendon.

6

Treatment

Treatment of calcific tendinitis depends on symptom intensity and phase. Because the condition is self-limiting, the goal is to relieve symptoms while the body takes care of the calcium itself.

Conservative treatment (first choice)

What can the chiropractor/physiotherapist do?

  • Manual therapy: Mobilization of shoulder and thoracic spine
  • Soft tissue treatment: Work on tight muscles around the shoulder
  • Exercise guidance: Tailored exercises to maintain mobility and strength
  • Ergonomic advice: Adaptation of work tasks during acute phase. See ergonomics and shoulder problems

Shockwave Therapy (ESWT)

ESWT (Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy) has Level I evidence for treating calcific tendinitis. Shock waves are sent toward the calcium deposit to stimulate resorption and relieve pain.

The KALK Study (BMJ 2023)

A Norwegian study (the KALK study) compared ESWT with placebo. Interestingly, the study showed no significant difference between groups at 4 and 12 months. This may be because patients with shorter symptom duration were included - where the condition might have resolved anyway.

Other studies with longer symptom duration have shown better effect of ESWT. The treatment may be most useful for patients with long-term symptoms that show no signs of spontaneous improvement.

Other treatment options

Ultrasound-guided barbotage (needling)

A needle is inserted into the calcium deposit under ultrasound guidance. Calcium slurry is aspirated and flushed out. Can be effective, especially in the resorptive phase when the calcium is soft and liquid.

Cortisone injection

Can provide good short-term pain relief, especially in the acute phase. Does not affect the calcium deposit itself, but reduces the inflammation around it.

Medications

NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen, diclofenac) can help with pain and inflammation in the acute phase. For severe pain, short-term use of stronger painkillers may be necessary.

Surgery

Surgical removal of the calcium is rarely necessary and is only considered when:

  • Conservative treatment over 6-12 months provides no improvement
  • Symptoms are very disabling
  • No signs of spontaneous resorption

Conservative treatment success rate

Studies show that 80-99% of patients recover with conservative treatment. The calcium will in most cases be reabsorbed naturally, even if it takes time. Patience is key.

7

Prognosis

The prognosis for calcific tendinitis is generally good. The condition is self-limiting, meaning the calcium will eventually disappear on its own.

The positives:

  • 80-99% recover without surgery
  • The calcium is naturally reabsorbed in most cases
  • Intense pain in the resorptive phase is short-lived (days to weeks)
  • Recurrence is rare once the calcium is gone

The realistic:

  • The course can take months to years
  • Symptoms can vary significantly in intensity
  • Some experience long-term problems despite treatment
  • About 10-20% have bilateral involvement

Factors affecting prognosis

  • Phase: Patients in the resorptive phase often recover faster
  • Size: Large calcium deposits can take longer
  • Location: Calcium near the tendon attachment can be more problematic
  • Activity level: Adapted activity promotes recovery
8

What You Can Do Yourself

In acute phase (resorption)

  • Ice or cold can relieve pain and swelling
  • Avoid movements that provoke pain
  • Use pain relievers/anti-inflammatories as needed
  • Sleep with a pillow under your arm for support
  • Be patient - this intense phase is short-lived

In chronic phase

  • Keep the shoulder moving within pain-free limits
  • Gradual strength training of the rotator cuff
  • Avoid prolonged static positions
  • Ergonomic adaptation of workstation

Exercises

Gentle mobility and strength exercises can help maintain function and prevent stiffness. Important to adapt intensity to symptoms:

  • Pendulum exercises: Let the arm hang and swing gently in circles
  • Wall climbing: Use your fingers to "climb" up a wall in front of you
  • External rotation with elastic: When pain level allows
  • Scapula exercises: Shoulder blade squeeze and retraction

Frequently Asked Questions

Can calcific tendinitis go away on its own?

Yes. Calcific tendinitis is a self-limiting condition. The calcium will eventually be reabsorbed naturally, but this can take anywhere from a few months to several years. Treatment can speed up the process and relieve symptoms in the meantime.

Does shockwave therapy (ESWT) help with calcific tendinitis?

ESWT has Level I evidence for calcific tendinitis. The KALK study (BMJ 2023) showed no difference from placebo for patients with shorter symptom duration. The treatment appears to be most useful for patients with long-term symptoms.

Do I need surgery for calcific tendinitis?

Rarely. 80-99% of patients recover without surgery. Surgery is only considered for long-term symptoms (over 6-12 months) after failed conservative treatment.

Why does the pain suddenly go from bad to very bad?

This happens in the resorptive phase when the body breaks down the calcium. The immune system activates, inflammation occurs, and this causes intense pain. Paradoxically, this is often a sign that the body is resolving the problem.

Can calcific tendinitis come back?

The recurrence rate is low once the calcium is fully reabsorbed. However, in rare cases, calcific tendinitis can occur in the other shoulder.

Should I avoid all activity?

No. In the acute phase, you should avoid movements that provoke pain, but complete rest is not recommended. Adapted activity within pain-free limits helps maintain mobility.
