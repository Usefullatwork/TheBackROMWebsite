Why Does My Shoulder Click? Causes and When to Worry

Click, pop, snap - what do the sounds mean and when is it harmless?

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Click. Pop. Snap. Many people experience their shoulder making sounds - and it can be quite unsettling the first time it happens. Is something about to break? Should you worry? If you experience shoulder pain in general, it may be worth reading more about the various causes.

The short version: Most of the time, clicking in the shoulder is completely harmless. The sounds can come from several normal processes and rarely mean something is wrong - as long as they are not accompanied by pain or other symptoms.

In this article, you'll learn what actually causes the sounds, when you can safely ignore them, and when it might be wise to get it checked.

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Usually completely harmless
  • Gas bubbles in joint are common cause
  • Tendons sliding over bone
  • Only worry with pain
  • Strength training can help

1

Different Types of Clicking Sounds

Not all clicking is the same. The sound and sensation can give hints about what's causing it:

"Pop" or "click"

A single, audible sound - like when you crack your knuckles

This is often gas bubbles being released in the joint (cavitation). Completely harmless and natural. You typically won't be able to reproduce the sound immediately afterwards.

"Crunching" or "crackling"

A more creaking, repetitive sound during movement

Can be caused by tendons sliding over bony structures, bursa irritation, or in some cases early arthritic changes. Often harmless, but worth observing.

"Clunk" or "snap"

A feeling of something "jumping" or shifting

Often a tendon (e.g., the biceps tendon) snapping over a bony prominence. Can occur with certain movements and is usually harmless.

"Grinding" or "grating"

A rough, scraping feeling/sound during movement

May indicate cartilage changes or arthritis. More relevant to investigate, especially if accompanied by stiffness or pain.

2

Harmless Causes of Clicking

Most clicking sounds in the shoulder are completely harmless. Here are the most common causes:

Cavitation (gas bubbles)

Harmless

The same process as when you crack your knuckles. Gas bubbles in the synovial fluid are released and make a "pop" sound. This doesn't damage the joint and requires no treatment.

Tendons sliding over bone

Harmless

The shoulder has many tendons that pass over bony prominences. During certain movements, these can "snap" or slide over the bone and make a sound. Common and normal.

Ligament tension

Harmless

Ligaments can suddenly tighten during movement and make a snapping sound. Especially common in people with increased joint mobility (hypermobility).

Muscle tightness

Harmless

Tight muscles around the shoulder can affect how tendons and other structures move, which can lead to temporary clicking. Often improves with stretching and activity.

The Main Message

Clicking without pain is almost always harmless. The body makes sounds - that's normal. As long as you have good function and no pain, there's rarely a reason to worry.

3

Causes That May Require Attention

In some cases, clicking can be a sign of a condition that should be investigated, especially if accompanied by other symptoms:

Labral tear

Consider examination

The labrum is a cartilage ring that surrounds the shoulder joint. Injuries here can cause a "click" or "catching" sensation, often with pain. More common after trauma or in throwing athletes.

  • Clicking/catching with certain movements
  • Feeling of instability
  • Pain with overhead activity

Biceps tendon subluxation

Consider examination

The long biceps tendon can slip out of its groove (sulcus) and make an audible/palpable "clunk" during rotational movements. Can be painful and give a sensation of something shifting.

Rotator cuff problems

Consider examination

Tendinopathy or partial tears in the rotator cuff can cause clicking, especially with arm lifting. Often accompanied by pain and reduced strength.

Arthritis (wear and tear)

Consider examination

Cartilage wear can cause crunching sounds (crepitus) during movement. More common with age and often accompanied by stiffness, especially in the morning. See also AC joint arthritis.

Snapping scapula syndrome

Consider examination

Clicking/crunching between the shoulder blade and rib cage. Often caused by muscular imbalance or bursitis in the area. Can cause discomfort with arm movements. Read about pain behind the shoulder.

4

When Should You Seek Help?

As a general rule: clicking without other symptoms is harmless. But certain signs indicate you should have your shoulder examined:

Seek help if clicking is accompanied by:

  • Pain - especially if the pain occurs at the same time as the clicking
  • Weakness - reduced strength in the arm or shoulder
  • Locking - the shoulder "gets stuck" in certain positions
  • Swelling or warmth - signs of inflammation
  • Instability - feeling that the shoulder might "come out of joint"
  • After injury - clicking that started after a fall or trauma

Painless clicking that doesn't affect your function usually requires no treatment.

What happens during an examination?

A chiropractor or other practitioner will:

  • Ask about when the clicking started and what provokes it
  • Examine shoulder mobility and stability
  • Test strength in relevant muscles
  • Provoke the clicking to identify the source
  • If needed, refer for imaging (ultrasound, MRI)
5

What Can You Do Yourself?

If the clicking is painless, there are often things you can do to reduce it - or at least ensure the shoulder stays healthy:

Strength training

Strengthening the shoulder muscles, especially the rotator cuff and scapula stabilizers, can improve muscle balance and control. This can reduce clicking caused by muscular imbalance.

  • External rotation with elastic band
  • Shoulder blade exercises (rows, face pulls)
  • Side raises with light resistance
  • Plank variations for scapula stability

Stretching and mobility

Tight muscles can contribute to clicking. Focus on:

  • Chest muscles (pectoralis)
  • Posterior shoulder capsule
  • Neck and upper back

Warm up properly

Many people experience more clicking when the shoulder is "cold". A thorough warm-up before training or physical activity can reduce the sounds and protect the structures.

What you should NOT do

  • Compulsive clicking: Don't try to make the shoulder click on purpose
  • Avoid all activity: Inactivity often makes things worse
  • Overthink it: Stress and anxiety can actually amplify the perception of symptoms

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it dangerous when my shoulder clicks?
Usually no. Most clicking sounds in the shoulder are harmless and are caused by normal processes such as gas bubbles in the joint or tendons sliding over bone. You should only worry if the clicking is accompanied by pain, swelling, weakness or locking.
Why does my shoulder click when I lift my arm?
When lifting the arm, tendons and muscles pass over bony prominences. Clicking here can be caused by tendons "snapping" over these points, bursa irritation, or in some cases rotator cuff problems. If it doesn't hurt, it's usually harmless.
Can I exercise even though my shoulder clicks?
Yes, if the clicking is painless. Strength training of shoulder muscles can actually reduce clicking over time by improving muscle balance and stability. Avoid exercises that consistently cause pain along with the clicking.
When should I seek help for clicking in the shoulder?
Seek help if the clicking is accompanied by pain, you experience reduced strength or mobility, the shoulder "locks up", there is swelling or warmth, or the clicking started after an injury.
Can shoulder clicking go away on its own?
Yes, it often does. Clicking caused by temporary tightness, muscle imbalance or postural changes can disappear with increased activity, stretching or strength training. Some forms of clicking will persist but remain harmless.
Do I need X-ray or MRI for clicking in the shoulder?
Usually not if the clicking is painless and doesn't affect function. Imaging is relevant only when there are accompanying symptoms such as pain, weakness or instability, or if there has been trauma.
