Cortisone Injection in Shoulder: Friend or Foe?

Benefits, risks, and when it's the right choice - Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Quick Summary

  • Effect typically lasts 6-8 weeks
  • Can weaken tendons with repeated use
  • Best for acute inflammation
  • Not first choice for tendinopathy
  • Exercises often better long-term
Book appointment

"You just need a cortisone injection, then it'll be fine." Many people with shoulder pain have heard this. Cortisone injections are one of the most common treatments for shoulder problems, and for some they can be a valuable part of treatment.

But research in recent years has significantly nuanced the picture. We now know more about both the benefits and drawbacks - and when cortisone is actually the right choice versus when other approaches may be better.

In this article, you'll get a balanced overview of what cortisone does, when it can help, what risks exist, and what alternatives you should consider.

1

What is Cortisone and How Does it Work?

Cortisone (corticosteroids) are synthetic versions of cortisol - a hormone the body produces itself. When injected locally, it has a powerful anti-inflammatory effect.

How cortisone works

  • Reduces inflammation: Inhibits inflammatory processes locally in the tissue
  • Reduces pain: Less inflammation = less pain
  • Reduces swelling: Reduces fluid accumulation in the area

Common cortisone preparations

There are different types of cortisone with varying strength and duration:

  • Triamcinolone (Kenacort): Medium strength, commonly used in shoulder
  • Methylprednisolone (Depo-Medrol): Medium strength
  • Betamethasone (Celestone): Stronger, faster effect

Cortisone is often combined with local anesthetic for immediate pain relief.

Where is the injection given?

For shoulder problems, cortisone can be injected in several places depending on the diagnosis:

Subacromial (under the acromion)

The most common injection site for shoulder pain. Reaches the bursa or rotator cuff. Used for impingement, bursitis and rotator cuff tendinopathy.

In the shoulder joint (glenohumeral)

Injection directly into the shoulder joint itself. Used for frozen shoulder, arthritis and rheumatoid arthritis.

In the AC joint

The small joint between the collarbone and shoulder blade. Used for AC joint arthritis.

2

The Benefits of Cortisone

Cortisone has its place in treating shoulder problems. Here are situations where it can be useful:

When cortisone can be a good choice

  • Acute, intense inflammation: When pain is so severe you can't do exercises
  • To enable rehabilitation: Pain relief so you can start training
  • Diagnostic tool: If the injection provides relief, it confirms the problem is local
  • Acute bursitis: Bursa inflammation often responds well
  • Frozen shoulder: Can help in the painful first phase
70-80% experience initial improvement
24-48h time to effect
6-8 weeks typical duration

Cortisone as a "window opener"

Perhaps the best use of cortisone is as a "window" for rehabilitation. The injection provides temporary pain relief so you can perform exercises and physical therapy that address the underlying cause. Cortisone alone without follow-up rehabilitation rarely provides lasting improvement.

3

The Drawbacks and Risks

Research in recent years has shown that cortisone isn't as innocent as previously thought. Here's what you should know:

Documented risks with repeated use

  • Tendon weakening: Cortisone inhibits collagen synthesis and can weaken tendon tissue over time
  • Increased risk of rupture: Studies show increased risk of tendon rupture after multiple injections
  • Cartilage damage: Cortisone can accelerate cartilage breakdown in joints
  • Fat tissue atrophy: Can cause local loss of subcutaneous fat
  • Pigment changes: Lighter skin at the injection site

Short-lived effect

One of the biggest limitations is that the effect is often temporary:

What research shows

  • At 6-8 weeks, the effect begins to wear off in most people
  • At 3 months, there's often no difference from placebo
  • At 6-12 months, those who received cortisone may actually have worse outcomes than the control group
  • The condition often returns if the underlying cause isn't addressed

"Cortisone flare"

About 2-5% experience temporary worsening of pain in the first 24-48 hours after injection. This is called a "cortisone flare" and is caused by a crystal reaction. It resolves on its own but can be uncomfortable.

The problem with tendinopathy

For chronic tendon irritation (tendinopathy), cortisone is particularly problematic:

  • Tendinopathy is not primarily an inflammatory condition - it's degenerative
  • Cortisone can interfere with the natural repair process in the tendon
  • Studies show worse long-term outcomes compared to exercises
  • Pain relief can mask the problem and lead to overloading
4

When Should You Avoid Cortisone?

Based on current knowledge, there are some situations where cortisone is generally not recommended:

Chronic tendinopathy

Avoid

For long-term tendon problems without acute inflammation, exercises and graded loading are better. Cortisone can actually worsen the prognosis long-term.

Partial tendon tears

Avoid

Cortisone can further weaken already damaged tendon tissue and increase the risk of complete rupture.

Repeated injections

Caution

More than 3-4 injections in the same area per year is discouraged. Repeated injections significantly increase the risk of tissue damage.

When exercises haven't been tried

Caution

Cortisone should rarely be the first choice. Structured rehabilitation should be attempted first in most cases.

Absolute contraindications

  • Active infection in or near the injection area
  • Known allergy to cortisone or local anesthetic
  • Uncontrolled diabetes (cortisone raises blood sugar)
  • Blood thinning medication (increased bleeding risk)
5

Alternatives to Cortisone

There are several effective alternatives that often provide better long-term results:

Structured rehabilitation (first choice)

For most shoulder conditions, exercise-based treatment is most effective long-term:

  • Eccentric exercises for tendinopathy
  • Graded loading that stimulates tissue adaptation
  • Strength training for rotator cuff and scapula stabilizers
  • Takes longer but provides more lasting results

Manual therapy / Chiropractic

Recommended

Combination of joint mobilization, soft tissue treatment and exercise guidance. Can provide good pain relief without the risks of cortisone.

Shockwave therapy (ESWT)

Recommended for calcific tendinitis

Level I evidence for calcific tendinitis. Stimulates biological healing without weakening tissue. Can also be used for chronic tendinopathy.

PRP injection

Variable evidence

Platelet-rich plasma from the patient's own blood. Theoretically stimulates healing. Evidence is mixed, but some studies show promising results for certain conditions.

NSAIDs as an alternative

For acute inflammation, anti-inflammatory tablets (ibuprofen, diclofenac) can provide similar effect to cortisone for some, without the risk of local tissue damage. Should be used short-term and in consultation with a doctor.

6

If You Choose Cortisone

If cortisone is the right choice for you, there are some things that increase the chance of a good result:

Before the injection

  • Make sure you have a clear diagnosis - cortisone works differently on different conditions
  • Discuss expectations with the doctor - cortisone is rarely a "quick fix"
  • Have a plan for follow-up rehabilitation
  • Inform about previous injections and any reactions

After the injection

  • First 24-48 hours: Take it easy, avoid heavy loading
  • After 2-3 days: Start cautiously with movement
  • After 1-2 weeks: Begin structured rehabilitation when pain allows
  • Important: Don't overload the shoulder even if the pain is gone - the tissue needs time

Common mistakes after cortisone injection

  • Thinking the problem is "solved" because the pain is gone
  • Returning to full activity too quickly
  • Not following up with exercises and rehabilitation
  • Getting repeated injections without trying other measures

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a cortisone injection in the shoulder last?
The effect varies significantly. Some experience relief for weeks to months, while others have short-lived or no effect. Research shows the effect often wears off after 6-8 weeks, and at 3-6 months there's often no difference from placebo.
Are cortisone injections dangerous?
Single injections are generally safe, but repeated injections can weaken tendons and cartilage. Studies show increased risk of tendon rupture and worsening of cartilage damage with many injections. The recommended maximum is usually 3-4 injections per area per year.
When is a cortisone injection in the shoulder the right choice?
Cortisone can be useful for acute, intense inflammation that prevents rehabilitation, as a diagnostic tool, or as a short-term measure when other treatments haven't helped. It should not be the first choice for chronic tendon conditions.
Can cortisone make shoulder problems worse?
Yes, in some cases. Cortisone can weaken tendon tissue over time, and some experience a "cortisone flare" with temporary increased pain in the first few days after injection. In tendinopathy, cortisone can interfere with the natural healing process.
What are the alternatives to cortisone injection?
Alternatives include physiotherapy/chiropractic with exercises, manual therapy, shockwave therapy (ESWT), and for some conditions PRP injections. For many shoulder conditions, active rehabilitation is more effective in the long term. Read more about ergonomics and prevention.
How many cortisone injections can you get?
Generally, a maximum of 3-4 injections in the same area per year is recommended, with at least 6 weeks between each. The more injections, the greater the risk of tissue damage. If 2-3 injections haven't provided lasting effect, other approaches should be considered.
