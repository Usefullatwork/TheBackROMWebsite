Pain Behind the Shoulder

Infraspinatus, internal impingement and treatment

You feel an aching pain behind your shoulder, perhaps down toward the shoulder blade. The pain worsens when you throw, serve in tennis, or hold your arm in throwing position. Perhaps you have also noticed that your shoulder has become stiffer, especially when rotating your arm inward.

Posterior shoulder pain - pain behind the shoulder - is common among throwing athletes and overhead sports athletes. The most common causes are infraspinatus tendinopathy, internal impingement, and GIRD (glenohumeral internal rotation deficit). This type of shoulder pain often requires a thorough evaluation for proper treatment.

The good news is that conservative treatment is effective for most people, with sleeper stretch being a documented effective exercise.

Quick Summary

  • Infraspinatus: 2nd most common rotator cuff injury
  • Common in throwing athletes
  • Sleeper stretch is effective
  • GIRD: 72% of handball players
  • Conservative treatment first choice
1

Common causes of posterior shoulder pain

Infraspinatus tendinopathy

Most common

Infraspinatus is one of four rotator cuff muscles and the second most common to injure. The muscle lies on the back of the shoulder blade and is responsible for external rotation of the shoulder.

  • Pain behind the shoulder, may radiate toward the elbow
  • Worsens with external rotation against resistance
  • Often related to swimming, throwing and overhead activity

Internal impingement

Occurs when the rotator cuff (especially infraspinatus and supraspinatus) gets pinched against the posterior labrum when the arm is in "throwing position" (abducted and maximally externally rotated).

  • Pain behind the shoulder in the "late cocking" phase of throwing
  • Common in baseball pitchers, handball players, volleyball - similar mechanics as in thrower's shoulder
  • Can cause secondary damage to labrum and rotator cuff

GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit)

Deficit in internal rotation of the shoulder, common in throwing athletes. 72% of handball players have GIRD. Often caused by tightening of the posterior shoulder capsule.

  • Reduced internal rotation compared to the opposite side
  • Increased external rotation (as compensation)
  • Increased risk of shoulder problems and injuries

Other causes

  • Teres minor tendinopathy: Similar picture to infraspinatus
  • Posterior labrum injury: Often together with internal impingement, can develop into SLAP lesion
  • Quadrilateral space syndrome: Rare, but can cause radiation
  • Referred pain: From neck (C5-C6 radiculopathy)
2

Symptoms

Typical symptoms

  • Localized pain: Behind the shoulder, over the shoulder blade
  • Activity-related: Worsens with throwing, serving, overhead movements
  • Stiffness: Reduced internal rotation
  • Radiation: Can radiate toward the elbow or up toward the neck

Activities that typically provoke symptoms

  • Throwing (handball, baseball)
  • Tennis serve and smash
  • Volleyball spikes
  • Swimming (especially crawl)
  • Lying on the affected side - see tips on sleep and shoulder pain
3

Diagnosis

Clinical examination

  • Range of motion assessment: Compare internal rotation on both sides (GIRD)
  • Infraspinatus test: External rotation against resistance
  • Internal impingement test: Pain in abducted/externally rotated position
  • Palpation: Tenderness over infraspinatus/teres minor

GIRD criteria

GIRD is defined as >15-20 degrees loss of internal rotation compared to the opposite side, or loss of total rotation range (internal + external) of >5 degrees.

4

Treatment

Conservative treatment (first choice)

  • Sleeper stretch: Documented effective for GIRD. Improves internal rotation by approximately 10 degrees.
  • Cross-body stretch: Stretches the posterior capsule
  • Rotator cuff strengthening: Especially external rotators and scapula stabilizers
  • Scapula control: Improve shoulder blade movement - good ergonomics is also important
  • Throwing mechanics: Optimize technique to reduce load

What can a chiropractor do?

  • Manual treatment: Mobilization of shoulder and thoracic spine
  • Soft tissue treatment: Work on infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior capsule - see also cortisone injection if needed
  • Exercise guidance: Sleeper stretch, strengthening exercises
  • Activity modification: Adapt training during rehabilitation

Sleeper stretch - proper execution

  • Lie on the affected side with shoulder at 90 degrees abduction
  • Use the opposite hand to press the forearm down toward the floor
  • Hold 30 seconds, 3-5 repetitions
  • Important: Don't let the shoulder lift from the surface
5

Prognosis

The positive:

  • Most respond well to conservative treatment
  • Sleeper stretch provides documented improvement
  • Many can return to throwing/overhead activity
  • 90% of thrower's shoulder responds to conservative treatment

The realistic:

  • Rehabilitation takes time (weeks to months)
  • Some may need to permanently modify activity
  • Maintenance exercises are important to prevent relapse
6

FAQ

What is infraspinatus tendinopathy?
Infraspinatus is a rotator cuff muscle on the back of the shoulder blade and the second most common to injure. Tendinopathy causes pain behind the shoulder, often related to overhead activity.
What is internal impingement?
Internal impingement occurs when the rotator cuff gets pinched against the posterior labrum in throwing position. Common in throwing athletes.
Does sleeper stretch help?
Yes. Sleeper stretch is documented effective for improving internal rotation and reducing GIRD. Studies show improvement of around 10 degrees.
Can I continue to throw?
With proper treatment, many can return to throwing. Focus on mobility, strength and optimized technique. Gradual return to full activity.
