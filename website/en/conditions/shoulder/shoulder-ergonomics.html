Shoulder-Friendly Workspace: How to Set Up Your Workstation

Prevent shoulder pain with proper ergonomics and break routines

After a long day in front of the computer, you feel it in your shoulders. Stiffness, tension, perhaps an aching feeling that extends up into the neck. You are far from alone - office work is one of the most common causes of shoulder pain and neck problems.

The problem is rarely a single thing you do wrong, but the sum of many small factors over time: the screen too far to the side, the mouse too far away, the chair too low, too few breaks.

In this article, you will get concrete tips for setting up a shoulder-friendly workstation - and simple exercises you can do at your desk.

Quick Summary

  • Screen at eye level, directly in front
  • Mouse and keyboard close to body
  • Shoulders relaxed, not raised
  • Break every 30 minutes
  • Variation is most important!
1

Why office work causes shoulder pain

The body was not designed to sit still in the same position for hours. When we do, several things happen that strain the shoulders:

Static muscle work

When you hold your arms in front of you to use the keyboard and mouse, the shoulder muscles constantly work to stabilize. Without rest, this leads to fatigue, tension and eventually pain.

Forward-leaning posture

Many lean forward toward the screen. This causes the head to come in front of the shoulders, which significantly increases the load on neck and shoulder muscles.

Raised shoulders

Stress, concentration or incorrect desk height causes us to unconsciously raise our shoulders. Over time, this leads to chronic tension in the trapezius muscle. This can also affect sleep quality.

One-sided strain

Using the mouse with the same arm all day, the keyboard too far to one side - such asymmetries strain one shoulder more than the other. Over time, this can contribute to conditions like biceps tendinopathy or AC joint arthritis.

2

Screen Setup

Screen placement has great significance for neck and shoulder position:

Correct screen height

  • Top of the screen should be at eye level or slightly below
  • You should look straight ahead or slightly down (approx. 10-20 degrees)
  • Use a screen stand, books or adjustable arm to achieve the correct height
  • For laptop: external screen or laptop stand is almost necessary

Correct distance

  • The screen should be about an arm's length away (50-70 cm)
  • You should be able to read text without leaning forward
  • Adjust font size rather than moving the screen closer

Avoid this

  • Screen too low (laptop on lap/table) - forces head down
  • Screen to the side - causes neck rotation and asymmetric shoulder load
  • Screen too far away - makes you lean forward
  • Two screens where you primarily use one - place main screen directly in front

The laptop problem

Laptops are ergonomically challenging because the screen and keyboard are connected. When the screen is at the right height, the keyboard is too high - and vice versa. The solution is an external keyboard/mouse combined with a laptop stand, or an external monitor.

3

Keyboard and Mouse

Keyboard and mouse placement is often underestimated, but has great significance for shoulder strain:

Keyboard placement

  • The keyboard should be directly in front of you, not angled
  • Place it so that upper arms hang straight down along the body
  • Forearms should be approximately parallel to the floor (90 degree angle at the elbow)
  • Wrists should be in neutral position - not bent up or down

Mouse placement

  • The mouse should be right next to the keyboard, not further out
  • You should not have to reach for the mouse
  • Keep the elbow close to the body when using the mouse
  • Consider an ergonomic mouse or vertical mouse with heavy mouse use

Compact keyboard

Recommended

A keyboard without a numeric keypad (tenkeyless) allows you to place the mouse closer to the midline. This reduces how far you have to extend your arm to the side, which significantly relieves the shoulder.

Common mistakes

  • Mouse too far to the side - forces you to abduct (lift out) the arm
  • Keyboard/mouse too high - causes you to raise your shoulders
  • Too much angle in the wrist - can cause tension up through the arm
4

Chair and Sitting Position

A good chair and correct adjustment lays the foundation for a relaxed shoulder position:

Chair height

  • Feet should be flat on the floor (or on a footrest)
  • Knees should be at approximately 90 degree angle or slightly more
  • Thighs should be approximately parallel to the floor

Armrests

  • Armrests should support forearms so shoulders can be relaxed
  • Adjust height so shoulders are neither raised nor pulled down
  • Armrests should not prevent you from getting close enough to the desk
  • Some prefer no armrests - then they avoid sitting too far from the desk

Back support

The backrest should support the lower back (lumbar region). Good lumbar support helps you maintain natural curvature in the back, which in turn makes it easier to maintain a relaxed shoulder position.

The best sitting position

More important than "perfect" posture is variation. There is no position that is ideal for hours. Alternate between leaning forward, leaning back, and upright. Stand up and move regularly. The next position is always the best position. For persistent pain, consider whether there may be an underlying condition like calcific tendinitis or TOS (thoracic outlet syndrome).

5

Variation and Breaks

No matter how perfect the ergonomics are, the body is not made for prolonged stillness. Variation and breaks are more important than the "perfect" setup:

Break structure

  • Micro-breaks (30 sec - 1 min): Every 20-30 minutes. Straighten up, roll shoulders, look away from the screen.
  • Short breaks (5-10 min): Every hour. Stand up, walk a few steps, stretch.
  • Longer breaks (15-30 min): Lunch etc. Get away from the workstation completely.

Sit-stand desk

A sit-stand desk allows you to alternate between sitting and standing work. It's not about standing all day (that's not good either), but about variation.

  • Start with short standing periods (15-30 min)
  • Alternate between sitting and standing several times a day
  • Remember that ergonomics must also be correct in standing position

Tips to remember breaks

  • Use app or timer to remind you of breaks
  • Drink plenty of water - then you also have to get up more often
  • Place the printer, coffee machine etc. so you have to walk
  • Take phone calls standing or walking
6

Exercises at the Desk

These simple exercises can be done sitting at the desk or standing beside it. They only take 30-60 seconds each:

1. Shoulder rolls

Roll your shoulders backward in large circles. Start by raising your shoulders toward your ears, then pull them back, down and forward again. Repeat 10 times.

30 seconds

2. Neck stretch

Gently tilt your head to one side (ear toward shoulder). Hold for 15-20 seconds. Repeat on the other side. Don't rotate your head, just side bending.

40 seconds

3. Chest opening

Lean back in the chair, open your arms out to the sides and push your chest forward. Squeeze your shoulder blades together. Hold for 10 seconds, repeat 3 times.

30 seconds

4. Shoulder blade squeeze

Pull your shoulder blades together as if trying to hold a pencil between them. Hold for 5 seconds, release. Repeat 10 times.

50 seconds

5. Chin tuck

Pull your chin straight back (double chin movement) while looking straight ahead. Hold for 5 seconds. This counteracts forward head posture. Repeat 10 times.

50 seconds

6. Arms overhead

Stretch both arms straight up above your head. Breathe in, stretch as high as you can. Breathe out, lower your arms. Repeat 5 times.

30 seconds

Make this a habit

Choose 2-3 of the exercises and do them every time you take a micro-break. Over time it becomes automatic, and your shoulders will thank you.

7

Checklist for Shoulder-Friendly Workspace

Go through this list:

  • Screen top at eye level
  • Screen directly in front of you (not to the side)
  • Screen about an arm's length away
  • Keyboard so upper arms hang down
  • Mouse right next to the keyboard
  • Elbows at approx. 90 degree angle
  • Wrists in neutral position
  • Feet flat on the floor
  • Back support for lower back
  • Shoulders relaxed (not raised)
  • Break rhythm every 20-30 minutes
  • Movement and variation throughout the day
8

Frequently Asked Questions

How high should the screen be to avoid shoulder pain?
The top of the screen should be at eye level or slightly below. This means you look straight ahead or slightly down, which provides a relaxed neck and shoulder position.
Can mouse use cause shoulder pain?
Yes. If the mouse is placed too far out to the side or too high, you have to extend your arm and raise your shoulder. Over time, this can cause tension and pain in the shoulder and neck.
How often should I take breaks from computer work?
Ideally, you should take a micro-break (30-60 seconds) every 20-30 minutes, and a longer break (5-10 minutes) every hour. Movement and variation are key.
Is a standing desk better for the shoulders?
Variation is more important than whether you sit or stand. A sit-stand desk allows you to alternate between positions throughout the day, which is beneficial for both shoulder, neck and back.
What exercises can I do at my desk?
Good exercises include shoulder rolls, neck stretches, chest opening and shoulder blade squeeze. These only take a few seconds and can be done sitting at your desk.
Does ergonomic equipment help?
Ergonomic equipment (mouse, keyboard, chair) can help, but it's not magic. The most important thing is correct setup of what you have, and above all breaks and movement. Expensive equipment doesn't replace good habits.
