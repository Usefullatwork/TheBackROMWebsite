Shoulder Osteoarthritis: Everything You Need to Know

Wear in the shoulder joint - causes, symptoms and treatment

You notice that your shoulder has become stiffer and that it hurts when you move your arm, especially during rotation. Perhaps you have heard creaking or crepitation from the joint. An X-ray has shown "wear" or "osteoarthritis".

But what does this actually mean? Shoulder osteoarthritis (glenohumeral osteoarthritis) is wear in the main shoulder joint. The good news is that although 16-20% of people over 65 have X-ray findings, less than 1% actually need treatment. This differs from AC joint osteoarthritis which affects a different joint in the shoulder.

Wear is a natural part of aging and does not necessarily mean you have a problem that requires treatment.

Quick Summary

  • 16-20% over 65 have X-ray findings
  • Less than 1% need treatment
  • Strength training is the cornerstone
  • AAOS: No to hyaluronic acid
  • Surgery rarely necessary
1

What is shoulder osteoarthritis?

Shoulder osteoarthritis (glenohumeral osteoarthritis) is wear of the cartilage in the main shoulder joint - where the upper arm bone (humerus) meets the socket on the shoulder blade (glenoid).

In osteoarthritis, the cartilage gradually becomes thinner and more uneven. The body often responds by forming bone spurs (osteophytes) and changing the structure of the bone beneath the cartilage.

16-20% over 65 have X-ray findings
<1% need treatment
60+ years typical onset age

Important perspective

Shoulder osteoarthritis is much rarer than osteoarthritis in the hip and knee. The shoulder joint bears less weight, and the surrounding muscles provide good support. Most people with degenerative changes on imaging have no or few symptoms.

2

Causes and risk factors

Types of shoulder osteoarthritis

Primary osteoarthritis

Most common

Age-related wear without a known triggering cause. Develops gradually over time.

Secondary osteoarthritis

Result of another condition or injury:

  • Previous shoulder fracture or dislocation
  • Rotator cuff tear (cuff tear arthropathy)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Avascular necrosis
  • Joint infection

Risk factors

  • Age over 60 years
  • Previous shoulder injury or surgery
  • Long-standing rotator cuff tear
  • Repeated dislocations
  • Heavy physical work over time
  • Rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory conditions
3

Symptoms

Symptoms of shoulder osteoarthritis usually develop gradually:

Typical symptoms

  • Pain: Deep, aching pain in the shoulder. Often worse with activity.
  • Stiffness: Reduced mobility, especially rotation
  • Crepitation: Creaking or grating during movement
  • Night pain: Can wake you up or make it difficult to find a sleeping position. Read more about sleep and shoulder pain
  • Activity-related pain: Worsens with use, improves with rest

Movements that often provoke symptoms

  • Putting on a jacket or sweater
  • Reaching behind the back (bra clasp, wallet)
  • Lifting the arm out to the side
  • Rotational movements

Difference from frozen shoulder

Shoulder osteoarthritis vs. frozen shoulder

  • Shoulder osteoarthritis: Gradual stiffness over years, pain with loading, crepitation
  • Frozen shoulder: Relatively rapid stiffness development (weeks-months), pronounced movement restriction in all directions, often resolves on its own
4

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made based on medical history, clinical examination and imaging.

Clinical examination

  • Reduced active and passive range of motion
  • Crepitation during movement
  • Pain when loading the joint
  • Muscle atrophy may be seen in long-standing cases

Imaging

X-ray

First choice. Shows classic osteoarthritis signs:

  • Narrowed joint space
  • Bone spurs (osteophytes)
  • Subchondral sclerosis
  • Subchondral cysts

MRI

Useful for assessing:

  • The condition of the rotator cuff
  • Cartilage status
  • Ruling out other conditions

Imaging findings do not equal symptoms

Many have significant degenerative changes on imaging without symptoms. The degree of wear on X-ray correlates poorly with pain level. Treatment is based on symptoms, not images.

5

Red flags

Seek medical attention if:

  • Sudden severe pain after trauma
  • Swelling, redness and warmth (may be infection or gout)
  • Fever combined with shoulder pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Rapidly increasing symptoms
  • Previous cancer
6

Treatment

Conservative treatment is first choice and effective for the vast majority.

Conservative treatment

Strength training and mobility

The cornerstone of treatment:

  • Rotator cuff strengthening: Maintains muscle support around the joint
  • Mobility exercises: Prevents stiffness
  • Scapula stabilization: Improves shoulder blade control
  • Gradual progression: Start light, increase load over time

What can a chiropractor do?

  • Manual treatment: Mobilization of shoulder, neck and thoracic spine
  • Soft tissue treatment: Work on tight muscles
  • Exercise guidance: Customized exercise program
  • Ergonomic advice: Adaptation of daily activities. See ergonomics for the shoulder

Injection treatment

Cortisone injection

Can provide short-term pain relief (weeks to months). Used during flare-ups or to enable starting exercise. Read more about cortisone in the shoulder.

Hyaluronic acid - NOT recommended

The American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS) recommends against the use of hyaluronic acid injections in the shoulder due to lack of evidence of effect.

Surgery

Only considered when conservative treatment over a long time (years) does not provide satisfactory function:

Shoulder replacement (arthroplasty)

May be relevant in severe osteoarthritis with significant functional impairment. Anatomical prosthesis or reverse prosthesis depending on the condition of the rotator cuff.

7

Prognosis

The positive:

  • Most get good symptom relief with conservative treatment
  • Less than 1% need surgical treatment
  • Shoulder replacement gives good results in the few who need it
  • Many live well with shoulder wear

The realistic:

  • The wear itself does not disappear
  • Symptoms can vary over time
  • Some activities may need to be permanently modified
  • Ongoing exercise is important to maintain function
8

What you can do yourself

Daily tips

  • Keep your shoulder moving - avoid prolonged immobilization
  • Heat before activity can relieve stiffness
  • Ice after activity for swelling or pain
  • Modify activities to avoid significant pain provocation

Exercises

  • Pendulum exercises: Let the arm hang and swing gently
  • Wall climbing: Gentle mobility up a wall
  • External rotation with band: Strengthens the rotator cuff
  • Rows: Strengthens posterior shoulder and back
9

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a shoulder replacement for shoulder osteoarthritis?
Rarely. Less than 1% of those with shoulder osteoarthritis need surgical treatment. Most get good symptom relief with conservative treatment.
Do hyaluronic acid injections help?
AAOS does not recommend hyaluronic acid injections in the shoulder due to lack of evidence. Cortisone injection can provide short-term pain relief.
Can I exercise with shoulder osteoarthritis?
Yes! Strength training and mobility exercises are cornerstones in treatment. Start carefully and increase gradually.
What is the difference between shoulder osteoarthritis and frozen shoulder?
Shoulder osteoarthritis is wear in the joint itself. Frozen shoulder is stiffness in the joint capsule. Frozen shoulder often causes more pronounced movement restriction and often resolves on its own.
Will the wear get worse?
Degenerative changes develop gradually, but symptoms don't always follow the degree of wear. Focus on function and symptoms, not images.
