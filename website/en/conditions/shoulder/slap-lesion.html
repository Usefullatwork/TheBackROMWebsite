SLAP Lesion: Everything You Need to Know

What the research actually shows about surgery and conservative treatment

Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Quick Summary

  • Norwegian study: Surgery = sham (placebo)
  • 78% return to sports conservatively
  • Only 6% of shoulder arthroscopies
  • Often overdiagnosed on MRI
  • Rehabilitation is key

You have been told that you have a SLAP lesion on your MRI. Perhaps the doctor has suggested surgery. But what does this finding actually mean, and is surgery really necessary? This condition is one of several causes of shoulder pain.

SLAP stands for "Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior" - an injury to the labrum (joint rim) at the top of the shoulder joint, where the long biceps tendon attaches. The condition is closely related to biceps tendinopathy. The diagnosis has received much attention in recent years, but research has also provided important corrections.

The Norwegian SLAP study (2017) showed that surgery was no better than sham surgery. This has changed how we think about treatment of SLAP lesions.

1

What is a SLAP lesion?

A SLAP lesion is an injury to the upper part of the labrum (joint rim) in the shoulder joint, where the long biceps tendon attaches. "SLAP" stands for "Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior" - meaning an injury that extends from front to back on the upper joint rim.

The labrum is a cartilage ring that surrounds the socket in the shoulder and contributes to stability. The long biceps tendon attaches at the top of this ring, and this is where SLAP lesions occur.

Classification

SLAP lesions are classified into four main types:

Type I - Degenerative changes

Wear and fraying in the labrum without detachment. Common age-related change. Rarely requires treatment.

Type II - Detachment of labrum and biceps attachment

Most common

The labrum and biceps attachment detach from the socket. This is the most clinically relevant type and the one most often discussed in relation to surgery.

Type III - "Bucket handle" tear

A part of the labrum tears off and can hang down into the joint like a bucket handle. Can cause mechanical symptoms.

Type IV - Tear involving the biceps tendon

Combination of type III where the tear extends into the biceps tendon.

6% of shoulder arthroscopies
Type II most common type
20-40 years typical age group

Causes and risk factors

SLAP lesions can occur in several ways:

Acute injury

  • Fall on outstretched arm: Compression forces push the upper arm bone up against the labrum
  • Direct trauma: Blow to the shoulder
  • Sudden pull: Heavy lifting or jerking of the arm

Overload (most common)

Repetitive overhead activity

Main cause

Throwing movements and overhead sports expose the biceps attachment to repeated pulling forces. Over time, this can lead to SLAP lesion. Common in:

  • Handball players (see thrower's shoulder)
  • Volleyball players
  • Baseball pitchers
  • Swimmers
  • Tennis players

Risk factors

  • Overhead sports, especially throwing sports
  • Age 20-40 years for traumatic lesions
  • Age over 40 for degenerative changes (type I)
  • Glenohumeral internal rotation deficit (GIRD)
  • Weak scapula control
  • Previous shoulder instability

Important perspective

Many have SLAP lesions on MRI without symptoms. A study found that up to 55% of asymptomatic shoulders in people over 45 years had labrum changes. This means an MRI finding does not necessarily explain your pain.

3

Symptoms

Symptoms of SLAP lesion can be vague and overlap with many other shoulder conditions:

Common symptoms

  • Deep pain: Often difficult to localize, "inside" the shoulder
  • Pain during overhead activity: Especially when throwing or hitting above the head
  • Clicking or snapping: Mechanical symptoms during movement (see clicking in the shoulder)
  • Pain in "late cocking" position: Arm back and up, ready to throw
  • Feeling of instability: Shoulder feels "loose" or "gives way"
  • Reduced throwing velocity/distance: In athletes

Symptoms that may point to SLAP

  • Pain at the end of the throwing motion
  • Clicking at specific shoulder positions
  • Pain during biceps loading combined with overhead pain
  • Feeling of "dead arm" after throwing

Diagnostic challenge

SLAP lesions are notoriously difficult to diagnose clinically. Symptoms overlap with many conditions (impingement, rotator cuff problems, biceps tendinopathy), and no single test is sufficiently reliable. Shoulder pain can also be worsened by poor ergonomics.

4

Diagnosis

Diagnosing SLAP lesion is challenging and often requires a combination of clinical findings and imaging.

Clinical tests

Several tests have been described, but none have high enough accuracy alone:

  • O'Brien's test (Active compression): Most used, but moderate sensitivity
  • Biceps load test: Positive with external rotation against resistance
  • Anterior slide test
  • Crank test

Limitations of tests

A meta-analysis showed that even the best clinical tests have limited accuracy. Combination of several positive tests increases the probability, but the diagnosis can rarely be made clinically alone.

Imaging

MR arthrography (gold standard)

MRI with contrast injected into the joint. Provides the best visualization of the labrum and can distinguish between different types of SLAP lesions. Sensitivity around 80-90%.

Regular MRI

Can detect SLAP lesions, but has lower sensitivity than MR arthrography. Useful for looking for concomitant pathology (rotator cuff, etc.).

Important caveats

  • Even MR arthrography has false-positive findings
  • SLAP changes are common in asymptomatic individuals
  • MRI findings must always be correlated with clinical findings
  • Age over 40: Type I changes are often normal findings
5

What does the research say?

Research on SLAP lesions has provided important corrections to previous treatment practices:

The Norwegian SLAP Study (BMJ 2017)

This study is a turning point in understanding SLAP treatment. Researchers compared SLAP repair with sham surgery (the patient was put under anesthesia and incisions were made, but no actual repair was performed).

  • Result: No significant difference between repair and sham
  • Conclusion: Arthroscopic SLAP repair is no better than placebo surgery for type II SLAP lesions
  • Implication: Strengthens the argument for conservative treatment as first choice

Other important findings

78% return to sports conservatively
63% return to same level after surgery
25-30% complications/dissatisfaction after op

What does this mean?

  • Conservative treatment should be first choice for most SLAP lesions
  • Surgery gives no better results than sham in many patients
  • Rehabilitation and strength training are likely the key components
  • Surgery may still have a role in selected cases (mechanical symptoms, significant instability)
6

Treatment

Based on the latest research, conservative treatment should be first choice for the vast majority with SLAP lesion.

Conservative treatment (first choice)

Rehabilitation program

  • Phase 1 (0-4 weeks): Pain relief, restore mobility, avoid provoking activities
  • Phase 2 (4-8 weeks): Strengthening of rotator cuff and scapula stabilizers
  • Phase 3 (8-12 weeks): Functional training, gradual return to activity
  • Phase 4 (12+ weeks): Sport-specific training, gradual return to throwing

Focus areas in rehabilitation

  • Rotator cuff strength: Especially external rotators and scapula muscles
  • Scapula control: Improve shoulder blade movement and stability
  • Posterior capsule stretching: Treat any GIRD
  • Kinetic chain training: Strengthen the entire chain from legs to arm
  • Throwing mechanics: Optimize technique to reduce load

Surgery - when?

Surgery is only considered in selected cases:

Possible indications for surgery

  • Significant mechanical symptoms (locking, clicking affecting function)
  • Type III/IV lesions with free fragment in the joint
  • Concomitant shoulder instability requiring surgical treatment
  • No improvement after 3-6 months of proper rehabilitation
  • Elite athletes where marginal improvement may be decisive

Surgical options

  • SLAP repair: Labrum is sewn back to the socket. Traditional approach.
  • Biceps tenodesis: Biceps tendon is moved and attached to the upper arm bone. Often preferred in older patients.
  • Biceps tenotomy: Biceps tendon is cut. Simplest procedure, fast rehabilitation.
7

Prognosis

The prognosis for SLAP lesion is generally good, especially with conservative treatment.

Conservative treatment

  • 78% of athletes return to sports
  • Good function is achieved in most
  • Rehabilitation typically takes 3-6 months
  • Many can continue with modified activity

After surgery

  • Rehabilitation takes 4-6 months for return to sports
  • Full return to throwing may take 9-12 months
  • 63% return to the same sports level
  • 25-30% have persistent complaints or are dissatisfied
  • Risk of stiffness and complications

Factors affecting prognosis

  • Age: Younger patients often have better results
  • Lesion type: Isolated type II has better prognosis than combined injuries
  • Compliance: Completing rehabilitation program is crucial
  • Concomitant pathology: Combined injuries have worse prognosis
  • Expectations: Realistic expectations give better satisfaction
8

What you can do yourself

In the acute phase

  • Avoid activities that provoke pain, especially overhead
  • Ice as needed for pain relief
  • Maintain gentle mobility
  • Avoid heavy lifting and throwing

Rehabilitation phase

  • Sleeper stretch: For posterior capsule/GIRD
  • External rotation with band: Strengthen the rotator cuff
  • Scapula exercises: Retraction, depression, lower trapezius
  • Rows: Strengthen upper back
  • Core training: Important for power transfer during throwing

Gradual return to activity

  1. Start with light throwing at short distance
  2. Gradually increase distance before intensity
  3. Then gradually increase intensity
  4. Finally, sport-specific training
  5. Full return when pain-free and strength is restored
9

Frequently asked questions

Is surgery necessary for SLAP lesion?
Often not. The Norwegian SLAP study (BMJ 2017) showed that surgery was no better than sham surgery. 78% of patients with type II SLAP lesion return to sports with conservative treatment alone.
How is SLAP lesion diagnosed?
Diagnosis is challenging. Clinical tests have moderate accuracy. MR arthrography is the gold standard, but even this has limitations. Many SLAP lesions are incidental findings without causing symptoms.
Can I continue throwing with a SLAP lesion?
With proper rehabilitation, many can return to throwing. Focus on rotator cuff strength, scapula control and gradual progression is key. Some may need to modify technique or reduce intensity.
What is the difference between SLAP lesion and biceps tendinopathy?
SLAP lesion affects the attachment where the biceps tendon attaches to the labrum, while biceps tendinopathy affects the tendon itself. They can cause similar symptoms and occur together, but SLAP is more associated with overhead sports and instability symptoms.
How long does rehabilitation take?
Conservative rehabilitation typically takes 3-6 months. With surgery, rehabilitation is longer (4-6 months for return to sports). Full return to throwing sports can take 9-12 months after surgery.
Does an MRI finding mean I have a problem?
Not necessarily. Many have labrum changes on MRI without symptoms - especially over 40 years. An MRI finding must always be seen in context with your symptoms. Focus on symptoms and function, not just the image.
