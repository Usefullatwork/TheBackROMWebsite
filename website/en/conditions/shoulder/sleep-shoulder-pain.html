Shoulder Pain and Sleep: How to Sleep Better

Practical tips for sleeping positions, pillows and nighttime pain relief

TL;DR - Quick Summary

  • Sleep on healthy side or back
  • Use pillow under/between arms
  • Avoid sleeping on the painful shoulder
  • Heat/cold before bedtime can help
  • Proper treatment leads to better sleep
Book appointment

Few things are as frustrating as lying awake night after night due to shoulder pain. You toss and turn, but no matter what position you lie in, it throbs and aches. Lack of sleep makes the pain feel even worse the next day - a vicious cycle many can relate to.

Night pain is actually one of the most common symptoms of shoulder conditions like rotator cuff problems, calcific tendinitis and frozen shoulder. These common forms of shoulder pain can significantly disrupt sleep. The good news is that there are concrete measures that can help you sleep better.

In this article, you will get practical tips on sleeping positions, pillow placement, and what you can do before and after bedtime to reduce shoulder pain at night.

1

Why does shoulder pain get worse at night?

It is not your imagination that your shoulder hurts more when you lie in bed. Several physiological and practical factors contribute to this:

Changed blood flow

When you lie down, blood flow to the shoulder increases. This can intensify inflammatory reactions and cause more swelling and pain, especially in acute conditions.

Pressure on structures

Lying position, especially on your side, puts direct pressure on the shoulder. This compresses tendons, bursae and other structures that are already irritated.

Lack of distraction

During the day, the brain is occupied with other things. At night, when it is quiet and dark, pain signals get more attention. We become more aware of the discomfort.

Cortisol decline

Cortisol levels, the body's natural anti-inflammatory hormone, are lowest at night. This can make inflammatory reactions feel stronger.

Which conditions cause the most night pain?

  • Calcific tendinitis in resorption phase - often intense night pain
  • Rotator cuff tendinopathy - typically worst when lying on affected side, may include biceps tendinopathy
  • Frozen shoulder - night pain is a cardinal sign
  • Bursitis - inflammation in the bursa often worsens at night, can be treated with cortisone injection
2

The best sleeping positions

Finding the right sleeping position is often the most important measure for better night sleep with shoulder pain. Here are the recommended options:

Option 1: Sleep on your back

This is often the best position for shoulder pain because it puts the least pressure on the shoulder.

  • Place a small pillow or rolled towel under the painful arm
  • Keep the arm slightly away from the body (about 30 degrees)
  • A pillow under the knees can relieve the back
  • Use a pillow that keeps the neck in neutral position

Option 2: Sleep on the healthy side

If you prefer to sleep on your side, lie on the shoulder that does not hurt.

  • Place a pillow in front of you and rest the painful arm on it
  • The pillow should be thick enough for the arm to be at shoulder height
  • A body pillow can provide extra support
  • Avoid letting the painful shoulder "fall" forward

Avoid this:

  • Do not sleep on the painful shoulder - this increases pressure and worsens pain
  • Do not sleep with arm above head - this compresses structures in the shoulder
  • Do not sleep on stomach - the neck twists and the shoulder ends up in unfavorable position

For those who usually sleep on their stomach

This is the hardest habit to change, but also the most important. Stomach sleeping forces the neck to rotate and places asymmetric load on the shoulders.

  • Try gradual transition to side sleeping with body pillow
  • A pillow along the side can prevent you from rolling onto your stomach
  • Be patient - it takes time to change sleeping habits
3

Pillows and equipment

The right pillow choice and placement can make a big difference for nighttime shoulder comfort.

The head pillow

The head pillow should keep the neck in neutral position - not too high and not too low:

  • For side sleepers: The pillow should fill the space between ear and mattress so the neck is straight
  • For back sleepers: A lower pillow that supports the neck's natural curve
  • Wrong pillow height can cause neck tension that radiates to the shoulder - read more about ergonomics and shoulder problems

Support pillows for the arm

In addition to the head pillow, you should use extra pillows to support the painful arm:

  • When on back: Small pillow under the upper arm for slight elevation
  • When on side: Thick pillow in front of the body that the arm can rest on
  • Body pillow: Long pillow that provides support from shoulder to knee

Tip: Create a "pillow fortress"

Many experience unconsciously rolling onto the painful shoulder during the night. Place pillows strategically around you to prevent this. A pillow behind your back and one in front of your body can help you maintain position.

The mattress

The mattress should be firm enough to provide support, but soft enough for the shoulder to sink in slightly when on your side:

  • Too firm mattress increases pressure on the shoulder
  • Too soft mattress provides poor support and unfavorable spinal position
  • A topper can adjust firmness without buying a new mattress
4

What you can do before bedtime

What you do in the last hour before bed can affect how well you sleep with shoulder pain.

Heat or cold

  • Cold (ice): Best for acute inflammation and swelling. Use for 10-15 minutes with a towel between ice and skin.
  • Heat: Best for chronic muscle tension and stiffness. Heat pad or warm shower can loosen muscles.
  • Try both: See what works best for you. Some prefer heat followed by cold.

Gentle stretches

Light stretching can help reduce tension before bed:

  • Pendulum exercises: Let the arm hang and swing gently in circles (30 seconds)
  • Chest stretch: Extend arm toward a wall and gently turn away
  • Neck stretch: Gentle side bending of the neck
  • Important: No movements should provoke pain

Medications

Talk to your doctor or pharmacist about timing of pain relievers:

  • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Diclofenac) taken 1-2 hours before bedtime can be effective through the night
  • Paracetamol can be combined with NSAIDs if needed
  • Short-term use is generally safe, but long-term use should be discussed with a doctor

Relaxation techniques

Pain and anxiety about pain can create a negative spiral. Relaxation techniques can help:

  • Deep breathing: 4 seconds in, 4 seconds hold, 6 seconds out
  • Progressive muscle relaxation: Tense and release muscle groups systematically
  • Avoid screen use in the last hour before bedtime
5

When you wake up from pain

It is frustrating to wake up in the middle of the night from shoulder pain. Here is what you can do:

Acute pain relief

  • Change position: Check if you have rolled onto the painful side
  • Adjust pillows: Make sure the arm is supported
  • Get up: A few minutes in upright position can relieve pressure
  • Gentle movement: Let the arm swing gently to increase circulation

Cold or heat

Have a cold or heat pack ready on the nightstand. 10-15 minutes with ice (wrapped in towel) or heat can provide enough relief to fall back asleep.

Do not do this:

  • Do not force yourself to sleep on the painful shoulder hoping to "get used to it"
  • Do not lie awake stressing - it makes the pain worse
  • Do not take extra pain medication without checking dosage
6

When should you seek help

Persistent night pain can significantly affect quality of life. Sleep deprivation worsens pain, reduces immune function and affects mood and function.

Seek a doctor or chiropractor if:

  • Night pain has lasted more than 2-3 weeks without improvement
  • You are sleeping less than 4-5 hours per night due to pain
  • Pain is so intense that nothing helps
  • You have fever in addition to shoulder pain
  • You experience numbness or tingling in the arm at night - may indicate TOS (thoracic outlet syndrome)

Persistent sleep problems require identifying and treating the underlying cause.

What can a chiropractor do?

  • Identify the cause of shoulder pain through examination
  • Provide manual treatment that reduces pain and stiffness
  • Guide on specific exercises and sleeping positions for your condition
  • Refer for imaging or specialist if needed

Frequently asked questions

Which side should I sleep on with shoulder pain?
Sleep on the healthy side with a pillow between your arms to support the painful shoulder. Alternatively, you can sleep on your back with a small pillow under the affected arm.
Why does shoulder pain get worse at night?
Several factors contribute: increased blood flow at rest causes more inflammation, lying position increases pressure in the shoulder, and lack of distraction makes us notice the pain more.
Can the wrong pillow worsen shoulder pain?
Yes. A pillow that is too high or too low can lead to unfavorable neck and shoulder positioning throughout the night, which can worsen pain.
Should I use heat or cold before bedtime?
For acute inflammation, cold (10-15 min) before bedtime can help. For chronic tension, heat may be better. Try both and see what works for you.
How long does night pain from shoulder problems last?
This varies depending on the cause. Acute conditions like calcific tendinitis in the resorption phase can cause intense night pain for days to weeks. Chronic conditions like shoulder osteoarthritis can cause prolonged sleep problems without proper treatment.
Does it help to sleep in a chair or upright?
Some experience relief sleeping semi-upright (recliner position) in acute phases. This reduces pressure and blood pooling in the shoulder. However, it is not a long-term solution.
