Thrower's Shoulder and GIRD: When Throwing Causes Shoulder Pain

Handball shoulder, internal impingement and movement deficit in athletes

You're a handball player and notice that it's starting to hurt when you throw. The pain is deep in the shoulder, especially in the back, and you feel that the arm doesn't move as freely as before. Shoulder pain can have many causes, but in throwing athletes, GIRD is particularly common.

These are classic signs of thrower's shoulder, also known as GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit). The condition is extremely common in throwing sports athletes.

The good news is that over 90% improve with conservative treatment, and many can continue their sport with proper follow-up.

Quick Summary

  • 72% of handball/volleyball players have GIRD
  • 90% improve with conservative treatment
  • Sleeper stretch is the main exercise
  • Total ROM should be equal on both sides
  • Strengthening posterior rotator cuff is important
1

What is Thrower's Shoulder and GIRD?

Thrower's shoulder is a collective term for shoulder problems that occur in athletes who perform repetitive throwing movements. The most common underlying condition is GIRD - Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit.

What is GIRD?

GIRD means the shoulder has lost internal rotation compared to the non-dominant arm. Typically you see:

  • Reduced internal rotation: Often 15-20 degrees less than the opposite side
  • Increased external rotation: Often correspondingly increased on the dominant side
  • Tight posterior capsule: The main cause of the reduced range of motion

GIRD is extremely common in throwing athletes. Studies show that up to 72% of handball and volleyball players have measurable GIRD. But here's the important thing:

When is GIRD normal vs. problematic?

A certain degree of GIRD is normal adaptation to throwing sports and doesn't necessarily cause problems. GIRD only becomes problematic when:

  • Total range of motion (internal rotation + external rotation) is reduced compared to the opposite side
  • It causes pain during throwing
  • It affects performance

Why Does Thrower's Shoulder Develop?

Throwing is one of the fastest human movements. During a hard ball throw, the arm can reach speeds of up to 7000 degrees per second. This extreme load leads to adaptations in the shoulder:

Adaptive process

1. Capsular changes

The posterior joint capsule gradually becomes tighter and thicker in response to repetitive stretch loads on the anterior capsule during throwing.

2. Bone changes

Young throwers may develop retroversion (backward tilt) of the humeral head. This contributes to increased external rotation and reduced internal rotation.

3. Muscular imbalances

The throwing muscles (anterior shoulder, chest muscles) often become stronger than the posterior stabilizers, reinforcing the imbalance.

When adaptation becomes pathology

When the posterior capsule becomes too tight, the rotation center in the shoulder shifts. This can lead to:

  • Internal impingement: Rotator cuff tendons are pinched between the humeral head and labrum posterosuperiorly
  • SLAP lesion: Traction pain at the biceps attachment
  • Partial rotator cuff tear: Especially the undersurface of supraspinatus

Risk factors for developing symptomatic GIRD

  • Rapid increase in training volume or intensity
  • Lack of periodization and recovery
  • Insufficient warm-up
  • Neglecting stretching and posterior strength
  • Poor throwing technique
3

Which Sports Are Affected?

GIRD and thrower's shoulder occur in all sports with repetitive overhead arm movements:

Throwing sports

Highest risk
  • Handball: Very common due to hard shots and high volume
  • Baseball/softball: Classic thrower's shoulder sport
  • Cricket: Both bowlers and batters
  • Javelin: Extreme load during throws

Striking sports

  • Volleyball: Especially attackers and servers
  • Tennis: Particularly serve and smash
  • Badminton: Fastest racket sport

Other sports

  • Swimming: Especially freestyle and butterfly
  • Water polo: Combines throwing and swimming
  • Climbing: Repetitive overhead grips

When do symptoms debut?

Many develop GIRD already in their teenage years, but symptoms often first appear when training load increases (e.g., transition to senior team) or when recovery becomes insufficient.

4

Symptoms of Thrower's Shoulder

Symptoms of thrower's shoulder are often subtle at first and gradually worsen:

Early signs

  • Stiffness in the shoulder after training
  • Feeling that the arm doesn't move as freely
  • Mild aching pain behind the shoulder
  • Reduced throwing speed or accuracy

More developed symptoms

Pain during throwing

  • Especially in late cocking phase (arm back) and early acceleration
  • Sharp pain deep in the shoulder
  • May radiate down into the upper arm

Posterior shoulder pain

  • Localized behind the shoulder
  • Aching pain after activity
  • May wake you at night when lying on the affected side. Read more about sleep and shoulder pain

Reduced performance

  • Reduced throwing speed ("dead arm")
  • Poorer control and accuracy
  • Faster fatigue in the arm

Red Flags - Seek Healthcare

  • Sudden severe pain during throw ("pop" in shoulder)
  • Significant weakness or inability to lift arm
  • Persistent numbness or tingling in arm
  • Visible swelling or discoloration

These may indicate acute SLAP lesion, rotator cuff rupture or nerve injury such as TOS.

5

How is the Diagnosis Made?

The diagnosis is made primarily through clinical examination focusing on movement patterns and provocation tests.

Movement assessment

Rotation measurement

Both shoulders are measured at 90 degrees abduction:

  • Internal rotation (IR): Normally about 70-90 degrees
  • External rotation (ER): Normally about 90-110 degrees
  • Total ROM: IR + ER should be approximately equal on both sides

GIRD criteria

Asymptomatic GIRD: Reduced IR on dominant side, but total ROM is preserved (increased ER compensates).

Symptomatic GIRD: Reduced total ROM (>5 degrees) compared to opposite side, or pain during testing.

Clinical tests

Posterior impingement test

The arm is brought into maximum external rotation and abduction. Pain in the back of the shoulder indicates internal impingement.

Sleeper stretch position

Test internal rotation with patient lying on side. Compare with opposite side.

SLAP tests

O'Brien's test, anterior slide, biceps load II to assess labral pathology.

Imaging

Imaging is rarely necessary to diagnose GIRD, but may be relevant for:

  • Suspected structural damage (SLAP, rotator cuff tear)
  • Lack of response to conservative treatment
  • Acute injury or trauma

MR arthrography is best for assessing labrum and rotator cuff in throwing athletes.

6

Treatment of Thrower's Shoulder

Conservative treatment is very effective for thrower's shoulder, with over 90% success rate. The main goals are to restore normal movement and strength.

Treatment principles

  • Posterior capsule stretching: Sleeper stretch is the main intervention
  • Posterior rotator cuff strengthening: Infraspinatus, teres minor
  • Scapular stabilization: Shoulder blade control
  • Gradual return to throwing: Structured throwing program

Phase 1: Acute phase (0-2 weeks)

  • Relative rest from throwing
  • Daily posterior capsule stretching
  • Soft tissue treatment of infraspinatus and teres minor
  • Ice after activity as needed

Phase 2: Rehabilitation phase (2-6 weeks)

  • Continued stretching with focus on reaching normal values
  • Progressive strength training for rotator cuff
  • Scapula exercises (serratus anterior, lower trapezius)
  • General shoulder and core training

Phase 3: Return to throwing (6-12 weeks)

  • Structured throwing program with gradual progression
  • Start with short distances and low intensity
  • Increase distance and power over several weeks
  • Full maintenance program to prevent recurrence

Main exercises

Sleeper Stretch (main exercise for GIRD)

  1. Lie on the affected side with shoulder at 90 degrees flexion
  2. Elbow bent 90 degrees, forearm pointing up
  3. Use opposite hand to gently press forearm down toward floor
  4. Hold stretch for 30-60 seconds
  5. Repeat 3-4 times, 2-3 times daily

Important: Avoid letting the shoulder roll backward. Keep shoulder blade stable against the surface.

Cross-body stretch

  1. Stand or sit upright
  2. Pull the affected arm across your chest with the opposite hand
  3. Keep elbow straight, pull arm toward opposite shoulder
  4. Hold 30 seconds, repeat 3-4 times

Side-lying external rotation (strengthening)

  1. Lie on opposite side with light weight or band
  2. Elbow bent 90 degrees and supported against body
  3. Rotate forearm outward in controlled movement
  4. 3 sets x 15 repetitions

When surgery?

Surgery is rarely needed for isolated GIRD. It may be indicated for:

  • Structural SLAP lesion not responding to conservative treatment
  • Significant rotator cuff tear
  • Persistent symptoms after 6+ months of conservative treatment
7

Prevention of Thrower's Shoulder

Prevention is crucial for throwing athletes. A structured maintenance program can significantly reduce the risk of symptomatic GIRD.

Daily routines

Recommended maintenance program

  • Sleeper stretch: 3x30 sec, daily (especially after training)
  • Cross-body stretch: 3x30 sec, daily
  • Posterior rotator cuff strength: 2-3 times per week
  • Scapula stabilization: 2-3 times per week

Load management

  • Periodization: Vary training intensity throughout the season
  • Pitch count programs: Monitor number of throws per week
  • Rest days: At least 1-2 throw-free days per week
  • Off-season: Significantly reduce throwing load

Warm-up for throwing

  • General warm-up (jogging, jumping)
  • Dynamic arm swings and circles
  • Light posterior shoulder stretching
  • Gradual increase in throwing distance and intensity

Warning signs to watch for

  • Stiffness in shoulder that doesn't ease with warm-up
  • Pain persisting more than 24 hours after training
  • Gradual decrease in throwing speed or accuracy
  • Feeling of "dead arm" after throwing

Early intervention at these signs can prevent more serious injuries.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is thrower's shoulder (GIRD)?
GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit) is a condition where the throwing arm loses internal rotation compared to the non-dominant arm. This is due to adaptations in the joint capsule from repeated throwing movements.
Is GIRD always a problem?
No. A certain degree of GIRD is normal in throwing athletes and represents adaptations to the sport. It only becomes a problem when total range of motion (internal rotation + external rotation) is reduced compared to the opposite side, or when it causes pain.
Which sports cause thrower's shoulder?
GIRD is most common in throwing sports such as handball, baseball, volleyball, tennis and badminton. It can also occur in swimming and other sports with repetitive overhead arm movements.
Can I continue playing with thrower's shoulder?
Often yes, but it depends on the symptoms. With proper treatment and an adapted training program, most can continue their sport. It's important to address the problem early to avoid secondary injuries.
What is the sleeper stretch?
The sleeper stretch is the main exercise for treating GIRD. You lie on your side with the shoulder at 90 degrees flexion and use the other hand to gently press the forearm down toward the floor. This stretches the posterior joint capsule.
How long does it take for thrower's shoulder to get better?
With consistent treatment, most see significant improvement within 6-8 weeks. Full return to throwing typically takes 8-12 weeks. Maintenance exercises should continue permanently to prevent recurrence.
