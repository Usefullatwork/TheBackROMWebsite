Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS): Numb Arm and Tingling

When nerves and blood vessels are compressed between neck and shoulder

You wake up at night with an arm that is completely dead. Tingling and numbness extend from the shoulder down to the fingers. Perhaps you also notice that the arm becomes cold or weak when you lift it overhead.

This could be a sign of thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS), a condition where nerves and/or blood vessels become compressed in the passage between the neck and shoulder. TOS is one of several possible causes of shoulder pain that radiates down the arm.

TOS is a challenging diagnosis because the symptoms can resemble many other conditions. But with proper examination and treatment, most can achieve good improvement without surgery.

Quick Summary

  • 90-97% have neurogenic TOS (nerve compression)
  • Women affected 3-4x more often than men
  • Conservative treatment first for 3-6 months
  • Posture correction and exercises are first choice
  • Vascular TOS requires prompt medical evaluation
1

What is Thoracic Outlet Syndrome?

Thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS) is a collective term for conditions where nerves, arteries or veins become compressed in the narrow passage between the neck and shoulder. This passage is called the "thoracic outlet."

Anatomical Background

The thoracic outlet is the area where nerves and blood vessels must pass from the neck out to the arm. There are three potential "bottlenecks" here:

  • Scalene triangle: Between the anterior and middle scalene muscles in the neck
  • Costoclavicular space: Between the collarbone and first rib
  • Subpectoral space: Under the pectoralis minor muscle in the chest

TOS is relatively rare, with an annual incidence of approximately 1-3 per 100,000. The condition affects women 3-4 times more often than men and is most common in the age group 20-50 years.

The Three Types of TOS

TOS is classified into three types based on which structures are compressed:

1. Neurogenic TOS (nTOS)

90-97% of cases

By far the most common form. The nerves in the brachial plexus (the nerve network supplying the arm) become compressed.

Typical symptoms:

  • Pain in neck, shoulder and arm
  • Numbness and tingling in fingers (often 4th and 5th finger)
  • Weakness in hand/grip strength
  • Symptoms worsen with overhead work
  • "Numb arm" at night

2. Venous TOS (vTOS)

2-3% of cases

Compression of the subclavian vein (the vein that carries blood from the arm).

Typical symptoms:

  • Swelling in arm and hand
  • Bluish discoloration (cyanosis)
  • Heaviness sensation in the arm
  • Visible dilated veins on shoulder/chest
  • Can lead to blood clot (Paget-Schroetter syndrome)

3. Arterial TOS (aTOS)

1% of cases

Compression of the subclavian artery (the artery that supplies blood to the arm). Rare but potentially serious.

Typical symptoms:

  • Cold, pale arm/hand
  • Pain with activity (claudication)
  • Weak or absent pulse in the wrist
  • Can lead to embolism or aneurysm

Red Flags - Seek Medical Attention Immediately

  • Sudden cold, pale or blue arm
  • Severe swelling in the arm that develops rapidly
  • Severe chest or arm pain without clear cause
  • Weak or absent pulse in the wrist

Vascular TOS (venous and arterial) requires prompt medical evaluation and may need acute treatment.

3

Causes and Risk Factors

TOS can be caused by anatomical variants, trauma or repetitive strain that makes the passage narrower:

Anatomical factors

  • Cervical rib: Extra rib from C7 (found in 0.5-1% of the population)
  • Fibrous bands: Abnormal connective tissue strands
  • Abnormal muscle variation: E.g., extra scalene muscle
  • Long transverse process on C7: Elongated bony projection from the neck vertebra

Acquired factors

  • Trauma: Whiplash, collarbone fracture, shoulder injuries
  • Repetitive movements: Work overhead, throwing (see thrower's shoulder), swimming
  • Poor posture: Forward head position, rounded shoulders - see tips on ergonomics and shoulder problems
  • Large breasts: Can pull the shoulders downward
  • Muscular imbalance: Tight chest muscles, weak back muscles

Why are women affected more often?

Several factors may explain the gender difference: Women generally have slimmer shoulders and narrower thoracic outlet, hormonal factors can affect connective tissue, and certain occupations with repetitive tasks have traditionally been female-dominated.

Common provoking activities

  • Sleeping with arm overhead - read about proper sleeping positions
  • Prolonged computer work
  • Carrying heavy bags on the shoulder
  • Throwing and hitting movements (sports)
  • Working with arms raised (painters, electricians)

Symptoms of TOS

Symptoms of TOS vary depending on type, but neurogenic TOS (the most common) typically causes:

Common neurogenic symptoms

Pain

  • Diffuse pain in neck, shoulder and arm
  • May radiate to chest or back
  • Worsens with overhead activity
  • Often worse at night - read more about sleep and shoulder pain

Sensory disturbances

  • Numbness and tingling, often in 4th and 5th finger (ulnar distribution)
  • Feeling that the arm "falls asleep"
  • Cold sensation in hand/fingers
  • Paresthesias that worsen in certain positions

Motor symptoms

  • Weakness in the hand
  • Reduced grip strength
  • Clumsy finger movements
  • Dropping things more easily

Position-dependent symptoms

A characteristic feature of TOS is that symptoms often worsen in certain positions:

  • Arms overhead (washing hair, painting ceiling)
  • Carrying heavy things
  • Driving (holding the steering wheel)
  • Sleeping on the affected side

How is the Diagnosis Made?

TOS is known as a "diagnosis of exclusion" because there is no single test that confirms the condition. The diagnosis is made through thorough clinical examination and ruling out other causes.

Clinical examination

I use several provocation tests to reproduce the symptoms:

Adson's test

The head is rotated toward the affected side while the patient takes a deep breath. Positive test: Reduced/absent pulse or reproduced symptoms.

Wright's test (hyperabduction test)

The arm is lifted overhead in 90 degrees abduction and external rotation. Positive test: Symptoms or pulse change.

Roos' test (EAST test)

Patient holds arms in 90 degrees abduction and opens/closes hands for 3 minutes. Positive test: Rapid fatigue, pain or paresthesias.

Costoclavicular maneuver

Shoulders are pulled back and down ("military posture"). Positive test: Symptoms or pulse change.

Important about provocation tests

No single test is diagnostic for TOS. Many healthy people have positive tests without symptoms. The tests must be interpreted in the context of medical history and other findings.

Imaging

  • X-ray neck/thorax: Can reveal cervical rib or long C7 transverse process
  • MRI: Assess soft tissue structures and rule out disc pathology
  • Ultrasound/Doppler: Assess blood flow
  • CT angiography: For suspected vascular TOS
  • Nerve conduction studies: Measure nerve conduction (often normal in nTOS)

Differential diagnoses

Several conditions can resemble TOS and must be ruled out:

  • Cervical radiculopathy (neck disc problems)
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Cubital tunnel syndrome (ulnar nerve at elbow)
  • Rotator cuff pathology, including biceps tendinopathy
  • Cervicobrachial syndrome
  • Fibromyalgia

Treatment of TOS

Conservative treatment is the first choice for neurogenic TOS, with surgery reserved for cases that don't respond after 3-6 months.

Conservative treatment (first choice)

The goal is to open the thoracic outlet and reduce compression through:

  • Posture correction: Reduce forward head position and rounded shoulders
  • Strength training: Especially between shoulder blades (rhomboids, lower trapezius)
  • Stretching: Scalene muscles, pectoralis minor, levator scapulae
  • Manual therapy: Soft tissue treatment, joint mobilization
  • Ergonomic guidance: Adaptation of workstation and sleeping habits

Specific exercises

Scalene stretching

Tilt head to opposite side of the tight muscle. Hold for 30-60 seconds. Repeat 3-4 times daily.

Corner stretch (pectoralis minor)

Stand in a corner with forearms against the wall. Lean gently in until you feel a stretch in the chest. Hold 30-60 seconds.

Shoulder blade squeeze

Pull shoulder blades together and down. Hold 5-10 seconds. Repeat 10-15 times, several times daily.

Chin tucks

Pull chin straight back as if making a "double chin." Hold 5 seconds. Repeat 10-15 times.

Surgical treatment

Surgery is considered when:

  • Conservative treatment has been tried for at least 3-6 months without sufficient effect
  • There is progressive neurological deterioration
  • For vascular TOS (often need for earlier intervention)

Common procedures include:

  • First rib resection: Remove first rib to create more space
  • Scalene myotomy: Cut or release the scalene muscles
  • Cervical rib removal: For anatomical variant

Prognosis with conservative treatment

Studies show that 50-90% of patients with neurogenic TOS achieve satisfactory improvement with conservative treatment. Success rate depends on early diagnosis, patient engagement and underlying cause.

7

What Can You Do Yourself?

Many measures can relieve symptoms and prevent worsening:

Ergonomics and daily activities

  • Workstation: Adjust screen height so you don't have to look down. Keep elbows close to body
  • Carrying: Avoid heavy bags on shoulder. Use backpack with both straps - read about ergonomics for the shoulder
  • Phone: Don't squeeze phone between ear and shoulder
  • Driving: Keep steering wheel low (8 and 4 o'clock). Use armrests

Sleeping position

  • Avoid sleeping on the affected side
  • Avoid sleeping with arm overhead
  • Use a supportive pillow that keeps neck neutral
  • Consider placing a pillow along body to prevent arm from falling out to side

Daily exercises

  • Do stretching exercises for neck and chest 2-3 times daily
  • Take frequent breaks during sedentary work
  • Be aware of posture throughout the day
  • Strengthen muscles between shoulder blades regularly

Activities to avoid/modify

  • Prolonged work with arms overhead
  • Heavy lifting with arms away from body
  • Activities that reproduce symptoms

Frequently Asked Questions

What is thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS)?
TOS is a collective term for conditions where nerves and/or blood vessels become compressed in the passage between the neck and shoulder. The most common form (90-97%) is neurogenic TOS which affects the nerves in the brachial plexus.
Why do I wake up with a numb arm?
A numb arm at night can be caused by compression of nerves in the thoracic outlet area, especially in certain sleeping positions. Position changes, pillows and posture exercises can often help. Other causes such as carpal tunnel syndrome or cervical radiculopathy should also be considered.
Does TOS require surgery?
Most people with TOS (especially neurogenic type) respond well to conservative treatment with physiotherapy and posture correction. Surgery is only considered after 3-6 months without sufficient improvement. Vascular TOS may require earlier surgical evaluation.
Can TOS be confused with other conditions?
Yes, TOS can resemble carpal tunnel syndrome, cervical radiculopathy (neck disc problems), shoulder problems or cubital tunnel syndrome. A thorough clinical examination is important for correct diagnosis. Often several conditions must be ruled out before the diagnosis is made.
Who gets thoracic outlet syndrome?
TOS affects women 3-4 times more often than men and is most common between ages 20-50. Risk factors include repetitive overhead movements, poor posture, trauma to shoulder/neck and anatomical variants such as cervical rib.
How long does it take for TOS to get better?
With consistent conservative treatment, many see improvement within 6-12 weeks. Full recovery can take 3-6 months or longer, depending on severity and underlying cause. Ongoing self-effort with exercises and ergonomics is important.
