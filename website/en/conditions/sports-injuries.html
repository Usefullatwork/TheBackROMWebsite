Sports Injuries - Complete Guide

Specialized treatment and rehabilitation for athletes | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Oslo Majorstua

Sports injuries can affect anyone who is physically active, from weekend warriors to elite athletes. These injuries range from acute trauma like sprains and strains to chronic overuse conditions that develop gradually over time.

This guide covers everything you need to know about sports injuries: common types, causes, treatment approaches, and strategies for safe return to sport. Whether you're dealing with an acute injury or a nagging overuse problem, understanding your condition is the first step toward recovery.

Key Facts

  • Overuse injuries account for 50-70% of all sports injuries
  • Rapid training increases are the most common cause of overuse injuries
  • Active rehabilitation leads to faster recovery than rest alone
  • Most sports injuries recover fully with proper conservative treatment
  • Prevention through proper training progression is more effective than treatment

Why Active Rehabilitation Works

Research consistently shows that active rehabilitation - gradually loading injured tissue through specific exercises - leads to better outcomes than rest alone. Movement promotes healing, maintains fitness, and builds the strength needed to prevent re-injury.

1

Types of Sports Injuries

Acute vs. Overuse Injuries

Sports injuries fall into two main categories, each requiring different treatment approaches:

Acute Injuries

Result from a single traumatic event - a fall, collision, or sudden movement. Examples include ankle sprains, muscle tears, and ligament ruptures. These typically cause immediate pain and swelling.

Overuse Injuries

Develop gradually from repetitive stress over time. Examples include tendinopathies (Achilles, patellar), stress fractures, and shin splints. Pain often starts mild and worsens if training continues unchanged.

Common Sports Injuries

  • Muscle strains: Tears in muscle fibers, often in hamstrings, quads, or calves
  • Ligament sprains: Stretching or tearing of ligaments, commonly ankle or knee
  • Tendinopathies: Degeneration of tendons from repetitive loading
  • Stress fractures: Small cracks in bone from cumulative overload
  • Joint injuries: Cartilage damage, labral tears, meniscus injuries
2

Causes and Risk Factors

Training Errors (Most Common)

Rapid increases in training volume, intensity, or frequency are responsible for most overuse injuries. The "10% rule" - increasing weekly load by no more than 10% - helps prevent this.

Biomechanical Factors

Poor movement patterns, muscle imbalances, weakness, and lack of flexibility can increase injury risk. These are often correctable with targeted exercise.

Equipment and Environment

Worn-out footwear, inappropriate equipment, poor playing surfaces, and inadequate protective gear all contribute to injury risk.

Previous Injury

A history of previous injury is one of the strongest predictors of future injury. Incomplete rehabilitation and premature return to sport significantly increase re-injury risk.

Treatment at the Chiropractor

Comprehensive Assessment

Treatment starts with a thorough evaluation including:

  • Detailed injury history and mechanism
  • Physical examination of the injured area
  • Biomechanical analysis and movement screening
  • Assessment of contributing factors
  • Training history review

Treatment Methods

Based on your specific injury and goals, treatment may include:

  • Manual therapy: Joint mobilization, soft tissue work, dry needling
  • Shockwave therapy: Particularly effective for chronic tendinopathies
  • Exercise prescription: Individualized rehabilitation program
  • Load management: Guidance on training modification
  • Taping/bracing: Support during return to sport when needed

Rehabilitation Phases

Structured return to sport typically follows these phases:

  • Phase 1: Pain management and protected movement
  • Phase 2: Restore range of motion and basic strength
  • Phase 3: Sport-specific strength and conditioning
  • Phase 4: Gradual return to training
  • Phase 5: Return to competition
4

Recovery Timeline

Recovery time varies significantly based on injury type and severity:

Muscle Strains

Grade 1 (mild): 1-2 weeks. Grade 2 (moderate): 3-6 weeks. Grade 3 (severe/complete): 3-6 months. Proper rehabilitation reduces re-injury risk.

Ligament Sprains

Grade 1: 1-2 weeks. Grade 2: 4-8 weeks. Grade 3: 8-12 weeks or longer. Some severe sprains may require surgical consultation.

Tendinopathies

Often require 3-6 months of consistent loading exercises. Quick fixes are rare - patience and progressive loading are key.

Key Principle: The goal isn't just pain relief - it's returning to sport with reduced re-injury risk. Rushing back before full rehabilitation is complete significantly increases the chance of re-injury.

5

Red Flags

Seek Emergency Care For:

  • Suspected fracture: Severe pain, deformity, inability to bear weight
  • Joint dislocation: Obvious deformity, severe pain, locked position
  • Concussion signs: Confusion, nausea, vision changes, loss of consciousness
  • Compartment syndrome: Severe pain, numbness, pale limb after injury
  • Circulation problems: Cold, pale extremity with absent pulse
  • Severe swelling: Rapid onset, significant joint effusion
6

Injury Prevention

Progressive Training

Follow the 10% rule for weekly increases. Allow adequate recovery between hard sessions. Include rest days and easier weeks in your program.

Strength and Conditioning

Regular strength training protects against injury. Focus on sport-specific movements and addressing muscle imbalances. 2-3 sessions per week is effective.

Proper Warm-Up

Dynamic warm-up before activity prepares muscles and joints. Include sport-specific movements. Save static stretching for after activity.

Equipment and Technique

  • Replace running shoes every 500-800 km
  • Use sport-appropriate protective equipment
  • Get coaching to improve technique
  • Ensure equipment is properly fitted

Frequently Asked Questions

How soon after injury should I seek treatment?

For acute injuries, assessment within the first few days is ideal. Early intervention can speed recovery and prevent complications. For overuse injuries, seek help when pain affects your training or doesn't improve with modified activity.

Can I continue training with a sports injury?

Often yes, with modifications. Many injuries allow continued training at reduced intensity or with alternative activities. Complete rest is rarely necessary and can actually delay recovery. A chiropractor can guide appropriate modifications.

Should I use ice or heat on my injury?

For acute injuries (first 48-72 hours), ice can help manage pain and swelling. After the acute phase, heat may help with muscle relaxation. However, active movement and appropriate loading are more important than either.

Do I need imaging for my sports injury?

Not always. A thorough clinical examination often provides sufficient information to start treatment. Imaging may be recommended if there's suspected fracture, complete ligament tear, or if symptoms don't improve as expected.

How do I know when I'm ready to return to sport?

Return to sport should be based on function, not time. You should have full pain-free range of motion, adequate strength (usually 90%+ of uninjured side), and be able to perform sport-specific movements without symptoms.

How can I prevent re-injury?

Complete your rehabilitation fully. Maintain strength and conditioning. Progress gradually back to full training. Address any biomechanical factors that contributed to the original injury.
