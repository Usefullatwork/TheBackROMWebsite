Thoracic Back Pain

Complete guide: Causes, symptoms and effective treatment

1 What is Thoracic Back Pain?

The thoracic spine consists of 12 vertebrae (T1-T12) and is the most stable part of the spinal column. Unlike the neck and lower back, the thoracic spine is attached to the ribs, providing extra support but also being a source of pain when the joints become dysfunctional.

Thoracic back pain is more common than many think, but often receives less attention than neck and lower back pain. The pain can vary from aching stiffness between the shoulder blades to sharp, stabbing pain radiating around the ribs.

15-35% annual prevalence
T4-T8 most common level
12 thoracic vertebrae
2-6 weeks typical duration

2 Symptoms of Thoracic Back Pain

Thoracic back pain can manifest in many different ways depending on the cause. Here are the most common symptoms:

Local stiffness and aching pain Feeling of tightness between the shoulder blades, often worse in the morning or after prolonged sitting in the same position.
Sharp, stabbing pain Sudden pain with certain movements, deep breathing, coughing or sneezing.
Radiation around the ribs Pain that follows the ribs from the back and around toward the chest or abdomen.
Pain with rotation Pain when turning, reaching, or twisting the upper body.
Breathing-related pain Pain that worsens with deep inhalation because the ribs move.

Did you know?

Because the nerves from the thoracic spine innervate the chest and abdominal region, thoracic back pain can sometimes feel like chest pain, abdominal pain, or even heart problems. This is a common cause of concern, but most cases are due to harmless musculoskeletal problems.

3 Common Causes of Thoracic Back Pain

Most thoracic back pain is caused by mechanical issues that can be effectively treated by a chiropractor:

Rib Joint Dysfunction (Costovertebral Dysfunction)

The most common cause of thoracic back pain. Occurs when the joints between the ribs and vertebrae become stiff or "locked."

  • Sharp pain with breathing and movement
  • Point tenderness at the rib attachment
  • Often triggered by twisting or lifting
  • Responds very well to chiropractic treatment

Costochondritis

Inflammation in the cartilage connecting the ribs to the breastbone. Causes pain at the front of the chest but often related to thoracic dysfunction.

  • Tender to touch at the breastbone
  • Can mimic heart pain
  • Worse with coughing and deep breathing
  • Often self-limiting, but treatment helps

Thoracic Facet Joint Dysfunction

Stiffness or irritation in the small joints between the vertebrae in the thoracic spine.

  • Stiff and aching pain between the shoulder blades
  • Worse with extension (bending backward)
  • Often related to poor posture
  • Common in office workers

Myofascial Trigger Points

Painful knots in the muscles, especially in the rhomboids, trapezius, and erector spinae.

  • Often refer pain to other areas
  • Can cause headaches and neck pain
  • Often related to stress and overload
  • Treated effectively with soft tissue treatment

Poor Posture and Ergonomics

Prolonged poor loading from office work, mobile phone use, or one-sided activity.

  • Forward head and rounded shoulders
  • Gradual development of stiffness
  • Often combined with neck pain
  • Requires exercises and ergonomic measures

4 Less Common Causes

Some conditions are rarer but important to be aware of:

Scheuermann's Disease

A developmental disorder causing increased kyphosis (rounding) in the thoracic spine, usually discovered in adolescence.

  • Abnormal rounding of the upper back
  • Stiffness and aching pain
  • Wedged vertebrae visible on X-ray
  • Can be treated conservatively in most cases

Thoracic Disc Herniation

Rarer than in the neck and lower back, but can occur. Accounts for only 1-2% of all disc herniations.

  • Often asymptomatic even when found on MRI
  • Can cause radiation around the ribs
  • Rarely requires surgery

Ankylosing Spondylitis

Rheumatic disease causing inflammation and gradual stiffening of the spine.

  • Morning stiffness over 30 minutes
  • Improves with activity
  • Often onset before age 40
  • Requires referral to rheumatologist

5 Red Flags - When Should You See a Doctor?

Most thoracic back pain is harmless, but some symptoms require prompt medical evaluation:

See a doctor immediately if you have:

  • Chest pain with breathing difficulties, sweating, or nausea - may be heart-related
  • Unexplained weight loss - more than 5% of body weight without obvious reason
  • Fever combined with back pain - may indicate infection
  • Nighttime pain that wakes you - not related to position
  • Numbness or weakness in the legs - may indicate spinal cord involvement
  • Problems with bladder or bowel function - emergency situation
  • Previous cancer history - especially breast, prostate, or lung cancer
  • Prolonged corticosteroid use - increased risk of compression fractures

Important to know

Chiropractors are trained to identify red flags and will refer you further if additional investigation is needed. When in doubt, it's always safe to seek evaluation from a healthcare professional.

6 Heart Pain vs. Thoracic Back Pain

Many worry that chest pain could be heart-related. Here's how to tell the difference:

Heart Pain (typical signs)

  • Squeezing, pressing sensation
  • Diffuse pain, hard to point to
  • Radiation to left arm, jaw, or back
  • Accompanied by breathing difficulty, sweating, nausea
  • Worsens with exertion
  • Not affected by pressure or movement
  • Persistent over time

Musculoskeletal Pain (typical signs)

  • Sharp, stabbing, or aching
  • Can point to exactly where it hurts
  • Reproducible with pressure or movement
  • Worsens with breathing, coughing, rotation
  • Varies with position and activity
  • Tender to palpation
  • Comes and goes

When in doubt - call emergency services!

If you're unsure whether your chest pain could be heart-related, it's always better to be wrong and seek help than to wait. Call emergency services or go to the emergency room immediately if you suspect a heart attack.

7 Treatment of Thoracic Back Pain

Chiropractic treatment is very effective for most mechanical thoracic back pain:

Chiropractic Treatment

Includes joint mobilization and manipulation of the thoracic spine and costovertebral joints. Particularly effective for rib dysfunction and thoracic stiffness. Most notice improvement after 2-4 treatments.

Soft Tissue Treatment

Massage, trigger point treatment, and myofascial release for tight muscles in the upper back and shoulder blade area. Effective for muscle tension and trigger points.

Exercises and Rehabilitation

Tailored exercises to increase mobility, strength, and prevent recurrence. Focus on thoracic mobility and posture-correcting exercises.

Ergonomic Advice

Guidance on workplace setup, break routines, and daily activities to reduce strain on the thoracic spine.

8 Exercises for Thoracic Back Pain

These exercises can help reduce stiffness and improve mobility in the thoracic spine:

1. Cat-Camel

A gentle mobilization exercise for the entire spine:

  1. Start on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips
  2. Round your back up toward the ceiling like an angry cat, let your head drop
  3. Lower your back down and lift your head, creating a sway
  4. Move slowly and controlled between positions
  5. Focus on movement in the thoracic spine area
10-15 repetitions, 2-3 times daily

2. Thread the Needle

Excellent for increasing rotational ability in the thoracic spine:

  1. Start on all fours
  2. Lift your right arm out to the side and rotate your upper body up toward the ceiling
  3. Then thread the arm under your body and extend it out on your left side
  4. Let your head rest on the floor in the rotation
  5. Return to start and repeat
8-10 repetitions on each side, 2 times daily

3. Thoracic Extension over Foam Roller

Effective for counteracting forward-leaning posture and stiffness:

  1. Lie on your back with a foam roller under your thoracic spine
  2. Support your head with your hands, keep elbows out to the side
  3. Let your thoracic spine extend backward over the roller
  4. Hold the position for 5-10 seconds
  5. Move the roller slightly up or down to work different levels
5-10 repetitions at each level, 1-2 times daily

Important about exercises

Stop the exercise if you experience increased pain. These exercises are meant as general advice - for persistent or severe pain, I recommend getting an individual assessment and tailored exercises from your chiropractor.

9 Frequently Asked Questions About Thoracic Back Pain

What does thoracic back pain feel like?

Thoracic back pain can vary from aching and stiff sensations to sharp, stabbing pain. You may experience pain between the shoulder blades, around the ribs, or pain radiating to the chest. Pain often worsens with deep breathing, coughing, or rotating movements.

Can thoracic back pain cause chest pain?

Yes, thoracic back pain can definitely cause chest pain. The nerve roots exiting from the thoracic spine innervate the area around the ribs and chest. Conditions like costochondritis, rib dysfunction, or thoracic dysfunction can all cause pain felt in the chest but originating in the back.

How do you distinguish thoracic back pain from heart problems?

Musculoskeletal chest pain is often reproducible with pressure or movement, localized to one point, and worsens with breathing or rotation. Heart pain is often diffuse, accompanied by breathing difficulty, sweating or nausea, and is not related to movement. When in doubt, seek medical attention immediately.

Is thoracic back pain dangerous?

Most thoracic back pain is caused by harmless muscle and joint problems like rib dysfunction or muscle tension. However, you should see a doctor if you have unexplained weight loss, fever, nighttime pain that wakes you, numbness/weakness in the legs, or previous cancer history.

What is the best treatment for thoracic back pain?

Treatment depends on the cause. For most mechanical thoracic back pain, chiropractic treatment with joint mobilization, soft tissue treatment and exercises is very effective. Self-exercises like cat-camel, thread the needle and thoracic extension over foam roller can also help significantly.

How long does thoracic back pain last?

Acute thoracic back pain often resolves within 2-6 weeks with proper treatment. Chronic pain may last longer but usually responds well to chiropractic treatment combined with exercises and ergonomic measures. Most experience significant improvement within 4-8 treatments.
