Thoracic Back Pain

Complete guide: causes, symptoms and effective treatment

1 What is Thoracic Back Pain?

The thoracic spine (thoracic column) consists of 12 vertebrae (T1-T12) and forms the most stable part of the spinal column. Unlike the neck and lower back, the thoracic spine is attached to the ribs, which provides extra support but can also be a source of pain when the joints become dysfunctional.

Thoracic back pain is more common than many think, but often receives less attention than neck and lower back pain. The pain can vary from aching stiffness between the shoulder blades to sharp, stabbing pains that radiate around the ribs.

2 Symptoms of Thoracic Back Pain

Thoracic back pain can manifest in many different ways depending on the cause. Here are the most common symptoms:

Local stiffness and aching pain Feeling of tightness between the shoulder blades, often worse in the morning or after long periods in the same position.
Sharp, stabbing pain Sudden pain with specific movements, deep breathing, coughing or sneezing.
Radiation around the ribs Pain that follows the ribs from the back and around towards the chest or abdomen.
Pain with rotation Painful when you turn, reach or twist your upper body.
Breathing-related pain Pain that worsens with deep inhalation because the ribs move.

Did you know?

Because the nerves from the thoracic spine innervate the chest and abdominal region, thoracic back pain can sometimes feel like chest pain, abdominal pain or even heart problems. This is a common cause of concern, but most cases are due to harmless musculoskeletal problems.

3 Common Causes of Thoracic Back Pain

Most thoracic back pain is caused by mechanical reasons that can be treated effectively by a chiropractor:

Rib dysfunction (costovertebral dysfunction)

The most common cause of thoracic back pain. Occurs when the joints between the ribs and vertebrae become stiff or "loose".

  • Sharp pain with breathing and movement
  • Point tenderness at rib attachment
  • Often triggered by twisting or lifting
  • Responds very well to chiropractic treatment

Costochondritis

Inflammation of the cartilage connecting the ribs to the sternum. Causes pain at the front of the chest, but often related to thoracic dysfunction.

  • Tender on pressure on the sternum
  • Can resemble heart pain
  • Worse with coughing and deep breathing
  • Often self-limiting, but treatment helps

Thoracic facet joint dysfunction

Stiffness or irritation in the small joints between the vertebrae in the thoracic spine.

  • Stiff and aching pain between shoulder blades
  • Worse with extension (backward bending)
  • Often related to poor posture
  • Common in office workers

Myofascial trigger points

Painful knots in the musculature, especially in the rhomboids, trapezius and erector spinae.

  • Often refers pain to other areas
  • Can cause headaches and neck pain
  • Often related to stress and overload
  • Treated effectively with soft tissue treatment

Poor posture and ergonomics

Prolonged incorrect loading from office work, mobile use or one-sided activity.

  • Forward head and rounded shoulders
  • Gradual development of stiffness
  • Often combined with neck pain
  • Requires exercises and ergonomic measures

4 Less Common Causes

Some conditions are rarer, but important to be aware of:

Scheuermann's disease

A developmental disorder causing increased kyphosis (rounding) in the thoracic spine, usually discovered in adolescence.

  • Abnormal rounding of upper back
  • Stiffness and aching pain
  • Wedge vertebrae visible on X-ray
  • Can be treated conservatively in most cases

Thoracic disc herniation

Rarer than in neck and lower back, but can occur. Accounts for only 1-2% of all disc herniations.

  • Often asymptomatic even when shown on MRI
  • Can cause radiation around the ribs
  • Rarely requires surgery

Ankylosing spondylitis

Rheumatic disease causing inflammation and gradual stiffening of the spine.

  • Morning stiffness over 30 minutes
  • Improvement with activity
  • Often onset before 40 years
  • Requires referral to rheumatologist

5 Red Flags – When Should You See a Doctor?

Most thoracic back pain is harmless, but some symptoms require rapid medical assessment:

Seek medical attention immediately if:

  • Chest pain with breathing difficulties, sweating or nausea – may be heart-related
  • Unexplained weight loss – more than 5% of body weight without obvious reason
  • Fever combined with back pain – may indicate infection
  • Night pain that wakes you – not related to position
  • Numbness or weakness in legs – may indicate spinal cord involvement
  • Problems with bladder or bowel function – acute situation
  • Previous cancer – especially breast, prostate or lung cancer
  • Long-term corticosteroid use – increased risk of compression fractures

Important to know

Chiropractors are trained to identify red flags and will refer you onwards if further investigation is needed. When in doubt, it's always safe to seek healthcare professionals for assessment.

6 Heart Pain vs. Thoracic Back Pain

Many worry that chest pain may be heart-related. Here's how you can distinguish between them:

Heart pain (typical signs)

  • Squeezing, pressing feeling
  • Diffuse pain, difficult to point to
  • Radiation to left arm, jaw or back
  • Accompanied by breathing difficulties, sweating, nausea
  • Worsens with exertion
  • Not affected by pressure or movement
  • Persistent over time

Musculoskeletal pain (typical signs)

  • Sharp, stabbing or aching
  • Can point to exactly where it hurts
  • Reproducible with pressure or movement
  • Worsens with breathing, coughing, rotation
  • Varies with position and activity
  • Tender on palpation
  • Comes and goes

When in doubt – call 113!

If you're unsure whether your chest pain may be heart-related, it's always better to be wrong and seek help than to wait. Call 113 or visit emergency services immediately if you suspect a heart attack.

7 Treatment of Thoracic Back Pain

Chiropractic treatment is very effective for most mechanical thoracic back pain:

Chiropractic treatment

Includes joint mobilisation and manipulation of the thoracic spine and costovertebral joints. Especially effective for rib dysfunction and thoracic stiffness. Most notice improvement after 2-4 treatments.

Soft tissue treatment

Massage, trigger point treatment and myofascial release for tight musculature in the upper back and shoulder blade area. Effective for muscle tension and trigger points.

Exercises and rehabilitation

Tailored exercises to increase mobility, strength and prevent relapse. Focus on thoracic mobility and posture-correcting exercises.

Ergonomic advice

Guidance on workplace setup, break routines and daily activities to reduce load on the thoracic spine.

8 Exercises for Thoracic Back Pain

These exercises can help reduce stiffness and improve mobility in the thoracic spine:

1. Cat-Camel

A gentle mobilisation exercise for the entire spine:

  1. Start on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips
  2. Round your back up towards the ceiling like an angry cat, drop your head
  3. Lower your back down and lift your head, creating a swayback
  4. Move slowly and controlled between positions
  5. Focus on movement in the thoracic area
10-15 repetitions, 2-3 times daily

2. Thread the Needle

Excellent for increasing rotation ability in the thoracic spine:

  1. Start on all fours
  2. Lift right arm out to the side and rotate upper body up towards ceiling
  3. Then thread the arm under your body and stretch it out on the left side
  4. Let your head rest on the floor in the rotation
  5. Return to start and repeat
8-10 repetitions on each side, 2 times daily

3. Thoracic extension over foam roller

Effective for counteracting forward-leaning posture and stiffness:

  1. Lie on your back with a foam roller under the thoracic spine
  2. Support your head with your hands, keep elbows out to the sides
  3. Let the thoracic spine extend backward over the roller
  4. Hold the position for 5-10 seconds
  5. Move the roller slightly up or down to work different levels
5-10 repetitions at each level, 1-2 times daily

Important about exercises

Stop the exercise if you experience increased pain. These exercises are meant as general advice – with persistent or severe pain, I recommend you get an individual assessment and tailored exercises from your chiropractor.

9 Frequently Asked Questions about Thoracic Back Pain

What does thoracic back pain feel like?

Thoracic back pain can vary from aching and stiff pain to sharp, stabbing sensations. You may experience pain between the shoulder blades, around the ribs, or pain that radiates to the chest. Pain often worsens with deep breathing, coughing, or rotating movements.

Can thoracic back pain cause chest pain?

Yes, thoracic back pain can absolutely cause chest pain. The nerve roots that exit from the thoracic spine innervate the area around the ribs and chest. Conditions like costochondritis, rib dysfunction or thoracic dysfunction can all cause pain felt in the chest, but originating from the back.

How do you distinguish thoracic back pain from heart problems?

Musculoskeletal chest pain is often reproducible with pressure or movement, localised to one point, and worsens with breathing or rotation. Heart pain is often diffuse, accompanied by breathing difficulties, sweating or nausea, and is not related to movement. When in doubt, seek medical attention immediately.

Is thoracic back pain dangerous?

Most thoracic back pain is caused by harmless muscle and joint problems like rib dysfunction or muscle tension. However, you should see a doctor if you have unexplained weight loss, fever, night pain that wakes you, numbness/weakness in legs, or previous cancer history.

What is the best treatment for thoracic back pain?

Treatment depends on the cause. For most mechanical thoracic back pain, chiropractic treatment with joint mobilisation, soft tissue treatment and exercises is very effective. Self-exercises like cat-camel, thread the needle and thoracic extension over foam roller can also help significantly.

How long does thoracic back pain last?

Acute thoracic back pain often resolves within 2-6 weeks with proper treatment. Chronic pain may last longer, but usually responds well to chiropractic treatment combined with exercises and ergonomic measures. Most experience significant improvement within 4-8 treatments.
