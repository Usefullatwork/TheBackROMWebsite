Thoracic Back Pain: Causes, Symptoms and Treatment

Complete guide to upper back pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Oslo Majorstua

The thoracic spine - the middle part of your spine between the neck and lower back - consists of 12 vertebrae that are all connected to the ribs. This area is naturally more stable than the neck and lower back, but this very stability can also make it vulnerable to stiffness and dysfunction.

When problems arise in the interaction between vertebrae, ribs and muscles here, many experience it as a "locked" feeling or constant aching pain. This differs from neck and lower back pain in that the pain often radiates forward towards the chest along the ribs - something that can be alarming but usually has a harmless explanation in the musculoskeletal system.

Key Facts

  • Thoracic back pain affects most people at some point - 15-35% experience it annually
  • Most common cause: Muscle and joint problems (over 90% of cases)
  • Stress and breathing patterns often play a key role
  • A chiropractor can help with examination and treatment without GP referral
  • Most experience improvement after 3-6 treatments combined with exercises
  • Call 113 for chest pain with breathing difficulties, arm/jaw radiation, or dizziness

How Common is Thoracic Back Pain?

Thoracic back pain is less studied than neck and lower back pain, but research shows it affects many people. A systematic review from 2009 (Briggs et al., BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders) found:

  • Lifetime prevalence: 15-20% experience thoracic back pain at some point
  • Annual incidence: 3.5-35% report thoracic back pain within a year
  • Higher prevalence in children/adolescents and women

Symptoms of Thoracic Back Pain

Symptoms vary depending on the underlying cause, but the most common are:

  • Aching pain between the shoulder blades - Often worst after prolonged sitting or one-sided work. May feel like tiredness or a burning sensation in the muscles.
  • Sharp pain with movement or breathing - Typical for rib dysfunction or irritation in the joints between ribs and spine. Can make it painful to take deep breaths.
  • Morning stiffness - Feeling of reduced mobility, especially with rotation. Often improves after you've moved around a bit.
  • Radiation along the ribs - Pain that follows the rib course from the back and forward towards the sternum. This can be frightening but usually has a harmless cause.
  • Feeling of not getting enough air - When the thoracic spine is stiff, rib movement is restricted. This can give a feeling of tightness in the chest without anything being wrong with the lungs.

Causes of Thoracic Back Pain

The vast majority of thoracic back pain is caused by problems in muscles, joints or connective tissue - so-called musculoskeletal complaints. Here I review the causes from most to least common:

Common Causes (90%+ of cases)

Prolonged Sitting and "Office Back"

People sit more than ever. Studies show that prolonged sitting (over 7 hours daily) is associated with reduced mobility in the thoracic spine and increased risk of pain. A forward-leaning posture in front of a PC or mobile phone significantly increases the load on the upper back muscles.

Among office workers, up to 34% report upper back complaints within a year (Briggs et al., 2009).

Stress and Breathing Patterns - The "Invisible" Cause

Stress often manifests physically in the body. With high stress, breathing patterns change to become more shallow - so-called chest breathing. This overloads the accessory muscles in the neck and thoracic spine.

The result is a vicious cycle: Tension leads to pain, pain leads to altered breathing patterns, altered breathing leads to more tension.

Joint Dysfunction (Facet Joints and Costovertebral Joints)

Stiff or irritated joints in the spine or where the ribs attach can cause sharp, localised pain. Many call this "rib lock" - a condition that responds well to treatment.

Posture-Related Problems

Increased kyphosis (forward-leaning posture) puts unnecessary strain on the thoracic spine. Screen work, mobile use and prolonged sitting are common triggering factors.

Less Common Causes

Costochondritis and Tietze's Syndrome

Inflammation of the cartilage connecting the ribs to the sternum. Causes pain at the front of the chest that can mimic heart disease, but has a musculoskeletal cause.

Scheuermann's Disease

A developmental disorder causing increased kyphosis in adolescents. Affects 1-8% of the population. Usually diagnosed between ages 12-17.

T4 Syndrome

Dysfunction in the upper thoracic spine that can cause radiation to arms and hands, often together with numbness or tingling. Usually responds well to manipulation.

Rare Causes (under 1%)

Thoracic Disc Herniation

Accounts for only 0.25-1% of all disc herniations. Most are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on MRI. Symptomatic cases are very rare - about 1 per million per year.

Compression Fractures

Associated with osteoporosis, especially in older women. Can occur after minimal loading or without obvious trauma.

Serious Disease

In very rare cases, thoracic back pain can be caused by infection, tumour or inflammatory disease such as ankylosing spondylitis. These are usually accompanied by other symptoms (see red flags below).

Red Flags - When to Seek Medical Attention

Call 113 or Visit A&E Immediately For:

  • Severe chest pain radiating to left arm, jaw or neck
  • Breathing difficulties, shortness of breath or clammy sweating
  • Sudden, intense "tearing" pain in the back
  • Weakness or paralysis in the legs, loss of bladder/bowel control
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with persistent back pain

Heart Pain vs. Rib Pain - How to Tell the Difference

Heart pain: Often diffuse/pressing, often comes with exertion, accompanied by sweating, nausea, breathing difficulties. Often radiates to arm, jaw or back. Does NOT change with movement or pressure on the chest.

Rib/muscle pain: Often sharp/stabbing, comes with certain movements or breathing. You can often point to exactly where it hurts. Worsens with pressure on the spot, rotation or deep breathing.

If in doubt? It's always safe to call 113 or A&E for an assessment. Better one trip too many than one too few.

Treatment at the Chiropractor

As a chiropractor, I am a licensed healthcare professional with specialist expertise in musculoskeletal complaints. You can come directly to me without a GP referral.

Examination

The first consultation always starts with a thorough review of your medical history and a clinical examination. I map mobility, muscle tension and neurological conditions to find the cause of the pain and rule out conditions requiring other follow-up.

Treatment Methods

Treatment is tailored to the individual, but may include:

Joint Manipulation/Mobilisation

Specific techniques to restore normal joint function. A systematic review (Gevers-Montoro et al., 2022) shows that thoracic manipulation has significant effect on pain and function compared to mobilisation alone.

Soft Tissue Treatment

Massage, trigger point treatment or instrument-assisted treatment to loosen tight muscles.

Exercises and Guidance

Specific exercises to strengthen and stabilise, as well as advice on ergonomics and work posture.

Breathing Guidance

For stress-related complaints, conscious work on breathing patterns can be an important part of treatment.

What can you expect? Many experience improvement within 3-6 treatments, but this varies based on how long you've had complaints and your general health. It's important to have realistic expectations - some complaints require time and self-effort to improve.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Activity is often the best medicine. Here are three exercises that can relieve stiffness in the thoracic spine:

1. Cat-Camel

Start on all fours. Round your back up while exhaling (like an angry cat), then arch your back while inhaling. Repeat calmly 10-15 times. Good for mobility throughout the spine.

2. Thread the Needle

Lie on your side with bent knees. Stretch your top arm forward, then guide it in an arc over your head and backwards while following your hand with your gaze. Hold for a few seconds and feel the stretch in your chest. 10 repetitions on each side.

3. Thoracic Extension Over Foam Roller

Lie with your thoracic spine over a foam roller (avoid the lower back). Hold your hands behind your head and lean back gently. Roll carefully back and forth. 1-2 minutes daily.

Tips

Do the exercises daily for 2-3 weeks for best effect. Stop if the exercises worsen your pain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it dangerous when my back cracks?

No. The sound during manipulation is due to the release of gas bubbles in the joint fluid - completely harmless and usually painless.

Can thoracic back pain come from the heart?

Pain from the heart can be felt in the chest and back, but is usually accompanied by other symptoms such as breathing difficulties, nausea or radiation to arm/jaw. When in doubt, contact a doctor.

Do I need X-ray or MRI?

Rarely. Imaging is only necessary when there's suspicion of serious underlying cause (red flags). For most musculoskeletal complaints, clinical examination provides sufficient information.

How long do the complaints last?

Acute complaints often improve within 2-6 weeks. For chronic complaints (over 12 weeks), it may take longer, and a combination of treatment and self-effort is important.

Can stress cause back pain?

Yes, absolutely. Mental stress leads to increased muscle tension and altered breathing patterns, which in turn can cause physical pain in the neck and thoracic spine. The connection is well documented.

Should I rest or be active?

Moderate activity is usually better than bed rest. Movement promotes blood circulation and prevents muscles from stiffening further.
