Thoracic Pain: Causes, Symptoms and Treatment

Chiropractor Mads Finstad | TheBackROM, Majorstua, Oslo

Quick Summary

  • Thoracic pain affects most people at some point in life - 15-35% experience this annually
  • Most common cause: Muscle and joint problems (over 90% of cases)
  • Stress and breathing patterns often play a key role - the body "remembers" tension
  • A chiropractor can help with examination and treatment without a doctor's referral
  • Many experience imprøvement after 3-6 treatments combined with self-exercises
  • Call emergency services for chest pain with breathing difficulties, radiation to arm/jaw, or dizziness

The thoracic spine - the middle part of the spine between the neck and lower back - consists of 12 vertebrae, all connected to the ribs. This area is naturally more stable than the neck and lower back, but this very stability can also make it vulnerable to stiffness and functional problems.

When dysfunction occurs in the interaction between vertebrae, ribs and muscles here, many experience it as a "locked" feeling or a constant aching pain. This differs from neck and lower back pain in that the pain can often radiate forward towards the chest along the ribs - which can be frightening, but usually has a harmless explanation in the musculoskeletal system.

Symptoms of Thoracic Pain

Symptoms vary depending on the underlying cause, but the most common are:

  • Aching pain between the shoulder blades - Often worst after prolonged sitting or one-sided work. Can feel like fatigue or a burning sensation in the muscles.
  • Sharp pain with movement or breathing - Typical for rib joint dysfunction or irritation in the joints between ribs and spine. Can make deep breaths painful.
  • Morning stiffness - Feeling of reduced mobility, especially with rotation. Often imprøves after some movement.
  • Radiation along the ribs - Pain that follows the rib's course from the back forward towards the sternum. This can be frightening but usually has a harmless cause.
  • Feeling of "not getting enough air" - When the thoracic spine is stiff, rib movement is restricted. This can give a feeling of tightness in the chest without anything being wrong with the lungs.

Causes of Thoracic Pain

The vast majority of thoracic pain is caused by problems in muscles, joints or connective tissue - so-called musculoskeletal complaints.

Common causes (90%+ of cases)

Sedentary lifestyle and "desk back"

People sit more than ever. Studies show that prolonged sitting (over 7 hours daily) is associated with reduced mobility in the thoracic spine and increased risk of pain. A forward-leaning posture in front of a PC or mobile significantly increases the load on the upper back muscles.

Among office workers, up to 34% report upper back complaints within a year.

Stress and breathing pattern - the "invisible" cause

Stress often manifests physically in the body. Under high stress, breathing patterns become more shallow - so-called chest breathing. This overloads the accessory muscles in the neck and thoracic spine.

The result is a vicious cycle: Tension leads to pain ? Pain leads to altered breathing pattern ? Altered breathing causes more tension.

Joint dysfunction (facet joints and costovertebral joints)

Stiff or irritated joints in the spine or where the ribs attach can cause sharp, localized pain. Many call this "rib lock" - a condition that responds well to treatment.

Posture-related complaints

Increased kyphosis (forward-leaning posture) places unnecessary strain on the thoracic spine. Screen work, mobile use and sedentary lifestyle are common triggers.

Red Flags - When to Seek Medical Help Immediately

Call emergency services or go to A&E immediately for:

  • Severe chest pain radiating to left arm, jaw or throat
  • Breathing difficulties, shortness of breath or clammy sweating
  • Sudden, intense "tearing" pain in the back
  • Weakness or paralysis in legs, loss of bladder/bowel control
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with persistent back pain

How Can Chiropractic Help?

As a chiropractor, I am a licensed healthcare professional with specialized competence in musculoskeletal complaints. You can come directly to me without a referral from a doctor.

Examination

The first consultation always starts with a thorough review of your medical history and a clinical examination. I map mobility, muscle tension and neurological conditions to find the cause of the pain and rule out conditions requiring other follow-up.

Treatment methods

Treatment is tailored to the individual, but may include:

Joint manipulation/mobilization

Specific techniques to restore normal joint function. A systematic review shows that thoracic manipulation has significant effect on pain and function compared to mobilization alone.

Soft tissue treatment

Massage, trigger point treatment or instrument-assisted treatment to loosen tight muscles.

Exercises and guidance

Specific exercises to strengthen and stabilize, as well as advice on ergonomics and work posture.

What can you expect? Many experience imprøvement within 3-6 treatments, but this varies based on how long you've had complaints and your general health. Relapses can occur, especially if underlying factors like work posture or stress are not addressed.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Activity is often the best medicine. Here are three exercises that can relieve stiffness in the thoracic spine:

1. Cat and Cow (Cat-Camel)

Get on all fours. Arch your back while exhaling (like an angry cat), and sway your back while inhaling. Repeat calmly 10-15 times. Good for mobility throughout the spine.

2. Thread the Needle

Lie on your side with bent knees. Extend your upper arm forward, then move it in an arc over your head and backward while following your hand with your gaze. Hold for a few seconds and feel the stretch in your chest. 10 repetitions on each side.

3. Thoracic extension over foam roller

Lie with your thoracic spine over a foam roller (avoid the lower back). Hold your hands behind your head and lean back gently. Roll slowly back and forth. 1-2 minutes daily.

Tip

Do the exercises daily for 2-3 weeks for best effect. Stop if the exercises worsen the pain.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it dangerous when my back cracks?

No. The sound from manipulation is caused by the release of gas bubbles in the joint fluid - completely harmless and usually painless.

Can thoracic pain come from the heart?

Pain from the heart can be felt in the chest and back, but is usually accompanied by other symptoms such as breathing difficulties, nausea or radiation to arm/jaw. If in doubt, contact a doctor.

Do I need X-ray or MRI?

Rarely. Imaging is only necessary if there is suspicion of a serious underlying cause (red flags). For most musculoskeletal complaints, clinical examination provides sufficient information.

How long do the symptoms last?

Acute complaints often imprøve within 2-6 weeks. For chronic complaints (over 12 weeks), it may take longer, and a combination of treatment and self-effort is important.

Can stress cause back pain?

Yes, absolutely. Mental stress leads to increased muscle tension and altered breathing patterns, which in turn can cause physical pain in the neck and thoracic spine. The connection is well documented.
