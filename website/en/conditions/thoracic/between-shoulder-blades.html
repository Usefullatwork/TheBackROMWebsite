Pain Between Shoulder Blades

Causes, symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Pain between the shoulder blades - what is called interscapular pain - is a common complaint that affects many people during their lifetime. This is one of the many conditions affecting the thoracic spine region. The area between the shoulder blades is a complex anatomical region where many structures can contribute to discomfort: muscles like the trapezius and rhomboid, facet joints in the thoracic and cervical vertebrae, nerves, and even internal organs.

A systematic review by Briggs and colleagues (BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders, 2009) found that the annual prevalence of chest pain is 15-27% in the general population. Women are affected more often than men - a French study showed 17% versus 9% (Roquelaure, Arthritis Care & Research, 2015). Office workers have particularly high risk, with prevalence up to 28% (Janwantanakul, 2008).

Brief Summary

  • What it is: Pain in the area between the shoulder blades, often related to muscle or joint problems in the chest or neck.
  • Most common causes: Muscle tension and trigger points (most common), referred pain from the neck, thoracic facet joints, posture-related problems.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence for short-term effect of manipulation combined with exercise.
  • Red flags: Sudden tearing pain, chest pain, breathing difficulties, arm numbness - require medical evaluation.
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up.

Causes and Risk Factors

The causes of pain between the shoulder blades can be divided from most common to rarest:

Muscular causes (most common)

Myofascial pain with trigger points

Myofascial pain with trigger points in the musculature is by far the most common cause of acute pain between the shoulder blades. A study in Pain Medicine (2016) found that 93% of patients with chronic neck pain had trigger points in the trapezius.

The muscles between the shoulder blades - especially the rhomboids and middle trapezius - often become overloaded from prolonged sitting with forward-leaning posture.

Referred pain from the neck

Cervicogenic pain

French orthopedist Robert Maigne described how up to 70% of "common" pain between the shoulder blades actually originates from the lower neck (C5-C7). The facet joints in the neck can refer pain to the area along the medial border of the shoulder blade.

This explains why many experience improvement in shoulder blade pain when the neck is treated.

Thoracic facet joints

Facet joint problems

Facet joints in the chest contribute to 34-48% of chronic chest pain according to studies with diagnostic blocks (Manchikanti, Pain Physician, 2004; 2020). These small joints between the vertebrae can become irritated or worn, especially with repetitive movements or prolonged poor posture. In more severe cases, it may involve thoracic disc herniation.

Dreyfuss and colleagues (Spine, 1994) mapped how these joints typically produce pain one segment down and slightly to the side.

Posture-related problems

Upper crossed syndrome

Vladimir Janda described in the 1980s what he called "upper crossed syndrome": an imbalance where anterior muscles (chest, neck) become tight while posterior muscles (rhomboids, middle trapezius) become weak and stretched. This pattern can be reinforced by rounded back (hyperkyphosis) or conversely by flat back (hypokyphosis).

This pattern is often seen in office workers and creates increased stress in the area between the shoulder blades.

Dorsal scapular nerve

An often overlooked cause

Sultan and colleagues (Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation, 2013) found that 52-62% of patients with unilateral pain between the shoulder blades had signs of involvement of this nerve. The nerve can become trapped where it passes through the musculature in the neck.

Visceral referred pain (less common)

Internal organs can refer pain

Internal organs can refer pain to the area between the shoulder blades. Gallbladder problems typically produce pain to the right shoulder blade, while the pancreas can produce pain to the left. Heart attack - especially in women - can present with pain between the shoulder blades rather than classic chest pain. For chest pain in front, see also costochondritis.

Serious pathology (rare)

About 1% of chest pain is due to serious underlying disease. The chest, however, has a higher probability of serious pathology than the neck or lower back, making good screening important. In elderly patients, osteoporosis and compression fractures should always be considered, as well as Scheuermann's disease in younger people.

2

Symptoms

Typical presentation varies depending on the cause:

With muscular causes

  • Dull, aching pain that is difficult to localize precisely
  • Worsening with prolonged sitting or repetitive movements
  • Tender points in the musculature that can trigger familiar pain when pressed
  • Relief with movement and stretching

With facet joint problems

  • Morning stiffness that improves after 30-60 minutes of movement
  • Worsening with backward bending and rotation
  • Pain stays on one side and does not cross the midline
  • May radiate along the ribs toward the side

With posture-related problems

  • Gradually increasing discomfort throughout the workday
  • Feeling of "fatigue" or "heaviness" between the shoulder blades
  • Improvement on weekends and holidays
  • Often combined with neck stiffness and headache (see T4 syndrome)
3

Diagnosis

A thorough clinical examination is far more important than imaging for most people with pain between the shoulder blades.

Chiropractic examination

The chiropractor conducts a systematic examination that includes detailed history, assessment of range of motion in the neck and chest, specific tests for facet joints and musculature, and neurological screening. Palpation (examination with hands) often reveals tender points, tight muscles or joints with reduced mobility.

Imaging is rarely necessary

MRI and X-ray often show age-related changes - such as disc degeneration or facet joint arthritis - in people without pain. A study found that 89% of people between 60 and 69 years have facet joint arthritis on MRI without having symptoms (Wood and colleagues).

The images can therefore be misleading. Clinical examination often provides more relevant information than what the images can tell.

Imaging is indicated when there is suspicion of serious pathology, after trauma, or with neurological deficits that do not improve.

4

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

Chiropractic treatment of pain between the shoulder blades is usually comprehensive and tailored to the individual's situation.

Manipulation and mobilization

A systematic review and meta-analysis by Masaracchio and colleagues (PLOS ONE, 2019) found that thoracic manipulation provided significantly better pain relief than standard treatment in the short term, with an average improvement of 13 points on a 100-point scale. Compared to placebo, the effect was less clear. The evidence level is rated as moderate.

Soft tissue treatment

Techniques such as trigger point treatment, dry needling and instrument-assisted soft tissue treatment (like Graston) are used to loosen tight muscles and reduce trigger points in the area.

Combination treatment gives best results: Hidalgo and colleagues found moderate to strong evidence that the combination of manual treatment and exercise gives better results than each intervention alone.

Important: Early treatment

Early treatment is associated with better outcomes. Pain that has lasted more than three months before starting treatment gives significantly worse prognosis (Knecht, 2020).

What can you do yourself?

  • Regular movement: Avoid prolonged sitting which can lead to stiff back. Get up and move at least every 20-30 minutes. Cornell University research shows that seated work increases the load on back muscles by 90% compared to standing.
  • Chest muscle stretching: Tight chest muscles pull the shoulders forward and overload the muscles between the shoulder blades. Daily stretching of the pectoralis can help.
  • Strength training for shoulder blade muscles: A meta-analysis in Frontiers in Neurology (2024) found moderate evidence that exercises strengthening the muscles around the shoulder blade provide pain relief.
  • Workplace ergonomics: Good lumbar support, screen at eye level, and keyboard/mouse at the right height reduce stress on the chest and shoulders.
  • Breathing exercises and stress management: Can be especially important for stress-related back pain.
  • Heat: Heat pack or hot shower can provide temporary relief for muscle tension.

Treatment course

A dose-response study by Haas and colleagues (2014) with 400 participants found that optimal treatment frequency for back and neck pain is around 12 treatments over 6 weeks. In practice, this means typically 2-3 treatments per week for the first weeks, then gradual reduction.

For acute cases (under 6 weeks duration) I often see rapid improvement. Chronic cases (over 12 weeks) usually require longer treatment courses and more focus on self-exercise and lifestyle changes.

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Seek medical help immediately for the following symptoms:

  • Sudden, intense "tearing" or "cutting" pain - may indicate aortic dissection, an acute life-threatening condition
  • Chest pain, breathing difficulties, cold sweats - especially in women, heart attacks often present with back pain rather than classic chest pain
  • Unexplained weight loss, fever or night sweats
  • Pain that wakes you at night and does not improve with position change
  • Previous cancer history
  • Numbness, tingling or weakness in the arms
  • Pain that worsens with eating - may indicate gallbladder or pancreatic problems
6

Prognosis

The outlook for most people with pain between the shoulder blades is good. Knecht and colleagues (BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders, 2020) followed 90 patients with chest pain for one year and found that 80% had a favorable course with rapid improvement within the first month.

Recurrence is common

At the same time, research shows that recurrence is common. Studies on back pain in general show that 24-80% experience at least one recurrence within 12 months (da Silva, Journal of Physiotherapy, 2019). Previous episodes increase the risk of new ones - if you have had more than two episodes, the chance of recurrence triples.

The single most important factor for prognosis is how long you have had the symptoms. Pain that has lasted more than three months at the start of treatment gives significantly worse outlook. This underscores the value of early intervention.

7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can pain between the shoulder blades come from the heart?

Yes, especially in women. Studies show that 85% of women with heart attacks have atypical symptoms, including pain between the shoulder blades. If you experience sudden pain combined with breathing difficulties, nausea or cold sweats, you should seek emergency help.

Do I need X-ray or MRI?

Usually not. Imaging is rarely necessary for common pain between the shoulder blades and often shows "normal" age-related changes that don't explain the symptoms. Clinical examination often provides more useful information.

How long does it take to get better?

Most people with acute symptoms experience significant improvement within 4-6 weeks. Chronic cases may require 8-12 weeks of structured treatment. About 80% follow a favorable course with conservative treatment.

Can poor posture cause pain between the shoulder blades?

Prolonged forward-leaning posture overloads the muscles between the shoulder blades and is a common contributing factor. However, the relationship between posture and pain is complex - many people with "poor" posture have no symptoms. See also our articles on rounded back and flat back.

Can a chiropractor help with pain between the shoulder blades?

Yes, chiropractic treatment has moderate evidence for short-term effect on chest pain. The combination of manual treatment, soft tissue work and targeted exercise often gives good results.

Should I avoid activity?

No, it is generally recommended to stay active. Prolonged rest can actually worsen the symptoms. Adjust the activity level to the pain, but avoid total inactivity.
