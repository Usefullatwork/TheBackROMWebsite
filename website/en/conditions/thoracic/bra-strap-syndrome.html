Bra Strap Syndrome and Shoulder Pain

Causes, symptoms and treatment in women | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Bra strap syndrome is a form of costoclavicular syndrome - a condition where bra straps compress the nerve plexus (brachial plexus) and blood vessels between the collarbone and first rib. When narrow straps bear the weight of the breasts hour after hour, day after day, the pressure can damage both nerves and muscle tissue.

The condition is underdiagnosed because many - including healthcare professionals - don't think of the bra as a possible cause of shoulder and neck problems. The typical sign is visible grooves or marks in the skin where the straps sit.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Condition where bra straps compress nerves and muscles over the shoulders
  • Most common causes: Wrong bra size (80% wear wrong size), large breasts (D-cup+), narrow straps
  • 59% of women report bra-related back, shoulder or neck pain
  • Chiropractic treatment: Soft tissue treatment, joint mobilization, exercise instruction
  • Red flags: One-sided arm weakness, unexplained weight loss, night pain, fever

When should you seek help immediately?

For one-sided arm weakness, unexplained weight loss, night pain that wakes you, or fever with pain - contact a doctor.

1

Who is affected by bra strap syndrome?

Bra strap syndrome primarily affects women with larger breasts who wear bras daily over extended periods. The condition is especially common in women with D-cup or larger.

59% of women report bra-related pain
80% wear wrong bra size
12-14 h daily bra use for most

Rheumatologist Silva described the typical skin grooves as "the most important clue to diagnosis" as early as 1986 (Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases).

2

Causes and Risk Factors

From most common to least common:

Wrong Bra Size (most common)

A full 80% of women wear the wrong size - 70% too small, 10% too large (Greenbaum et al., 2003). When the band is too large, more weight ends up on the straps.

Large Breasts (D-cup or larger)

Studies show a significant correlation between cup size and shoulder/neck pain (p<0.05). Women with the largest breasts have up to 12 times higher odds of back pain.

Narrow Bra Straps

Research from University of Wollongong (2015) shows that wide straps (4.5 cm) reduce peak pressure by approximately 35% compared to standard 2.5 cm straps.

Other Factors

  • Long daily wear time: Most women wear bras 12-14 hours daily. Pressure of just 9 kPa over 2 hours can cause skin and muscle damage.
  • Poor posture: Rounded shoulders increase the load on the straps and can increase compression.
  • Racerback bras: Bring straps closer to the neck and can increase pressure on sensitive areas.
3

Symptoms

The typical symptom pattern has a characteristic pattern:

Typical Symptoms

  • Shoulder and neck pain - often on both sides, but can be worse on one side
  • Pain is minimal in the morning and gradually worsens throughout the day
  • Relief when the bra is removed - this is an important diagnostic sign
  • Numbness or tingling down the arm (due to nerve compression)
  • Visible grooves or marks where the straps sit
  • Headache - often from the back of the head and upward
  • Fatigue in the shoulders, especially during prolonged standing or carrying bags

Important Diagnostic Sign

Symptom relief when the bra is removed, and worsening throughout the day, distinguishes bra strap syndrome from other shoulder/neck problems.

4

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is made primarily through clinical examination. The medical history is often revealing: pain that varies with bra use, visible strap grooves, and improvement on weekends or vacations when the patient wears a bra less.

Differential Diagnoses to Consider

Condition Characteristics Distinguished by
Cervical Radiculopathy Typically one-sided, follows dermatome Positive Spurling's test, reflex changes
Thoracic Outlet Syndrome / T4 Syndrome Similar symptoms, often one-sided No visible strap grooves, vascular symptoms
Rotator Cuff Problems Painful arc, weakness in certain movements Positive impingement tests, pain with active movements

About Imaging

MRI and X-rays are rarely necessary for bra strap syndrome. These examinations often show "normal findings" like degenerative changes that don't explain the pain. Clinical examination - focusing on strap grooves, symptom patterns and response to bra removal - is more important than images.

5

Treatment

What can the chiropractor do?

There is limited research on manual treatment specifically for bra strap syndrome. However, chiropractic treatment can address the secondary muscle and joint problems that often accompany it:

Chiropractic Treatment

  • Soft tissue treatment: Massage and trigger point treatment of trapezius and neck muscles can relieve tension.
  • Joint mobilization: Treatment of stiff joints in the neck and upper back can reduce reflex tension.
  • Posture guidance: Advice on work position and ergonomics.
  • Exercise instruction: Strengthening exercises for the upper back and shoulder blade stabilization.

Important

Manual treatment alone rarely solves the problem if bra fit isn't changed. A study from Mayo Clinic found that conservative treatment including physical therapy and weight loss "did not provide sufficient lasting relief" in women with symptomatic macromastia.

What can you do yourself?

The most important single factor is often finding the right bra:

Bra Fitting

  • Get a professional bra fitting: Many stores offer free fitting. With 80% wearing the wrong size, chances are your current bra doesn't fit optimally.
  • Choose wide straps: Straps at least 4-4.5 cm distribute pressure better than narrow straps.
  • Prioritize support from the band: Ideally, 80% of support should come from the band around the chest, not from the straps.
  • Avoid racerback bras: These bring straps closer to the neck and can increase pressure.

Lifestyle Measures

  • Take breaks: If possible, remove the bra for periods throughout the day, especially at home.
  • Strength training: Exercises for the upper back and shoulder blades can improve posture and reduce strain.

Treatment Course

With bra strap syndrome, the course is often different from other musculoskeletal problems. Many experience significant improvement within 1-2 weeks after switching to the right bra. Trigger point pain and muscle tension can often be relieved with 3-4 treatments.

6

Prognosis

The prognosis for bra strap syndrome is generally good, provided the underlying cause is addressed:

1-2 weeks to improvement with right bra
3-4 treatments for muscle tension
71% improvement after breast reduction

Long-term

  • With bra change: Many experience noticeable improvement within 1-2 weeks.
  • With manual treatment: Muscle tension and trigger points can often be resolved in 3-4 treatments.
  • Long-term: With proper bra fit and posture work, problems can be kept under permanent control.
  • Relapse: Is common if returning to poorly fitting bras.
  • For severe macromastia: For women where conservative treatment doesn't provide lasting relief, breast reduction may be relevant. A meta-analysis from Mayo Clinic showed that 71% experienced significant improvement in shoulder pain after surgery.
7

When should you seek help?

Red flags - see a doctor if you experience:

  • One-sided arm weakness or increasing numbness
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Fever with neck/shoulder pain
  • Previous cancer
  • Bladder or bowel disturbances along with pain

Planned Assessment

  • Persistent shoulder/neck pain affecting function
  • Visible strap grooves that don't improve
  • Pain affecting sleep and daily activities

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if I'm wearing the wrong bra size?
Signs of wrong size include: the band rides up at the back, straps slide off or dig in, breast tissue "spills over" the cup, or you constantly adjust your bra throughout the day. With 80% wearing the wrong size, a professional fitting is worth trying.
Can a chiropractor help with bra strap syndrome?
Yes, the chiropractor can treat the secondary muscle and joint problems that develop. Soft tissue treatment, joint mobilization and exercise instruction can provide relief. But the most important measure is often correcting bra fit.
How wide should bra straps be?
Research from University of Wollongong (2015) shows that 4.5 cm straps provide approximately 35% lower peak pressure than standard 2.5 cm straps. For women with D-cup or larger, wide, padded straps are recommended.
Is breast reduction covered by public healthcare?
In Norway, breast reduction can be covered through the public healthcare system for documented problems. Criteria usually include BMI under 27, breast volume over 800 ml per breast, and persistent pain. Referral goes through GP to plastic surgeon.
Why is pain worse at the end of the day?
This is typical for bra strap syndrome. Cumulative load throughout the day - often 12-14 hours - causes progressive muscle fatigue and increasing nerve compression. This distinguishes the condition from, for example, cervical radiculopathy, which doesn't have the same time pattern.
Can weight loss help?
Weight loss can reduce breast volume and thus the load. But studies show this is rarely sufficient alone for symptomatic macromastia. A combination of weight control, proper bra and possibly strength training often gives the best results.
