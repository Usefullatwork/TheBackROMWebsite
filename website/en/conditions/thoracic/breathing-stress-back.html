Breathing, Stress and Back

How posture affects your breathing and stress levels | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

"I feel like I never breathe all the way out." This is a sentence I hear surprisingly often at the clinic. The patient has usually been to the doctor, had an ECG and lung function tests - all normal. But the feeling of not getting enough air persists.

The explanation often lies not in the lungs or heart, but in the thoracic spine and ribs. Norway has the world's highest prevalence of neck pain according to the Global Burden of Disease Study (Safiri et al., 2020). Combined with the fact that we spend increasingly more hours in front of screens, this has created a wave of posture-related complaints that affect everything from breathing to digestion and stress levels.

Brief Summary

  • What it's about: A stiff thoracic spine and poor posture can reduce diaphragm movement by up to 35%, affecting both breathing and stress levels
  • Most common causes: Prolonged sitting, forward head posture (phone use), stress/hyperventilation, muscular tension
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence for thoracic mobilization; strong evidence for breathing exercises as a supplement
  • Red flags: Sudden breathing difficulties, chest pain, leg swelling, persistent cough
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

When should you seek immediate help?

For sudden breathing difficulties, chest pain radiating to arm/jaw, or blue discoloration of lips - call 113 or go to the emergency room.

1

How Posture Affects Your Breathing

The Diaphragm - The Main Breathing Muscle

The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle that separates the chest cavity from the abdominal cavity. During inhalation, it contracts and descends downward. This creates negative pressure in the lungs, and air flows in. At the same time, the external intercostal muscles (between the ribs) lift the rib cage outward.

For this to function optimally, the thoracic spine must be mobile. The ribs attach to the vertebrae at the back and the sternum at the front. When the thoracic spine becomes stiff - which happens with prolonged forward-leaning posture - the ribs cannot rotate freely. With rib dislocation or costochondritis, this can become even more apparent.

The Numbers Behind the Problem

A Brazilian study (Goncalves et al., 2018) measured diaphragm movement in people with and without hyperkyphosis (excessive curvature in the thoracic spine, often called rounded back). The result:

35% reduced diaphragmatic movement

In those with a stiff, curved back compared to the control group

The famous Framingham Study (Lorbergs et al., 2017) followed 275 people over 16 years and found that women with the most pronounced kyphosis lost 100 ml more lung capacity than those with normal posture - roughly equivalent to the effect of light smoking.

A systematic review from 2024 showed that forward head posture alone reduces forced vital capacity (FVC) by 0.25-0.81 liters.

2

When You Don't Breathe With Your Belly

Dysfunctional Breathing Is More Common Than You Think

Approximately 6-10% of the general population has dysfunctional breathing (Thomas et al., 2005; Boulding et al., 2016). Among asthma patients, this rises to 29-47%.

Dysfunctional breathing means you use the wrong muscles. Instead of letting the diaphragm do the work, the body recruits accessory muscles in the neck and shoulders - muscles that are actually meant for short-term effort.

The Vicious Cycle

Accessory muscles overworked
→
Neck and shoulder pain
→
Stress response increases
→
Breathing becomes more shallow

Signs That You're Breathing Wrong

  • Shoulders rise during inhalation (instead of the belly expanding)
  • Feeling of never getting enough air, even at rest
  • Tension in neck and shoulders
  • Frequent sighing or yawning
  • Fatigue that isn't explained by lack of sleep
3

Breathing, Stress and the Autonomic Nervous System

Sympathetic Activation From the Thoracic Spine

The sympathetic nervous system - the part that controls the "fight or flight" response - has its nerve centers along the thoracic spine from T1 to T12. This has led to theories that stiffness in the thoracic spine can "irritate" these structures and contribute to chronic stress activation. With T4 syndrome, autonomic symptoms are often seen.

However, one must be honest about the evidence

The most comprehensive review (Kovanur Sampath et al., 2024) analyzed 14 studies with 618 participants and concluded that there is low quality evidence that spinal manipulation affects the autonomic nervous system. Measurements of heart rate variability, blood pressure, and stress hormones showed no consistent effect.

This doesn't mean the connection between posture and stress is imagined - just that the mechanism is probably more complex than direct nerve stimulation.

Hyperventilation and Exhaustion

A study from Karolinska Institute (Ristiniemi et al., 2014) found a strong correlation between hyperventilation and exhaustion syndrome (r=0.77). People with exhaustion had significantly higher scores on the Nijmegen Questionnaire for hyperventilation (26.57 vs. 15.14 in healthy individuals).

This aligns with clinical experience: Chronic stress changes breathing patterns, which in turn worsens the stress response.

4

The Jaw and Posture Are Connected

What Does the Research Show?

Numbers have circulated that "49% of people with poor posture have jaw problems." I found no scientific support for this after extensive searching.

What is actually documented (Xiao et al., 2023) is a weak but statistically significant correlation between forward head posture and temporomandibular disorders (TMD). The correlation is r=0.16-0.23 - thus much weaker than the viral claims.

More interesting is that 88.5% of TMD patients have at least one postural deviation (Di Francesco et al., 2023). Forward head posture is 2.4 times more common in people with muscular TMD than in controls (Cortese et al., 2017).

What Does This Mean in Practice?

The connection is likely bidirectional: Poor posture can contribute to jaw tension, and jaw tension can affect neck and head posture. For many patients, it makes sense to treat both simultaneously.

5

Digestion and the Diaphragm - An Overlooked Connection

Reflux and Breathing Function

Here the evidence is surprisingly strong. The diaphragm contributes approximately 85% of the pressure function at the junction between the esophagus and stomach (Mittal, 1993). When the diaphragm functions poorly, the risk of acid reflux increases.

A study on patients with diaphragmatic paresis (Kaufman et al., 2022) found that 26.3% had GERD (gastroesophageal reflux disease). With bilateral paresis, this rose to 36.8%.

Breathing Training for Reflux - Does It Work?

Yes, actually better than many think. Several randomized controlled trials show convincing results:

Eherer et al. (2012)

30 minutes of daily belly breathing for 4 weeks reduced acid exposure from 9.1% to 4.7%. At 9-month follow-up, participants had reduced PPI use to one-third.

Sun et al. (2016)

After 6 months of diaphragm biofeedback, 82% had stopped acid-suppressing medications (vs. 6% in the control group).

Halland et al. (2021)

Diaphragmatic breathing increased pressure in the lower esophageal sphincter from 23.1 to 42.2 mmHg.

6

Breathing Exercises That Actually Work

What Does the Research Say?

A meta-analysis from 2023 (Fincham et al., Scientific Reports) analyzed 12 randomized studies with 785 participants. Conclusion: Breathing exercises provide significant stress reduction with an effect size of Hedges' g = -0.35 compared to control groups.

The Stanford study (Yilmaz Balban et al., 2023) compared four techniques in 108 participants over 28 days. The surprise: Cyclic sighing (extended exhalation) was more effective than mindfulness meditation for improving mood and reducing stress.

Practical Techniques

Diaphragmatic Breathing

Place one hand on your belly and one on your chest. Breathe in through your nose so that your belly rises while your chest stays relatively still. Breathe out slowly through your mouth.

Frequency: 4-6 breaths per minute

Duration: Minimum 5 minutes

Frequency: Daily, preferably several short sessions

Cyclic Sighing (from the Stanford Study)

  1. Breathe in through your nose until your lungs feel full
  2. Take another small breath to fill up completely
  3. Breathe out slowly and completely through your mouth
  4. Repeat for 5 minutes

4-4-4-4 (Box Breathing)

Breathe in for 4 seconds, hold for 4 seconds, breathe out for 4 seconds, hold for 4 seconds. Repeat.

This technique is used by US Navy SEALs for stress management, but the Stanford study showed that cyclic sighing was more effective.

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Thoracic Mobilization

Treatment of stiffness in the thoracic spine and rib joints can help restore normal movement. This includes mobilization of the costovertebral joints (where the ribs meet the vertebrae) and general thoracic mobilization. Read more about thoracic pain and treatment options.

Note

The evidence that spinal treatment directly affects autonomic functions is limited (as discussed above). But many patients experience that better mobility in the thoracic spine makes it easier to breathe deeply and relaxed.

Posture Guidance

An important part of treatment is awareness about work position, phone use, and everyday habits. Simple adjustments can have a big effect over time.

Breathing Training as Part of Treatment

Chiropractors with competence in breathing function can guide in diaphragmatic breathing and integrate this into the treatment course. In Norway, there is a tradition of interdisciplinary collaboration - psychomotor physiotherapy (NPMP) has combined breathing, posture, and stress treatment for over 50 years.

What Can You Do Yourself?

Daily Measures

  • Movement breaks: Get up and stretch every 30-45 minutes during sedentary work
  • Phone posture: Hold the phone up instead of bending your neck down
  • Thoracic stretching: Lie over a foam roller or rolled-up towel placed across under the upper back. Let the arms fall out to the sides and breathe deeply for 1-2 minutes
  • Breathing exercises: 5 minutes daily with diaphragmatic breathing or cyclic sighing

Simple Exercises for the Thoracic Spine

Chest Stretch in Doorway

Stand in a doorway with your forearms against the frame. Take a small step forward until you feel a stretch across your chest. Hold 15-30 seconds, repeat 3 times. A study (Tokita et al., 2021) showed that this exercise significantly increases the length of the pectoralis minor and improves shoulder blade position.

Thoracic Rotation

Sit on a chair with hands crossed over your chest. Slowly rotate your upper body to one side, hold for 3 seconds, and rotate to the other. Do 10 repetitions to each side, 2-3 times daily.

9

Red Flags - When You Must See a Doctor

Seek doctor or emergency room for:

  • Sudden, unexplained breathing difficulties
  • Chest pain, especially during exertion or radiating to arm/jaw
  • Coughing up blood
  • Leg swelling combined with heavy breathing
  • Fever and breathing problems
  • Blue discoloration of lips or fingers
  • New-onset palpitations along with dizziness

These symptoms may indicate heart or lung disease and require medical evaluation.

10

Expected Course and Realistic Expectations

Most people with posture-related breathing complaints experience improvement within a few weeks with combined treatment and self-exercises. But honesty is important:

  • Long-standing habits take time to change
  • Relapses are common, especially during periods of increased sitting or stress
  • Continuous maintenance work (exercises, movement habits) is often necessary

Systematic reviews (Gonzalez-Galvez et al., 2019) show that exercise programs can improve thoracic kyphosis by an average of 5-13 degrees over 8-12 weeks. Younger people respond better than older ones. For athletes who experience that stiffness affects performance, read more about stiff back and sports.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can poor posture really affect breathing?
Yes. Studies show that hyperkyphosis reduces diaphragm movement by up to 35% and lung capacity by 0.25-0.81 liters. These are measurable, physiological changes.
Does it help to consciously "straighten up"?
In the short term yes, but active muscle use is tiring. The goal is to improve thoracic mobility so that good posture requires less effort. Training and mobilization are more effective than just "thinking about sitting straight."
Can breathing exercises really reduce stress?
Research shows moderate effect (effect size 0.35). Extended exhalation activates the parasympathetic nervous system and can lower cortisol levels. The effect comes after a few days of regular practice.
How long do I need to do exercises before it helps?
Most studies show effect after 4-8 weeks of regular training (2-3 sessions per week). Breathing exercises can provide immediate relaxation, but lasting changes require time.
Is it dangerous to have a rounded back?
Not necessarily. Many people live well with varying degrees of kyphosis. The problem arises when stiffness limits function or causes symptoms. A flexible kyphosis is less problematic than a rigid one.
Should I get an X-ray or MRI?
Usually not for typical posture-related complaints. Clinical examination often provides sufficient information. Imaging may be relevant if structural causes such as Scheuermann's disease or compression fractures are suspected.
Does posture affect digestion?
Yes, indirectly. The diaphragm contributes to the pressure function at the junction between the esophagus and stomach. Studies show that diaphragmatic breathing training can significantly reduce reflux symptoms.
