Costochondritis and Chest Pain

Treatment at Chiropractor in Oslo | Mads Finstad, Klinikk for alle Majorstua

Chest pain is frightening. Most people who experience pain in the front of their chest immediately think of the heart. Fortunately, the reality is often far less serious: in primary care, 20-50% of all chest pain has musculoskeletal causes (AAFP, 2021). Costochondritis alone explains 13-36% of cases (StatPearls, 2024).

Costochondritis is an inflammatory condition in the cartilage that connects the ribs to the breastbone (sternum). The condition most commonly affects ribs 2-5, and more than 90% of patients have multiple ribs involved.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Inflammation in the cartilage between ribs and breastbone - one of the most common causes of chest pain
  • Common causes: Overexertion (heavy lifting, exercise), prolonged coughing, poor posture, stiffness in the thoracic spine
  • Chiropractic treatment: Limited evidence, but promising results from manual therapy targeting thoracic spine and ribs
  • Red flags: Pressing chest pain with radiation to arm/jaw, difficulty breathing, sweating - call emergency services
  • Prognosis: 90%+ improvement within 3-4 weeks, recurrence rate only 4%

Who is Affected by Costochondritis?

A large Swiss primary care study (Verdon et al., 2007, n=672) found chest wall syndrome in 44.6% of patients with chest pain. German research shows similar figures of 46.6% (Bösner et al., 2010). In other words: muscular causes are far more common than heart disease in most people seeking healthcare for chest pain.

The condition is most common in women over 40 years of age. A study by Disla et al. (1994) found that 69% of patients were women, with an average age around 40-50 years.

Costochondritis vs. Tietze's Syndrome

Many confuse these two conditions. The most important difference is visible swelling: With Tietze's syndrome, you can see a clear swelling over the affected rib - this is absent in costochondritis.

Characteristic Costochondritis Tietze's Syndrome
Swelling No visible swelling Visible, often spindle-shaped
Age group Over 40 years Under 40 years
Number of joints Multiple (>90% of cases) Usually one (70-80%)
Most common ribs 2nd-5th rib 2nd-3rd rib

Causes and Risk Factors

Causes of costochondritis, ranked from most common to rarest:

Common Causes

  • Overexertion and repetitive movements - heavy lifting, intense training, prolonged coughing (most common)
  • Post-viral conditions - often after respiratory infection with prolonged coughing
  • Poor posture - combined with stiffness in the thoracic spine, can also cause pain between the shoulder blades
  • Direct trauma - blow to the chest, falls (see also rib dislocation)
  • Fibromyalgia - increases the risk of costochondritis (less common)
  • Idiopathic - in many cases no clear cause is found

The Connection Between Thoracic Spine and Costochondritis

In clinical practice, I often see that patients with costochondritis have concurrent stiffness in the thoracic spine. Grindstaff et al. (Journal of Manual & Manipulative Therapy, 2010) suggest a biomechanical explanation: when the joints in the back (facet joints and costovertebral joints) become stiff, the joints in the front of the chest must compensate with increased movement. Over time, this can irritate the cartilage.

Biomechanical Connection

The thoracic spine and ribs form a biomechanical ring - movement in one place affects the entire structure. This insight is important for treatment: by restoring normal mobility in the thoracic spine, you can often reduce the load on the cartilage connections in front.

4

Symptoms

Typical symptoms of costochondritis:

  • Sharp, stabbing or aching pain along the breastbone, often on one side
  • Worsening with deep breathing, coughing and sneezing
  • Increased pain with movement of the upper body or arms
  • Local tenderness that can be reproduced by pressing on the cartilage connections
  • Radiation along the ribs to the back, or to arms and shoulders

Pain can vary from mild to intense. Many describe a feeling that "something is stuck" in the chest. Symptoms can last from a few weeks to several months without treatment.

5

Diagnostics

Costochondritis is a clinical diagnosis - there is no blood test or imaging that confirms the condition. The diagnosis is made based on medical history and physical examination.

The Most Important Examination Finding

Pain can be reproduced by pressing on the affected cartilage connections. This is called reproducible tenderness and is characteristic of the condition.

When is Imaging Necessary?

X-ray and MRI are rarely indicated for typical costochondritis. Imaging may be relevant to rule out other causes with atypical symptoms, prolonged course, or suspected underlying disease.

Validated Clinical Tool

Chest Wall Syndrome Clinical Prediction Rule (Gencer et al., BMC Family Practice, 2012) uses six factors to identify chest wall syndrome with 88.6% specificity: localized muscle tension, stabbing pain character, reproducible pain on palpation, absence of cough, pain that is not retrosternal or pressing, and no known coronary disease.

6

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES FOR:

  • Pressing, squeezing chest pain radiating to left arm, jaw or back
  • Difficulty breathing combined with chest pain
  • Sweating, nausea or dizziness together with chest pain
  • Chest pain during exertion that improves at rest
  • Sudden, tearing pain between the shoulder blades

Important to Know

10-15% of patients with acute coronary syndrome may have reproducible chest wall tenderness (Journal of Urgent Care Medicine). Reproducible pain therefore does not completely rule out heart disease.

7

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Manual treatment for costochondritis has limited but promising evidence. The only randomized controlled trial on musculoskeletal chest pain (Stochkendahl et al., Denmark, 2012, n=115) compared chiropractic treatment with self-management. The results showed significant improvement after 4 and 12 weeks in the chiropractic group. A follow-up study (2016) found that chiropractic treatment was cost-effective.

Treatment Targets the Entire Biomechanical Chain

  • Thoracic spine mobilization - restores mobility in the T1-T6 area (related to T4 syndrome)
  • Rib mobilization - treats stiffness in costovertebral and costotransverse joints
  • Soft tissue treatment - releases tension in intercostal muscles and chest muscles
  • Posture guidance - prevents recurrence, important for kyphosis or flat back

A case series (Zaruba & Wilson, International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy, 2017, n=8) on patients with chronic costochondritis (average duration 6.3 months) showed an average pain reduction of 5.1 points on a 10-point scale after an average of 4.8 treatments over 3-4 weeks.

What Can You Do Yourself?

  • Avoid activities that worsen the pain - give the cartilage time to calm down
  • Heat can provide relief - heating pad or hot shower over the sore area
  • Gentle stretching of the chest muscles, for example in a doorway
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) can help in the short term - talk to your doctor/pharmacist
  • Breathing exercises - calm diaphragmatic breathing can reduce tension in the chest

Treatment Course

Based on available literature, you can expect:

  • Acute costochondritis (<6 weeks): Most people experience significant improvement within 2-4 treatments when the thoracic spine is included in treatment.
  • Chronic costochondritis (>12 weeks): The study by Zaruba & Wilson (2017) showed that even long-standing cases (average 6+ months) responded well to 4-5 treatments over 3-4 weeks.
  • Conservative treatment: An observational study (Disla et al., 1994) showed that 91% experienced symptom relief within 3 weeks with rest and anti-inflammatory medication.
8

Prognosis

Costochondritis generally has a good prognosis. Over 90% of patients experience symptom relief within 3-4 weeks with conservative treatment (StatPearls, 2024). Most cases last from a few weeks to 1-2 months.

90%+ improve within 3-4 weeks
4% recurrence risk
50% may have some discomfort after 1 year

Recurrence risk: The study by Disla et al. (1994) found a recurrence rate of only 4% after two years. This is relatively low compared to many other musculoskeletal conditions.

Long-term prognosis: About 50% of patients may still have some discomfort after 1 year, and about 33% still have tenderness on palpation at 12-month follow-up (Medscape; Spalding et al., 2003). Chronic or treatment-resistant cases may require evaluation by a rheumatologist to rule out inflammatory diseases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can costochondritis be dangerous?
No, costochondritis is a benign condition that does not harm the heart or other organs. The challenge is that the symptoms can resemble heart disease, so it's important to rule out serious causes first.
How do you distinguish costochondritis from a heart attack?
Costochondritis typically causes sharp, localized pain that worsens with movement and breathing, and can be reproduced by pressing on the area. Heart attack often causes pressing, diffuse pain with radiation, accompanied by sweating, nausea and difficulty breathing. When in doubt - call emergency services.
Can a chiropractor treat costochondritis?
Yes, a chiropractor can assess and treat costochondritis. Treatment focuses on restoring normal mobility in the thoracic spine and ribs. Studies show promising results, although the evidence base is limited.
How long does it take for costochondritis to resolve?
Most people experience significant improvement within 3-4 weeks with proper treatment. Some cases can last up to 1-2 months, and some may have mild symptoms for up to a year.
Do I need X-ray or MRI?
Usually not. Costochondritis is diagnosed clinically. Imaging is primarily used to rule out other conditions with atypical symptoms or lack of treatment response.
Can costochondritis come back?
The recurrence rate is relatively low (about 4% over two years). Good posture, avoiding overexertion, and maintaining mobility in the thoracic spine can reduce the risk.
What is the difference between costochondritis and Tietze's syndrome?
The main difference is visible swelling, which is only found in Tietze's syndrome. Tietze's most often affects younger patients and usually involves only one joint, while costochondritis affects multiple joints in older patients.
