Fibromyalgia with Chest Pain

Symptoms, treatment and prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition characterized by widespread pain, severe fatigue and sleep disturbances. The condition affects approximately 2.7% of the global population (Heidari et al., Rheumatology International, 2017). Fibromyalgia is one of several conditions that can cause pain in the thoracic spine and chest region.

Women are affected more often than men, at a ratio of approximately 3:1 when modern diagnostic criteria are used (ACR 2016). Typical onset is in the 40s-50s, and women receive the diagnosis on average 9 years earlier than men.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Fibromyalgia is a chronic pain condition where the nervous system becomes oversensitive
  • Chest pain: Affects 29-54% of patients with fibromyalgia
  • Common causes: Central sensitization, sleep disturbances, psychological stress, genetic predisposition
  • Treatment: Exercise has the strongest evidence. Soft tissue treatment can be a supplement
  • Red flags: Chest pain that could be heart or lung-related must always be ruled out first

When should you seek help immediately?

For pressing, squeezing chest pain, pain radiating to the left arm, jaw or neck, or breathing problems along with chest pain - call emergency services or go to the emergency room.

1

Who is affected by fibromyalgia?

Fibromyalgia affects approximately 2.7% of the global population. In Norway, the numbers are uncertain, but an older study from Arendal found a prevalence of 10.5% among women aged 20-49 (Forseth & Gran, Scandinavian Journal of Rheumatology, 1992).

2.7% Affected globally
54% Have chest pain
3:1 Women vs men
2

Chest pain in fibromyalgia

Many people with fibromyalgia experience pain in the chest region. A study in Arthritis Research & Therapy (2011) found that 54% of fibromyalgia patients reported chest pain, compared to only 4% in the control group. Costochondritis-like symptoms occur in approximately 29% (cross-sectional study, 2016).

Why chest pain in fibromyalgia?

This type of chest pain can be frightening because it can resemble heart disease. It is therefore important to have a thorough examination to rule out serious causes.

3

Causes and risk factors

Fibromyalgia has no single known cause. The condition likely involves several factors that together make the nervous system oversensitive. Stress and psychological burden often play a role, which can also affect breathing patterns and cause tension in the thoracic spine.

What happens in the body?

Central sensitization is the core of fibromyalgia. This means that the nervous system amplifies pain signals - like a volume knob turned up too high. Normal touch can be perceived as painful (allodynia), and mild stimuli feel intensely painful (hyperalgesia).

Research has documented the following changes:

  • Elevated substance P (pain-transmitting substance) in spinal fluid
  • Reduced serotonin, noradrenaline and dopamine
  • Weakened natural pain inhibition from the brain
  • Altered activity in brain areas that process pain (Gracely et al., 2002)

Risk factors (ranked by evidence)

A systematic review of 37 prospective cohort studies published in PAIN (Creed, 2020):

  1. Sleep disturbances/non-restorative sleep - strongest evidence
  2. Previous headache and other pain conditions
  3. Depression
  4. Illness behavior (excessive worry about symptoms)
  5. Female sex and middle age (40-60 years)
4

Symptoms

Fibromyalgia causes widespread pain that often varies from day to day. With chest presentation, many experience:

Chest symptoms

  • Pain around the sternum and rib cage
  • Tenderness when pressing on the junction between ribs and cartilage
  • Pain that worsens with deep breathing or stretching
  • Varying location - the pain "moves around"
  • Muscle tightness in neck, shoulders and upper back - often pain between the shoulder blades

Other common symptoms

  • Severe fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
  • Sleep problems and non-restorative sleep
  • "Fibro fog" - concentration difficulties and memory problems
  • Headache
  • Irritable bowel symptoms
  • Sensitivity to sound, light and temperature
5

Diagnosis

Fibromyalgia is diagnosed based on the ACR 2016 criteria (Wolfe et al., Seminars in Arthritis and Rheumatism, 2016). There is no blood test or imaging examination that confirms the diagnosis.

Important about diagnosis

The diagnosis is made based on the symptom picture and exclusion of other conditions. A thorough clinical examination is the foundation for the diagnosis.

6

Treatment

What does the research say?

EULAR (European League Against Rheumatism) published revised guidelines in 2017. Exercise is the only treatment with a strong recommendation. A network meta-analysis from 2024 (51 studies, 2873 patients) showed that water exercise is most effective short-term, while strength training is best long-term.

Treatment options

  • Exercise (strong recommendation): Water exercise most effective short-term, strength training best long-term. 51 studies with 2873 patients document the effect.
  • Massage therapy (5+ weeks): Shows moderate effect for pain, anxiety and depression. Myofascial release (soft tissue treatment) had the best effect.
  • Manual treatment/chiropractic: EULAR guidelines give a "strong against" recommendation for chiropractic for fibromyalgia. A systematic review (Ernst, 2009) concluded "insufficient evidence".

What can the chiropractor do?

Given the limited evidence, a consultation is primarily about:

  • Thorough examination - ruling out other causes of chest pain
  • Soft tissue treatment - gentle treatment of muscle tightness, not aggressive manipulation
  • Exercise guidance - help getting started with adapted exercise
  • Pain management - explanation of central sensitization, sleep hygiene, stress management

It is important to be honest:

Chiropractic is not a first-line treatment for fibromyalgia based on current research. Exercise and cognitive therapy have far better evidence.

7

Red flags - when to see a doctor

Seek doctor/emergency room IMMEDIATELY for:

  • Pressing, squeezing chest pain
  • Pain radiating to left arm, jaw or neck
  • Sweating, nausea or breathing problems along with chest pain
  • Sudden onset, intense pain
  • Chest pain with exertion that is relieved by rest

These symptoms may be due to heart attack, pulmonary embolism or other life-threatening conditions.

8

Prognosis - What happens?

Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition. A 10-year follow-up study (Wolfe et al., 1996) showed that 100% still had some symptoms, but 66% experienced improvement. Remission occurs in 17-47% after 1-2 years of active treatment.

Factors that improve prognosis:

Exercise The most important modifiable factor
Early diagnosis and comprehensive treatment
Sleep Treatment of depression and sleep problems

For older patients, one should also be aware of osteoporosis as a possible additional diagnosis.
