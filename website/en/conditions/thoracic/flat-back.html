Flat Back (Hypokyphosis)

When the spine is too straight | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Your thoracic spine should normally have a natural backward curve called kyphosis. This curve functions as a biological shock absorber when you walk and run, and provides space for the lungs and heart in the chest cavity.

Normal thoracic spine curvature ranges between 20 and 40 degrees (Fon et al., American Journal of Roentgenology, 1980). When the curve becomes less than 20 degrees, this is called hypokyphosis - a back that is too flat.

Brief Summary

  • What it is: A condition where the thoracic spine lacks its natural backward curve (less than 20 degrees kyphosis).
  • Who is affected: Young adults (95% under 40 years), gymnasts, ballet dancers, and people after certain spinal surgeries.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Limited research (only case reports), but individual approach focusing on breathing technique and mobilization can help.
  • Red flags: Chest pain, palpitations, breathing difficulties require medical evaluation first.
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

Important for chest symptoms

For chest pain, palpitations or breathing difficulties - see a doctor first to rule out heart and lung disease.

Two important terms

  • Hypokyphosis refers to reduced thoracic spine curvature, regardless of cause. The back looks "too straight" - the opposite of rounded back (hyperkyphosis).
  • Straight Back Syndrome (SBS) is a specific congenital variant where the lack of kyphosis reduces chest cavity space and can lead to compression of the heart and lungs.

The condition is relatively rare in the general population, but studies show that among patients with idiopathic scoliosis, approximately 49% have concurrent hypokyphosis (Clement et al., 2014).

Who is Affected?

Hypokyphosis and Straight Back Syndrome have a clear age pattern. A review of 114 SBS patients showed that 95% were under 40 years, and 60% were between 20 and 40 years (Jiang & Li, referenced in Kong et al., World Journal of Cardiology, 2023). The condition appears to become rarer with age.

Groups with increased prevalence

  • Gymnasts and ballet dancers more often develop flat back, probably due to repeated extension movements. A study of 33 ballet dancers found hypokyphosis in 9.1% (MDPI, 2021).
  • Patients with idiopathic scoliosis often have concurrent reduced kyphosis as part of the three-dimensional spinal deformity.
  • After spinal surgery: Historically, Harrington rods were known to flatten the back. Modern techniques provide better control, but studies still show that 31% of scoliosis surgery patients have reduced kyphosis at follow-up (PMC, 2013).
2

Symptoms - More Than Just Back Pain

What makes hypokyphosis and especially Straight Back Syndrome challenging are the symptoms that can be confused with heart disease.

A classic study by Davies et al. (Quarterly Journal of Medicine, 1980) documented the symptom profile:

  • Palpitations: 38% of patients (most common symptom)
  • Chest pain: 29%
  • Breathing difficulty: 19%

Chest pain and "heart anxiety"

When the chest cavity lacks the normal curvature, space is reduced. The heart can be pressed against the sternum, and this can cause pain resembling angina (heart cramp). In some cases, ECG may show changes resembling a heart attack, even when the heart is healthy (Shiba & Kenzaka, Internal Medicine Case Reports Journal, 2022).

Connection with heart valve problems

A striking finding is that 58-67% of patients with Straight Back Syndrome have concurrent mitral valve prolapse (Davies, 1980; Ansari, Clinical Cardiology, 1985). Of these, a significant proportion (77% in one series) later require heart surgery.

Breathing problems

A flat thoracic spine reduces chest mobility. The ribs need the curvature to rotate optimally during inhalation, and problems here can resemble rib lock. Lung function tests may show reduced capacity - one case report documented over 2 liters improvement in lung capacity after treatment that increased the kyphosis angle by 10 degrees (PMC, 2017). Read more about the connection between breathing, stress and back.

Musculoskeletal symptoms

Pain between the shoulder blades is common. The shoulder blades lack their natural "shelf" on the ribcage when the curve is flat. A study found that hypokyphosis increased the risk of shoulder impingement (OR 2.14) (LOHAS study, 2014).

3

Diagnosis

Clinical examination

An experienced clinician can often see and feel a flat back during examination. The chest may appear narrow when viewed from the side (reduced anterior-posterior diameter).

X-ray

To confirm the diagnosis, standing x-ray of the thoracic spine is used. The Cobb angle is measured from T4 to T12. Digital measurement has high reliability with ICC >0.99 (PMC, 2019). An angle below 20 degrees confirms hypokyphosis.

When is further investigation needed?

For symptoms such as chest pain, palpitations or breathing difficulties, heart and lung disease must be ruled out first. This often involves ECG, possibly echocardiography, and lung function tests.

4

Treatment

IMPORTANT WARNING

Treatment of hypokyphosis is NOT the same as treatment of common back pain or rounded back.

Traditional back exercises that focus on "straightening" the back through extension are counterproductive and potentially harmful for a person who already has a back that is too straight.

What can the chiropractor do?

Research on treatment of hypokyphosis is very limited - there are no randomized controlled trials. Available evidence comes from case reports and small patient series.

Promising results from case reports

  • A 33-year-old man with Straight Back Syndrome achieved 10 degrees improvement over 36 treatments (PMC, 2017)
  • A series of 10 patients showed an average 11.3 degrees improvement over 25 treatments (Oakley et al., 2018)

Evidence level: Very low. Only case reports exist, and independent replication is lacking.

Treatment course

Based on available case reports, treatment requires significant effort: 3-4 times per week over 9-16 weeks, totaling 25-36 treatments. This is far more intensive than standard back treatment.

5

What Can You Do Yourself?

Recommended measures

  • Breathing technique (diaphragmatic breathing): Focus on deep abdominal breathing instead of shallow chest breathing can help expand the chest cavity. Small studies show that combining breathing exercises with corrective exercises gives better results than exercises alone (Rashed, 2024).
  • Shoulder blade stabilization: Exercises for serratus anterior and lower trapezius can provide better stability.

Avoid

  • Aggressive backward bending exercises
  • Lying over a foam roller to "open the chest"
6

Contraindications - Who Should NOT Have This Treatment

Osteoporosis: Absolute contraindication

A study from Mayo Clinic (Sinaki & Mikkelsen, 1984) examined 59 postmenopausal women with spinal osteoporosis:

Important finding

  • 89% of those who did flexion exercises developed new compression fractures
  • Compared to only 16% in the group that did extension exercises

WHO defines osteoporosis as T-score <= -2.5 on bone density measurement. At this degree of bone fragility, high-velocity spinal adjustment is contraindicated (StatPearls, 2024).

Important

Women over 65 years and postmenopausal women with risk factors should be screened for osteoporosis before intensive back treatment (USPSTF, 2025).

7

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

IMPORTANT: See a doctor FIRST for the following symptoms:

  • Chest pain - may resemble heart disease and must be investigated
  • Palpitations - especially during exertion
  • Breathing difficulties - that limit daily activities
  • Fainting or near-fainting
  • New heart sounds (murmur)

Remember

With Straight Back Syndrome, 58-67% have concurrent heart valve problems. Cardiological evaluation is recommended before treatment.

8

Prognosis

Untreated hypokyphosis

A 30-year follow-up study found that patients with hypokyphosis had 10 times increased risk of needing neck surgery compared to those with normal kyphosis (MDPI, 2022).

With treatment

Available case reports show that structural improvement is possible - average 10-14 degrees increase in kyphosis in motivated patients. Pain often reduces significantly.

Realistic expectations

Treatment evidence is very limited. Not everyone will have the same benefit, and some structural changes cannot be reversed. The goal is often to improve function and reduce symptoms.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a flat back the same as good posture?

No, this is a common misconception. The spine should have natural curves. A completely straight back lacks natural shock absorption and can actually cause more problems.

Can I do strength training with a flat back?

Yes, but with modifications. Avoid exercises that press the back even straighter. Heavy deadlifts should be adapted - especially if you have osteoporosis.

How do I know if my chest pain is from my back or heart?

You cannot know this yourself. Chest pain should always be evaluated by a doctor first to rule out heart disease.

Is the condition hereditary?

Straight Back Syndrome has shown signs of autosomal dominant inheritance, and several gene variants have been identified.

Does it get worse with age?

Interestingly, aging can actually "help" as the natural increase in thoracic kyphosis compensates for the originally flat back.
