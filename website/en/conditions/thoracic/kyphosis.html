Kyphosis and Poor Posture

Causes, symptoms, treatment and exercises | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Do you spend a lot of time in front of a screen and notice your back becoming more rounded? You're not alone. Many people find their posture worsening over time, but the good news is that functional kyphosis can often be significantly improved with the right approach.

Your thoracic spine naturally has a backward curve - this is called thoracic kyphosis and is completely normal. The problem occurs when this curve becomes too large, which is called hyperkyphosis. Normal kyphosis angle is between 20-45 degrees measured on X-ray, while hyperkyphosis is defined as over 50 degrees (Katzman et al., 2010).

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Increased curvature in the upper back (>50 degrees) often caused by muscular imbalances and poor posture habits - not structural changes.
  • Most common causes: Prolonged sitting (>4 hours/day increases risk 2.5x), smartphone use, weak back muscles.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate evidence - combined manual therapy and exercises provide 5-13 degrees improvement over 8-12 weeks.
  • Red flags: Rigid curve that doesn't straighten, neurological symptoms, rapid worsening, night pain.
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up.

Causes and Risk Factors

There are two main types: Functional (postural) hyperkyphosis is caused by muscular imbalances and poor habits - the curve straightens when you lie flat or consciously straighten up. Structural hyperkyphosis (like Scheuermann's disease) involves actual changes in the vertebrae and is rigid.

From most common to least common:

1. Prolonged Sitting (Most Common)

A Thai study from 2022 showed that office workers who sit more than 4 hours daily have a 2.5 times higher risk of musculoskeletal problems compared to those who sit less (Jalayondeja et al., BMC Public Health 2022). EU-OSHA recommends maximum 2 hours of continuous sitting before taking a break.

2. Smartphone Use ("Tech Neck")

When you look down at your phone, your head bends forward and dramatically increases the load on your neck. Hansraj (2014) calculated that for every 15 degrees the head bends, the perceived head weight increases from about 5 kg to 12 kg - and at 60 degrees of flexion, a full 27 kg. A 2023 study showed that two-handed smartphone use acutely increased thoracic curves by about 12 degrees.

3. Weak Back Muscles

Katzman et al. (2017) found in the SHEAF study that people with hyperkyphosis had 19% reduced strength in the back extensor muscles compared to the control group. Weak muscles make it harder to maintain good posture over time.

4. Age

Kyphosis angle naturally increases by about 3 degrees per decade after age 40. Women show faster progression than men, especially after menopause (Fon et al., 1980). Among adults over 60, 20-40% have hyperkyphosis (Katzman et al., 2010).

5. Structural Causes (Least Common)

Scheuermann's disease affects 0.4-8% of the population and causes rigid, structural kyphosis with wedge-shaped vertebrae. Osteoporosis with compression fractures is another structural cause - each fracture increases kyphosis by an average of 3.8 degrees (Kado et al., 2013). These require a different approach than functional kyphosis.

Upper Crossed Syndrome

Czech physician Vladimir Janda described a pattern he called "upper crossed syndrome" - an interplay between tight and weak muscles often seen with poor posture. Typically one sees tight chest muscles and neck extensors combined with weak deep neck flexors and muscles between the shoulder blades. This pattern is found in approximately 35-67% of office workers (Xu, 2024; Dey & Musale, 2022).

Symptoms - More Than Just Appearance

Kyphosis isn't just about how you look. Many people experience a range of symptoms they don't connect to their posture:

Neck and Shoulder Problems

Increased thoracic curvature forces the head forward to maintain horizontal gaze. This overloads neck muscles and joints, which can lead to pain between the shoulder blades. Research shows that forward head posture mediates the relationship between thoracic kyphosis and reduced neck mobility. Granito et al. (2012) documented that reduced thoracic mobility correlated with 23% reduced shoulder mobility.

Headaches

Watson and Trott (1993) found that 83% of patients with cervicogenic headache had increased thoracic kyphosis (>40 degrees). A 2023 study on migraine patients showed significantly higher kyphosis angle (57 degrees vs 38 degrees) compared to healthy controls. The headache often comes from overloaded neck structures compensating for the thoracic curvature.

Arm Pain and Numbness

A stiff thoracic spine can affect shoulder blade position and nerve function. One study found that 78% of cases with tennis elbow or golfer's elbow had concurrent stiff thoracic spine. Numbness and tingling in the hands can be caused by compression or stretch of nerves in the kinetic chain.

Fatigue and Reduced Function

Holding the body upright against a curved back requires constant muscle work. Many experience unexplained fatigue. With severe kyphosis, lung function can be affected because the chest is compressed. For more on the connection between breathing and the back, see breathing, stress and back.

3

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

A thorough clinical examination is often more important than imaging. The chiropractor assesses posture from the side, checks if the curve straightens when you lie flat (flexibility test), tests neck and shoulder mobility, and examines muscle strength and balance.

Wall Test

A simple wall test can reveal forward head posture: stand with your heels against the wall and measure the distance from the back of your head to the wall.

When is Imaging Necessary?

X-ray or MRI is rarely necessary with typical functional kyphosis. Imaging may be appropriate if structural cause is suspected (rigid curve), after trauma, if osteoporosis or fracture is suspected, or with neurological symptoms. Remember that many findings on imaging, such as age-related changes, don't necessarily explain your symptoms.

4

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Chiropractic treatment of functional kyphosis focuses on increasing mobility, reducing muscle tension, and strengthening weak muscles. A meta-analysis by Gonzalez-Galvez et al. (2019) showed large effect of exercise programs on kyphosis angle. Kamali et al. (2016) found that manual therapy provided similar improvement as exercise alone - but the combination is often most effective.

Typical Treatment Methods Include

  • Joint mobilization and manipulation of stiff thoracic vertebrae
  • Soft tissue treatment of tight chest and neck muscles
  • Specific exercises to strengthen back extensors and shoulder blade stabilizers
  • Postural guidance and ergonomic advice

Treatment Course

Research indicates that 8-12 weeks of treatment with 2-3 sessions per week yields best results. Expected improvement is 5-13 degrees reduction in kyphosis angle (Gonzalez-Galvez et al., 2019; Ponzano et al., 2021). Younger patients generally respond better than older ones. The SHEAF study showed that improvements from a 3-month exercise program were maintained at 3-year follow-up - provided maintenance training continued.

Important About Treatment Timing

Treatment should be started early. There's no reason to "wait and see" - the sooner you start, the better the outlook for improvement.

5

What Can You Do Yourself?

Self-effort is crucial for lasting improvement. Here are three exercises with documented effect:

1. Thoracic Extension on Roller

Yoo (2018) documented 5.8 degrees reduction in kyphosis angle over just 12 days with this exercise.

Lie on your back with a foam roller placed crosswise under your upper back. Support your head with your hands and gently lean back over the roller. Hold for 5-10 seconds, repeat 5-10 times. Move the roller slightly up or down to target different levels.

2. Wall Angels

Activates the weak muscles between the shoulder blades.

Stand with your back against the wall, back of head, shoulder blades and lower back in contact with the wall. Hold your arms at 90 degree angles ("hands up" position) against the wall. Slowly slide your arms up and down along the wall while maintaining contact. 3 sets x 10 repetitions.

3. Doorway Chest Stretch

Tokita et al. (2021) showed that this stretch significantly increased pectoral muscle length and improved shoulder position.

Stand in a doorway with forearms against the door frame, elbows at shoulder height. Take a small step forward and lean your chest through the opening until you feel a stretch in front of your shoulders. Hold 15-30 seconds, repeat 3-5 times.

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

Contact a Doctor If You Experience:

  • Rigid curve that doesn't straighten when you lie flat
  • Neurological symptoms: Numbness, weakness, difficulty walking, bladder/bowel problems
  • Rapid worsening of the curvature
  • Night pain that wakes you
  • Breathing difficulties
  • Unexplained weight loss or fever
  • Trauma/injury that triggered the symptoms
7

Prognosis

The prognosis for functional kyphosis is generally good with active treatment. Meta-analyses show that structured exercise programs can reduce kyphosis angle by 5-13 degrees over 8-12 weeks. Younger patients show better response than older ones.

Important to know: Improvements require maintenance. Without continued exercise, most will gradually return to previous posture patterns. Therefore, home exercises and awareness of daily habits are crucial for long-term results.

With structural kyphosis (Scheuermann's disease, osteoporosis), the prognosis is more variable and depends on severity. These conditions cannot be reversed, but function and quality of life can often be significantly improved.

8

Frequently Asked Questions

Can kyphosis be completely straightened?

With functional kyphosis, the curvature can often be significantly reduced (5-13 degrees), but "perfect" posture is an unattainable goal for most people. Focus instead on better function and fewer symptoms. Structural kyphosis cannot be reversed, but symptoms can be alleviated.

How long does it take to see results?

Most people notice improvement in symptoms after 4-6 weeks of consistent treatment and home exercises. Measurable change in kyphosis angle typically takes 8-12 weeks. Lasting postural changes require 12+ weeks of maintenance training.

Is having a rounded back dangerous?

Mild to moderate functional kyphosis is usually not dangerous but can cause symptoms like neck/headaches and reduced function. With severe kyphosis (>70 degrees), lung function can be affected. Contact a doctor if you experience neurological symptoms or rapid worsening.

Do posture braces/supports help?

Research on posture supports shows mixed results. They can provide short-term awareness of posture, but passive support can also weaken muscles over time. Active training is more effective for lasting change. Bracing may be appropriate for structural kyphosis in adolescents.

Should I get an X-ray?

Not necessarily. With typical functional kyphosis without red flags, clinical examination is sufficient. X-ray is relevant if structural cause is suspected, after fracture, or if treatment doesn't produce expected results.

Can children and teenagers develop kyphosis?

Yes, both postural kyphosis and Scheuermann's disease can occur during adolescence. Scheuermann's affects 0.4-8% and is more common in boys. Early intervention is important, especially with structural kyphosis. If concerned, have the child examined.
