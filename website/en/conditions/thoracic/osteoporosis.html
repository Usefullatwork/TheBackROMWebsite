Osteoporosis and Compression Fractures in the Back

When the vertebrae fail from the inside - and what the chiropractor can contribute | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Osteoporosis is a condition where bone mass gradually decreases and bone structure weakens. The vertebrae in the spine have a hard outer shell and a porous interior. When the porous bone tissue becomes too weak, the vertebra can collapse under load - even during everyday activities such as bending over or lifting something light. This often leads to pain in the thoracic spine.

In Norway, this is a significant public health problem. About 300,000 Norwegians live with osteoporosis, and around 230,000 people over 50 years have had at least one compression fracture in the back (FHI, 2025). It is estimated that 15,000 new compression fractures occur annually in Norway.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Osteoporosis (brittle bones) can cause vertebrae to collapse without obvious trauma - so-called compression fractures
  • Most common causes: Postmenopausal estrogen deficiency (most common), high age, vitamin D deficiency, corticosteroid use, low body weight, previous fractures
  • Chiropractic treatment: Acute phase requires medical assessment. After 8-12 weeks of healing, gentle low-force techniques, soft tissue treatment and rehabilitation exercises may be relevant
  • Red flags: Sudden, intense back pain in person over 50; height loss; neurological symptoms; known cancer history
  • Consultation: 45 min new patient, 30 min follow-up

What is osteoporosis and compression fractures?

Norway is among the world leaders in hip fractures, and bone mass in the Norwegian population appears to be among the lowest in Europe (NOREPOS, 2014). Combined with widespread vitamin D deficiency - around 40% of adult Norwegians have levels that are too low - this creates a vulnerable starting point for many elderly people (HUNT study, 2014).

Lifetime risk

The lifetime risk of vertebral fracture at age 50 is 16-18% for women and 5-11% for men. By age 80, up to 40% have had at least one compression fracture (NCBI StatPearls, 2024).

2

Who is affected?

Globally, it is estimated that 18-20% of adults have osteoporosis, and the condition affects women twice as often as men - 23% versus 12% respectively (Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery and Research, 2021).

The most important risk factors, ranked by importance:

  1. Postmenopausal estrogen deficiency - the most common cause in women
  2. High age - risk increases significantly after 70 years
  3. Previous fracture - one fracture significantly increases risk for new ones
  4. Vitamin D deficiency - particularly relevant in Norway with little sunlight
  5. Long-term corticosteroid use - for asthma, rheumatic diseases etc.
  6. Low body weight and small frame
  7. Smoking and high alcohol consumption
  8. Family history of osteoporosis or hip fracture
3

Symptoms

Here comes a surprising fact: 65-75% of all vertebral fractures give no or minimal symptoms (AAFP, 2016). Many are discovered incidentally on X-rays or MRI taken for other reasons.

When symptoms occur, I often see:

  • Sudden, intense pain in the middle of the back or in the thoracic/lumbar transition - similar to thoracic back pain
  • Pain worsens with change of position, coughing or lifting
  • Improvement with rest and lying down
  • Gradual height loss over time
  • Increasing curvature of the upper back (kyphosis) - see kyphosis

The typical scenario

An elderly person, often a woman, experiences sudden strong back pain after bending down or lifting something light. There was no distinct fall or trauma.

Useful clinical signs

Two clinical signs have proven useful:

  • Closed-fist percussion test (tapping lightly on the vertebra) has a sensitivity of 87.5% and specificity of 90%.
  • "Supine sign" - that the patient cannot lie flat with only one pillow due to pain - has sensitivity 81% and specificity 93% (PMC, 2011).
4

Diagnosis

Imaging

X-ray is the first line for detecting compression fractures and shows reduced vertebral height. MRI is recommended when one needs to differentiate between acute and old fracture, or upon suspicion of cancer or infection. Acute fractures show bone marrow edema on MRI (high signal on T2/STIR sequences) - this is important information since regular X-ray cannot distinguish between fresh and healed fractures.

Bone density scan (DEXA)

WHO criteria for interpretation of T-score:

Category T-score
Normal T-score >= -1.0
Osteopenia (reduced bone density) T-score -1.0 to -2.5
Osteoporosis T-score <= -2.5
Severe osteoporosis T-score <= -2.5 with low-energy fracture

Fracture risk doubles for every standard deviation below normal. The FRAX calculator (available for Norway) calculates 10-year fracture risk and is used to assess treatment need.

5

Red flags - when you must see a doctor

Seek medical attention immediately if:

  • Sudden, intense back pain in person over 50-55 years
  • Neurological symptoms: Weakness in legs, altered bladder/bowel function, numbness in the seat area
  • Known cancer disease (especially breast, prostate, lung, kidney, thyroid)
  • Unexplained weight loss over 10% in the last 6 months
  • Night pain that is not relieved by change of position
  • Fever or signs of infection
  • Fracture after minimal trauma in person under 55 years

These signs can indicate serious underlying disease such as cancer spread to the back, myeloma (bone marrow cancer) or infection - conditions that require rapid medical investigation.

6

Medical treatment

Treatment of the underlying osteoporosis is the doctor's responsibility. Here is a brief overview of what exists:

Drug treatment

Bisphosphonates (alendronate, zoledronate) are first-line treatment. Meta-analyses show 38% reduction in total fracture risk, 45% reduction in vertebral fractures and 34% reduction in hip fractures (PMC, 2017).

Teriparatide (PTH analogue) shows greatest effect on vertebral fractures - up to 77% risk reduction - and is used for high-risk patients (Bone, 2020).

Denosumab is an alternative when bisphosphonates are not tolerated, but the effect disappears quickly upon discontinuation with risk of rebound fractures.

Vitamin D and calcium are standard supplements for osteoporosis: 800-2000 IU vitamin D daily and 1000-1200 mg calcium. This is especially important in Norway where vitamin D deficiency is widespread.

The acute phase

The first days after an acute compression fracture, treatment focuses on pain relief (paracetamol, possibly short-term opioid), short bed rest (1-3 days, but not prolonged!) and possibly back brace for stabilization.

7

What can the chiropractor do?

WHO guidelines classify acute fracture as an absolute contraindication for spinal manipulation, and osteoporosis as a relative contraindication (WHO, 2005). This means that normal high-velocity manipulation should not be performed, but adapted techniques can be relevant.

Timing is crucial

0-6 weeks

Acute phase

No manual treatment. The patient should be under medical follow-up.

6-12 weeks

Healing phase

Gentle soft tissue treatment can be introduced.

8-12 weeks+

After consolidation

Low-force techniques and rehabilitation can be started, assuming stable X-ray findings and symptom improvement.

Suitable treatment techniques

When healing has taken place, the following approaches may be relevant:

Activator technique

Instrument-assisted low-force impulse. Case studies show safe use in patients with compression fractures.

Cox flexion-distraction

Gentle stretching without rotation, well suited for elderly patients.

Soft tissue treatment

Musculature around the back, trigger points, fascial release.

Exercise therapy

Back extension training (flexion exercises are contraindicated), balance training, strength training. See also flat back for posture exercises.

Prevention and rehabilitation

The chiropractor can contribute significantly to:

  • Screening: Identifying patients at risk for manipulation treatment
  • Fall prevention: Balance training reduces fall risk by up to 39% (RCT, Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation, 2010)
  • Posture guidance: Avoid forward bending that loads fractured vertebrae. Important to prevent pain between the shoulder blades
  • Coordination: Ensure the patient receives medical treatment for osteoporosis
8

Prognosis

Standard healing time for compression fractures is 8-12 weeks, but in osteoporotic patients, full recovery can take up to 12 months (Cleveland Clinic; NYU Langone).

About two-thirds experience significant pain relief within 4-6 weeks. But here comes the more challenging reality: 80% of patients report continued pain and functional limitation one year after acute fracture (European Spine Journal, 2008). Quality of life measured by EQ-5D is significantly reduced - comparable to COPD. Long-term complaints can resemble fibromyalgia-related chest pain.

Risk of new fractures

The most important complication is "vertebral fracture cascade" - that one fracture increases the risk of more:

Previous fractures 1-year risk Relative risk
None previous 1.9% Reference
1 previous fracture 4.6% 2.6
2+ previous fractures 12.5% 7.3

Imminent fracture risk

Significantly, 19.2% of women who get a vertebral fracture will get a new one within one year - so-called "imminent fracture risk". This underscores the importance of medical treatment to prevent the cascade.

9

What you can do yourself

Strength training

Weight-bearing exercises 2-3 times weekly strengthens the skeleton (Grade A evidence).

Back extension

Exercises that extend the back, not bend it. Avoid forward bending.

Balance training

Tandem stance, one-leg stance, heel-to-toe walking - at least 15 minutes, 1-3 days a week.

Vitamin D and calcium

Talk to your doctor about supplements, especially in winter.

Smoking cessation

Smoking accelerates bone loss.

Fall prevention

Remove trip hazards at home, use correct footwear, good lighting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a chiropractor treat me if I have osteoporosis?
Yes, but with adapted techniques. Normal manipulation is not recommended, but low-force methods like Activator and Cox flexion-distraction can be safe alternatives. I always assess the risk individually.
How do I know if I have had a compression fracture?
Many are discovered incidentally on imaging. Sudden back pain in a person over 50, especially without clear trauma, should be investigated. Height loss over 4 cm is a warning sign.
Can I exercise with osteoporosis?
Absolutely - strength training is actually recommended! Avoid forceful forward bending and high-impact activities like jumping. Weight-bearing exercises and balance training are safe and useful.
How long does it take for me to get better?
The fracture itself usually heals over 8-12 weeks. Pain relief comes gradually over 2-3 months. Some have persistent complaints, so realistic expectations are important.
Are there Norwegian guidelines?
No, not at the moment. The old guidelines were withdrawn in 2016, and new ones are expected earliest autumn 2027 (Sykepleien, 2025). Norwegian clinicians follow international guidelines in the meantime.
When should I have a bone density scan (DEXA)?
All women over 65 and men over 70 should be considered. Younger persons with risk factors - previous fracture, long-term corticosteroid use, early menopause - should also be tested.
