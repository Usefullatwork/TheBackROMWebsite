Rib Dislocation vs. Rib Fracture

Is it a fracture or dislocation? How to distinguish between two common causes of acute chest pain | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You wake up with sharp pain in your chest that hurts when you breathe. Or you've fallen and feel intense tenderness at the ribs. Is it a fracture? Or is it something else? This article is part of our comprehensive guide to thoracic spine problems.

Both rib fractures and rib dislocation (costovertebral dysfunction) can cause significant pain that affects breathing and daily activities. But the treatment is completely different - and that's precisely why an accurate diagnosis is so important.

Brief Summary

  • What it is: Two different conditions that cause similar chest pain - rib fracture (bone fracture) and rib dislocation (joint dysfunction)
  • Most common cause: Rib dislocation is more common in clinical practice. Fractures typically require significant trauma.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Good effect for rib dislocation. For fractures: Treatment only after the acute phase (3-5 days).
  • Red flags: Breathing difficulties at rest, blue skin/lips, paradoxical chest movement, coughing up blood
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

When should you seek immediate help?

For severe breathing difficulties, bluish skin/lips, paradoxical chest movement, or coughing up blood - call 113 or go to the emergency room.

1

What is the Difference?

Rib Fracture (costa fracture)

A rib fracture occurs when one or more ribs get a crack or a complete break in the bone structure. This typically happens after trauma - a fall, a blow to the chest, or a car accident.

Rib fractures are relatively common injuries. Approximately 10% of all trauma patients admitted to hospital have one or more rib fractures (StatPearls, 2024). In older people with osteoporosis, fractures can occur with much less force - sometimes just from severe coughing.

Rib Dislocation (costovertebral dysfunction)

Rib dislocation is not about fractures at all. Here it is the joints between the ribs and the spine (the costovertebral joints) that have become stiff or "locked" - they don't move as they should.

The condition often occurs without any clear trauma. A sudden movement, heavy lifting, prolonged poor posture, or just an awkward twist can be enough.

How Common is This?

Musculoskeletal pain in the chest accounts for 10-30% of all chest pain that is not caused by the heart (NCBI, 2022).

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Rib Fracture - From Most Common to Rarest

  • Motor vehicle accidents - account for approximately 60% of cases (European Journal of Cardio-Thoracic Surgery, 2003)
  • Falls - make up about 22% of cases, especially in older adults
  • Direct blows - sports, violence, workplace accidents
  • Severe coughing - especially in people with osteoporosis
  • Stress fractures - in athletes, especially rowers (6-12% prevalence according to Sports Medicine Review, 2011)

Risk factors: Age over 65 significantly increases the risk - older patients account for 50% of all rib fractures in the emergency room. Osteoporosis gives approximately double the risk. Previous rib fracture after age 45 gives 5.4 times higher risk for new fractures (NORA Study, 2011).

Rib Dislocation - Typical Triggers

  • Prolonged sitting - office position, driving, can contribute to stiff back
  • Sudden rotational movements - golf, tennis, throwing
  • Heavy lifting - especially with twisting
  • Sleeping position - waking up with pain after lying crooked
  • Repetitive movements - painting, washing, gardening

Women are affected 2.5 times more often than men, and the condition is most common in younger adults. The non-dominant side is most often affected, typically between T1 and T5 (Frontiers in Pain Research, 2022).

3

Symptoms - How to Tell Them Apart?

Both conditions cause pain that worsens with breathing and movement. But there are some important differences:

Characteristic Rib Fracture Rib Dislocation
Trauma history Clear - you remember exactly when/how Often vague or none
Pain onset Immediately after trauma Gradual or sudden without trauma
With pressure Extreme tenderness, possible crepitation Moderate tenderness, trigger points
Swelling/bruising Often visible after 1-2 weeks None or minimal
Breathing Shallow, very limited Can take deep breaths (but it hurts)
Pain intensity Very intense Moderate to strong

The Main Rule: With rib fractures, the patient remembers exactly when and how the pain started - there was trauma. With rib dislocation, the history is often more vague: "I just woke up with it" or "It came after I had been sitting for a long time."

4

Diagnostics

Many believe that X-ray is the definitive answer for detecting rib fractures. The truth is more nuanced.

Imaging for Rib Fractures

X-ray has surprisingly low accuracy

Standard X-ray has only about 50% sensitivity (StatPearls, 2024). This means that approximately half of all rib fractures are not visible on X-ray because the fracture surfaces remain in place.

Oslo Emergency Department's handbook (2018) states that rib fractures are "primarily a clinical diagnosis." X-ray is not required for uncomplicated cases - the diagnosis is made based on medical history and examination.

When imaging is necessary: When lung complications are suspected (pneumothorax, hemothorax), with high-energy trauma, or with more than three consecutive fractures. CT or ultrasound provide much better accuracy than X-ray.

Imaging for Rib Dislocation

For rib dislocation, imaging is usually unnecessary. The condition is not visible on X-ray or MRI. The diagnosis is made clinically through examination of joint mobility, palpation tenderness, and pain provocation.

5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

For Rib Dislocation

Chiropractic treatment is often very effective for rib dislocation. A meta-analysis from 2010 showed that chest manipulation has significant effect on pain and function (Masaracchio et al.). Treatment may include:

  • Joint mobilization or manipulation of the costovertebral joints to restore normal mobility
  • Soft tissue treatment of the intercostal muscles
  • Mobilization of surrounding structures
  • Ergonomic guidance to prevent recurrence

Many experience noticeable improvement after the first treatment. Typical treatment course is 3-6 treatments over 2-4 weeks.

For Rib Fractures

In the acute phase (first 3-5 days) I do not treat the fractured rib directly. Focus is on pain control and avoiding complications such as pneumonia.

After the acute phase chiropractic treatment can contribute with soft tissue treatment of surrounding muscles, mobilization of neighboring ribs and spine for better breathing function, as well as guidance on activity and ergonomics.

What I do NOT do: Manipulation of the fractured rib, aggressive techniques that can worsen the fracture, or treatment in the completely acute phase.

What Can You Do Yourself?

For Rib Dislocation

  • Gentle stretching exercises for the chest (in a doorway, for example)
  • Breathing exercises with focus on slow, deep breathing
  • Heat (heat pack, warm shower) can relieve muscle tension
  • Avoid positions and activities that worsen the pain
  • Maintain normal activity within pain limits

For Rib Fractures

  • Pain relievers (paracetamol, ibuprofen) as needed
  • Breathing exercises - take deep breaths regularly, even though it hurts
  • Sleep preferably with your head elevated
  • Move within pain limits - complete rest is not recommended
  • Elastic chest wrap can help somewhat, but should not restrict breathing

Treatment Course

Rib dislocation: Many get clear improvement after 1-3 treatments. Full recovery within 3-4 weeks is common. Treatment frequency varies, but typically 1-2 times per week initially.

Rib fracture: The fracture heals in 6-8 weeks for uncomplicated cases, but the pain is often worst the first week and gradually improves. Chiropractic follow-up can start after 3-5 days and contribute to better function during healing.

6

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

Call 113 immediately for:

  • Severe breathing difficulties - you cannot breathe properly even at rest
  • Bluish skin, lips or nails (cyanosis) - sign of oxygen deficiency
  • Paradoxical chest movement - part of the chest moves opposite to the rest
  • Coughing up blood
  • Chest pain radiating to left arm or jaw (heart attack must be ruled out)
  • Signs of shock: Pale, clammy skin, rapid pulse, confusion
  • Sudden worsening after a few days of improvement

Complications from Rib Fractures

Pneumothorax (air in the chest cavity) and hemothorax (blood in the chest cavity) are serious complications that can occur with rib fractures. Pneumothorax is seen in 31-37% of patients with rib fractures (European Journal of Cardio-Thoracic Surgery, 2003).

7

Prognosis

Rib Dislocation

The prognosis is generally good. Most experience clear improvement within 1-2 weeks with treatment, and full recovery within 3-4 weeks. Recurrence can occur if the triggering factors (posture, work ergonomics, repetitive movements) are not addressed.

Rib Fracture

Uncomplicated fractures heal in 6-8 weeks. With multiple fractures, healing time can increase to 8-12 weeks. Pain is typically worst the first week and gradually improves.

An Important Nuance

Even though the fracture heals, functional recovery can take longer. Approximately 60% are not back to full work after six months, and 70% still have some pain after one year (Nebraska Medicine, 2024). Age plays a major role - older patients have higher risk of complications and longer rehabilitation time.

Each additional rib that breaks increases mortality risk by 19% and pneumonia risk by 27% in older adults (Bulger et al., 2000). That's why prevention of complications - especially breathing exercises - is so important.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my rib is broken or just dislocated?
The most important clue is the trauma history. With a fracture, you remember exactly what happened - a fall, a blow, an accident. With dislocation, the history is often more vague. The intensity is also different - fractures typically cause more intense pain that makes it difficult to take deep breaths. A clinical examination can usually distinguish between the conditions.
Do I need an X-ray to know if my rib is broken?
Not necessarily. Norwegian guidelines state that rib fractures are primarily a clinical diagnosis. X-rays also have low accuracy (about 50%) and rarely change treatment for uncomplicated fractures. Imaging becomes important when complications are suspected or with serious trauma.
Can I exercise with rib dislocation?
Yes, but adapt the activity. Avoid exercises that provoke pain, especially heavy rotational movements. Light activity like walking is often perfectly fine. After treatment, you can gradually resume normal training.
How long am I on sick leave with rib fractures?
It depends on the nature of the work. For physically demanding work, 4-6 weeks may be necessary. For sedentary work, many can return earlier, but with adjustments. As a chiropractor, I can write sick leave for up to 12 weeks.
Why does it hurt so much to breathe?
The nerves that run along the ribs (intercostal nerves) become irritated with both fractures and dislocation. Every time you breathe, the ribs move - and this provokes the irritated nerves. With fractures, bone fragments can also move and cause pain.
Can rib dislocation cause pain that feels like heart problems?
Yes, the costovertebral joints can cause pain that radiates to the front of the chest and can resemble heart pain. This resembles the symptoms of costochondritis. A study from the Copenhagen Heart Study showed that 18% of patients with suspected heart disease had musculoskeletal pain as the cause. With chest pain, heart problems should always be considered first.
Can I come directly to a chiropractor, or do I need a referral?
You can come directly. Chiropractors in Norway have been primary contact practitioners since 2006, which means you don't need a referral from your GP. I can also refer you further to a specialist or imaging if needed.
