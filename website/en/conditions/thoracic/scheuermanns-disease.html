Scheuermann's Disease

A lifecycle guide from adolescent issues to adult daily life | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Scheuermann's disease is a growth disorder of the spine that occurs during puberty. During the growth spurt, several vertebrae develop unevenly - the front part grows slower than the back part. The result is wedge-shaped vertebrae that create a permanent, increased curvature in the thoracic spine.

The condition was first described by Danish orthopedist Holger Werfel Scheuermann in 1920. His Danish colleague Kaj Sorensen established the diagnostic criteria in 1964, which are still used internationally today.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: A growth disorder causing permanent rounding of the upper back (structural hyperkyphosis)
  • Prevalence: Affects 2-4% of adolescents, more often boys than girls (Damborg et al., 2006)
  • Adolescent Treatment: Brace (16+ hours daily) and specific exercises can slow progression
  • Adult Treatment: Focus on pain relief, mobility and function - the structure cannot be reversed
  • Prognosis: Curves under 60 degrees into adulthood generally have a good prognosis
  • Consultation: 45 min new patient, 30 min follow-up

Data from the Danish Twin Registry (46,418 twins) shows a prevalence of 2.8% for symptomatic, doctor-documented Scheuermann's disease (Damborg et al., J Bone Joint Surg Am, 2006). Boys are affected about twice as often as girls (3.6% vs 2.1%). The condition has a strong genetic component - heritability is estimated at 74%.

1

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause is not fully understood, but several factors appear to play a role:

Main Factors

  • Genetics (most common): With 74% heritability, genetic factors are the dominant risk factor. The condition often runs in families.
  • Mechanical Stress: Theory of traction apophysitis - pull on growth plates during rapid growth.
  • Vascular Factors: Possible reduced blood supply to parts of the vertebrae during growth.
  • Biomechanics: Tight hamstring muscles can increase the load.

Important to Know

Scheuermann's disease is NOT caused by poor posture - it is a structural condition, not a result of habits.

PART 1: Adolescents and Growth Phase

2

Symptoms in Adolescents

Many young people with Scheuermann's disease have few or no symptoms and discover the condition by chance. When symptoms occur, they typically include:

  • Visible rounding or "hump" in upper back (see also kyphosis)
  • Mild to moderate back pain, especially with activity
  • Stiffness in thoracic spine, especially after rest
  • Back fatigue after school or activity
  • With severe curves: breathing difficulties (rare in adolescents)

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is made by clinical examination combined with X-rays. Sorensen criteria require:

Diagnostic Criteria (Sorensen)

  • Thoracic kyphosis over 40 degrees (measured with Cobb angle)
  • At least 3 consecutive vertebrae with wedging of 5 degrees or more
  • Often irregular endplates and Schmorl's nodes on X-ray

Important Differentiation

In postural (posture-related) kyphosis, the patient can actively straighten the back by hyperextension. In Scheuermann's, the curve is rigid and cannot be actively corrected - this is a key finding in clinical examination.

Severity

Grade Cobb Angle Measures
Mild 40-50 degrees Observation, exercise
Moderate 50-70 degrees Brace + exercise
Severe Over 70-75 degrees Surgical assessment
3

Treatment During Growth Phase

Treatment during growth aims to slow or stop progression of the curve. When the skeleton is fully developed (typically 16-18 years old), the possibilities for influencing structure are significantly reduced.

Brace Treatment

Bracing is the best-documented conservative treatment for adolescents with moderate Scheuermann's disease. A long-term study (Auliså et al., Eur J Phys Rehabil Med, 2023) followed 158 patients and showed average correction from 57.8 degrees to 43.3 degrees - an improvement that remained stable at 10+ years follow-up.

Important Prerequisites for Effect

  • Use minimum 16 hours daily (preferably 16-20 hours)
  • Active growth remains (Risser stage 0-2)
  • Curve under 65 degrees at start
  • Combined with exercise for best result

Norwegian Context

The Norwegian Electronic Health Library (Helsebiblioteket) points out that bracing for kyphosis is used relatively little in Norway compared to international recommendations. Discuss with a specialist if this is relevant.

Exercises and Training

A randomized controlled trial (Bezalel et al., Asian Spine J, 2019) compared Schroth therapy with standard anti-gravity exercises. Both groups had improvement, but the Schroth group showed statistically better reduction in thoracic kyphosis and improved quality of life.

Recommended Exercise Types

  • Thoracic spine extension (stretching exercises)
  • Strengthening of spinal extensors
  • Stretching chest muscles and hamstrings
  • Core stabilization
  • Postural awareness training

Recommended Activities

  • Swimming - gentle and good for the whole back
  • Walking and jogging
  • Yoga and pilates with focus on extension
  • Ball games with varied movement

Be careful with: Heavy weightlifting with axial loading, repeated forward bending under load, and high-impact activities during the growth period.

PART 2: Adults with Scheuermann's Disease

4

Symptoms in Adults

When the skeleton is fully developed, treatment goals change. The structural kyphosis cannot be reversed, but symptoms, function, and quality of life can be significantly improved.

The symptom picture in adults is often more complex than in adolescents:

  • Chronic or recurrent pain between the shoulder blades
  • Morning stiffness that improves with activity
  • Reduced mobility in thoracic spine
  • Muscle fatigue and tension (can resemble fibromyalgia-related chest pain)
  • With curves over 75 degrees: reduced breathing capacity and endurance (see breathing, stress and back)
  • Aesthetic concerns for some

Uplifting News

A Swedish study of older men (69-81 years) with Scheuermann's disease found no increased incidence of neck or back pain compared to peers without the condition (Jonsson et al., Acta Orthop, 2023). As many as 25% of adults with Scheuermann's report actually no back pain.

5

What can the chiropractor do?

We must be honest about the evidence base: There are currently no randomized controlled trials on chiropractic treatment specifically for Scheuermann's disease. The available research consists of case reports.

That does not mean treatment is without value. Based on clinical experience and general back research, chiropractic treatment can contribute to:

Chiropractic Treatment Can Contribute To

  • Improved mobility in stiff thoracic segments
  • Reduction of muscle tension and trigger points
  • Improved rib movement and breathing mechanics
  • Pain relief through joint mobilization
  • Guidance on exercises and ergonomics
  • Functional improvement even if structure remains

Important Expectation Management

Chiropractic treatment can NOT "straighten out" a structural kyphosis in adults. The goal is symptom relief and better function, not structural change.

Treatment Course

For adults with symptomatic Scheuermann's disease, a typical course will involve a trial period of 6-8 treatments over 4-6 weeks to assess response. Most who respond to treatment experience improvement in pain and function within this period. With good response, maintenance treatment with 4-8 week intervals may be useful for some.

Response varies significantly from person to person. Some have good and lasting effect, others experience only short-term relief, and some do not respond noticeably. An honest evaluation after the trial period is important.

6

What you can do yourself

Recommended Self-Help Measures

  • Daily Mobility: Short sessions (10-15 min) with stretching and mobilization of thoracic spine
  • Strength Training: Focus on back extensors, shoulder blade muscles and core
  • Ergonomics: Correct screen height, varied working posture, frequent breaks
  • Breathing Exercises: Deep breathing with focus on expanding the rib cage
  • Activity: Swimming, walking and other low-impact activities
7

Red flags - when you should see a doctor

Seek medical attention quickly if:

  • Sudden severe worsening of pain
  • Numbness, tingling or weakness in arms or legs
  • Problems with bladder or bowel function
  • Severe breathing problems
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Night pain that wakes you up and is not relieved by position change
8

Surgery - when is it relevant?

Surgery is reserved for the most severe cases. Indications include:

Surgical Indications

  • Kyphosis over 75 degrees that progresses
  • Curves 60-75 degrees with significant pain not responding to 6+ months conservative treatment
  • Neurological symptoms (rare)
  • Severely reduced lung function

A study by Tsirikos and Carter (Bone Joint J, 2021) followed 88 patients and showed average correction from 94.5 degrees to 47.5 degrees with significant improvement in quality of life. Complication rate was 12.5%, and 12-21% need revision long-term.

9

Prognosis

The most important long-term study is Murray et al. (J Bone Joint Surg Am, 1993) which followed 67 patients for 32 years. Main findings were:

Main Findings from Long-Term Study (32 years)

  • Persons with untreated Scheuermann's experienced slightly more back pain than control group
  • No difference in education level, sick leave, or ability to perform daily activities
  • Restrictive lung disease occurred only with curves over 100 degrees

Progression after Skeletal Maturity

Curves continue to increase slowly also in adulthood - approx. 0.45 degrees per year according to Garrido et al. (Spine Deform, 2021). This means an 18-year-old with 60 degrees kyphosis can have around 75 degrees at age 50 if the curve is not stabilized. In older age, osteoporosis can further alleviate the kyphosis.

Expected Course by Curve Size

Curve at Maturity Expected Course
Under 60 degrees Generally good prognosis with minimal complaints
60-75 degrees Can develop chronic pain; often responds well to conservative treatment
Over 75 degrees Reduced exercise capacity; surgical assessment may be relevant
Over 100 degrees Risk of restrictive lung disease; requires specialist follow-up
10

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Scheuermann's disease be cured?

The structural change in the vertebrae is permanent. In adolescents, correct treatment (brace + exercise) can slow or stop progression. In adults, treatment focuses on symptoms and function, not structure.

Is Scheuermann's the same as poor posture?

No. Scheuermann's is a structural condition where the vertebrae themselves are shaped differently. Postural kyphosis is due to muscular habits and can be corrected actively. A person with Scheuermann's cannot "straighten up" in the same way.

Will I always have pain?

Not necessarily. As many as 25% of adults with Scheuermann's report no back pain. With proper exercise and lifestyle adaptation, many live well with the condition.

Can I exercise normally?

Yes, most forms of exercise are both safe and recommended. Swimming, strength training, running and yoga are all good alternatives. Be careful with very heavy lifting with axial loading (like heavy squats and deadlifts) if you have symptoms.

Will my children inherit the condition?

There is a significant genetic component (74% heritability), so children have increased risk. Be aware of posture changes in puberty and seek assessment if concerned.

Do I need X-ray or MRI?

X-ray confirms diagnosis and measures curve (Cobb angle). MRI is rarely necessary unless there is suspicion of other conditions or neurological symptoms.

Does it get worse with age?

The curve typically increases by approx. 0.45 degrees per year throughout adulthood. But this does not necessarily mean more pain - many have stable or even improved symptom burden with proper follow-up.
