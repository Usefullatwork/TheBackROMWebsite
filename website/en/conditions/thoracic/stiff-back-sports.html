How a Stiff Back Ruins Your Golf Swing and Tennis Serve

Thoracic Stiffness and Sports Performance | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

You have trained technique, strength, and endurance. Yet you feel like your swing lacks power, or your shoulder protests after a long training session. The problem may not be where you think.

Your thoracic spine - the 12 vertebrae between your neck and lower back - is designed to rotate. In fact, this part of the spine accounts for most of the upper body's rotation, with 4-9 degrees of movement per vertebra (White and Panjabi, 1990). In comparison, each lumbar vertebra can only contribute 1-2 degrees of rotation.

When the thoracic spine becomes stiff - something that happens gradually for most of us - the force and movement must come from somewhere else. And this is exactly where the problems begin.

Key Summary

  • What it's about: Stiffness in the thoracic spine forces the lower back to compensate - affecting both performance and injury risk.
  • Who it affects: Golfers, tennis players, and anyone involved in rotational sports or overhead movements.
  • What research shows: Lower back pain affects 15-55% of golfers. An 8-week mobility program improved tennis serve accuracy from 41% to 55%.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Moderate to good evidence for manual techniques combined with exercises.
  • Consultation: 45 min initial consultation, 30 min follow-up

The Golfer: When the Lower Back Takes the Hit

The power of the golf swing comes from what researchers call the "kinetic chain" - a coordinated transfer of energy from the ground, through the legs, hips, and trunk, and out to the arms and club. A study by Nesbit and Serrano from 2005 showed that the back and hip joints together account for 68-72% of the total work in a golf swing.

Modern golf technique is built on what is called the "X-factor" - the angle between shoulders and pelvis at the top of the backswing. Skilled golfers create around 30-60 degrees of separation between upper and lower body, depending on measurement method (Cole and Grimshaw, 2016). This "coils" the muscles and stores elastic energy that is released in the downswing.

But what happens when the thoracic spine is stiff?

Then the lumbar spine must compensate with more rotation than it is designed for. And the lumbar spine has only 1-2 degrees of rotational capacity per vertebra - compared to the thoracic vertebrae's 4-9 degrees.

The result is predictable: Lower back pain is the most common injury among golfers. Epidemiological studies show that 15-34% of amateurs and 22-24% of professionals experience golf-related back injury (McHardy et al., 2007). Combined, lower back problems account for 18-54% of all golf-related complaints.

3x Higher Lifetime Risk

Professional golfers have three times higher lifetime risk of lower back injury compared to amateurs (Kay et al., 2024).

The Tennis Player: The Serve That Reveals the Stiffness

The tennis serve is one of the most demanding movements in sports. Kibler and colleagues established as early as 1995 that legs, hips, and trunk together contribute approximately 51% of kinetic energy and 54% of total force in a serve. The rest comes from shoulder, arm, and wrist.

A stiff thoracic spine limits how much energy can be generated and transferred through this chain. The shoulder must then compensate with higher velocity to achieve the same result - and this increases load and injury risk.

The Good News from Research

A study published in Frontiers in Sports and Active Living (Le Solliec et al., 2023) followed 22 competitive tennis players through an 8-week program of stretching, strength, and mobilization exercises:

  • The percentage of successful serves increased from 41% to 55%
  • Serve velocity and accuracy improved significantly (effect size 0.54-0.65)
  • Shoulder mobility increased markedly, especially external rotation (effect size 1.49)

The program focused on thoracic and shoulder mobility combined with strength exercises - four sessions per week for eight weeks. And this was without any form of manual treatment.

The Runner: When Breathing Becomes Limited

Hyperkyphosis - excessive curvature of the thoracic spine - affects not only movement but also breathing capacity. Learn more about the connection between breathing, stress, and back. The Framingham Study, which followed participants over 16 years, found that women with pronounced kyphosis had measurably greater decline in lung function over time compared to those with normal posture (Lorbergs et al., 2017).

For athletes with severe hyperkyphosis (over 75 degrees) - as seen in Scheuermann's disease - a study by Barrios et al. (2019) showed that VO2max - a measure of maximum oxygen uptake - was significantly lower than in the control group. This can directly affect endurance and performance.

Posture Affects Breathing Function

A recent study from 2025 (Chiba et al., Cureus) tested the effect of simulated kyphotic posture during running. Even in healthy young men, respiratory load increased measurably - higher carbon dioxide production and respiratory quotient - even though oxygen consumption was the same.

Why Compensation Leads to Injury

The body is remarkably good at finding detours when the main road is blocked. The problem is that these detours often have lower capacity and higher "tolls." With prolonged thoracic pain, the musculature between the shoulder blades can become overloaded.

A study by Wasser and colleagues (2018) analyzed 187 baseball pitchers and found that college pitchers on average had -25 degrees of lumbar extension (backward bend in the lower back) at maximum arm rotation. Most pitchers spent 45% of the pitch with more than 30 degrees of lumbar extension.

In throwing athletes generally, the prevalence of spondylolysis (stress fracture in the pars) is 26.67%.

The Mechanism Summarized

Kim et al. (2016) summarized the mechanism clearly: "Excessive movements in the lumbar spine are due to limited movements in the thoracic vertebrae." When the thoracic spine doesn't rotate sufficiently, the lower back must take over - and it isn't built for the job.

What Can Be Done?

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Treatment of stiff thoracic spine focuses on restoring normal mobility through a combination of manual techniques and exercises. A systematic review in BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine (Spencer et al., 2020) identified 38 different thoracic exercises used in sports settings, but noted that randomized controlled studies specifically for athletes are lacking.

The available research shows moderate effect of mobilization and manipulation for improving thoracic rotation and flexibility. For rib problems, treatment may also include the costovertebral joints. One study found that thoracic rotation increased from 50 to 55 degrees after treatment - while lumbar rotation was reduced, suggesting better distribution of load.

In Clinical Practice I Often Combine:

  • Joint mobilization and manipulation of stiff segments in the thoracic spine
  • Soft tissue treatment of shortened muscles (chest muscles, upper trapezius)
  • Specific exercises to maintain and improve mobility
  • Review of technique and posture in daily life

What Can You Do Yourself?

Based on the Le Solliec study and other research, the following exercises can help:

Thoracic Extension Over Foam Roller

Lie with the roller across your upper back and gently extend backward. Hold for 30 seconds, repeat at three different heights.

Thread the Needle

Start on all fours, thread one arm under your body and rotate your upper body. Hold for 5 seconds, 10 repetitions per side.

Doorway Stretch for Chest Muscles

Three angles (low, middle, high) to target all fibers. Hold for 30-60 seconds.

Wall Angels

Stand with your back against the wall and move your arms up and down like a snow angel. Focus on keeping your entire back in contact with the wall.

Training Frequency

Research suggests that 4 sessions per week over 8 weeks provides measurable effect. Consistency is more important than intensity.

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

Although most cases of stiff thoracic spine and related problems are harmless, you should see a doctor if you experience:

Seek Medical Attention Immediately

  • Weakness or numbness in legs or arms
  • Problems with bladder or bowel control
  • Unexplained weight loss combined with back pain
  • Night pain that wakes you up
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Rapid worsening of posture or kyphosis, especially in young people
9

Prognosis

Most people who address thoracic stiffness early experience good improvement. The Le Solliec study showed measurable improvements after just 8 weeks of dedicated training.

At the same time, it's important to be realistic: Stiffness in the thoracic spine is often something that develops over years, and maintenance requires continuous effort. Research shows that the effect gradually diminishes if exercises are not maintained.

For athletes, early intervention is particularly important. Younger athletes generally respond better to treatment than older ones, according to meta-analyses that have examined age-stratified results.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my thoracic spine is stiff?
A simple test: Sit on a chair with your arms crossed over your chest. Rotate your upper body as far as you can to each side without moving your hips. Normal thoracic rotation is around 40-45 degrees to each side. If you notice a clear limitation, especially to one side, it may indicate stiffness.
Can a stiff thoracic spine be the cause of my shoulder pain?
Yes, this is a common connection. When the thoracic spine doesn't contribute sufficiently to movement, the shoulder must compensate. Research shows that thoracic mobilization often provides immediate improvement in shoulder mobility.
How long does it take before I notice improvement?
Most people notice some improvement after 2-4 weeks of regular training and/or treatment. Significant improvement in sports performance typically takes 6-8 weeks, based on available research.
Do I need X-rays or MRI?
Rarely. Imaging is usually unnecessary for functional stiffness without red flags. Clinical examination provides sufficient information to start treatment. Imaging is considered if symptoms don't respond as expected or if structural changes are suspected.
Can I continue playing golf/tennis while being treated?
Yes, in most cases. The goal is to improve function while maintaining your activity level. I may adjust training intensity for a period and focus on exercises that support treatment.
Will this come back?
Thoracic spine stiffness is often a chronic problem that requires maintenance. Lifestyle factors such as prolonged sitting, one-sided training, and poor posture contribute to the problem recurring. A good home exercise program is the key to long-term success.
