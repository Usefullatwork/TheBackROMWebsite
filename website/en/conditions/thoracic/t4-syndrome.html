T4 Syndrome: When Upper Thoracic Spine Causes Hand Symptoms

Symptoms, diagnosis and treatment | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

T4 syndrome is a clinical diagnosis describing a symptom pattern where dysfunction in the upper thoracic spine - particularly around the fourth thoracic vertebra (T4) - causes symptoms that radiate into the arms and hands. Typically, the patient experiences tingling, numbness, or a "glove-like" sensation in both hands, often without imaging or nerve conduction studies showing anything abnormal.

The condition was first described in 1986 by Australian physiotherapist Geoffrey Maitland and N. McGuckin, who observed that patients with upper thoracic spine problems had a characteristic symptom pattern that responded to thoracic spine mobilization.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: A clinical condition where stiffness in the upper thoracic spine (around the T4 vertebra) causes tingling or numbness in both hands
  • Most common causes: Prolonged sitting, poor posture, office work - exact prevalence is not known
  • Chiropractic treatment: Limited evidence (only case reports), but clinical experience suggests good response to manual treatment
  • Red flags: Unilateral numbness, weakness, gait or balance problems, bladder/bowel disturbances
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

Key Feature: "Glove Pattern"

Bilateral numbness or tingling in both hands - entire hand and fingers, like a glove

It's important to be honest: T4 syndrome is a clinical observation, not a well-defined medical diagnosis with validated criteria. A systematic review from 2017 found only 8 peer-reviewed articles on the condition, and none of these were randomized controlled trials on actual patients (Karas & Pannone, 2017). There are also no published prevalence data - we simply don't know how common the condition is.
1

Causes and Risk Factors

Since T4 syndrome is poorly defined in research, we don't have reliable data on causation. The leading theory involves the sympathetic nervous system:

Sympathetic Nerve Theory (Theoretical)

Sympathetic nerve fibers supplying the upper extremities originate from spinal cord levels T2-T5. The sympathetic ganglia are located directly in front of the costovertebral joints. The theory is that stiffness or dysfunction in these joints can irritate sympathetic structures and cause referred symptoms to the hands (Evans, 1997; Maitland, 1986).

Risk Factors Based on Case Reports

From available case descriptions, we see some patterns:

  • Prolonged sitting and office work
  • Forward head posture ("computer neck"), which can also contribute to kyphosis
  • Women are affected more often than men - case reports suggest a ratio of 3:1 to 4:1 (DeFranca & Levine, 1995)
  • The 30-50 age group is most represented in the literature

It must be emphasized that these figures come from individual case reports with very few patients, not from epidemiological studies.

2

Symptoms

The most characteristic feature of T4 syndrome is the bilateral, "glove-like" distribution of symptoms. Unlike carpal tunnel syndrome or nerve root compression from the neck, the symptoms do not follow a specific dermatomal pattern.

Typical Symptoms

  • Tingling or numbness in both hands - entire hand and fingers, like a "glove"
  • Feeling of swelling or heaviness in the hands (often without visible swelling)
  • Altered temperature sensation - hands may feel cold or warm without objective change
  • Stiffness and discomfort in the upper thoracic spine (see also pain between shoulder blades)
  • Headache, often originating from the back of the head
  • Neck pain

Time Pattern

  • Symptoms are often worse in the evening or early morning
  • Prolonged sitting often worsens symptoms
  • Forward leaning posture worsens symptoms

Important

With T4 syndrome, neurological examination is normal - there is no weakness, reflexes are normal, and there are no dermatomal deficits. If you have clear neurological findings, other diagnoses must be ruled out.

3

Diagnosis

T4 syndrome is a diagnosis of exclusion. There are no validated diagnostic criteria or specific tests that can confirm the diagnosis (Karas & Pannone, 2017; NHS Scotland, 2019). The diagnosis is made by:

  1. Ruling out other conditions - especially neurological and vascular causes
  2. Recognizing the symptom pattern - bilateral, non-dermatomal distribution
  3. Reproducing the symptoms - through palpation and mobilization of the upper thoracic spine

Imaging

MRI and X-ray are usually normal with T4 syndrome. Images may show age-related changes such as wear or degeneration, but these rarely explain the symptoms. Imaging is primarily used to rule out other conditions - not to confirm T4 syndrome (DeFranca & Levine, 1995).

Differential Diagnoses to Consider

Condition Key Differences from T4 Syndrome
Carpal Tunnel Syndrome Median nerve area (thumb to middle finger), positive Tinel/Phalen, pathological nerve conduction
Cervical Radiculopathy Dermatomal pattern, unilateral, neurological deficits, positive Spurling
Thoracic Disc Herniation / Thoracic Outlet Syndrome Usually unilateral, ulnar distribution, vascular signs
Peripheral Polyneuropathy "Stocking-glove" pattern, begins in feet, systemic associations
Multiple Sclerosis Other neurological symptoms, MRI findings, variable course
4

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

Treatment of T4 syndrome focuses on restoring mobility in the upper thoracic spine. It's important to be clear that all treatment evidence comes from case reports (evidence level 5) - there are no randomized controlled trials on patients with T4 syndrome (Karas & Pannone, 2017).

Techniques Described in Literature

1

Joint Mobilization of Upper Thoracic Spine (T2-T7)

Gentle mobilization to restore normal mobility

2

Manipulation Treatment

High velocity, low amplitude - several case reports report immediate symptom relief

3

Soft Tissue Treatment

Treatment of surrounding musculature

4

Neurodynamic Mobilization

Techniques to improve nerve gliding

Expectations

Case reports often describe rapid and significant improvement after few treatments. In clinical practice, we see that many patients experience noticeable improvement after the first treatment. NHS Scotland recommends that the patient should show progression within up to 3 treatments - if not, the diagnosis should be reconsidered (NHS Scotland, 2019).

What Can You Do Yourself?

Self-treatment and prevention focus on posture and mobility. Also read about breathing, stress and back for more tips:

Thoracic Extension

Lie back over a foam roller or towel in the upper back, 1-2 minutes daily

Posture Correction

Tuck your chin in and lift your chest to counteract forward leaning position

Regular Breaks

Stand up and move every 30-45 minutes during sedentary work

Ergonomics

Adjust screen height and workstation to avoid forward leaning posture

Treatment Course

Since there are no controlled studies, we don't have reliable data on treatment duration. Based on available case reports and clinical experience:

  • Many experience improvement after 1-3 treatments
  • A typical course may last 2-6 treatments over 2-4 weeks
  • Continued posture work is important to prevent recurrence

If you don't experience improvement after 3 treatments, the diagnosis should be reconsidered and other conditions possibly ruled out (NHS Scotland, 2019).

5

Red Flags - When to See a Doctor

See a Doctor or Emergency Room If You Experience:

  • Progressive weakness in arms or legs
  • Numbness or weakness in both legs
  • Gait or balance problems
  • Bladder or bowel disturbances
  • Hyperactive reflexes or clonus phenomena
  • Unexplained weight loss or night sweats
  • Known cancer with new symptoms
  • Fever combined with neck stiffness

These symptoms may indicate serious conditions such as myelopathy, MS, or other neurological diseases requiring rapid investigation.

6

Prognosis

There are no published data on long-term prognosis or recurrence rates for T4 syndrome. What is known is based on case reports and clinical experience:

What We Know

Short-term Prognosis

Case reports often describe rapid symptom relief after manual treatment - some patients experience immediate improvement, others gradual improvement over weeks. Hirai & Thomson (2016) reported complete symptom resolution after manipulation treatment.

Recurrence Risk

NHS Scotland suggests that the clinical course probably resembles back and neck complaints in general - meaning recurrence is common, and symptoms may have an episodic pattern. Continued attention to posture and ergonomics is probably important for preventing recurrence.

Realistic Expectations

Most patients who actually have T4 syndrome respond well to treatment. But it's important to remember that the diagnosis is uncertain - if treatment doesn't produce results, it may be because the symptoms have another cause.
