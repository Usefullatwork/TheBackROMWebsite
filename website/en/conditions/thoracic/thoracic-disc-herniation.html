Thoracic Disc Herniation

Symptoms, Diagnosis, Treatment and Prognosis | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Thoracic disc herniation occurs when the gel-like core (nucleus pulposus) of a disc in the thoracic spine (T1-T12) pushes through the outer fiber ring and presses on nerves or the spinal cord.

The thoracic spine is the most stable part of the spinal column because the ribs provide extra support. The disc material here is also less elastic than in the neck and lower back. This combination makes herniations in this area rare - but when they do occur, the consequences can be serious because the spinal canal is narrower here than elsewhere.

Key Summary

  • What it is: Herniation (prolapse) of an intervertebral disc in the thoracic spine that can press on nerves or spinal cord.
  • Prevalence: Only 0.25-0.75% of all symptomatic disc herniations - very rare (StatPearls, 2024).
  • Most common causes: Degenerative changes (wear), previous trauma, Scheuermann's disease.
  • Chiropractic treatment: Limited role - primarily differential diagnosis and referral. No RCTs for manual treatment of thoracic disc herniation.
  • Red flags: Weakness in both legs, bladder/bowel disturbances, gait difficulties, rapidly progressive symptoms.

Prevalence and Statistics

Thoracic disc herniation is very rare. Only 0.25-0.75% of all symptomatic disc herniations occur in the thoracic spine. In comparison, the lumbar spine accounts for about 90% and the cervical spine for about 8%.

0.25-0.75% of all symptomatic herniations
70% develop myelopathy
73-80% recover without surgery

Prevalence and Demographics

MRI studies show that 11-37% of healthy people have asymptomatic thoracic disc bulges (Wood et al., 1995). Symptomatic cases requiring treatment occur in approximately 1 per million per year (Shirzadi et al., 2013).

Average age at diagnosis is 48 years, with highest prevalence between 40 and 60 years. Gender distribution is approximately equal (European Spine Journal, 2012).

2

Causes and Risk Factors

Causes are ranked from most to least common:

1. Degenerative Changes (Most Common)

The most common underlying cause. Disc material loses water content and elasticity over time.

2. Trauma

37-49% of patients have previous injury such as fall, car accident, or sports mishap (StatPearls, 2024).

3. Scheuermann's Disease

Juvenile kyphosis with altered spinal morphology increases risk (P<0.01) (EFORT Open Reviews, 2021). Read more about Scheuermann's disease.

4. Smoking

Associated with 18% reduction in disc height over time (EFORT Open Reviews, 2021).

5. Repetitive Rotational Loading

Sports like golf, softball, and baseball with repeated twisting.

Approximately 75% of thoracic herniations occur below the T8 level, with T11-T12 being the most common location, followed by T7-T8 (21.8%) and T8-T9 (15%) (Sarsilmaz et al., 2018).

3

Symptoms

The symptom picture varies depending on whether the herniation presses on nerve roots, spinal cord, or both.

Most Common Symptoms

  • Back pain: Occurs in 92% of patients with symptomatic thoracic herniation (PMC, 2025). See also thoracic pain for other causes.
  • Radicular pain: Band-like pain following a rib space, which may resemble rib dysfunction. Occurs in about 52%.
  • Sensory disturbances: Numbness, tingling, or altered sensation in chest, abdomen, or legs (59%).
  • Motor deficits: Weakness, especially in legs (37%).

Diagnostic Delay

Because symptoms can resemble heart, lung, or gastrointestinal disease (including costochondritis), it takes an average of 14-16 months from symptom onset to correct diagnosis (European Spine Journal, 2012).

Myelopathy (Spinal Cord Involvement)

In symptomatic patients, about 70% develop myelopathy - involvement of the spinal cord itself (Barrow Neurological Institute, 2024). This is because the spinal canal in the thoracic spine is particularly narrow, and 70% of thoracic herniations are calcified (StatPearls, 2024).

4

Diagnosis

Clinical Examination

When thoracic herniation is suspected, the chiropractor examines for neurological signs indicating spinal cord involvement:

Test Sensitivity Specificity
Hyperreflexia (increased tendon reflexes) 89-94% -
Hoffman's sign 80-81% 79-100%
Babinski's sign 53-57% 93-100%
Clonus 35-39% 96-99%
Gait disturbances >80% -

Imaging

MRI is the gold standard with approximately 81% sensitivity and specificity for disc herniation (PMC, 2018). If calcified herniation is suspected, CT can provide additional information.

Important About MRI Findings

73% of asymptomatic people have positive MRI findings in the thoracic spine, and 37% have disc bulges without symptoms (Wood et al., 1995). Imaging findings must always be interpreted in the context of clinical signs.

5

Treatment

What Can the Chiropractor Do?

The chiropractor's primary role in thoracic disc herniation is differential diagnosis - identifying the condition and assessing severity.

Evidence Base

There are no randomized controlled trials (RCTs) examining chiropractic or manual treatment specifically for thoracic disc herniation (ScienceDirect, 2006). Published case reports document serious complications after manipulation with known thoracic herniation (JAMA Neurology, 1987).

For confirmed herniation without myelopathy, gentle soft tissue treatment and mobilization of surrounding areas may potentially relieve muscular compensation pain. High-velocity manipulation should be avoided when disc pathology in the thoracic spine is suspected.

With signs of myelopathy or progressive neurological deficits, the patient should be referred immediately.

Conservative Treatment Generally

For patients without myelopathy, conservative treatment is recommended as first choice:

  • Rest and activity modification: Short rest period (1-2 days) followed by gradual increase in activity
  • Medication: Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) after medical evaluation
  • Physical therapy: Structured treatment for 4-6 weeks

About 80% of patients return to previous activity level with conservative treatment (StatPearls, 2024). The classic study by Brown et al. (1992) followed 55 patients - 73% never needed surgery.

Surgery

Surgery is reserved for:

  • Progressive myelopathy or neurological deficits
  • Large herniations (>40-50% canal narrowing)
  • Persistent symptoms after 6-12 weeks of conservative treatment

Newer posterolateral surgical techniques show complication rate of 12.4% and neurological worsening in 3.5% (JOSR, 2023).

6

Red Flags - When You Should See a Doctor

SEEK IMMEDIATE MEDICAL EVALUATION FOR:

  • Increasing weakness in one or both legs
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Numbness around rectum/genitals (saddle paresthesia)
  • Gait difficulties or unsteadiness
  • Rapidly worsening symptoms (hours-days)

These signs may indicate spinal cord compression requiring urgent surgical evaluation.

7

Prognosis

Prognosis depends on symptom type and severity:

0% asymptomatic findings became symptomatic (26 months)
73-80% recover without surgery
80-90% experience improvement after surgery

Spontaneous regression: Documented in only 3-4 case reports worldwide for thoracic herniation - significantly rarer than for lumbar herniations (BMJ Case Reports, 2016).

Frequently Asked Questions

How common is thoracic disc herniation?
Very rare. Only 0.25-0.75% of all symptomatic disc herniations occur in the thoracic spine. In comparison, the lumbar spine accounts for about 90% and the cervical spine for about 8%.
Can a chiropractor treat thoracic disc herniation?
The chiropractor's main role is to identify the condition and assess severity. For confirmed herniation without neurological deficits, gentle treatment of surrounding structures may be considered. With myelopathic signs, the patient is referred onward.
Why does it take so long to get a diagnosis?
Symptoms can resemble heart disease, lung conditions, or gastrointestinal problems. Average delay is 14-16 months from symptom onset to correct diagnosis.
Do I need surgery?
Most people don't need surgery. About 73-80% recover with conservative treatment. Surgery is indicated for progressive neurological symptoms or lack of improvement after 6-12 weeks.
Are MRI findings always significant?
No. 37% of healthy people without symptoms have disc bulges in the thoracic spine on MRI. Imaging findings must always be interpreted together with symptoms and clinical findings.
Can the condition resolve on its own?
Symptoms can improve, but spontaneous regression of the actual herniation is very rarely documented for thoracic cases - unlike lumbar herniations.
Which sports increase the risk?
Sports with repetitive rotation such as golf, tennis, and softball can increase the load on thoracic discs.
