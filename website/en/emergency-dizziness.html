About Acute Dizziness

Acute dizziness can strike suddenly and be very debilitating. Symptoms range from a spinning sensation to unsteadiness and nausea. Most cases are caused by benign conditions that can be treated effectively.

BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo) is the most common cause and accounts for 20-30% of all dizziness cases. With proper treatment, most experience rapid improvement.

2

What Could It Be?

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo) - The Most Common Cause

BPPV accounts for about 20-30% of all dizziness cases. You recognize it by short, intense spinning attacks triggered when you turn over in bed, lie down, or look up at a shelf. Symptoms typically last less than a minute but often leave you with nausea and unsteadiness.

Treatment with repositioning maneuvers like the Epley maneuver is simple and effective. About 74% become symptom-free after one treatment, and 74-85% after 1-3 treatments.

Read more about BPPV

Vestibular Neuritis (Virus on Balance Nerve)

Vestibular neuritis causes sudden, intense dizziness lasting hours to days. You feel like the world is constantly spinning - even when lying completely still. This requires prompt medical evaluation. Research shows that treatment with corticosteroids within 72 hours can improve outcomes.

Read more about vestibular neuritis

Cervicogenic Dizziness (Neck-Related)

Cervicogenic dizziness occurs when stiffness in the neck sends incorrect signals to the brain. You experience diffuse unsteadiness and a feeling that the ground is swaying - often combined with headache. This type of dizziness responds well to manual treatment of the neck.

Read more about cervicogenic dizziness

Dizziness After Concussion

Dizziness after concussion can persist for weeks or months. Symptoms often worsen in environments with lots of movement or visual stimuli, like shopping malls or when using screens.

Dizzy and Nauseous?

Are you acutely nauseous? This is your body's normal reaction to incorrect signals from the balance organ. Nausea doesn't mean something is dangerous - but it's very uncomfortable and can make it difficult to function normally.

How I Assess and Treat Dizziness

Thorough Medical History

A thorough medical history is often half the diagnosis. I ask about when the dizziness started, what triggers it, how long the attacks last, and whether you have other symptoms.

Clinical Examination

Clinical examination includes testing of balance, coordination, and nerve function. To detect BPPV, I use specific positional tests like Dix-Hallpike and Supine Roll Test.

Video Nystagmography (VNG)

Special goggles that record eye movements in the dark. This detects more cases of BPPV than regular examination and provides objective data.

TRV Chair

For complex cases of BPPV, a TRV chair is used. This specialized equipment enables precise treatment in all directions.

4

What Can You Expect?

For BPPV

Most experience noticeable improvement already after the first treatment. Some need 2-3 treatments for full effect. About 10% experience mild worsening the first day - this is normal and will pass.

BPPV can recur. Studies show 22-50% recurrence over 5 years. New treatment is just as effective as the first time. Research suggests that vitamin D deficiency may increase the risk of recurrence.

For Cervicogenic Dizziness

Many notice relief the same day.

For Vestibular Neuritis

I help with symptom control and starting rehabilitation, while collaboration with your GP ensures any medication treatment needed.

If Treatment Doesn't Help

If the dizziness doesn't improve after 2-3 treatments, I refer to a doctor or neurology specialist for further assessment.
