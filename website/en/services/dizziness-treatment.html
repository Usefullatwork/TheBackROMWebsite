Dizziness Treatment

Specialized assessment and treatment with Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Dizziness can feel like the room is spinning, like walking on a rocking boat, or as a more diffuse unsteadiness and "cotton wool in the head." Some experience short, intense attacks. Others have a constant, troublesome imbalance throughout the day.

Our balance system has three main components that send information to the brain: the inner ear (the vestibular system), vision, and body sense from muscles and joints (proprioception). When these signals don't match, dizziness occurs.

Studies show that 10-15% of the population experiences dizziness each year. Most cases are harmless and can be treated effectively. But accurate diagnosis is important for choosing the right treatment.

Quick Summary

  • What I treat: BPPV (crystal disease), neck-related dizziness, post-concussion dizziness, balance problems after vestibular neuritis
  • Diagnostics: Video Frenzel goggles (VNG), TRV chair, caloric testing when needed
  • Treatment: Repositioning maneuvers, vestibular rehabilitation, manual treatment of the neck
  • Expected outcome: BPPV often improves after 1-3 treatments. Complex conditions require longer follow-up
  • Practical: No referral needed. The clinic is centrally located at Majorstua in Oslo

Acute Dizziness Should Be Assessed Quickly

Have you suddenly become dizzy with head movements? It's wise to get an examination as soon as possible - don't wait and see if it goes away on its own.

Quick assessment is important for several reasons:

  • Rule out serious illness: The most important thing is to clarify whether the dizziness is due to something requiring acute treatment
  • Make the right diagnosis: It's easier to find the cause when symptoms are fresh and clear
  • Faster treatment: Many cases of crystal disease can be resolved in one treatment - why suffer unnecessarily?
2

Crystal Disease (BPPV)

Crystal disease, or benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), is a common cause of acute dizziness. Each year approximately 80,000 Norwegians are affected. The condition occurs when small calcium crystals in the inner ear become dislodged from the otolith organs and enter the semicircular canals.

Typical Characteristics

  • Brief dizziness episodes (from a few seconds to about one minute)
  • Typically triggered by turning in bed, lying down, getting up or looking up
  • Often accompanied by nausea
  • Can feel like intense "carousel sensation" - but many experience more diffuse symptoms

The crystals can be located in different canals, such as the posterior, horizontal or anterior. Each variant needs a specific treatment maneuver. That's why precise diagnostics are important.

Other Dizziness Conditions I Treat

Neck-Related Dizziness

The neck has a close connection to the balance system through muscles, joints and nerve signals. Stiffness and reduced function in the upper neck can send incorrect signals to the brain about head position.

Typical characteristics: Diffuse unsteadiness or "rocking" sensation, worsening with static work (especially at computer), often combined with neck pain or headache.

Vestibular Neuritis (Virus on Balance Nerve)

Vestibular neuritis is caused by inflammation of the balance nerve, often following a viral infection. Unlike crystal disease, this condition causes constant dizziness not linked to specific head movements.

Typical characteristics: Sudden onset with intense, persistent dizziness lasting days to weeks, severe nausea and vomiting in acute phase, spontaneous nystagmus (involuntary eye movements), gradual improvement over weeks but may leave residual symptoms.

Dizziness After Concussion

A blow to the head can affect balance, eye movements and neck function simultaneously. Many experience persistent problems in the weeks and months following the injury.

Typical characteristics: Unsteadiness in environments with much visual movement (shops, public transport, screen use), increased fatigue and concentration difficulties, increased tension in neck and shoulders.

Other Conditions

I also see patients with vestibular migraine (often in collaboration with GP or neurologist), PPPD (persistent postural-perceptual dizziness), and combined cases where both balance organ, neck and headache play a role.

If I suspect a condition requiring specialist healthcare, I refer onward.

How I Assess Dizziness

Consultation and Assessment

The first part of the consultation is used for a thorough medical history:

  • When and how did the dizziness start?
  • What triggers the symptoms? Head movements, position, stress, screens?
  • How long do episodes last? Seconds, minutes, hours or constant?
  • Have you had head injury, neck trauma, ear problems or migraine?
  • How do the problems affect work, sleep and activity?

Clinical Examination

I then perform a structured examination which may include neurological tests, head impulse test, balance and gait analysis, specific position tests (Dix-Hallpike, roll test, Bow and Lean), and assessment of neck joints, muscles and movement.

Video Frenzel Goggles (VNG)

Videonystagmography (VNG) is a central part of dizziness assessment. The goggles have infrared cameras that film eye movements in the dark. Here nystagmus, or involuntary eye movements, becomes clearer because vision cannot suppress them.

VNG examination makes it possible to:

  • Detect weak nystagmus that is impossible to see with the naked eye
  • Distinguish between different variants of crystal disease
  • Differentiate between peripheral (ear) and central (brain) causes
  • Choose the right repositioning maneuver based on objective findings
  • Document treatment response

TRV Chair (Rotation Chair)

The TRV chair (Thomas Richard Vitton) is a specialized tool for diagnosis and treatment of crystal disease. The chair can be rotated 360 degrees in plane with each individual canal.

The TRV chair is especially useful for:

  • Limited neck mobility
  • Crystals that are stuck - the chair allows use of acceleration to dislodge them
  • Multi-canal BPPV - when crystals are in multiple canals
  • Refractory cases - when standard bench treatment hasn't worked

Caloric Testing

If vestibular neuritis or other balance nerve damage is suspected, I use caloric testing. This measures the function of each balance organ separately. Tempered water (warm and cold) is gently flushed into the ear canal, and the nystagmus response is measured and compared between sides. Asymmetry over 25% indicates reduced function on one side.

5

Treatment Methods

Treatment is always tailored to the findings. The goal is to relieve symptoms and improve function. Treatment response varies between patients.

Repositioning Maneuver for Crystal Disease

For crystal disease, I use targeted maneuvers to move the crystals back to the correct area of the inner ear. Choice of maneuver depends on which canal is affected:

  • Posterior canal (most common): Epley maneuver or Semont maneuver
  • Horizontal canal: BBQ Roll (Lempert) or Gufoni maneuver
  • Anterior canal (rare): Yacovino maneuver or Kim maneuver

During the maneuver you may experience temporary dizziness. It's uncomfortable but expected and a sign that the crystals are actually moving. Most experience clear improvement after 1-3 treatments.

TRV Chair Treatment

When standard bench treatment doesn't give good results, or when the patient has limited mobility, I use the TRV chair. The chair provides precise positioning and makes it possible to add controlled acceleration to dislodge crystals that are stuck.

Research from Haukeland University Hospital shows that TRV treatment is effective, especially for complex cases and horizontal canal BPPV.

Vestibular Rehabilitation

For more persistent dizziness problems, after vestibular neuritis, or for residual symptoms after crystal disease treatment, active exercises are important. Vestibular rehabilitation is well documented and stimulates the brain's ability to compensate for imbalance.

The program may include:

  • Gaze stabilization exercises: Trains the vestibulo-ocular reflex
  • Habituation exercises: Gradual exposure to movements or environments that trigger symptoms
  • Balance training: Progressive exercises on different surfaces, with and without vision

Manual Treatment of the Neck

When dizziness is connected to neck problems, manual treatment may be appropriate: joint mobilization or specific adjustments, muscle and connective tissue treatment, and home exercises for strength and control.

Common Questions About Dizziness Treatment

How quickly can I expect improvement?
With crystal disease, many experience clear improvement after the first treatment. With vestibular neuritis or dizziness after concussion, rehabilitation takes longer - typically several weeks.
Is the treatment uncomfortable?
Repositioning maneuvers trigger temporary dizziness - that's actually the goal, because it means the crystals are moving. The discomfort typically lasts a few seconds to a minute.
Can the dizziness come back?
Crystal disease can return - studies show recurrence in about 15-20% within the first year. The good news is that it responds just as well to new treatment. Vitamin D supplementation may reduce the risk of recurrence.
Do I need a referral from a doctor?
No. Chiropractors have primary contact status in Norway, meaning you can come directly without a referral. I collaborate with GPs and specialists when needed.
What's the difference between you and an ENT specialist?
ENT specialists are experts in ear diseases and can do more comprehensive testing of hearing and balance. I assess and treat the most common dizziness conditions such as crystal disease and neck-related dizziness. If I suspect a condition requiring specialist assessment, I refer onward.
Can I do exercises at home?
Yes, home exercises are often an important part of treatment. With crystal disease, it's important to know which canal is affected before doing exercises on your own - the wrong exercise can move crystals to another canal. That's why professional assessment is recommended first.
