Vestibular Rehabilitation

Specific training for dizziness and balance problems | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

Vestibular rehabilitation (VRT) is not regular exercise - it's "physiotherapy for the brain." The goal is to teach the brain to interpret balance signals anew when the inner ear or balance nerve is damaged.

Approximately 7.4-7.8% of the population experiences vestibular dizziness during their lifetime (Neuhauser, 2005). Among adults over 40, 35% have balance problems, increasing to 85% in people over 80.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Specific training that teaches the brain to interpret balance signals anew
  • Who it helps: BPPV/crystal disease, vestibular neuritis, concussion dizziness, PPPD
  • Evidence: Strong (2.67x higher improvement vs. no treatment - Cochrane review)
  • Duration: 12-20 min daily training, typically 4-8 weeks for acute conditions
  • Consultation: 45 min first visit, 30 min follow-up

What is Vestibular Rehabilitation?

Vestibular rehabilitation (VRT) is an evidence-based treatment that uses specific exercises to treat dizziness and balance problems. When the balance organ in one ear is damaged, the brain receives erroneous information.

VRT forces the brain to use the remaining signals better, rely more on other senses (vision and proprioception), and stop reacting to erroneous signals.

Strong Evidence

The updated clinical guidelines from the Academy of Neurologic Physical Therapy (2022) give vestibular rehabilitation a strong recommendation (Grade A) for treatment of both acute and chronic vestibular hypofunction.

Who Benefits from Vestibular Rehabilitation?

VRT is effective for several common causes of dizziness:

1. BPPV / Crystal Disease (Most Common)

Lifetime prevalence 2.4%, accounts for 17-42% of all dizziness consultations. Small calcium crystals become loose in the inner ear and cause intense, brief dizziness episodes with head movements. Treated primarily with the Epley maneuver (90-97% success rate), but VRT can be useful for residual symptoms.

2. Vestibular Neuritis / Labyrinthitis

Annual incidence 3.5-15.5 per 100,000. Viral inflammation of the balance nerve causes sudden, intense dizziness lasting for days. A Norwegian study from Haukeland showed that early VRT initiation gave statistically significant improvement at both 3 and 12 months.

3. Dizziness After Concussion

50-84% of athletes report dizziness after concussion. VRT started within 30 days is associated with faster return to sports and school.

4. PPPD (Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness)

15-20% of patients with vestibular symptoms. Chronic unsteadiness that persists after the original cause is gone. Often requires combination treatment with VRT, cognitive therapy, and possibly medication.

3

How Does Vestibular Rehabilitation Work?

The brain is plastic - it can change. By doing specific exercises that provoke some dizziness, you send a signal to the brain that "there's something here that needs calibrating."

Without these exercises, the brain will often try to avoid the problem - for example, by having you stop turning your head. This makes you stiff and anxious, but not well.

The Three Main Principles

Adaptation

Trains the VOR reflex (vestibulo-ocular reflex) - the ability to keep your gaze stable while the head moves. This is fundamental for being able to read, drive, and walk without getting dizzy.

Habituation

Gradual exposure to movements that trigger dizziness. Through repeated exposure, the brain learns that these movements are not dangerous, and symptoms decrease.

Substitution

Training to use vision and proprioception to compensate for reduced function in the balance organ.

Dosage - How Much Do You Need to Train?

The 2022 clinical guidelines (ANPT Guidelines) are clear on their recommendations:

Phase Frequency Total Time
Acute/subacute (first month) 3x daily Min. 12 min
Chronic (>3 months) 3-5x daily Min. 20 min

Expected Duration

  • Acute conditions: 4-6 weeks
  • Chronic conditions: 4-8 weeks
  • PPPD: 3-6 months or longer

Important Principle

The exercises should provoke mild to moderate dizziness. If you don't notice anything, the exercises are too easy. The dizziness should subside within 15-30 minutes after the exercise session.

Red Flags - When to Seek Medical Help

Call 113 or Go to the Emergency Room for:

  • Sudden, intense dizziness with double vision, speech difficulties, or swallowing problems
  • Unsteady gait where you cannot walk without support
  • Vertical or direction-changing nystagmus
  • Dizziness combined with severe headache
  • Reduced consciousness or confusion

The HINTS Examination

The HINTS examination (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) has 96-100% sensitivity for detecting stroke in acute dizziness - better than CT or MRI in the acute phase.

6

Prognosis - What Can You Expect?

BPPV / Crystal Disease

90-97% become symptom-free after 1-3 Epley treatments. Recurrence is common: 18-26% in the first year, about 50% over a lifetime.

Vestibular Neuritis

Acute dizziness improves quickly (1-2 days), most improvement occurs within 10 weeks. 30-50% develop chronic dizziness. Long-term prognosis depends more on psychological factors than nerve damage.

PPPD

Chronic condition that rarely resolves completely, but can be managed well. Typical course: 6 months to 2 years for maximum improvement.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to get better?
Most people with acute conditions notice clear improvement within 4-6 weeks with daily training. Chronic conditions take longer - typically 3-6 months.
Is it normal to get dizzy from the exercises?
Yes. Mild to moderate dizziness during the exercises is necessary for the brain to calibrate itself. The dizziness should subside within 15-30 minutes.
Can the dizziness come back?
Yes, relapses are common - especially with BPPV. This doesn't mean the treatment has failed, just that you need another round of treatment.
Do I need a referral?
No. Chiropractors have had primary contact status in Norway since 2006, so you can book an appointment directly.
What's the difference between VRT and regular balance training?
VRT specifically targets the vestibular system and uses principles like adaptation and habituation. Regular balance training focuses more on muscle strength and coordination. VRT is more targeted toward dizziness symptoms.
