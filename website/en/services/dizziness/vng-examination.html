VNG Examination (Videonystagmography)

Diagnostics for dizziness and balance problems | Chiropractor Mads Finstad, Majorstua

VNG (videonystagmography) is a diagnostic examination that uses infrared cameras to record eye movements with high precision. The principle is based on the close connection between the balance organ in the inner ear and eye movements - when damaged or dysfunctional, characteristic involuntary eye movements called nystagmus occur.

This examination has largely replaced older ENG technology (electronystagmography) because it provides better accuracy and can capture rotating eye movements that ENG cannot detect.

Quick Summary

  • What it is: Examination that measures involuntary eye movements to assess balance function
  • Indications: Dizziness, vertigo, unsteadiness, suspected BPPV or vestibular neuritis
  • Key findings: Distinguishes between peripheral (ear) and central (brain) causes
  • Duration: 60-90 minutes for complete examination
  • Accuracy: Caloric test: 74.7% sensitivity, 83.5% specificity

What is a VNG Examination?

VNG stands for videonystagmography - a diagnostic examination that uses infrared cameras to record eye movements with high precision. This examination has largely replaced older ENG technology because it provides better accuracy and can capture rotating eye movements.

The principle behind VNG is based on the close connection between the balance organ in the inner ear and eye movements. When the balance organ is stimulated, it sends signals that automatically adjust the eyes' position - a reflex called the vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR).

Technical Information

VNG equipment consists of special goggles with infrared cameras that record pupil movements in complete darkness. This is important because visual fixation can suppress nystagmus. Modern systems operate at 50-60 Hz frame rate, while advanced units reach 220-250 Hz.

2

The Three Main Parts of VNG

A complete VNG examination consists of three parts that together provide a comprehensive picture of balance function:

1. Oculomotor Tests

Assesses the brain's control of eye movements. Tests the ability to follow a moving target (smooth pursuit), rapid precise eye movements (saccades), and stability during fixation. Abnormal findings may indicate central (neurological) causes.

2. Positional Tests

Examines whether specific head positions or head movements trigger nystagmus. The most well-known is the Dix-Hallpike test for crystal disease (BPPV). A positive test shows characteristic rotating nystagmus with 2-15 seconds delay.

3. Caloric Test

The only examination that can test each ear completely independently of the other. Uses temperature stimulation to assess balance function in each ear separately.

3

The Caloric Test

The caloric test was developed by Robert Barany in the early 1900s, which earned him the Nobel Prize in Medicine in 1914. The principle is surprisingly simple: Temperature differences create fluid currents in the horizontal semicircular canal.

How it Works

During the test, the patient lies on their back with the head elevated 30 degrees. Then water or air at a controlled temperature is introduced into the ear canal:

Stimulation Water Temperature Air Temperature Duration
Warm 44°C 50°C 30-60 sec
Cold 30°C 24°C 30-60 sec

Memory Aid: COWS

Cold Opposite, Warm Same - Warm stimulation causes nystagmus that beats toward the stimulated ear, while cold stimulation produces the opposite response. A difference of 25% or more between ears indicates reduced function.

4

What is Observed on VNG?

Normal Findings

  • No spontaneous nystagmus at rest
  • Symmetrical response to caloric testing (less than 25% difference)
  • Normal saccades and smooth pursuit
  • No position-triggered nystagmus

Findings in Common Conditions

BPPV (Crystal Disease)

Characteristic pattern during Dix-Hallpike: Rotating nystagmus with 2-15 seconds delay lasting under one minute and decreasing with repeated testing. Lifetime prevalence of 2.4%.

Vestibular Neuritis

Typical horizontal-rotatory spontaneous nystagmus beating away from the affected ear, significant unilateral weakness on caloric testing (often 50-100%), and nystagmus that is suppressed by visual fixation.

Red Flags - Signs of Central Cause

  • Direction-changing gaze-evoked nystagmus
  • Pure vertical nystagmus
  • Nystagmus that is NOT suppressed by visual fixation
  • Immediate nystagmus during positional testing without delay
  • Abnormal saccades or smooth pursuit

In acute vestibular syndrome, the HINTS examination (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) is highly valuable, with 99.2% sensitivity for detecting stroke.

Where is VNG Performed in Norway?

Public Healthcare

The Balance Laboratory at Haukeland University Hospital in Bergen is Norway's National Competence Center for Vestibular Diseases. They examine approximately 1000-1100 patients annually. The co-payment is about 403 NOK (exemption card applies).

Private Sector

Several private ENT clinics offer VNG examination. Prices vary but are typically around 490 NOK for the VNG examination itself, in addition to consultation fees.

Chiropractors with Special Expertise

Certain chiropractors with additional training in vestibular conditions have invested in VNG equipment or video nystagmoscopy goggles for dizziness assessment.

6

Preparation

48-72 hours before the examination, avoid:

  • Vestibular suppressant medications (antihistamines, motion sickness tablets)
  • Sedatives (benzodiazepines)
  • Strong painkillers (opioids)
  • Alcohol

On the day of the examination:

  • Avoid eye makeup (mascara, eyeliner) - it interferes with camera recording
  • Eat a light meal 2-3 hours before
  • Bring comfortable clothes
  • Expect to feel somewhat dizzy afterward - preferably have someone accompany you home
7

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the VNG examination painful?
No, the examination is not painful. The caloric test may feel uncomfortable because it induces dizziness, but this only lasts a few minutes.
How long does the examination take?
A complete VNG examination usually takes 60-90 minutes. Some clinics offer shorter protocols of 30-45 minutes.
Can I drive home afterward?
It is recommended to have someone accompany you home, especially after the caloric test. The dizziness usually subsides within 30-60 minutes.
What if the examination is normal but I'm still dizzy?
A normal VNG does not rule out balance problems. Episodic conditions may be normal between attacks. Dizziness can also be caused by migraine, anxiety, low blood pressure, neck problems, or medication side effects.
Does insurance cover VNG examination?
Most private health insurances cover VNG examination when medically justified. In the public healthcare system, you only pay the co-payment (exemption card applies).
