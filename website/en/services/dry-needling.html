Dry Needling

Treatment of muscle knots with needles at chiropractic clinic Majorstua

Tight, tender spots in your neck or shoulders that feel like hard knots under the skin. A deep, aching pain that worsens when you press on certain areas. Perhaps the pain radiates to other areas - the neck sends discomfort up toward the head, or a point in the shoulder radiates down the arm.

These knots are called myofascial trigger points. They are hypersensitive areas in tight muscle bands that can create both local pain and referred pain to other parts of the body. Research shows that trigger points are found in up to 85 percent of people with musculoskeletal problems.

Quick Summary

  • What: Thin needles are inserted into trigger points (muscle knots) to release tension
  • Difference from acupuncture: Based on Western anatomy and neurophysiology, not energy meridians
  • Evidence: Moderate to good evidence for short-term pain relief (0-12 weeks)
  • Best effect: When treatment is combined with exercises and rehabilitation
  • Common reaction: Muscle soreness for 24-48 hours, like after hard training
1

What is Dry Needling?

Dry needling is a treatment technique where I use thin, sterile single-use needles to relieve muscle pain. The word "dry" is used because the needles don't contain medication - it's pure mechanical stimulation.

The needle is inserted directly into the tense, irritable area in the muscle - the trigger point. The goal is to release the tight muscle fibers and reset the tension.

Dry Needling

  • Based on Western medicine - anatomy, physiology and neuroscience
  • Targets specific trigger points identified through clinical examination
  • Needles often go deeper into muscle tissue
  • Stimulation lasts seconds to minutes per muscle

Acupuncture

  • Based on traditional Chinese medicine
  • Focuses on energy pathways (meridians) and energy balancing
  • Diagnosis through pulse and tongue analysis
  • Needles placed along meridians for 15-30 minutes

How It Works

When a muscle becomes overloaded - through acute injury, repetitive strain or prolonged stress - parts of the muscle fiber can lock in persistent contraction. This creates an area with reduced blood supply and accumulation of waste products that cause pain.

What Does the Research Say?

Research on dry needling has grown significantly in recent years. A large umbrella review from 2023 included data from 36 systematic reviews and over 24,000 patients.

Key Research Findings

Good short-term effect Strongest evidence that treatment can reduce pain in the first 0-12 weeks. Documentation is strongest for neck pain.
Limited long-term effect alone Documentation for lasting improvement beyond 3-6 months is weaker when dry needling is used as the only treatment.
Best with combination treatment The most important finding is that dry needling provides significant additional effect when combined with exercises and other treatment.

A systematic review from 2025 confirms that dry needling is effective for short-term pain relief in chronic neck pain and improves functional outcomes, although the effect on mobility is more limited.

What Can Be Treated?

Dry needling may be relevant for trigger point-related pain in many areas:

Neck and shoulders Low back and mid-back Hip and thigh muscles Tension headache Jaw pain (TMD) Tennis elbow

Treatment response varies between people. Some notice clear improvement after one treatment, others need more. Factors like how long you've had the problems and your general health affect the result.

5

How Treatment Works

1

Examination First

I always start with a thorough examination. I need to know that the pain actually comes from the muscles and isn't due to other underlying causes. I use my hands to feel for tight muscle bands and active trigger points that reproduce your familiar pain.

2

The Treatment Itself

During treatment, I use thin, sterile single-use needles (0.16-0.30 mm thick). Most people barely notice the needle going through the skin. When the needle hits the trigger point, you may experience the characteristic twitch response - a brief, deep cramp lasting less than a second. The needle may stay in from a few seconds to 10-15 minutes.

3

After Treatment

It's normal to feel muscle soreness for 24-48 hours afterward - similar to the feeling after hard training. Drink plenty of water, keep moving gently, but avoid hard training of the treated muscle the same day.

Treatment Course

Acute problems (like a kink in the neck): Often sufficient with 1-3 treatments

Chronic conditions: May require 6-10 treatments, usually one week apart

If it doesn't help after 3-4 treatments, I try other approaches. The goal is always to make you self-sufficient as quickly as possible.

6

Safety and Risk

Dry needling is generally safe when performed by qualified healthcare professionals with thorough anatomical knowledge. As with all treatment involving needle sticks, there are risks you should know about.

Common Side Effects

  • Soreness and tenderness: Like after hard training, goes away after 1-2 days
  • Bruising: Occurs in about 8-16% of treatments
  • Fatigue: Some feel a bit heavy in the body right after

Rare Complications

  • Pneumothorax: When treating the chest region, there is a risk of hitting the lung membrane
  • Risk is minimized with good anatomical knowledge and safe needle techniques
  • I always use techniques designed to avoid the lung membrane

Seek medical attention immediately if you experience

  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain after treatment
  • Swelling, redness or fever
  • Symptoms that significantly worsen

Who should not receive treatment?

  • Have needle phobia (I have many other techniques)
  • Use strong blood thinning medications
  • Have compromised immune system or infection in the area
  • Are pregnant (especially first trimester)
  • Are under 16 years old

Frequently Asked Questions

Does it hurt?
The needle stick itself is barely noticeable. When the needle hits the muscle knot, you may feel a brief cramp (twitch). Most describe it as a "good-hurt" feeling lasting less than a second.
How many treatments will I need?
For acute problems, 1-3 treatments may be enough. For chronic problems, more may be needed, but I continuously evaluate the effect.
Do I have to get dry needling?
No, it's just one of many techniques I use. I discuss it if I think it can help, but you decide. I have many other treatment methods like massage, mobilization and exercises.
Can I exercise afterward?
Yes, you can be active. But avoid maximum lifts or very hard training of the treated muscle the same day. Light movement often helps with the soreness.
Does insurance cover treatment?
Many private health insurances cover chiropractic treatment including dry needling. Check with your insurance company.
