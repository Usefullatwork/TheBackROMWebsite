What is Rehabilitation?

The back that gives out when you try to stand up from a chair. The neck so stiff you can't look over your shoulder when reversing the car. The knee that fails on stairs. Many people live with problems that gradually limit everyday life.

Rehabilitation is targeted training and exercises that restore function after injury or for long-term problems. This differs from general training in that everything I do has a specific purpose related to your problems and goals.

Think of the body as a complex system where all parts affect each other. When one part fails, other parts compensate. Over time, this can create new problems. Rehabilitation is about identifying these patterns and rebuilding.

"Don't be a slave to methods. Methods should serve goals."

— Dr. Karel Lewit

My approach is built on the principles of Dr. Craig Liebenson, founder of First Principles of Movement and author of "Rehabilitation of the Spine: A Person-Centered Approach". His philosophy is about being principle-driven, not method-enslaved.

First Principles of Movement: The Four Principles

Liebenson studied directly under legendary clinicians like Dr. Karel Lewit and Professor Vladimir Janda from 1987, and has researched at Professor Stuart McGill's biomechanics laboratory. His four foundational principles create a structure that works regardless of whether you're a competitive athlete or want to play with your grandchildren.

1

Reassurance

"Pain doesn't necessarily mean damage – movement isn't dangerous"

This is about building trust in movement. Many who have lived with pain have developed a fear – they believe "pain = injury" and that activity is dangerous. This fear can be more limiting than the injury itself.

Example: You who are afraid to bend forward due to back pain start with small, controlled movements while sitting safely in a chair.
2

Reactivation

"Motion is lotion – movement is the best medicine"

When fear begins to subside, I introduce movement in a positive and safe way. I find "the hardest thing you can do safely" as a starting point. Movement is dosed like medicine – the right amount at the right time.

Example: Instead of avoiding all lifting due to back pain, you learn proper lifting technique with light weight and gradually build up.
3

Resilience

"Rehabilitation is training in the presence of injury"

This principle is about building robustness so you don't just return to where you were, but become stronger. I manage you toward load, not away from it. The goal is to avoid "rehab purgatory" – the state where people never really get better.

Example: You who have had jumper's knee don't just learn to run again, but develop better jumping technique and leg strength than you had before the injury.
4

Variability

"Prepare for the unpredictable"

Life is unpredictable. The final progression is about preparing you for situations you can't plan for. This is what Liebenson calls "anti-fragile" – you don't just become robust, you get better from challenges.

Example: You learn to use the traffic light system to navigate activity level based on symptoms, so you can adapt without fear.
3

The Traffic Light System: Navigating Pain

To help you distinguish between acceptable discomfort and problematic pain, I use the traffic light system. The goal is always to challenge your body to become stronger, never to threaten it.

🟢
0-3/10
Green Light

Mild, acceptable discomfort. Safe to continue.

🟡
4-6/10
Yellow Light

Discomfort increasing or feels uncertain. Modify exercise or reduce load.

🔴
7-10/10
Red Light

Unacceptable, sharp or increasing pain. Stop and reassess.

You don't just get exercises to do at home. I combine active training with manual treatment – joint treatment, soft tissue techniques and other modalities when needed to support the training process.

What Can You Expect?

1

The First Weeks

Focus on safety and restoring basic mobility through combined manual and active treatment. I build trust in movement and find your safe starting point.

2

After 4-6 Weeks

Clear functional improvement. You'll be able to do more with less worry. Home exercises become an important part of success. I begin to increase the load.

3

Realistic Expectations

Rehabilitation is rarely a straight line. There will be good and bad days. But with the right approach, the trend will be positive, and you'll learn to handle setbacks without panic.

⚠️ When Should You Seek Help Immediately?
  • Sudden, severe weakness or paralysis
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Numbness in genital area or inner thighs
  • Severe pain combined with fever or unexplained weight loss

Call emergency services or go to the emergency room immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does rehabilitation take?
There's no standard timeline, because everyone is unique. I focus on milestones, not the calendar. A milestone might be walking 10 minutes without pain, lifting shopping bags safely, or completing a workout without discomfort the next day.
Is it okay to exercise with pain?
A little muscle stiffness or mild, recognizable discomfort during a new exercise can be completely acceptable. It's often a sign that the body is working and adapting. Sharp, stabbing or increasing pain, however, is a signal to stop or adjust. Use the traffic light system as a guide.
Do I have to stop the activities I love?
The goal is almost never for you to stop activities for good – on the contrary, the goal is to get you back to them. Often it's about temporary modification, building a stronger foundation, and then gradual reintroduction with improved technique.
What separates this approach from regular physiotherapy?
First Principles of Movement focuses on being person-centered rather than protocol-driven. I start from your goals and concerns, not just the diagnosis. The combination of manual treatment and active rehabilitation is adapted to you – not the other way around.
Do I need a referral?
No, chiropractors have been primary contacts in Norway since 2006. You can book an appointment directly without a referral from a doctor.
