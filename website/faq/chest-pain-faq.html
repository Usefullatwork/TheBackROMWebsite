Chest Pain FAQ

Answers to the most common questions about chest/thoracic pain, rib pain and treatment

What is thoracic pain?

Pain in the middle part of the back, between the neck and lower back. Often localized between the shoulder blades. Can give a feeling of locking and pain with deep inhalation or rotation.

What is pain between the shoulder blades?

Usually muscle tension or irritation of joints between the thoracic vertebrae and ribs. Often related to poor posture and office work. Home office since 2020 has increased these complaints.

What is rib locking?

When the joint between a rib and the thoracic vertebra becomes irritated or locked. Causes local pain that can worsen with breathing, coughing, or movement. Often feels like a sharp stab under the shoulder blade.

Can thoracic pain affect breathing?

Yes. Stiffness in the joints between ribs and vertebrae can limit chest movement, giving a feeling of not getting a full breath. Treatment of these joints often provides rapid relief.

Can chest pain come from the back?

Yes. Posterior rib joints can radiate pain around to the chest front. Costochondritis (inflammation of the cartilage in front) also causes chest pain.

Important: Chest pain should always be evaluated to rule out heart problems.

Can home office cause pain between the shoulder blades?

Yes. Poor ergonomics at home is a common cause. Many sit at the kitchen table or sofa without proper support. Correct screen height and regular breaks are important.

How do I distinguish heart pain from muscle pain?

Musculoskeletal chest pain: Affected by movement, breathing, or pressure on the area.

Heart pain: Often crushing/pressure, can radiate to left arm/jaw, accompanied by breathing difficulties or sweating. If in doubt - call 113.

Research shows that 20% referred for heart exams actually have musculoskeletal pain.

Which exercises help with thoracic pain?

Thoracic rotations, foam roller mobilization, and stretching of chest muscles. Scapular exercises and general strengthening of the upper back are also useful.

⚠️ RED FLAGS: When is thoracic pain dangerous?

Call 113 (emergency) if chest pain:

  • Radiates to left arm or jaw
  • Is accompanied by breathing difficulties or sweating
  • Occurs with exertion
  • Feels like pressure/squeezing over the chest

If in doubt, it is always right to call.
